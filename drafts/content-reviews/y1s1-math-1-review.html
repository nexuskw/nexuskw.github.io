Lesson 01 · Functions, units, and engineering magnitudes

Lecture

§1 · Numbers that carry units

Engineering mathematics begins one step before calculus: every quantity you will ever differentiate or integrate carries a unit, and the units obey algebra just as the numbers do. When a pump curve says head is 42 m, that number is meaningless until you know it multiplies through \( P = \rho g Q H \) to give watts:

\[ [P] = \frac{\mathrm{kg}}{\mathrm{m}^3}\cdot\frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}^2}\cdot\frac{\mathrm{m}^3}{\mathrm{s}}\cdot \mathrm{m} = \frac{\mathrm{kg\,m^2}}{\mathrm{s}^3} = \mathrm{W} \tag{1} \]

Check the units first and half of all calculation errors die before they are born. This is the dimensional analysis discipline: both sides of any physical equation must carry identical dimensions, and arguments of \( \sin \), \( \ln \), \( e^x \) must be dimensionless.

§2 · Functions as machine behavior

A function \( f(x) \) is a machine's behavior written down: give it a flow, it returns a pressure drop; give it a time, it returns a temperature. The pump curve \( H(Q) \), the motor torque curve \( T(\omega) \), the cooling curve \( T(t) \) — the whole plant is a library of functions. Domain matters physically: \( H(Q) \) exists only for \( Q \ge 0 \), and beyond runout flow the curve is not merely undefined but forbidden.

§3 · Magnitudes and scales

An engineer estimates before calculating. Powers of ten are the estimating language: a 100 m³/h pump moves \(\sim\)28 L/s; a kilowatt is ten 100 W lamps; a bar is \(\sim\)10 m of water column. On the plant floor this means you can smell a wrong answer: if a small transfer pump computes to 300 kW, the arithmetic is wrong before you re-check it — the machine on the skid is the size of a suitcase, and suitcases don't dissipate 300 kW.

§4 · The unit-check working method

Knowing that units must balance is not the same as having a method. Use this three-step protocol on every equation you meet, in this course and every one after it. Step one: write the equation symbolically and substitute units, not numbers — run the algebra on the units alone and confirm both sides match, exactly as equation (1) did for pump power. Step two: convert every input to base SI before any arithmetic: flows to m³/s, pressures to Pa, lengths to m. Most real-world calculation errors are unit errors, and most unit errors happen when kPa meets Pa or m³/h meets m³/s in the same line. Step three: after computing, translate the answer back into the working units of the trade — kW, bar, m³/h — and test it against an order-of-magnitude estimate before you write it down.

Two traps deserve names. The gauge trap: gauge and absolute pressure differ by one atmosphere, and equations built on absolute pressure quietly fail when fed gauge readings. The prefix trap: a dropped factor of 1000 from k, M, or m survives every later step and grows more embarrassing the longer it lives. Scientific notation is the antidote — write \( 2.5\times10^5 \) Pa and exponents stay visible and checkable.

Closing. Units are algebra, functions are machine behavior, and order-of-magnitude sense is the cheapest error detector you will ever own. Every lesson that follows leans on all three.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Hydraulic and shaft power

Problem. A cooling-water pump at a Gulf petrochemical utility area delivers \( Q = 120\ \mathrm{m^3/h} \) of water against a total head \( H = 42\ \mathrm{m} \). Pump efficiency is 65%. Given: \( \rho = 1000\ \mathrm{kg/m^3},\ g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). Find: hydraulic power and shaft power.

Solution. (1) Convert flow: \( Q = 120/3600 = 0.0333\ \mathrm{m^3/s} \). (2) Hydraulic power: \( P_h = \rho g Q H = 1000 \times 9.81 \times 0.0333 \times 42 = 13.7\ \mathrm{kW} \). (3) Shaft power: \( P_s = P_h/\eta = 13.7/0.65 = 21.1\ \mathrm{kW} \).

Answer: \( P_h \approx 13.7\ \mathrm{kW},\ P_s \approx 21.1\ \mathrm{kW} \). Sanity check: a 22 kW motor frame on a 120 m³/h duty is entirely ordinary — had we forgotten the 3600, we'd have gotten 49 MW, a power-station number.

SOLVE 2

Unit conversion under pressure

Problem. A compressed-air header reads 6.0 bar(g). Find: the gauge pressure in kPa and psi, and the approximate equivalent water column.

Solution. (1) \( 6.0\ \mathrm{bar} = 600\ \mathrm{kPa} \). (2) \( 600 \times 0.145 = 87\ \mathrm{psi} \). (3) Water column: \( h = P/\rho g = 600{,}000 / (1000 \times 9.81) = 61.2\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: 600 kPa ≈ 87 psi ≈ 61 m H₂O. Sanity check: the 10 m-per-bar rule gives 60 m — our exact figure lands within 2%.

SOLVE 3

Scaling a transmitter signal

Problem. A level transmitter on a 2.5 m measuring range outputs a linear 4–20 mA signal: 0 m maps to 4 mA, 2.5 m maps to 20 mA. Find: the function \( L(I) \) converting current to level, the level at a reading of 11.2 mA, and the function's physical domain.

Solution. (1) The span is 16 mA for 2.5 m, so \( L(I) = 2.5\,\dfrac{I - 4}{16} \) metres, with \( I \) in mA. (2) At \( I = 11.2 \): \( L = 2.5 \times 7.2/16 = 1.125\ \mathrm{m} \). (3) Domain: \( 4 \le I \le 20 \) mA — readings below 4 mA are not levels but fault signals, which is precisely why the live-zero convention exists.

Answer: \( L(I) = 2.5(I-4)/16 \); 1.125 m at 11.2 mA; domain 4–20 mA. Sanity check: 11.2 mA sits at \( 7.2/16 = 45\% \) of the current span, and 45% of 2.5 m is 1.125 m — the function agrees with the proportion.

MCQ 1

A transfer pump delivers \( Q = 90\ \mathrm{m^3/h} \) of water (\( \rho = 1000\ \mathrm{kg/m^3} \)) against a total head \( H = 25\ \mathrm{m} \), with pump efficiency \( \eta = 75\% \). Taking \( g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), what shaft power must the motor supply?

  1. \( 4.6\ \mathrm{kW} \)
  2. \( 6.1\ \mathrm{kW} \)
  3. \( 8.2\ \mathrm{kW} \) ✓
  4. \( 29.4\ \mathrm{MW} \)

Convert the flow first: \( Q = 90/3600 = 0.025\ \mathrm{m^3/s} \), so the hydraulic power is \( P_h = \rho g Q H = 1000 \times 9.81 \times 0.025 \times 25 = 6.13\ \mathrm{kW} \), and the shaft power is \( P_s = P_h/\eta = 6.13/0.75 = 8.2\ \mathrm{kW} \). Efficiency divides rather than multiplies: the motor must supply more power than the water receives, which eliminates 4.6 kW (the classic multiply-by-\( \eta \) error) and 6.1 kW (efficiency ignored). The 29.4 MW figure comes from leaving \( Q \) in \( \mathrm{m^3/h} \) — an order-of-magnitude check rejects it at once, since a small transfer pump is not a power station.

MCQ 2

A tank cooling curve is modelled as \( T(t) = T_\infty + (T_0 - T_\infty)\,e^{-t/\tau} \), where \( t \) is time in seconds. What does dimensional homogeneity require of the constant \( \tau \)?

  1. \( \tau \) must carry units of seconds, so that the exponent \( t/\tau \) is dimensionless ✓
  2. \( \tau \) must carry units of kelvin, to match the temperatures in the equation
  3. \( \tau \) must be a pure number, since constants in physical equations are dimensionless
  4. \( \tau \) may carry any units, because the exponential function converts them automatically

The argument of \( e^x \) — like those of \( \sin \) and \( \ln \) — must be dimensionless, so \( \tau \) must share the dimension of \( t \): time. The kelvin answer commits the classic error of matching the constant to the equation's output units instead of to the argument it sits inside; the temperature dimensions are already balanced by the prefactor \( (T_0 - T_\infty) \). No function absorbs units, and constants in physical equations routinely carry dimensions — homogeneity is checked term by term and argument by argument.

MCQ 3

A water pump moves \( 100\ \mathrm{m^3/h} \) against 30 m of head, with pump efficiency around 70%. Which shaft-power magnitude is plausible?

  1. about 120 W
  2. about 12 kW ✓
  3. about 120 kW
  4. about 1.2 MW

Hydraulic power is \( \rho g Q H = 1000 \times 9.81 \times (100/3600) \times 30 \approx 8.2\ \mathrm{kW} \); dividing by the 0.70 efficiency gives ≈12 kW of shaft power. The 120 W and 1.2 MW answers are the classic unit slips — forgetting the 3600 in the flow conversion moves the result by exactly these factors. Order-of-magnitude sense is the fastest error detector: a pump this size is a two-person lift, not a power-station item.

MCQ 4

A chilled-water line gauge reads \( 250\ \mathrm{kPa} \). Using \( \rho = 1000\ \mathrm{kg/m^3} \) and \( g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), what height of water column does this pressure correspond to?

  1. \( 25.5\ \mathrm{m} \) ✓
  2. \( 0.025\ \mathrm{m} \)
  3. \( 2.55\ \mathrm{m} \)
  4. \( 250\ \mathrm{m} \)

Convert to base units before substituting: \( h = P/(\rho g) = 250000/(1000 \times 9.81) = 25.5\ \mathrm{m} \). The rule of thumb that 1 bar is roughly 10 m of water confirms it: 250 kPa is 2.5 bar, hence about 25 m. The 0.025 m answer is the classic slip of feeding kPa into an SI formula without converting to Pa, and 250 m follows from misconverting 250 kPa into 25 bar — both fail the order-of-magnitude sanity check for a building chilled-water loop.

MCQ 5

An energy report lists a consumption of 3.6 MJ. What is that in kilowatt-hours?

  1. 1.0 kWh ✓
  2. 3.6 kWh
  3. 0.36 kWh
  4. 60 kWh

One kilowatt-hour is 1000 W sustained for 3600 s: \( 1000 \times 3600 = 3.6\times10^6\ \mathrm{J} = 3.6\ \mathrm{MJ} \) — so 3.6 MJ is exactly 1.0 kWh. Choosing 3.6 kWh treats MJ and kWh as the same unit with different labels, the classic energy-billing slip; the two differ by precisely the 3600 seconds in an hour.

Lesson 02 · Limits and continuity

Lecture

§1 · Why limits exist in a plant

A limit answers one question: what value does a function approach as its input approaches a point? Formally, \( \lim_{x\to a} f(x) = L \) means we can force \( f(x) \) arbitrarily close to \( L \) by taking \( x \) close enough to \( a \). The definition sounds abstract; the plant meaning is concrete: what does the sensor settle toward, what does the ratio become, what happens at the operating edge?

§2 · The ratio that defines everything

The most important limit in this course is a ratio of vanishing quantities,

\[ \lim_{\theta\to 0}\frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} = 1 \tag{1} \]

Try it numerically: at \( \theta = 0.1 \) rad the ratio is 0.9983; at 0.01, 0.99998. The two quantities shrink together but their ratio converges — that is the whole mental model for the derivative in the next lesson, where a vanishing rise over a vanishing run converges to a slope.

§3 · Continuity — and what it means for signals

\( f \) is continuous at \( a \) when \( \lim_{x\to a} f(x) = f(a) \): no jump, no hole, no blow-up. Physical processes — temperature, level, pressure — are continuous; their measurements need not be. A thermocouple that jumps 40 °C between scans hasn't witnessed magic; it has witnessed electrical noise, a loose connection, or a step change in what is being touched. Knowing which physical variables can and cannot jump is a diagnostic tool: pressure can step (valve slam), metal temperature cannot.

One-sided limits close the lesson: a relay approaching its trip point from below and from above sees two different worlds, \( \lim_{x\to a^-} \) and \( \lim_{x\to a^+} \). Hysteresis is one-sided limits wearing work clothes.

§4 · How to evaluate a limit — the decision tree

Faced with a limit, work a fixed sequence. First, substitute. If the function is continuous there, the limit is the value and you are done — most limits in practice end here. Second, if substitution returns 0/0, the form is telling you a common factor is hiding: factor polynomials, or multiply by the conjugate when square roots appear, cancel, and substitute again. Third, check the sides. When the expression involves absolute values, piecewise definitions, or a physical threshold, compute the left and right limits separately; the two-sided limit exists only when they agree. Fourth, verify numerically. Evaluate the expression at inputs marching toward the point — 0.1, 0.01, 0.001 away — and watch the trend. This is not cheating; it is how an engineer confirms an algebraic result the same way a technician confirms a calibration: with converging evidence.

Use continuity as a diagnostic tool the same disciplined way. Before blaming a sensor or believing a spike, ask which physical variables can genuinely jump (pressure, force, current) and which are integrals of finite flows and therefore cannot (metal temperature, tank level, accumulated volume). The mathematics sorts plant events from instrument events before the first work order is written.

Closing. Limits formalize approach; continuity formalizes no surprises. Tomorrow they combine into the derivative.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

A hole, not a wall

Problem. Evaluate \( \displaystyle\lim_{x\to 3}\frac{x^2-9}{x-3} \), which appears as a 0/0 form. Find: the limit.

Solution. (1) Factor: \( \frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3} = x+3 \) for \( x \ne 3 \). (2) The simplified function is continuous, so the limit is \( 3+3 = 6 \).

Answer: 6. Sanity check: at \( x=3.001 \), the original ratio gives \( (9.006-9)/0.001 = 6.001 \) — converging on 6 as promised.

SOLVE 2

Sensor step or process step?

Problem. During pasteurizer start-up at a dairy filling plant, the product-outlet RTD logs 71.8, 71.9, 71.9, 112.4, 72.0 °C at 1 s scan. Find: is the 112.4 reading a process event or an instrument event, and which continuity argument decides it?

Solution. (1) Product temperature obeys a thermal time constant of tens of seconds: its true trajectory is continuous and cannot traverse +40 °C and −40 °C within 2 s. (2) An instrument channel — connector, noise burst, scan glitch — can jump discontinuously. (3) Therefore the spike violates physical continuity but not signal behavior: instrument event.

Answer: single-scan instrument artifact; flag the channel, don't chase a process excursion. Sanity check: energy to heat the product stream 40 °C in 1 s would exceed the pasteurizer's entire installed heating duty by an order of magnitude.

SOLVE 3

A limit that will become a slope

Problem. Evaluate \( \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sqrt{4+h}-2}{h} \), which substitution renders as 0/0. (Next lesson this exact limit will be recognized as the derivative of \( \sqrt{x} \) at \( x=4 \).)

Solution. (1) Multiply by the conjugate: \( \dfrac{\sqrt{4+h}-2}{h}\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{4+h}+2}{\sqrt{4+h}+2} = \dfrac{(4+h)-4}{h(\sqrt{4+h}+2)} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{4+h}+2} \). (2) Substitute \( h = 0 \): \( 1/(2+2) = 1/4 \).

Answer: 1/4. Sanity check: at \( h = 0.01 \), \( (\sqrt{4.01}-2)/0.01 = 0.2498 \) — marching on 0.25 exactly as the algebra promises.

MCQ 1

Evaluate \( \displaystyle\lim_{x\to 4}\frac{x^2-16}{x-4} \). Direct substitution gives the indeterminate form 0/0.

  1. 0
  2. 16
  3. 8 ✓
  4. The limit does not exist

A 0/0 form signals a common factor, not a missing limit: \( x^2-16 = (x-4)(x+4) \), so the ratio equals \( x+4 \) everywhere except at \( x=4 \), and the limit is 8. Choosing "does not exist" is the classic division-by-zero reflex — the function value is undefined at 4, but a limit describes approach, not arrival, and it can exist exactly where the value does not. The answer 0 comes from reading a 0/0 form as literally zero; 16 comes from evaluating only the squared term after an illegal cancellation.

MCQ 2

With \( \theta \) measured in radians, evaluate \( \displaystyle\lim_{\theta\to 0}\frac{\sin 3\theta}{\theta} \).

  1. 3 ✓
  2. 1
  3. 0
  4. 1/3

Rescale so the sine's argument and the denominator vanish as the same quantity: \( \frac{\sin 3\theta}{\theta} = 3\cdot\frac{\sin 3\theta}{3\theta} \to 3\cdot 1 = 3 \). Answering 1 is the classic pattern-match error: the standard limit \( \sin u/u \to 1 \) applies only when numerator argument and denominator are identical, which they are not here. The answer 0 comes from substituting \( \theta = 0 \) into the numerator alone, and 1/3 from performing the rescaling upside-down; note the whole computation also presumes radians, since the standard limit is false in degrees.

MCQ 3

Evaluate \( \displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{|x|}{x} \).

  1. 0
  2. 1
  3. −1
  4. The limit does not exist ✓

From the right, \( |x|/x = 1 \); from the left, \( |x|/x = -1 \). The one-sided limits disagree, so the two-sided limit does not exist — the graph is a genuine step. This is the archetype of threshold behavior: whenever an expression contains an absolute value or a piecewise switch, check the sides separately before declaring a limit. Answering 1 or −1 reports only one approach direction; answering 0 averages two worlds the function never occupies.

MCQ 4

A compressor trips and the check valve on its discharge line slams shut. Four plant variables are logged at a 1 s scan. Which one can genuinely change as a step at the trip instant — a true physical discontinuity rather than an instrument artifact?

  1. Casing metal temperature of the compressor
  2. Line pressure downstream of the slamming check valve ✓
  3. Liquid level in the suction knockout drum
  4. Pipe-wall temperature of the discharge line

A slamming valve imposes a pressure step that is genuine at any practical scan rate — pressure is the lesson's example of a physical quantity that can truly jump. Metal temperatures and liquid level are accumulations of finite fluxes (heat through thermal mass, flow into a vessel), so their true trajectories are continuous, with time constants far longer than one scan. Selecting a temperature channel is the classic error of mistaking a logged jump for a process event: a step in a thermally massive variable indicates an instrument artifact, exactly as in the pasteurizer RTD case.

MCQ 5

The function \( g(x) = \dfrac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} \) is undefined at \( x = 1 \). What kind of discontinuity lives there?

  1. Removable — the limit exists (it is 2) but the definition misses the point ✓
  2. A jump — the one-sided limits disagree
  3. Infinite — the function blows up
  4. None — the function is continuous at 1

Factoring gives \( g(x) = x + 1 \) for \( x \ne 1 \), so both one-sided limits equal 2: the graph is a straight line with a single hole at \( (1, 2) \) — a removable discontinuity. It is not a jump (the sides agree) and not a blow-up (nothing grows without bound); calling it continuous confuses the limit existing with the value existing, which is exactly the distinction this lesson draws.

Lesson 03 · The derivative as a rate

Lecture

§1 · From average rate to instantaneous rate

Between Monday and Friday a bearing housing warmed from 62 °C to 74 °C: average rate 3 °C/day. But right now, this hour, how fast is it heating? Squeeze the interval and the average rate converges — Lesson 2's limit — to the instantaneous rate:

\[ \frac{dT}{dt} = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0}\frac{T(t+\Delta t)-T(t)}{\Delta t} \tag{1} \]

That is the derivative: the local slope of the trend, the speed of change at an instant. Units divide: °C per hour, m³ per second, mm/s of vibration per week. A derivative always has units, and always tells a maintenance story.

§2 · The derivative as the trend's confession

Condition monitoring is derivative-reading as a profession. A flat vibration trend with \( dV/dt \approx 0 \) buys you time; the same amplitude with a steep positive slope buys you a work order. Two machines at identical 4 mm/s are in different worlds if one arrived there over three years and the other over three weeks. Value tells you where it is; the derivative tells you where it's going.

§3 · Computing from a model

Given a fitted trend \( T(t) = 40 + 0.8t + 0.05t^2 \) (°C, hours), the power rule (proved next lesson) gives \( T'(t) = 0.8 + 0.1t \): the heating accelerates, because the rate itself grows. Set \( T'(t) \) against an alarm rate and you get a time-to-act, not just a number: solve \( 0.8+0.1t = 2.5 \) → \( t = 17\ \mathrm{h} \). The derivative converts a curve into a deadline.

§4 · Computing derivatives from data — the working method

Real trends arrive as samples, not formulas, so the working derivative is a finite difference. From the two latest points you can only form the forward-looking secant \( (f_{now} - f_{prev})/\Delta t \); mid-record, prefer the central difference \( (f_{i+1} - f_{i-1})/(2\Delta t) \), which cancels the first layer of error and tracks the true tangent far better on smooth data. Three disciplines make the estimates trustworthy. State the units — a slope is always something per something, and writing mm/s per week forces the physical meaning into view. Respect noise: differentiation amplifies it, so difference over intervals long enough for the real change to dominate the scatter; a derivative computed from two noisy readings an hour apart is an opinion, not a measurement. Distinguish model from data: when a curve has been fitted, differentiate the model symbolically and use the data-side difference as its check — when the two disagree badly, the fit, the data, or the machine has changed, and every one of those is worth knowing.

Then read the result the way §2 taught: the value says where the machine is, the derivative says where it is going, and the difference between those two sentences is the difference between recording a number and making a decision.

Closing. The derivative is a limit of average rates, it carries units, and on the plant floor it is the difference between watching a number and reading its intent.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Time-to-alarm from a fitted trend

Problem. A gearbox housing temperature trend fits \( T(t) = 40 + 0.8t + 0.05t^2 \) (°C, \(t\) in hours since the shift began). Site practice flags any heating rate above 2.5 °C/h. Find: the rate at \( t=10\ \mathrm{h} \) and the time the rate limit is reached.

Solution. (1) Differentiate: \( T'(t) = 0.8 + 0.1t \). (2) At \( t=10 \): \( T'(10) = 0.8 + 1.0 = 1.8\ \mathrm{°C/h} \) — under the limit. (3) Set \( 0.8 + 0.1t = 2.5 \Rightarrow t = 17\ \mathrm{h} \).

Answer: 1.8 °C/h now; the 2.5 °C/h threshold arrives at \( t = 17\ \mathrm{h} \). Sanity check: the rate grows 0.1 °C/h every hour; from 1.8 at \(t=10\), seven more hours adds 0.7 → 2.5. Consistent.

SOLVE 2

Filling rate from level

Problem. A vertical cylindrical tank, diameter 3.0 m, shows level rising at 0.40 m per minute. Find: the inflow in m³/h.

Solution. (1) \( V = A h \) with \( A = \pi D^2/4 = \pi (3.0)^2/4 = 7.07\ \mathrm{m^2} \). (2) Chain of rates: \( \dot V = A \, \dot h = 7.07 \times 0.40 = 2.83\ \mathrm{m^3/min} \). (3) Convert: \( 2.83 \times 60 = 170\ \mathrm{m^3/h} \).

Answer: ≈170 m³/h. Sanity check: the tank gains 24 m³ every 10 minutes — about 3.4 m of level per hour, which a 3 m-diameter receiving tank plausibly does during transfer. Units chain cleanly: m² × m/min → m³/min.

SOLVE 3

A derivative from historian samples

Problem. A vibration trend is sampled weekly: \( V = 2.0,\ 2.3,\ 2.9 \) mm/s at \( t = 0, 1, 2 \) weeks. Find: the rate of change at \( t = 1 \) week by forward difference and by central difference, with units, and state which estimate to trust on a smooth trend.

Solution. (1) Forward difference: \( (2.9 - 2.3)/1 = 0.60 \) mm/s per week. (2) Central difference: \( (2.9 - 2.0)/2 = 0.45 \) mm/s per week. (3) On smooth data the central difference is the better tangent estimate — its first-order errors cancel; the forward difference looks half a step into the future and overstates a rising, accelerating trend.

Answer: forward 0.60, central 0.45 mm/s per week; prefer the central estimate mid-record. Sanity check: the weekly increments are 0.3 then 0.6 — the instantaneous rate at \( t=1 \) should sit between them, and 0.45 does.

MCQ 1

A machine's vibration trend has been fitted as \( V(t) = 1.2 + 0.03t^2 \) (mm/s, with \( t \) in weeks since installation). What is the instantaneous rate of change of vibration at \( t = 5 \) weeks?

  1. 0.30 mm/s per week ✓
  2. 0.15 mm/s per week
  3. 1.95 mm/s per week
  4. 0.06 mm/s per week

Differentiating gives \( V'(t) = 0.06t \), so \( V'(5) = 0.30 \) mm/s per week. The distractor 0.15 comes from dropping the factor of 2 when differentiating \( t^2 \), and 1.95 is the value \( V(5) \) itself — confusing where the trend is with how fast it is moving. Answering 0.06 means the derivative was formed correctly but never evaluated at \( t = 5 \).

MCQ 2

A pump bearing's temperature follows the fitted trend \( T(t) = 35 + 1.2t + 0.04t^2 \) (°C, \( t \) in hours since start-up). Site practice requires intervention once the heating rate reaches 3.0 °C/h. At what time does the rate reach that limit?

  1. \( t = 22.5 \) h ✓
  2. \( t = 45 \) h
  3. \( t = 37.5 \) h
  4. \( t = 75 \) h

The rate is \( T'(t) = 1.2 + 0.08t \); setting \( 1.2 + 0.08t = 3.0 \) gives \( t = 1.8/0.08 = 22.5 \) h — the derivative turns the curve into a deadline. The distractor 45 h comes from differentiating \( 0.04t^2 \) as \( 0.04t \) (dropping the factor of 2), and 37.5 h comes from dividing 3.0 by 0.08 without first subtracting the constant rate term 1.2 °C/h.

MCQ 3

A bearing temperature \( T(t) \) is logged with \( T \) in °C and \( t \) in hours. What are the units of \( T'(t) \)?

  1. °C
  2. °C/h ✓
  3. h/°C
  4. It is dimensionless

The derivative is a limit of \( \Delta T/\Delta t \), so its units are the ratio of the units: °C per hour. A derivative always carries units, and stating them is not pedantry — it is what turns "the trend is 1.8" into the actionable "the housing is heating at 1.8 °C every hour." Answering °C confuses the rate with the reading; dimensionless confuses mathematics with the pure-number world it models.

MCQ 4

A recorded trend is continuous at \( t = t_0 \) but shows a sharp corner there, like \( |t - t_0| \)-shaped behaviour after a sudden load change. Which statement is correct?

  1. The function is continuous at \( t_0 \) but not differentiable there: the one-sided secant slopes converge to different values, so no single tangent slope exists. ✓
  2. Continuity at \( t_0 \) guarantees differentiability at \( t_0 \), so the derivative must exist.
  3. The function is differentiable at \( t_0 \) but cannot be continuous there.
  4. The derivative at \( t_0 \) exists and equals the average of the left-hand and right-hand slopes.

At a corner the limit defining the derivative fails: the left and right difference quotients approach different values, yet the function value has no jump — continuous but not differentiable. The classic error is reversing the implication: differentiability implies continuity, never the converse. Averaging the two one-sided slopes is a tempting repair, but the defining limit simply does not exist, so no derivative can be assigned.

MCQ 5

Between \( t = 2 \) h and \( t = 6 \) h a trend rises from 10 to 22 units — an average rate of 3 units/h. What can be said about the instantaneous rate at \( t = 4 \) h?

  1. It is exactly 3 units/h
  2. It is at least 3 units/h
  3. It cannot be determined from the average alone ✓
  4. It is zero, since 4 h is the midpoint

An average over an interval constrains nothing at any particular interior instant: the trend could be rising at 6 units/h at \( t=4 \) and crawling elsewhere, or vice versa. (The mean value theorem promises the instantaneous rate equals 3 somewhere in the interval — but not at any point you get to choose.) Reading an interval average as a point value is the classic trend-review error this lesson exists to retire.

Lesson 04 · Differentiation rules and the chain rule

Lecture

§1 · The rules — machinery for slopes

Yesterday's limit definition is correct and slow. Today we industrialize it. For constants \(c\), functions \(u, v\):

\[ (cu)' = cu', \qquad (u+v)' = u' + v', \qquad (x^n)' = nx^{n-1} \tag{1} \] \[ (uv)' = u'v + uv', \qquad \left(\frac{u}{v}\right)' = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2} \tag{2} \]

Each is proved once from the limit and used forever. The power rule follows from expanding \( (x+\Delta x)^n \) and watching every term above first order vanish with \( \Delta x \) — the same "higher-order terms die" argument that engineering approximation lives on.

§2 · The chain rule — rates through a chain of causes

Plant variables cause each other in chains: valve position sets flow, flow sets level, level sets pressure. If \( y = f(u) \) and \( u = g(x) \):

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx} \tag{3} \]

Rates multiply along the chain. This single rule powers every sensitivity argument you will make: how much does discharge pressure move per percent of speed? Differentiate through the chain of dependencies and multiply the local sensitivities.

§3 · Relative rates — the engineer's favorite

Divide a derivative by its function and you get relative sensitivity. For a fan law \( P = kN^3 \), logarithmic differentiation gives \( \frac{dP}{P} = 3\frac{dN}{N} \): a 2% speed increase costs ≈6% more power ((1.02)³ = 1.061 exactly). This is why slowing a fan or pump slightly is the cheapest energy project in any plant — the cube works for you on the way down.

§4 · Choosing the rule — structure before symbols

Differentiation errors rarely come from forgetting a rule; they come from misreading the structure of the expression. So classify before you differentiate. Is the expression a sum of powers? The power rule handles each term independently — after rewriting roots and reciprocals as powers, \( \sqrt{x} = x^{1/2} \), \( 1/x = x^{-1} \). Is it two varying quantities multiplied — force times velocity, pressure times flow? That is a genuine product: product rule. A ratio with the variable in the denominator? Quotient rule, with the subtraction order fixed. A function of a function — something raised to a power, an exponential of an expression? Chain rule, differentiating outer times inner, working from the outside in.

For the power laws that fill engineering — \( P = kN^3 \), \( \Delta P = kQ^2 \) — logarithmic differentiation earns its keep: take logs, differentiate, and relative rates appear directly, \( dP/P = n\,dN/N \). That one line is the professional's sensitivity language: percent out per percent in. Keep the honest distinction from §3 visible — the relative-rate figure is a first-order estimate, exact only in the limit; for large changes, compute the ratio exactly and let the linear estimate serve as its sanity check.

Finally, make numerical verification a reflex. Any symbolic derivative can be audited in ten seconds: evaluate the function at \( x \) and at \( x + 0.001 \), divide the difference by 0.001, and compare with the formula's prediction. Agreement to three figures means the algebra survived; disagreement means a dropped inner derivative or a sign error is hiding in the work. Professionals check their derivatives the way welders check their fit-up — before the error is buried under everything built on top of it.

Closing. Rules (1)–(2) mechanize differentiation; the chain rule (3) walks rates through cause-and-effect; relative rates turn calculus into percentage talk that managers act on.

SOLVE 1

Chain rule, straight up

Problem. \( y = (3x^2+2)^4 \). Find: \( y'(1) \).

Solution. (1) Outer: \( \frac{d}{du}u^4 = 4u^3 \) with \( u = 3x^2+2 \). (2) Inner: \( u' = 6x \). (3) Chain: \( y' = 4(3x^2+2)^3 \cdot 6x = 24x(3x^2+2)^3 \). (4) At \( x=1 \): \( 24 \times 5^3 = 24 \times 125 = 3000 \).

Answer: 3000. Sanity check: numerically, \( y(1.001) - y(1) \approx 628.0\!-\!625 = 3.0 \) over \( \Delta x = 0.001 \) → slope ≈ 3000. Confirmed.

SOLVE 2

The affinity-law energy argument

Problem. A 90 kW cooling fan runs at fixed speed with dampers throttling. A VFD retrofit would slow it 10%. Using the fan law \( P = kN^3 \), find the approximate and exact power saving.

Solution. (1) Relative rates: \( dP/P = 3\,dN/N = 3 \times (-10\%) = -30\% \) (first-order estimate). (2) Exact: \( (0.9)^3 = 0.729 \) → 27.1% saving. (3) Power: \( 90 \times 0.271 = 24.4\ \mathrm{kW} \) saved continuously.

Answer: ≈24 kW (exact 27.1%, linear estimate 30%). Sanity check: the linear estimate overshoots because the cube curve bends — for a 10% change the two agree within a few points, exactly what a first-order method promises.

SOLVE 3

Power through the product rule

Problem. During a controlled winch lower-away, cable tension falls as \( F(t) = 200 - 5t \) N while payout speed rises as \( v(t) = 2 + 0.3t \) m/s. Mechanical power is \( P = Fv \). Find: \( P'(10) \) and interpret its sign.

Solution. (1) Product rule: \( P' = F'v + Fv' = (-5)(2 + 0.3t) + (200 - 5t)(0.3) \). (2) At \( t = 10 \): \( (-5)(5) + (150)(0.3) = -25 + 45 = +20 \) W/s. (3) Interpretation: tension is falling, but the speed growth outweighs it — power is still rising at 20 watts per second.

Answer: \( P'(10) = +20 \) W/s; power increasing. Sanity check: \( P(10) = 150 \times 5 = 750 \) W and \( P(11) = 145 \times 5.3 = 768.5 \) W — a rise of 18.5 W over the next second, consistent with a +20 W/s instantaneous rate that is itself easing off.

Lesson 05 · Exponentials, logarithms, and growth/decay

Lecture

§1 · The function that is its own derivative

One function grows at a rate equal to its own size: \( \frac{d}{dt}e^{t} = e^{t} \). Scale time by a constant and \( \frac{d}{dt}e^{kt} = k e^{kt} \): growth or decay proportional to the current amount. That single property makes \( e^{kt} \) the native language of charging capacitors, cooling castings, decaying vibration transients, draining accumulators, and compounding costs.

§2 · Decay with an offset — the plant's favorite curve

A hot object doesn't decay to zero; it decays to ambient. The working form is

\[ T(t) = T_\infty + (T_0 - T_\infty)\,e^{-t/\tau} \tag{1} \]

where \( \tau \) is the time constant: after one \( \tau \), 63% of the gap to ambient is closed; after 3\( \tau \), 95%; after 5\( \tau \), it's over. Ask "how many time constants?" and any first-order transient becomes readable at a glance.

§3 · Logarithms — solving for time

The logarithm inverts the exponential; it is how you extract when:

\[ t = \tau \,\ln\!\frac{T_0 - T_\infty}{T(t) - T_\infty} \tag{2} \]

Logs also compress scale (decibels, pH, Richter) and straighten exponentials: plot \( \ln(T - T_\infty) \) against \( t \) and first-order data falls on a line whose slope is \( -1/\tau \). Fitting a straight line to a log plot is how you measure a time constant from plant data.

§4 · The transient protocol

Every first-order transient yields to the same four questions, asked in order. What is the destination? Identify \( T_\infty \) — ambient, setpoint, asymptote — because everything is measured as a gap from it. What is the gap? Compute the current excess over the destination; the transient's whole story is this gap shrinking. What is the tempo? Get \( \tau \) — from a datasheet, from a fit, or from data via the semilog method of §3: plot \( \ln(\text{gap}) \) against time, and the slope is \( -1/\tau \). When? Turn any target into time with \( t = \tau\,\ln(\text{gap}_0/\text{gap}_{target}) \), or count in the coarse currency — one \( \tau \) closes 63% of the gap, three close 95%, five end the story.

The same machinery runs upward for growth. Compounding at rate \( r \) per period doubles in \( \ln 2/\ln(1+r) \) periods — the honest version of the rule of 70 — and any quantity that grows in proportion to itself, from an escalating tariff to a fouling deposit, is the exponential wearing different clothes. When data arrives, straighten it first: if the semilog plot is not a line, the process is not first-order, and no amount of \( \tau \)-talk will make it one. The plot is the test; the constants come after.

Know the honest failure modes too. A curve on the semilog plot usually means two time constants stacked — a fast surface effect riding on a slow bulk one — and a flat early section means deadtime: transport delay before the response begins. Both are information, not nuisance. Report what the data shows, fit the region the model genuinely describes, and say so; a stated approximation is engineering, an unstated one is luck.

Closing. \( e^{kt} \) encodes self-proportional change; \( \tau \) makes transients countable; logarithms solve for time and straighten curves so data confesses its constants.

Lesson 06 · Extrema, curve behavior, and optimization

Lecture

§1 · Where curves turn

A smooth function can only reverse direction where its derivative is zero. Those points — where \( f'(x) = 0 \) — are the critical points, and among them live the maxima and minima that engineering actually cares about: least material, least energy, most throughput. The second derivative classifies them: \( f'' > 0 \) cups upward (minimum), \( f'' < 0 \) caps downward (maximum).

§2 · The optimization recipe

Every optimization in this course follows one recipe:

\[ \text{model} \to \text{constraint} \to \text{single-variable objective} \to f'(x)=0 \to \text{check ends} \tag{1} \]

Write the quantity to optimize; use the constraint to eliminate variables; differentiate; solve; then — the step everyone forgets — check the boundary. In a plant, optima frequently live at the constraint (valve fully open, maximum allowed speed) rather than at an interior zero of the derivative. The mathematics is honest about this only if you ask.

§3 · A worked shape decision

Minimum-surface tanks, minimum-loss pipe runs, maximum-volume boxes from fixed sheet — these classic problems all reduce to recipe (1). In §Worked Examples we size an open-top square tank for 4 m³ with least plate area — the kind of trade a fabrication shop quotes weekly — and find the optimum has height equal to half the base side, a proportion worth memorizing.

§4 · From words to optimum — the translation discipline

The calculus in recipe (1) is the easy half; the profession lives in the translation that precedes it. Practice the sequence deliberately. Name the objective — the single quantity to minimize or maximize — and write its formula with every variable it depends on. Name the constraint — the fixed volume, the required duty, the forbidden region — as an equation, and use it to eliminate variables until the objective depends on one. Differentiate, set to zero, solve — then classify with the second derivative, and walk the boundary: evaluate the objective at every end of the allowed interval, because constrained optima are the plant's normal case, not its exception. Finally, sanity-check the geometry of the answer: optima usually land on proportions — height equals half the base, cost terms equal at the minimum — and a result that looks arbitrary is a result worth re-deriving.

Two honest cautions. A mathematical optimum is only as good as the model's costs: a plate that is cheapest by area may not be cheapest by welding length, and writing the model down makes that assumption visible and challengeable. And when the objective is nearly flat near its optimum — as it often is — precision fetishism wastes effort: report the flat region, not a false fourth digit.

Real decision variables are also rarely continuous. Batches come in integers, plate comes in stock sizes, speeds come in motor steps. The calculus still earns its keep: find the continuous optimum, then evaluate the objective at the feasible values on either side of it and take the better — the curve's flatness near the optimum usually makes the rounding cost negligible, and computing it proves that instead of assuming it.

Closing. Derivative zero finds candidates; the second derivative sorts them; the boundary check keeps you honest. Optimization is calculus with a purchase order attached.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Least steel for a square tank

Problem. A fabrication shop must build an open-top, square-base tank holding \( V = 4\ \mathrm{m^3} \). Find: base side \( x \) and height \( h \) minimizing plate area.

Solution. (1) Objective: \( A = x^2 + 4xh \) (base + four walls). (2) Constraint: \( x^2 h = 4 \Rightarrow h = 4/x^2 \). (3) Substitute: \( A(x) = x^2 + 16/x \). (4) Differentiate: \( A' = 2x - 16/x^2 = 0 \Rightarrow x^3 = 8 \Rightarrow x = 2\ \mathrm{m} \), so \( h = 1\ \mathrm{m} \). (5) Classify: \( A'' = 2 + 32/x^3 > 0 \) → minimum. \( A = 4 + 8 = 12\ \mathrm{m^2} \).

Answer: 2 m × 2 m base, 1 m high; 12 m² of plate. Sanity check: nudge to \( x = 1.8 \): \( A = 3.24 + 8.89 = 12.13\ \mathrm{m^2} \) — worse, as a minimum demands.

SOLVE 2

The boundary wins

Problem. Pump energy cost falls with impeller trim \( x \) (%) as \( E(x) = 100 - 1.2x \) while vibration risk cost rises as \( R(x) = 0.05x^2 \); trims beyond 15% are forbidden by the OEM. Find: the trim minimizing total cost \( C = E + R \) on \( [0, 15] \).

Solution. (1) \( C(x) = 100 - 1.2x + 0.05x^2 \). (2) \( C' = -1.2 + 0.1x = 0 \Rightarrow x = 12 \) — interior candidate, \( C(12) = 100 - 14.4 + 7.2 = 92.8 \). (3) Check ends: \( C(0) = 100 \), \( C(15) = 100 - 18 + 11.25 = 93.25 \).

Answer: trim 12% (interior optimum beats both boundaries). Sanity check: \( C'' = 0.1 > 0 \) confirms a minimum; had the OEM cap been 10%, the answer would have been the boundary, 10% — recipe (1)'s final step earning its keep.

SOLVE 3

The batch size that balances two costs

Problem. Producing a part in batches of size \( x \) costs, per part, \( C(x) = 400/x + 0.25x \) — the first term spreads a fixed setup cost, the second carries inventory. Find: the batch size minimizing cost per part, the minimum cost, and the structural property of the optimum.

Solution. (1) \( C'(x) = -400/x^2 + 0.25 = 0 \Rightarrow x^2 = 1600 \Rightarrow x = 40 \). (2) \( C(40) = 10 + 10 = 20 \) per part; \( C''(x) = 800/x^3 > 0 \) confirms a minimum. (3) Structure: at the optimum the two cost terms are equal (10 and 10) — the classic signature of a fixed-cost-versus-carrying-cost trade.

Answer: batch of 40; cost 20 per part; setup and carrying costs balance exactly at the optimum. Sanity check: at \( x = 30 \): \( 13.3 + 7.5 = 20.8 \); at \( x = 50 \): \( 8 + 12.5 = 20.5 \) — both worse, bracketing the minimum as they must.

MCQ 1

A fabrication shop must build a closed square-base tank (base, four walls, and a lid) holding \( V = 8\ \mathrm{m^3} \). With base side \( x \) and height \( h \), the plate area is \( A = 2x^2 + 4xh \) and the constraint is \( x^2 h = 8 \). What is the minimum plate area?

  1. \( 16\ \mathrm{m^2} \)
  2. \( 20\ \mathrm{m^2} \)
  3. \( 24\ \mathrm{m^2} \) ✓
  4. \( 40\ \mathrm{m^2} \)

The constraint gives \( h = 8/x^2 \), so \( A(x) = 2x^2 + 32/x \); setting \( A'(x) = 4x - 32/x^2 = 0 \) yields \( x^3 = 8 \), hence \( x = h = 2\ \mathrm{m} \) and \( A = 8 + 16 = 24\ \mathrm{m^2} \). Since \( A''(x) = 4 + 64/x^3 \gt 0 \), this critical point is a minimum — note the closed tank optimizes to a cube, unlike the open-top case. The distractor \( 20\ \mathrm{m^2} \) is the classic slip of reusing the open-top formula \( x^2 + 4xh \) and forgetting the lid, while \( 40\ \mathrm{m^2} \) follows from mis-writing the constraint as \( xh = 8 \), which drops a dimension and gives \( h = 4\ \mathrm{m} \).

MCQ 2

A cooling fan's operating cost is \( C(x) = x^2 - 8x + 20 \) (in kW-equivalent), where the speed setting \( x \) is restricted by the manufacturer to \( 0 \le x \le 3 \). What is the minimum cost achievable within the allowed range?

  1. \( 4 \)
  2. \( 5 \) ✓
  3. \( 8 \)
  4. \( 20 \)

Solving \( C'(x) = 2x - 8 = 0 \) gives \( x = 4 \), but this critical point lies outside the allowed interval, so the recipe's final step — check the ends — decides: \( C \) is decreasing on \( [0,3] \) since \( C'(x) \lt 0 \) there, and the minimum sits at the boundary, \( C(3) = 9 - 24 + 20 = 5 \). The distractor \( 4 \) is the classic error of evaluating at the unconstrained critical point \( x = 4 \) while ignoring the operating limit. Choosing \( 20 \) assumes the lowest speed is cheapest, and \( 8 \) comes from mis-solving \( 2x - 8 = 0 \) as \( x = 2 \).

MCQ 3

For \( f(x) = x^3 - 12x \), the critical points are \( x = \pm 2 \). Which hosts the local maximum?

  1. \( x = -2 \) ✓
  2. \( x = 2 \)
  3. \( x = 0 \)
  4. Neither — the function has no local maximum

\( f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12 = 0 \) at \( x = \pm 2 \); the second derivative \( f'' = 6x \) is negative at \( x = -2 \) (cap: local maximum, \( f(-2) = 16 \)) and positive at \( x = 2 \) (cup: local minimum, \( f(2) = -16 \)). Choosing \( x = 2 \) applies the test's sign backwards; \( x = 0 \) is where \( f'' = 0 \) — an inflection, not a critical point, since \( f'(0) = -12 \ne 0 \).

MCQ 4

At a point \( x = c \), a smooth function satisfies \( f'(c) = 0 \) and \( f''(c) = 0 \). What can be concluded about \( c \)?

  1. \( c \) must be an inflection point
  2. \( c \) must be a local minimum
  3. \( c \) cannot be an extremum
  4. Nothing yet — the second-derivative test is inconclusive, and \( c \) must be classified by the sign change of \( f' \) around it ✓

When \( f''(c) = 0 \) the second-derivative test gives no verdict: \( f(x) = x^4 \) has \( f'(0) = f''(0) = 0 \) with a genuine minimum at \( 0 \), while \( f(x) = x^3 \) satisfies the same conditions with no extremum at all, so both outcomes are possible. The reliable fallback is to examine whether \( f' \) changes sign across \( c \). The first distractor is the classic error of equating \( f'' = 0 \) with an inflection point — concavity must actually flip there, which \( x^4 \) shows it need not.

MCQ 5

On a trend chart, \( f''(x) \) changes sign at \( x = c \) while \( f'(c) \neq 0 \). What is at \( c \)?

  1. An inflection point — the trend changes how it bends ✓
  2. A local maximum
  3. A local minimum
  4. A discontinuity

A sign change of the second derivative is precisely an inflection: concavity flips — a rise that was accelerating begins decelerating, or the reverse. It is not an extremum, because the first derivative is nonzero and the trend is still climbing or falling through the point; and nothing about curvature change implies a break in the function. On real trends, inflections are early news: the character of change changing is often the first visible symptom of a new regime.

Lesson 07 · The integral as accumulation

Lecture

§1 · Accumulation is the other half of calculus

The derivative asks: how fast? The integral asks the inverse question: how much, in total? A flow meter reads m³/h instant by instant; the invoice wants m³. Adding up a changing rate over time is integration:

\[ V = \int_{0}^{T} Q(t)\,dt \tag{1} \]

Geometrically this is the area under the \( Q(t) \) curve — approximated by stacking thin rectangles \( Q \cdot \Delta t \) and letting \( \Delta t \to 0 \). Every totalizer in the plant is performing equation (1) in silicon, all day long.

§2 · The Fundamental Theorem — rates and totals are inverses

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus says accumulation and differentiation undo each other: if \( F(t) = \int_a^t f(x)\,dx \), then \( F' = f \). Consequently

\[ \int_a^b f(t)\,dt = F(b) - F(a) \quad\text{where } F' = f \tag{2} \]

Find any antiderivative, evaluate at the ends, subtract. The theorem is why energy (kWh) is the antiderivative of power (kW), why distance is the antiderivative of speed, and why a totalizer can be checked against two readings of a cumulative register.

§3 · Reading areas like an engineer

Area under a pump's power trend = energy consumed. Area under a flow curve = volume transferred. Area between inflow and outflow curves = inventory change. When the curve is data rather than formula, the integral is done numerically — trapezoids on historian samples — which is Lesson 9's business. The concept is identical: total = area.

§4 · Setting up an integral — the working method

Every accumulation problem is set up the same way, and the setup is where the marks are won or lost. Identify the rate — the quantity per time (or per length, per angle) being accumulated — and write its units down. Fix the interval: the start and end of the accumulation, in the rate's own time variable. Integrate, and check the units of the result: a rate in m³/h integrated over hours must land in m³, and if it doesn't, the setup — not the arithmetic — is wrong. Then exploit the Fundamental Theorem in both directions. Given a formula, find an antiderivative and evaluate at the ends. Given a cumulative register — a totalizer, an energy meter, an odometer — the theorem says the accumulation over any window is simply the difference of two readings: engineering's most underrated free lunch.

Sketch first, always. A rough graph of the rate shows whether the answer should be near the peak-times-width rectangle or well under it, whether any region runs negative, and where the accumulation concentrates. The sketch is the estimate, the integral is the precision, and disagreement between them is the error alarm that catches transposed limits and dropped signs before they reach a report.

One caution completes the method: an integral answers exactly the question its limits ask, no more. A total over a shift says nothing about which hour did the accumulating, and a net accumulation of zero can hide furious movement in both directions. Keep the question and the interval welded together when you present the number, and totals stop being ambushed by follow-up questions.

Closing. Integrals accumulate rates into totals; the Fundamental Theorem makes them computable from antiderivatives; and the plant's totalizers, energy meters, and inventory balances are integrals with tag numbers.

SOLVE 1

Volume from a ramping flow

Problem. During a transfer, flow ramps as \( Q(t) = 20 + 4t \) m³/h (\( t \) in hours) for six hours. Find: the total volume moved.

Solution. (1) \( V = \int_0^6 (20+4t)\,dt \). (2) Antiderivative: \( 20t + 2t^2 \). (3) Evaluate: \( (120 + 72) - 0 = 192\ \mathrm{m^3} \).

Answer: 192 m³. Sanity check: average flow is \( (20+44)/2 = 32\ \mathrm{m^3/h} \) over 6 h → 192 m³. The trapezoid agrees with the antiderivative, as it must for a linear ramp.

SOLVE 2

Energy from a power trend

Problem. A compressor's electrical demand across a shift is modeled \( P(t) = 250 - 10t + t^2 \) kW over \( 0 \le t \le 8 \) h. Find: the energy consumed and the average power.

Solution. (1) \( E = \int_0^8 (250 - 10t + t^2)\,dt = \left[250t - 5t^2 + t^3/3\right]_0^8 \). (2) \( = 2000 - 320 + 170.7 = 1850.7\ \mathrm{kWh} \). (3) Average power \( = E/8 = 231.3\ \mathrm{kW} \).

Answer: ≈1851 kWh; average ≈231 kW. Sanity check: the parabola dips mid-shift (minimum \( P(5) = 225 \) kW) and ends at \( P(8)=234 \); an average of 231 kW sits sensibly between the minimum and the endpoints.

SOLVE 3

Auditing a week from two register readings

Problem. A water totalizer — a cumulative register, i.e. an antiderivative of flow — read 10,450 m³ on Monday 08:00 and 12,730 m³ on Friday 08:00 (96 hours later). Find: the volume consumed and the true average flow for the period, and name the theorem doing the work.

Solution. (1) By the Fundamental Theorem, consumption over the window is the register difference: \( 12{,}730 - 10{,}450 = 2{,}280\ \mathrm{m^3} \) — no flow curve needed. (2) True average flow: \( 2{,}280/96 = 23.75\ \mathrm{m^3/h} \). (3) The register is \( F(t) \) with \( F' = Q \); two readings evaluate \( \int Q\,dt \) exactly, whatever the flow did in between.

Answer: 2,280 m³; 23.75 m³/h average. Sanity check: a line that idles nights could still spike far above 23.75 by day — the average from registers is exact as an average, and silent about peaks; that distinction is the next lesson's business.

During a controlled draw-down, a tank's outflow falls linearly as \( Q(t) = 50 - 4t \ \mathrm{m^3/h} \) for \( 0 \le t \le 6 \) h. What total volume leaves the tank during the six hours?

  1. \( 300\ \mathrm{m^3} \)
  2. \( 156\ \mathrm{m^3} \)
  3. \( 228\ \mathrm{m^3} \) ✓
  4. \( 288\ \mathrm{m^3} \)

Volume is the accumulated rate: \( \int_0^6 (50-4t)\,dt = \left[50t - 2t^2\right]_0^6 = 300 - 72 = 228\ \mathrm{m^3} \). The distractor 156 m³ comes from the classic antiderivative slip of writing \( 50t - 4t^2 \), forgetting to divide by the new power when reversing the power rule. The distractor 300 m³ multiplies the initial rate by the duration as if the flow never declined, ignoring that the integrand changes over the interval.

MCQ 2

A pump motor's electrical demand over a 4-hour run is modeled by \( P(t) = 120 + 30t - 3t^2 \) kW. What is the average power drawn over the run?

  1. \( 656\ \mathrm{kW} \)
  2. \( 164\ \mathrm{kW} \) ✓
  3. \( 156\ \mathrm{kW} \)
  4. \( 192\ \mathrm{kW} \)

Energy is the area under the power curve: \( E = \int_0^4 (120 + 30t - 3t^2)\,dt = \left[120t + 15t^2 - t^3\right]_0^4 = 656\ \mathrm{kWh} \), and the average power is that total divided by the interval, \( 656/4 = 164\ \mathrm{kW} \). Evaluate \( \displaystyle\int_1^3 (2t + 1)\,dt \).

  1. 10 ✓
  2. 14
  3. 8
  4. 22

The antiderivative is \( t^2 + t \); evaluating, \( (9 + 3) - (1 + 1) = 10 \). Answering 14 forgets to subtract the lower-end value — the most common definite-integral slip; 22 is the integrand's value at \( t=3 \) times the width, treating a changing rate as constant at its end value. MCQ 4

Historian tags record a tank's inflow \( Q_{\text{in}}(t) \) and outflow \( Q_{\text{out}}(t) \), both in \( \mathrm{m^3/h} \). What physical quantity does \( \int_0^T \big[ Q_{\text{in}}(t) - Q_{\text{out}}(t) \big]\,dt \) equal?

  1. The change in stored volume between \( t=0 \) and \( t=T \), in \( \mathrm{m^3} \) ✓
  2. The tank's absolute liquid volume at \( t=T \), in \( \mathrm{m^3} \)
  3. The instantaneous net inflow at \( t=T \), in \( \mathrm{m^3/h} \)
  4. The average net inflow rate over the interval, in \( \mathrm{m^3/h} \)

The integrand \( Q_{\text{in}} - Q_{\text{out}} \) is the net rate of accumulation, so by the Fundamental Theorem its definite integral over \( [0,T] \) is the net volume added — the inventory change \( V(T) - V(0) \), in \( \mathrm{m^3} \). It is not the absolute volume at \( t=T \): that classic error forgets the initial inventory \( V(0) \), which the definite integral cannot see. The remaining options confuse the integral with its integrand (a rate at one instant) or with the average rate, which would require dividing the integral by \( T \).

MCQ 5

During a filter backflush, a line's flow \( Q(t) \) runs negative for part of an interval. What does \( \int Q\,dt \) over that interval report?

  1. The net volume delivered, with backflow counted negative ✓
  2. The gross volume that moved in either direction
  3. Zero — integrals of negative regions are discarded
  4. The average flow rate

The definite integral is signed area: forward flow accumulates positive, reverse flow negative, and the result is net delivery — which is exactly what an inventory balance needs. Gross movement would require integrating \( |Q| \), a different (and occasionally also useful) quantity. Discarding negative regions is not a convention mathematics offers, and an average is the integral divided by the width, not the integral itself.

Lesson 08 · Integration techniques

Lecture

§1 · Substitution — the chain rule reversed

Most integrals you meet are chain-rule results wearing a disguise. Substitution unmasks them: set \( u = \) the inner function, trade \( dx \) for \( du \), integrate, return.

\[ \int x\,(x^2+1)^3 dx \;\xrightarrow{\,u = x^2+1,\ du = 2x\,dx\,}\; \tfrac12\int u^3 du = \frac{(x^2+1)^4}{8} + C \tag{1} \]

The skill is seeing the derivative of the inside sitting beside it. Flow, decay, and geometry integrals in plant work overwhelmingly fall to substitution.

§2 · Integration by parts — the product rule reversed

When the integrand is a genuine product — a polynomial times an exponential, say — parts applies:

\[ \int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du \tag{2} \]

Choose \( u \) to simplify when differentiated, \( dv \) to stay tame when integrated. The workhorse case \( \int t\,e^{-t}dt \) models energy in decaying transients and first-moment calculations, and §Worked Examples runs it end to end.

§3 · Knowing when to stop

Real plant data rarely hands you a clean antiderivative. The professional sequence is: try substitution; try parts; recognize a table form; otherwise integrate numerically — trapezoids or Simpson on sampled data — and say so. A numerically honest answer beats a heroic symbolic detour every time. Software does symbolic work well; your job is to set the integral up correctly and sanity-check its output.

§4 · Choosing the technique — a decision procedure

Integration is pattern recognition with a fallback ladder, and the ladder has exactly four rungs. Rung one: is the derivative of an inner function sitting beside it? If the integrand contains \( g(x) \) inside another function and \( g'(x) \) — up to a constant — as a factor, substitution finishes it in one line. Train the eye on the constant: in \( \int x\,e^{x^2}dx \), the \( x \) outside is \( \tfrac12 \) of the needed \( 2x \), and the half simply rides along. Rung two: a genuine product of unrelated factors — polynomial times exponential, polynomial times sine — goes to parts, choosing \( u \) as the factor that simplifies when differentiated. Rung three: recognize a table form and write the answer. Rung four: integrate numerically — trapezoids on the samples you have — and say so plainly.

Two habits turn technique into reliability. Verify every antiderivative by differentiating it — ten seconds that catch dropped halves and sign slips before they propagate. And when limits are involved in a substitution, transform them with the variable so the evaluation happens in \( u \)-space and nothing needs converting back. The techniques are few; the discipline around them is what makes the answers dependable.

Know also what the ladder is for. In professional work, symbolic software integrates faster than any human — but it cannot decide whether the integral was set up correctly, whether the limits mean what the question meant, or whether the answer's magnitude is plausible. The techniques you practice here build exactly that judgment: someone who has integrated by hand recognizes a wrong machine answer on sight, the way a machinist who has cut threads manually reads a bad CNC part at a glance. Learn the moves to own the judgment, then delegate the labor freely.

Closing. Substitution reverses the chain rule, parts reverses the product rule, and numerical integration is the licensed fallback — not a defeat.

SOLVE 1

Substitution with limits

Problem. Evaluate \( \displaystyle\int_0^2 x\,(x^2+1)^3\,dx \).

Solution. (1) \( u = x^2+1 \), \( du = 2x\,dx \); limits map \( x{=}0 \to u{=}1 \), \( x{=}2 \to u{=}5 \). (2) Integral becomes \( \tfrac12 \int_1^5 u^3 du = \tfrac12 \cdot \frac{u^4}{4}\Big|_1^5 \). (3) \( = \frac{625 - 1}{8} = 78 \).

Answer: 78. Sanity check: the integrand at \( x=2 \) is \( 2 \times 125 = 250 \), at 0 it's 0; a rough average of ~40 over width 2 gives ~80 — same neighborhood as 78.

SOLVE 2

Parts on a decaying transient

Problem. A quantity accumulates as \( \int_0^2 t\,e^{-t}\,dt \) (dimensionless model of energy delivered by a decaying pulse). Find: its value.

Solution. (1) Parts with \( u = t,\ dv = e^{-t}dt \Rightarrow du = dt,\ v = -e^{-t} \). (2) \( \int t e^{-t} dt = -t e^{-t} + \int e^{-t} dt = -t e^{-t} - e^{-t} \). (3) Evaluate 0→2: \( \left[-(t+1)e^{-t}\right]_0^2 = -(3)(0.1353) + 1 = 1 - 0.406 = 0.594 \).

Answer: ≈0.594. Sanity check: the full integral to infinity equals 1 exactly; by \( t=2 \) (two time constants) most of the pulse has landed — 59% here, with the tail's remainder consistent with \( (t+1)e^{-t} \) at \( t=2 \) being 0.406.

SOLVE 3

Integrating data that has no formula

Problem. A flow is sampled every 15 minutes: 40, 52, 60, 54, 42 m³/h. Find: the volume transferred over the hour by the trapezoid rule.

Solution. (1) Step \( h = 0.25 \) h. Trapezoid rule: \( V = h\left[\tfrac{f_0 + f_4}{2} + f_1 + f_2 + f_3\right] \). (2) \( V = 0.25\left[\tfrac{40+42}{2} + 52 + 60 + 54\right] = 0.25\,(41 + 166) = 51.75\ \mathrm{m^3} \).

Answer: ≈51.8 m³. Sanity check: the flow averaged roughly 50 m³/h over one hour, so ~50 m³ is the expected neighborhood; the trapezoid answer sits just above it because the curve spends more time near its 60 m³/h crest than at its ends. This is rung four of the technique ladder — used openly, it is not an approximation of last resort but the standard tool for sampled data.

MCQ 1

Evaluate \( \displaystyle\int_0^2 \frac{x}{x^2+1}\,dx \).

  1. \( \ln 5 \approx 1.609 \)
  2. \( \arctan 2 \approx 1.107 \)
  3. \( \tfrac{1}{2}\ln 5 \approx 0.805 \) ✓
  4. \( \tfrac{1}{2}\ln 3 \approx 0.549 \)

Set \( u = x^2+1 \), so \( du = 2x\,dx \) and the limits map to \( u=1 \) and \( u=5 \), giving \( \tfrac12\int_1^5 \frac{du}{u} = \tfrac12\ln 5 \). The answer \( \ln 5 \) is the most common substitution slip: dropping the factor \( \tfrac12 \) that \( du = 2x\,dx \) forces. \( \arctan 2 \) comes from pattern-matching the table form \( \int dx/(x^2+1) \) while ignoring the \( x \) in the numerator, and \( \tfrac12\ln 3 \) results from converting the upper limit as \( 2+1 \) instead of \( 2^2+1 \).

MCQ 2

Using integration by parts, evaluate \( \displaystyle\int_0^1 x\,e^{x}\,dx \).

  1. \( e - 1 \approx 1.718 \)
  2. \( 1 \) ✓
  3. \( \tfrac{e}{2} \approx 1.359 \)
  4. \( 2e - 1 \approx 4.437 \)

Take \( u = x \), \( dv = e^x dx \), so \( \int x e^x dx = x e^x - \int e^x dx = (x-1)e^x \); evaluating from 0 to 1 gives \( 0 - (-1) = 1 \). The value \( e/2 \) is the classic error of integrating the two factors independently as \( (x^2/2)e^x \) — integration has no product rule, which is exactly why parts exists. \( 2e-1 \) follows from a sign slip in the parts formula (writing \( uv + \int v\,du \)), and \( e-1 \) is \( \int_0^1 e^x dx \) alone, i.e., forgetting the boundary term \( uv \).

What is \( \displaystyle\int e^{3t}\,dt \)?

MCQ 4

Each integrand below is a polynomial factor times an exponential or composite factor. For which integral does substitution alone fail, making integration by parts the required technique?

Lesson 09 · Applications: areas, volumes, work, and averages

Lecture

§1 · Average value — what the meter should have read

The average of a continuously varying quantity is its integral spread evenly across the interval:

\[ \bar f = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(t)\,dt \tag{1} \]

Average demand, average flow, average temperature — every KPI that begins with the word "average" is equation (1), whether computed honestly or not. When someone averages three spot readings taken at convenient times, you now know precisely what they did wrong.

§2 · RMS — the average that heats

Electrical heating goes as the square of current, so the meaningful "average" for AC is the root of the mean of the square:

\[ f_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T f^2(t)\,dt}, \qquad \text{for } f = A\sin\omega t:\ f_{rms} = \frac{A}{\sqrt 2} \tag{2} \]

The sine result follows because \( \sin^2 \) averages to exactly ½ over a cycle. This is why 230 V mains has a 325 V peak, and why vibration severity standards quote RMS velocity: RMS tracks energy, and energy does the damage.

§3 · Work by integration

When force varies along the path, work is accumulated force-times-distance: \( W = \int F\,dx \). Pumping a tank empty is the signature case: each layer of liquid at depth \( y \) has weight \( \rho g A\,dy \) and must rise a different height. Summing layers is an integral, executed in §Worked Examples for a real tank. The same layer-summing pattern computes hydrostatic forces on gates and centroids of plates — one method, many billables.

§4 · Choosing the right average — a field guide

Three different averages answer three different questions, and choosing the wrong one is a quiet, expensive error. The plain mean — equation (1) — answers "how much per unit time on the invoice": billing, throughput, duty totals. The RMS — equation (2) — answers "what steady level would heat, stress, or wear the same": electrical heating, vibration severity, fatigue-relevant loading, because damage physics runs on squares. The peak answers "will it survive the worst instant": trips, relief sizing, insulation. A signal has all three numbers at once, and the crest factor — peak over RMS — is the single figure that says how far apart they live: near 1.4 for a clean sine, climbing whenever impacts and spikes enter, which is why analysts watch it as an early bearing-damage flag.

On sampled data the integrals become sums without changing meaning: mean the samples for equation (1), mean the squares and take the root for equation (2), weighting by interval when the sampling is uneven. For work-by-layers problems, the recipe is mechanical: draw the layer, write its weight, write its travel distance as a function of position, integrate over the geometry — then check against the centroid shortcut, which agrees every time the geometry is uniform and catches the setup when it isn't.

The same layer-summing pattern prices hydrostatic force on a gate, the metal in a tapered column, and the moment of a distributed load — one method, many invoices. What changes between problems is only what each layer carries: weight, pressure, or load per metre. Master the pattern once, in the tank problem, and every later course that says "integrate over the geometry" is asking for a rerun.

Closing. Integrals define honest averages (1), energy-true averages (2), and path-dependent work — three tools that turn "roughly" into "defensibly."

SOLVE 1

RMS of a sine, derived

Problem. Show \( f_{rms} = A/\sqrt2 \) for \( f = A\sin\omega t \) over one period.

Solution. (1) \( f^2 = A^2\sin^2\omega t = \frac{A^2}{2}(1 - \cos 2\omega t) \). (2) The cosine term integrates to zero over a full period, so \( \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T f^2 dt = \frac{A^2}{2} \). (3) Square root: \( f_{rms} = A/\sqrt2 \approx 0.707A \).

Answer: \( A/\sqrt2 \). Sanity check: 230 V RMS × √2 = 325 V peak — the familiar mains numbers reproduce.

SOLVE 2

Emptying a tank over the rim

Problem. A vertical cylindrical water tank, radius 1.5 m, holds 4.0 m of water. Find: the minimum work to pump the entire contents over the rim (at the 4.0 m level).

Solution. (1) Layer at height \( y \) (from the bottom), thickness \( dy \): volume \( A\,dy \) with \( A = \pi(1.5)^2 = 7.07\ \mathrm{m^2} \); lift height \( (4-y) \). (2) \( W = \rho g A \int_0^4 (4-y)\,dy = 1000 \times 9.81 \times 7.07 \times \left[4y - \tfrac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^4 \). (3) Bracket: \( 16 - 8 = 8 \). So \( W = 69{,}300 \times 8 = 555\ \mathrm{kJ} \).

Answer: ≈555 kJ (≈0.154 kWh). Sanity check: total mass 28.3 t lifted an average of 2 m: \( 28{,}300 \times 9.81 \times 2 = 555\ \mathrm{kJ} \) — the centroid shortcut confirms the integral exactly.

SOLVE 3

Mean versus RMS on a duty cycle

Problem. A motor draws 40 A for 2 s, then 10 A for 8 s, repeating every 10 s. Find: the mean current and the RMS current, and state which one sizes the motor thermally.

Solution. (1) Mean: \( (40 \times 2 + 10 \times 8)/10 = 160/10 = 16\ \mathrm{A} \). (2) RMS: mean of squares \( = (40^2 \times 2 + 10^2 \times 8)/10 = (3200 + 800)/10 = 400 \), so \( I_{rms} = 20\ \mathrm{A} \). (3) Heating goes as \( I^2R \), so the 20 A figure — not 16 A — is the thermally equivalent steady current.

Answer: mean 16 A, RMS 20 A; thermal sizing uses RMS. Sanity check: RMS ≥ mean always, with equality only for constant signals; a motor sized on the 16 A average would run 56% hot on an \( I^2 \) basis \( (400/256 = 1.56) \) — the exact mistake the RMS concept exists to prevent.

A reactor jacket temperature follows \( T(t) = 20 + 4t - t^2 \) (in Celsius, with \( t \) in hours) over a 3-hour batch, \( 0 \le t \le 3 \). What is the true mean temperature of the batch?

  1. 8 ✓
  2. 16
  3. 24

MCQ 4

Why does the RMS value, rather than the plain time-average, represent the heating effect of an AC current in a resistor?

  1. Instantaneous dissipation scales as the square of the current, and the plain mean of a symmetric sinusoid over a cycle is zero, so only the mean of the squared signal tracks the energy delivered. ✓
  2. The RMS of any waveform equals its peak divided by \( \sqrt{2} \), which makes it directly comparable to a DC value.
  3. RMS equals the mean of the absolute value of the current, which is the quantity a heating element responds to.
  4. RMS is always larger than the plain mean, so it builds a conservative safety margin into thermal ratings.

Power in a resistor is \( i^2 R \), so the physically meaningful average must be taken on \( i^2 \); the square root then restores current units, giving the DC-equivalent amplitude that produces the same heating. The \( \sqrt{2} \) relation in the second choice is the classic overgeneralisation — peak divided by \( \sqrt{2} \) holds only for pure sinusoids, and the crest factor of other waveforms differs. The third choice confuses RMS with the rectified (mean-absolute) average, a distinct and generally smaller quantity.

MCQ 5

Which signal has a crest factor of exactly \( \sqrt{2} \approx 1.41 \)?

  1. A pure sinusoid ✓
  2. A constant (DC) signal
  3. A square wave
  4. An impulsive signal with sharp spikes

For a sine, peak \( = A \) and RMS \( = A/\sqrt2 \), so peak/RMS \( = \sqrt2 \). A constant and a square wave both have RMS equal to their peak — crest factor 1 — while impulsive signals push the ratio well above 2, which is precisely why a rising crest factor on a bearing's vibration flags early impact-type damage while the RMS still looks calm. Crest factor is the one number that says how "spiky" a signal is.

Lesson 10 · First-order differential equations

Lecture

§1 · Equations that contain their own rates

A differential equation relates a quantity to its own rate of change. The plant's archetype: a hot mass losing heat in proportion to its excess over ambient,

\[ \frac{dT}{dt} = -\frac{T - T_\infty}{\tau} \tag{1} \]

Read it aloud: the hotter it is above ambient, the faster it cools. Most first-order plant dynamics — thermal soak, tank draining, filter loading, RC circuits, instrument response — are equation (1) with different nouns.

§2 · Solving by separation

Separable equations split variables to opposite sides and integrate:

\[ \int\frac{dT}{T-T_\infty} = -\int\frac{dt}{\tau} \;\Rightarrow\; \ln|T-T_\infty| = -\frac{t}{\tau}+C \;\Rightarrow\; T = T_\infty + (T_0-T_\infty)e^{-t/\tau} \tag{2} \]

Lesson 5's cooling curve wasn't handed down; it is derived here, in three lines, from a physical statement. The constant of integration is fixed by the initial condition \( T(0) = T_0 \) — every first-order ODE needs one known point to pin its trajectory.

§3 · Reading dynamics without solving

Much of an ODE's story is available without solution. Set the rate to zero and you find equilibria (here \( T = T_\infty \)). The sign of the rate tells direction of travel; its size sets speed. The coefficient \( 1/\tau \) is the speed of the whole approach — measure it from data and you know the system's tempo. Engineers read ODEs more often than they solve them; the solving builds the reading instinct.

§4 · From balance statement to equation — the modeling method

First-order models are not found; they are written, and the writing follows one template. State the balance: rate of change of the stored quantity equals rate in minus rate out. Express each rate in the state variable: outflow proportional to level, heat loss proportional to excess temperature, contaminant leaving at concentration times flow. Divide through until the equation reads \( \dot{y} = -(y - y_\infty)/\tau \), and read \( \tau \) directly from the coefficients — \( V/Q \) for a flushed tank, thermal mass over loss coefficient for a cooling body. The physics enters in the rates; the mathematics is the same three lines every time.

Respect the sign conventions and the model's edges. A negative rate coefficient decays toward equilibrium; a positive one runs away, and recognizing which you have written is a stability judgment made before any solving. Step inputs — a heater switching on, a setpoint moving — reuse the same solution with the roles renamed: the response climbs \( 1 - e^{-t/\tau} \) toward its new destination. And when the data refuses the model — curved semilog plots, delayed starts — the honest conclusion is a second time constant or a deadtime, not a forced fit. Write what the balance supports; report what the data shows.

This template is the most reused page in the course. The same three lines will reappear as the RC circuit in the electrical course, the thermal soak in heat transfer, the flushed volume in fluid mechanics, and the instrument lag in controls — identical mathematics under different nouns. Learn to see the balance statement through the surface vocabulary and every one of those courses starts a lesson ahead.

Closing. First-order ODEs encode self-referential change; separation solves the classics; initial conditions select the one real trajectory among the many possible.

Problem. A 10 m³ well-mixed tank of contaminated water (concentration \( C_0 \)) is flushed with clean water at 0.5 m³/min, overflowing at the same rate. Find: \( C(t) \) and the time to reach 10% of \( C_0 \).

Problem. A motor winding, tripped at \( t=0 \), reads 95 °C at \( t=10 \) min and 65 °C at \( t=30 \) min; ambient is 35 °C. Assuming first-order cooling, find \( \tau \) and the temperature at \( t=60 \) min.

Lesson 11 · Taylor series and approximation

Lecture

§1 · Every smooth function is locally a polynomial

Zoom into any smooth curve and it straightens; zoom with more care and it bends like a parabola. Taylor's theorem makes this precise: near \( x = a \),

\[ f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + \frac{f''(a)}{2}(x-a)^2 + \cdots \tag{1} \]

The first two terms are the tangent line — linearization — and the third begins to track curvature. Engineering lives on truncations of (1): every "small-angle," "small-signal," and "about the operating point" argument you will ever meet is a Taylor series with the tail cut off.

§2 · The classic small approximations

\[ \sin\theta \approx \theta - \frac{\theta^3}{6}, \qquad \cos\theta \approx 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2}, \qquad e^x \approx 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2} \tag{2} \]

At \( \theta = 10° = 0.1745 \) rad, \( \sin\theta = 0.17365 \): the one-term approximation \( \theta \) errs by 0.5%, and adding the cubic term shrinks the error to 0.0004%. This is why pendulum periods, beam deflections, and control models replace \( \sin\theta \) with \( \theta \) below ~15° and nobody blushes.

§3 · Knowing the size of the tail

An approximation without an error bound is a wish. The first neglected term estimates the error: truncating after \( \theta \) leaves \( \sim\theta^3/6 \). Professional habit: state the operating range, evaluate the first dropped term at the range's edge, and write both down. That habit is what separates "we linearized about 50% flow" from hand-waving — and it is exactly how instrument nonlinearity specs and control-model validity limits are written.

§4 · Building a linearization you can defend

Linearization is a contract with three clauses, written in this order. Choose the operating point deliberately — the flow, load, or angle the system actually lives at — because the model is exact there and degrades with distance. Compute the two numbers: the function's value and its derivative at that point; the tangent model is just \( f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) \), a straight line wearing the local slope. Price the error before anyone asks: evaluate the first dropped term, \( \tfrac12 f''(a)(x-a)^2 \), at the edge of the range you intend to use, and write both the range and the error into the work. A linearization delivered with its validity range and worst-case error is engineering; delivered bare, it is a hope with a slope.

The quadratic error growth is the practical lever: halve the excursion and the error quarters, which is why tight-range instruments tolerate linear scaling that wide-range ones cannot. Run the contract in reverse to audit existing systems — find where a legacy scaling block or control model was linearized, evaluate the dropped term at today's operating range, and you have a defensible number for how much the old approximation now costs. The habit generalizes: every model is an approximation; professionals differ only in whether the error is priced.

Carry that sentence out of the course. Instrument linearity specifications, control-model validity ranges, and datasheet accuracy classes are all Taylor-series contracts written by someone else — read them by asking where the expansion point sits and how far the promised range strays from it. The engineer who can price an approximation can also audit one.

Closing. Taylor series turn smooth functions into local polynomials; linearization is its two-term truncation; the first dropped term prices the honesty of the approximation. This lesson closes the course by naming the approximation culture the whole curriculum will use.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Pricing the small-angle lie

Problem. A pendulum-style level float swings ±12°. The controls model uses \( \sin\theta \approx \theta \). Find: the worst-case percentage error of that model across the swing.

Solution. (1) \( 12° = 0.2094 \) rad; \( \sin(0.2094) = 0.20791 \). (2) Error \( = (0.2094 - 0.20791)/0.20791 = 0.72\% \). (3) First-dropped-term estimate: \( \theta^3/6 = 0.00153 \) → \( 0.00153/0.2079 = 0.74\% \) — the bound predicts the actual error almost exactly.

Answer: ≤0.75% across the swing — negligible against a ±2% level transmitter. Sanity check: error grows with \( \theta^2 \) relative terms; at 24° it would be ~3% — still quotable, but now worth stating.

SOLVE 2

Quick exponential estimates

Problem. Estimate \( e^{0.1} \) with two and three terms of (2), against the true 1.10517.

Solution. (1) Two terms: \( 1 + 0.1 = 1.1 \) — error 0.47%. (2) Three terms: \( 1 + 0.1 + 0.005 = 1.105 \) — error 0.015%.

Answer: 1.1 (coarse), 1.105 (fine). Sanity check: next term \( x^3/6 = 0.000167 \) predicts the residual error of the three-term estimate — matches the 0.00017 shortfall exactly.

SOLVE 3

Linearizing a square-root scaling block

Problem. A flow computer infers flow from differential pressure as \( Q = 5\sqrt{\Delta P} \) (m³/h with \( \Delta P \) in kPa). A legacy block linearizes about the normal operating point \( \Delta P_0 = 16 \) kPa. Find: the linearized model, its prediction at 20 kPa, and the percentage error there.

Solution. (1) Value: \( Q(16) = 5 \times 4 = 20 \). Slope: \( Q'(\Delta P) = \tfrac{5}{2\sqrt{\Delta P}} \), so \( Q'(16) = 5/8 = 0.625 \). (2) Model: \( Q \approx 20 + 0.625\,(\Delta P - 16) \). At 20 kPa: \( 20 + 0.625 \times 4 = 22.5 \). (3) True: \( 5\sqrt{20} = 22.36 \). Error: \( (22.5 - 22.36)/22.36 = 0.62\% \).

Answer: \( Q \approx 20 + 0.625(\Delta P - 16) \); 22.5 vs 22.36 m³/h; ≈0.6% high. Sanity check: the square root curves downward \( (f'' < 0) \), so the tangent must over-predict above the operating point — the sign of the error was known before the arithmetic. Double the excursion to 8 kPa and the error should roughly quadruple: the quadratic growth clause of the linearization contract.

MCQ 1

A process variable follows \( f(x) = \sqrt{1+x} \). Using the linearization of \( f \) about \( x = 0 \), estimate \( \sqrt{1.06} \).

  1. 0.97
  2. 1.03 ✓
  3. 1.06
  4. 1.12

Since \( f'(x) = 1/(2\sqrt{1+x}) \), the linearization is \( f(0) + f'(0)\,x = 1 + x/2 \), which at \( x = 0.06 \) gives 1.03 — within 0.05% of the true 1.02956. Choosing 1.06 is the classic slip of dropping the derivative factor and writing \( \sqrt{1+x} \approx 1 + x \); 1.12 comes from confusing the derivative of \( \sqrt{x} \) with that of \( x^2 \) and using slope 2. The option 0.97 applies the correct slope with the wrong sign.

MCQ 2

The small-angle model \( \sin\theta \approx \theta \) is applied at \( \theta = 0.3 \) rad. Using the first dropped term of the Taylor series, estimate the relative error of the model at this angle.

  1. 9%
  2. 4.5%
  3. 1.5% ✓
  4. 0.45%

The first dropped term is \( \theta^3/6 = 0.0045 \); dividing by \( \theta = 0.3 \) gives a relative error of \( \theta^2/6 = 0.015 \), i.e. 1.5%, which matches the actual error of 1.52% almost exactly. Answering 4.5% swaps in the cosine series' \( \theta^2/2 \) error coefficient — a classic formula swap between the two small-angle expansions. Quoting 0.45% mistakes the absolute error 0.0045 for the percentage itself, skipping the division by \( \theta \).

MCQ 3

Using two terms of the cosine series, estimate \( \cos(0.2\ \mathrm{rad}) \).

  1. 0.980 ✓
  2. 0.960
  3. 1.020
  4. 0.800

\( \cos\theta \approx 1 - \theta^2/2 = 1 - 0.02 = 0.980 \), against a true value 0.98007 — four-figure agreement from one subtraction. Choosing 0.960 uses \( \theta^2 \) without the half; 1.020 flips the sign, forgetting cosine falls from 1; 0.800 subtracts \( \theta \) itself, importing the sine pattern into the cosine series. The small-angle set is three separate formulas — mixing their patterns is the classic error.

MCQ 4

A control model is built by linearizing a smooth plant characteristic \( f(x) \) about the operating point \( x = a \). Which statement about the model's truncation error is correct?

  1. It is zero at \( x = a \) and grows approximately like the quadratic term \( \tfrac{1}{2}f''(a)(x-a)^2 \), so doubling the deviation from \( a \) roughly quadruples the error. ✓
  2. It grows linearly with \( x - a \), so doubling the deviation from \( a \) doubles the error.
  3. It is a fixed percentage of the reading throughout the instrument's range.
  4. It vanishes wherever \( f \) is continuous, because the tangent line then coincides with the curve.

The first dropped term \( \tfrac{1}{2}f''(a)(x-a)^2 \) prices the truncation error, and its quadratic dependence on \( x - a \) means doubling the excursion from the operating point roughly quadruples the error. The linear-growth option is the classic misreading of a first-order model as having first-order error. The fixed-percentage option confuses truncation error with a gain-style instrument specification, and continuity alone guarantees nothing — the tangent line matches the curve only at \( a \) itself.

MCQ 5

The approximation \( e^x \approx 1 + x \) gives 2 at \( x = 1 \), while \( e^1 = 2.718 \) — an error of 26%. Why does the same formula work beautifully at \( x = 0.05 \)?

  1. The dropped terms scale as \( x^2/2 \) and higher powers, so shrinking \( x \) collapses the error rapidly ✓
  2. The exponential is only defined for small arguments
  3. Rounding happens to cancel at small \( x \)
  4. It doesn't — the percentage error is the same at every \( x \)

The first dropped term is \( x^2/2 \): at \( x = 1 \) that is 0.5 — a quarter of the true value — while at \( x = 0.05 \) it is 0.00125, about 0.12%. Truncation error is governed by the first term you dropped, and its power-of-\( x \) scaling is why "small" is the operative word in every small-signal model. Nothing cancels by luck, the exponential is defined everywhere, and the error is emphatically not scale-free — pricing it at the range edge is the whole discipline.
