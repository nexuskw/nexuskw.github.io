Lesson 01 · Forces, vectors, and resultants

Lecture

§1 · Force is a vector, and the arrow is the contract

A force has magnitude, direction, and a point of application — drop any of the three and the analysis is fiction. In 2-D we work in components: a force \( F \) at angle \( \theta \) above the horizontal carries \( F_x = F\cos\theta \) and \( F_y = F\sin\theta \). The reverse trip is just as routine:

\[ R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}, \qquad \theta = \tan^{-1}\!\frac{R_y}{R_x} \tag{1} \]

Resultants are found by summing components, never by adding magnitudes — two 10 kN forces can combine to anything from 0 to 20 kN depending on their directions.

§2 · Weight is not mass

Mass is the amount of matter, in kilograms; weight is the gravitational force on that mass, \( W = mg \) with \( g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), in newtons. A 250 kg motor does not load its sling with "250 kg" — it loads it with \( 250 \times 9.81 = 2452.5\ \mathrm{N} \approx 2.45\ \mathrm{kN} \). Bolt-load calculations, sling charts, and pressure-area checks all fail the same way when someone feeds a mass where a force belongs. Carry units through every line; they are the cheapest error detector you will ever own.

§3 · Cables and struts resolve the same way

A taut cable pulls along its own axis; a straight two-force strut pushes or pulls along its axis. Both therefore hand you a force whose direction is known from geometry — only the magnitude is unknown. Resolving such members into components is the mechanical habit this whole course is built on, and §Worked Examples drills it on a crane cable and a motor lift.

§4 · The component working method

Every force problem in this course, and most in the courses after it, runs through one mechanical procedure — run it the same way every time and errors lose their hiding places. Choose axes deliberately: horizontal-vertical by default, but rotate the axes onto an incline the moment most forces align with it — the algebra shrinks. Build a sign table: before any arithmetic, write each force's components with signs from the geometry, in a small table, so every \( \cos \) and \( \sin \) is committed once and visibly. Sum, then recompose: add the columns for \( \Sigma F_x \) and \( \Sigma F_y \), recompose into magnitude and angle — and state the quadrant from the signs of the sums, not from the calculator's arctangent, which cheerfully reports the wrong quadrant half the time.

Then run the two closing audits. Recomposition: resolve your resultant back into components; if they don't reproduce the sums, a sign slipped. Magnitude sense: the resultant can never exceed the sum of the magnitudes and can never be less than the largest minus the rest — an answer outside that band is wrong before any rechecking. Ten disciplined seconds close out a page of arithmetic.

Closing. Components in, resultant out, units carried, weight computed from mass — four small disciplines that remove most of the errors this course will otherwise spend weeks catching.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Resolving a cable force

Problem. A crane cable applies \( F = 10\ \mathrm{kN} \) to a padeye, at \( 30^\circ \) above the horizontal. Find: the horizontal and vertical components.

Solution. (1) \( F_x = 10\cos 30^\circ = 8.66\ \mathrm{kN} \). (2) \( F_y = 10\sin 30^\circ = 5.00\ \mathrm{kN} \). (3) Check by recomposition: \( \sqrt{8.66^2 + 5.00^2} = 10.0\ \mathrm{kN} \) — the original magnitude returns.

Answer: \( F_x = 8.66\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( F_y = 5.00\ \mathrm{kN} \). Sanity check: at \( 30^\circ \) the force leans closer to horizontal than vertical, so \( F_x > F_y \) — as computed.

SOLVE 2

Weight, then sling tension

Problem. A 250 kg motor hangs from two identical sling legs, each at \( 60^\circ \) above the horizontal. Find: the weight and the tension per leg.

Solution. (1) Weight: \( W = mg = 250 \times 9.81 = 2452.5\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) Vertical equilibrium, two legs sharing symmetrically: \( 2T\sin 60^\circ = W \). (3) \( T = 2452.5 / (2 \times 0.866) = 1416\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: \( W \approx 2.45\ \mathrm{kN} \); each leg carries \( \approx 1.42\ \mathrm{kN} \). Sanity check: each leg carries more than \( W/2 = 1226\ \mathrm{N} \) because only the vertical component of the leg tension supports load — flattening the legs toward horizontal would drive \( T \) higher still, which is why rigging charts penalize shallow sling angles.

SOLVE 3

Three forces, one resultant

Problem. Three concurrent forces act on a padeye: 300 N along +x, 200 N along +y, and 150 N at 210° (measured CCW from +x). Find: the magnitude and direction of the resultant.

Solution. (1) Components: \( \Sigma F_x = 300 + 0 + 150\cos 210° = 300 - 129.9 = 170.1\ \mathrm{N} \); \( \Sigma F_y = 0 + 200 + 150\sin 210° = 200 - 75 = 125\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) Magnitude: \( R = \sqrt{170.1^2 + 125^2} = 211\ \mathrm{N} \). (3) Direction: both sums positive → first quadrant; \( \theta = \tan^{-1}(125/170.1) = 36.3° \) above +x.

Answer: \( R \approx 211\ \mathrm{N} \) at 36.3°. Sanity check: the magnitude band: largest force alone is 300 N and the sum of magnitudes is 650 N — but the 150 N force opposes the other two, so a resultant below 300 N is expected; recomposing 211 N at 36.3° returns (170.1, 125.0) exactly.

MCQ 1

A 20 kN cable force acts at \( 60^\circ \) above the horizontal. What is its vertical component?

  1. 10.0 kN
  2. 17.3 kN ✓
  3. 20.0 kN
  4. 34.6 kN

\( F_y = F\sin\theta = 20\sin 60^\circ = 17.3\ \mathrm{kN} \). Choosing 10.0 kN is the classic swap — that is \( 20\cos 60^\circ \), the horizontal component. The component along an axis uses the sine of the angle measured from the other axis; sketch the triangle before reaching for a formula.

MCQ 2

Forces of 6 kN and 8 kN act at a point. Which resultant magnitude is impossible?

  1. 2 kN
  2. 10 kN
  3. 14 kN
  4. 16 kN ✓

Two forces bound their resultant between their difference and their sum: \( |8-6| = 2 \) kN (opposing) up to \( 8+6 = 14 \) kN (aligned). 10 kN sits inside the range (it is the perpendicular case, \( \sqrt{6^2+8^2} \)). 16 kN exceeds the sum — no orientation can produce it.

MCQ 3

A 100 N force acts at 60° above the horizontal. What are its horizontal and vertical components?

  1. 50.0 N and 86.6 N ✓
  2. 86.6 N and 50.0 N
  3. 100 N and 60 N
  4. 60 N and 100 N

\( F_x = 100\cos 60° = 50.0\ \mathrm{N} \), \( F_y = 100\sin 60° = 86.6\ \mathrm{N} \). The swapped pair is the classic cosine–sine mix-up — at angles above 45° the vertical component must be the larger, which settles it without a calculator. The other options confuse the magnitude and the angle with components, which have force units both.

MCQ 4

A crane load cell reads 500 (calibrated in kg). What force does the load apply to the hook?

  1. 500 N
  2. \( \approx 4.9\ \mathrm{kN} \) ✓
  3. \( \approx 50\ \mathrm{kN} \)
  4. 500 kg is already a force

\( W = mg = 500 \times 9.81 = 4905\ \mathrm{N} \approx 4.9\ \mathrm{kN} \). Kilograms measure mass; the hook feels newtons. Writing \"500 N\" understates the load nearly tenfold — the exact unit slip Lesson 1's vignette catches in a lift plan.

MCQ 5

Two 100 N forces act on a bracket at right angles to each other. The resultant's magnitude is:

  1. ≈141 N ✓
  2. 200 N
  3. 100 N
  4. 0 N

Perpendicular forces add vectorially: \( R = \sqrt{100^2 + 100^2} = 141\ \mathrm{N} \). Answering 200 N adds magnitudes algebraically — only legal when forces are collinear and aligned; 0 N would require them opposed. The resultant of two forces always lies between their difference and their sum: here between 0 and 200, and 141 sits exactly where the right angle puts it.

Lesson 02 · Moments and couples

Lecture

§1 · Moment: the turning effect of a force

The moment of a force about a point measures its tendency to rotate a body about that point:

\[ M = F d \tag{1} \]

where \( d \) is the perpendicular distance from the point to the force's line of action — not the distance to where the force happens to be applied. A 500 N pull on a 0.6 m wrench gives 300 N·m only if the pull is perpendicular to the handle; at \( 60^\circ \) to it, the effective arm shrinks and \( M = 500 \times 0.6 \sin 60^\circ = 259.8\ \mathrm{N \cdot m} \). Sign convention: pick counterclockwise positive and never renegotiate mid-problem.

§2 · Varignon's theorem

The moment of a force equals the sum of the moments of its components. This is not a curiosity — it is the standard labor-saving device. Rather than hunt for a perpendicular distance through awkward geometry, resolve the force into \( F_x \) and \( F_y \) at its point of application and take each component's moment with its own easy arm. The wrench case above: \( F_y = 500\sin 60^\circ \) acting 0.6 m from the nut gives the same 259.8 N·m in one line.

§3 · Couples: pure rotation

Two equal, opposite, non-collinear forces form a couple. Their force resultant is zero — a couple does not push a body anywhere — but their moment \( M = Fd \) (\( d \) the separation) is the same about every point in the plane. That free-floating property is why we may slide a couple anywhere on a rigid body, and why any force system reduces to one force plus one couple at a chosen point — the standard equivalent system used from here to machine design.

§4 · The moment working method

Moments punish improvisation, so fix the routine. Declare the convention first — counterclockwise positive, written at the top of the page — and never renegotiate it mid-problem. Choose the moment center tactically: the point that kills the most unknowns, usually where reaction lines cross; the moment of a force through the chosen point is zero, and that choice is free information. Pick the computation route per force: if the perpendicular distance is visible, \( M = Fd \) is fastest; if the geometry is angled and awkward, split the force into components at its point of application and let Varignon's theorem sum the pieces — components times their own clean arms beat one force times a distance you can't see.

Treat couples as the privileged objects they are: a couple's moment is the same about every point, so it moves freely around the diagram without recalculation — the one moment that never asks "about where?". And keep the units honest: newton-metres, force times length, always stated. A moment reported as a bare number is an invitation to the classic error of adding it to a force — dimensional homogeneity, learned in the mathematics course, polices this line for free.

Closing. Moment is force times perpendicular arm; Varignon converts geometry into components; couples turn without pushing and belong to no particular point. These three facts carry every equilibrium equation that follows.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Wrench moment, two ways

Problem. A 500 N force acts at the end of a 0.6 m wrench, at \( 60^\circ \) to the handle axis. Find: the moment about the nut, by perpendicular arm and by Varignon.

Solution. (1) Perpendicular arm: \( d = 0.6\sin 60^\circ = 0.520\ \mathrm{m} \), so \( M = 500 \times 0.520 = 259.8\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \). (2) Varignon: the component across the handle is \( 500\sin 60^\circ = 433.0\ \mathrm{N} \) with arm 0.6 m: \( M = 433.0 \times 0.6 = 259.8\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \). The along-handle component's line of action passes through the nut — zero arm, zero moment.

Answer: \( M = 259.8\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \) by both routes. Sanity check: the perpendicular case bounds it: \( 500 \times 0.6 = 300\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \) is the maximum possible, and \( 259.8 < 300 \). ✓

SOLVE 2

A couple on a valve handwheel

Problem. Two hands apply equal and opposite 200 N forces at diametrically opposite points of a 0.3 m-diameter handwheel. Find: the moment, and the moment about a point 1 m away from the wheel's center.

Solution. (1) Couple moment: \( M = Fd = 200 \times 0.3 = 60\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \). (2) About the distant point: a couple's moment is identical about every point in the plane — still \( 60\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \). The two forces' moments about that point differ individually, but their sum is unchanged.

Answer: \( 60\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \), everywhere. Sanity check: the force resultant is \( 200 - 200 = 0 \) — the wheel is turned, not translated, which is exactly what a handwheel is for.

SOLVE 3

The angled pull on a valve wrench

Problem. A 250 N pull is applied at the end of a 0.6 m wrench arm, at 40° to the arm. Find: the moment about the pivot, and the pull angle that would maximize it.

Solution. (1) Only the component perpendicular to the arm turns it: \( M = F\,r\sin\theta = 250 \times 0.6 \times \sin 40° = 150 \times 0.643 = 96.4\ \mathrm{N\,m} \). (2) The maximum comes at \( \theta = 90° \): \( M_{max} = 250 \times 0.6 = 150\ \mathrm{N\,m} \) — pulling square to the arm.

Answer: 96.4 N·m; maximum 150 N·m at 90°. Sanity check: \( \sin 40° = 0.643 \) means the angled pull delivers 64% of the perpendicular pull's moment — exactly the fraction computed, and exactly why a fitter instinctively squares up on a stubborn valve.

MCQ 1

A 400 N force acts perpendicular to a wrench, 0.25 m from the nut. The moment about the nut is:

  1. 100 N·m ✓
  2. 400 N·m
  3. 1600 N·m
  4. 25 N·m

\( M = Fd = 400 \times 0.25 = 100\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \). Perpendicular application means the full 0.25 m is the moment arm — no sine factor needed. 1600 N·m divides instead of multiplying; 25 N·m forgets the force entirely.

MCQ 2

A couple acting on a bracket is evaluated about a point 2 m away instead of at the bracket. Its moment:

  1. doubles
  2. halves
  3. is unchanged ✓
  4. drops to zero

A couple's moment is the same about every point in the plane — its force resultant is zero, so moving the reference point adds equal and opposite contributions that cancel. That location-independence is what makes couples freely movable on a rigid body.

MCQ 3

Two 80 N forces form a couple with 0.5 m between their lines of action. The couple's moment about a point 3 m away from the pair is:

  1. 40 N·m — a couple's moment is the same about every point ✓
  2. 240 N·m — moment grows with distance
  3. 20 N·m
  4. Cannot be found without the exact point

\( M = F s = 80 \times 0.5 = 40\ \mathrm{N\,m} \), and the defining property of a couple is that this value is identical about every point — the two forces' individual moments change with the point, but their sum never does. Growing the moment with distance imports single-force thinking; that is exactly the habit the couple concept exists to break.

MCQ 4

  1. summing the moments of the force's components ✓
  2. doubling the force and halving the arm
  3. taking moments only about the centroid
  4. ignoring forces through the reference point

The moment of a force equals the sum of the moments of its components — so resolve at the point of application and use each component's easy rectangular arm. (Forces whose line of action passes through the moment point do contribute zero, but that is a corollary, not the theorem.)

MCQ 5

Varignon's theorem lets you compute a force's moment by:

  1. Summing the moments of the force's components about the same point ✓
  2. Multiplying the force by any convenient distance
  3. Averaging the moments about two points
  4. Replacing the force with its resultant

Lesson 03 · The free-body diagram

Lecture

§1 · Isolate, replace, label

A free-body diagram is built in three deliberate moves. Isolate: draw the body alone, severed from every support and contact. Replace: at each cut, draw the force or moment that the removed part exerted on the body. Label: every force gets a symbol, a direction, and — for knowns — a value with units. The most common omission is the body's own weight, acting at its center of gravity; the second most common is a forgotten contact force at a surface the sketch made look innocent.

§2 · Supports are force vocabularies

Each support type supplies a fixed menu of reactions. A roller or smooth surface: one force, normal to the rolling surface. A pin: two force components, unknown in direction until solved. A fixed (built-in) support: two force components plus a moment — that resisting moment is what a pin cannot supply, and it is why a flagpole stays up while a pinned pole would fall. A taut cable: one pull, along the cable, never a push.

§3 · Every equilibrium error is an FBD error

When a statics answer comes out wrong, the algebra is rarely the culprit; the diagram is. A force drawn at the wrong point, a reaction assumed in a direction the support cannot supply, an internal force shown on a body that was never cut — each corrupts the equations before they are written. The professional habit is to treat the FBD as a deliverable in itself: a diagram a colleague could take and write the equations from without asking you anything. §Worked Examples applies the discipline to a simply supported beam and a cantilever.

§4 · The FBD checklist

The free-body diagram is a procedure, not an artwork. Run the checklist in order. One — isolate: redraw ONLY the chosen body, stripped of everything it touches. Two — replace every contact: each support, cable, surface, and neighbor becomes the force set it can transmit — a cable becomes one pull along its line, a smooth surface one normal push, a pin two components. Three — add the field forces: weight at the center of gravity, always. Four — name every unknown with a symbol and an assumed direction; a wrong assumption costs nothing — the algebra answers with a minus sign. Five — count: unknowns versus available equations, before solving; a mismatch caught here saves a page of doomed algebra.

Two disciplines keep diagrams honest. Forces the body exerts on other things never appear — an FBD shows only what acts on the body, and mixing the action–reaction pair onto one diagram is the classic corruption. And when a problem involves several connected bodies, draw a separate FBD per body plus, where helpful, one of the whole assembly — the pair of views cross-checks itself, because the internal forces must cancel in the assembly view.

Closing. Isolate, replace, label; know each support's force menu; never write \( \Sigma F = 0 \) until the picture is finished. The FBD is not preparation for the analysis — it is the analysis.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Beam on a pin and a roller

Problem. A 4 m beam is pinned at A and rests on a roller at B (right end). A 2 kN load acts 1.5 m from A. Neglect beam weight. Find: the reactions.

Solution. (1) FBD: pin at A gives \( A_x, A_y \); roller at B gives vertical \( R_B \); load 2 kN down at 1.5 m. (2) \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \Rightarrow A_x = 0 \) (no horizontal loads). (3) \( \Sigma M_A = 0 \): \( R_B \times 4 - 2 \times 1.5 = 0 \Rightarrow R_B = 0.75\ \mathrm{kN} \). (4) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \): \( A_y = 2 - 0.75 = 1.25\ \mathrm{kN} \).

Answer: \( A_y = 1.25\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( R_B = 0.75\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( A_x = 0 \). Sanity check: the load sits nearer A, so A carries more — 1.25 > 0.75, and the two sum to 2 kN. ✓

SOLVE 2

Cantilever: the moment reaction earns its keep

Problem. A bracket is built into a wall (fixed support) and carries 3 kN downward at 2 m from the wall. Find: the wall reactions.

Solution. (1) FBD: at the wall draw \( V \) (vertical force), \( H \) (horizontal force), and moment \( M_w \). (2) \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \Rightarrow H = 0 \). (3) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \Rightarrow V = 3\ \mathrm{kN} \) upward. (4) \( \Sigma M_{wall} = 0 \Rightarrow M_w = 3 \times 2 = 6\ \mathrm{kN\cdot m} \), opposing the load's turning.

Answer: \( V = 3\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( M_w = 6\ \mathrm{kN\cdot m} \). Sanity check: replace the fixed support with a pin: no \( M_w \) is available, nothing balances the 6 kN·m, and the bracket rotates — the moment reaction is not optional bookkeeping, it is what "built-in" means.

SOLVE 3

Free body on an incline

Problem. A 50 kg crate rests on a smooth 25° incline, held by a rope parallel to the slope. Draw the free-body diagram and find the rope tension and the normal force. \( g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \).

Solution. (1) FBD: weight \( W = 50 \times 9.81 = 490.5\ \mathrm{N} \) vertically down; normal \( N \) perpendicular to the slope; tension \( T \) up the slope. Axes along and normal to the incline. (2) Along the slope: \( T = W\sin 25° = 490.5 \times 0.4226 = 207\ \mathrm{N} \). (3) Normal to it: \( N = W\cos 25° = 490.5 \times 0.9063 = 445\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: \( T \approx 207\ \mathrm{N} \), \( N \approx 445\ \mathrm{N} \). Sanity check: \( \sqrt{207^2 + 445^2} = 490.8 \approx W \) — the two components reassemble the weight, as the rotated axes demand; and a steeper slope would trade N for T, exactly as intuition says.

MCQ 1

Which support supplies a moment reaction?

  1. Roller
  2. Pin
  3. Cable
  4. Fixed (built-in) support ✓

Only the fixed support resists rotation, adding a moment to its two force components. A pin gives two force components but spins freely; a roller gives one normal force; a cable one axial pull. This menu is why a flagpole (fixed) stands while the same pole on a pin would fall.

MCQ 2

The most commonly omitted force on a free-body diagram is:

  1. the body's own weight ✓
  2. the applied load
  3. the roller reaction
  4. wind load

Self-weight, acting at the center of gravity, is invisible on the sketch and therefore chronically forgotten — the first item to check on any suspect FBD. Applied loads and support reactions are at least prompted by the drawing; gravity prompts nothing.

MCQ 3

Which force does NOT belong on the free-body diagram of a crate resting on the floor?

  1. The force the crate exerts on the floor ✓
  2. The crate's weight
  3. The normal force from the floor on the crate
  4. A rope tension pulling the crate

An FBD shows only forces acting on the chosen body. The crate-on-floor force acts on the floor — it is the Newton's-third-law partner of the normal force, and the pair never appears on one diagram together. Including it is the most common FBD corruption, and it silently doubles the contact force in the equations.

MCQ 4

A taut cable attached to a body can exert on it:

  1. a pull or a push along the cable
  2. only a pull along the cable ✓
  3. a force in any direction
  4. a pull plus a moment

Cables carry tension only — they pull along their own axis and buckle rather than push. Drawing a cable force as a push (or at an angle to the cable) is an FBD error that no amount of correct algebra downstream can repair.

MCQ 5

A planar body is supported by one pin and one roller. How many unknown reaction components appear on its FBD?

  1. 3 ✓
  2. 2
  3. 4
  4. 6

The pin contributes two components; the roller one, normal to its surface: three unknowns — exactly matching the three planar equilibrium equations, which is why pin-plus-roller is THE standard determinate support scheme. Four would demand more equations than statics owns; two would leave the body a mechanism.

Lesson 04 · Equilibrium of a particle

Lecture

§1 · Concurrent forces, one point, two (or three) equations

A particle in statics is any body whose forces all pass through one point — a hook, a ring, a connection node. Equilibrium then needs only force balance:

\[ \Sigma F_x = 0, \qquad \Sigma F_y = 0 \tag{1} \]

(add \( \Sigma F_z = 0 \) in 3-D). No moment equation exists to help: concurrent forces have no arm about their common point. Two equations means two unknowns per 2-D node — count before solving, and if unknowns exceed equations, another FBD is required, not more algebra.

§2 · Components or geometry — choose deliberately

With exactly three concurrent forces in equilibrium, the vectors must close into a triangle tip-to-tail; the law of sines then hands over the answer quickly and the geometric fact is worth having on its own. With four or more forces, or awkward angles, components (1) win every time and never mislead. The professional default is components; the triangle is a fast lane you take when the problem is small and the geometry clean.

§3 · Cables, rings, and pulleys

Cable systems are particle equilibrium in the field. Each cable pulls along its axis; a frictionless pulley changes a cable's direction but not its tension — the same \( T \) runs through. A single movable pulley therefore halves the holding force: two rope parts share the load. These idealizations (massless cable, frictionless sheave) are stated assumptions, not truths — real sheave friction adds a few percent, which is why rigging tables carry efficiency factors. §Worked Examples solves the classic two-cable suspension and a movable-pulley hold.

§4 · The particle equilibrium recipe

Concurrent-force problems yield to a fixed sequence. FBD of the point — the ring, hook, or knot where the forces meet — with every cable's pull drawn along its own line. Axes and components: resolve each force with the sign table from Lesson 1. Write both equations: \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \) and \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \), giving two equations for at most two unknowns. Solve simultaneously — substitution is usually cleanest: express one tension from the x-equation, feed it to the y-equation. Check physically: substitute the answers back until both sums close to zero within rounding, and confirm every cable came out in tension — a negative cable force is not a solution, it is a diagnosis that the geometry cannot hang that load that way.

Read the answers like an engineer afterward. The steeper cable always carries more of a hung load's weight; as a cable approaches horizontal its tension grows without bound — which is why "just pull it a little tighter" is how straight spans of cable snap hardware. The recipe produces numbers; the reading of them produces judgment, and the judgment is what the courses downstream — and the plant floor — actually pay for.

Closing. One point, force balance only, unknowns counted against equations, triangle for three clean forces, components for everything else. Particle equilibrium is small — and it is the kernel of every truss joint in Lesson 6.

SOLVE 1

Two cables, one weight

Problem. A 500 N weight hangs from a ring supported by cable A at \( 30^\circ \) above horizontal (up-left) and cable B at \( 45^\circ \) above horizontal (up-right). Find: both tensions.

Solution. (1) FBD of the ring: \( T_A \) up-left at 30°, \( T_B \) up-right at 45°, 500 N down. (2) \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \): \( -T_A\cos 30^\circ + T_B\cos 45^\circ = 0 \Rightarrow T_A = 0.8165\,T_B \). (3) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \): \( T_A\sin 30^\circ + T_B\sin 45^\circ = 500 \). (4) Substitute: \( T_B(0.8165 \times 0.5 + 0.7071) = 500 \Rightarrow T_B = 448.3\ \mathrm{N} \), \( T_A = 366.0\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: \( T_A = 366\ \mathrm{N} \), \( T_B = 448\ \mathrm{N} \). Sanity check: back-substitute: \( 366.0\sin 30^\circ + 448.3\sin 45^\circ = 183.0 + 317.0 = 500\ \mathrm{N} \). ✓ The steeper cable (B) carries more of the vertical — as its geometry says it must.

SOLVE 2

The movable pulley

Problem. A 100 kg load hangs from a single frictionless movable pulley; one rope end is anchored, the other held by a worker, both rope parts vertical. Find: the holding force.

Solution. (1) Load weight: \( W = 100 \times 9.81 = 981\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) FBD of the pulley block: two rope parts up, each with the same tension \( T \) (frictionless sheave), \( W \) down. (3) \( 2T = 981 \Rightarrow T = 490.5\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: \( \approx 490\ \mathrm{N} \) — half the load. Sanity check: the worker pays for the halved force with doubled rope travel; energy balances. A real sheave adds friction, so field pull runs a few percent higher — the idealization is stated, not smuggled.

SOLVE 3

Two cables, one load

Problem. A 1200 N load hangs from a ring supported by two cables: cable 1 at 30° above horizontal to the left, cable 2 at 45° above horizontal to the right. Find: both cable tensions.

Solution. (1) FBD of the ring; equilibrium of the concurrent system. (2) \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \): \( T_1\cos 30° = T_2\cos 45° \Rightarrow T_2 = 1.2247\,T_1 \). (3) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \): \( T_1\sin 30° + T_2\sin 45° = 1200 \Rightarrow 0.5\,T_1 + 0.866\,T_1 = 1200 \Rightarrow T_1 = 1200/1.366 = 878\ \mathrm{N} \). (4) \( T_2 = 1.2247 \times 878 = 1076\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: \( T_1 \approx 878\ \mathrm{N} \), \( T_2 \approx 1076\ \mathrm{N} \). Sanity check: the steeper cable (45°) carries more — as it must — and substituting back: \( 878\times0.5 + 1076\times0.7071 = 439 + 761 = 1200\ \mathrm{N} \) ✓; both tensions positive, so both cables genuinely pull.

MCQ 1

Three concurrent forces hold a particle in equilibrium. Drawn tip-to-tail they must:

  1. form a closed triangle ✓
  2. be equal in magnitude
  3. be mutually perpendicular
  4. all pass through the ground

\( \Sigma F = 0 \) means the vector chain returns to its start — a closed triangle. Magnitudes need not be equal (each opposes the resultant of the other two), and perpendicularity is a special case, not a requirement. The closed triangle is also the fast graphical solution route.

MCQ 2

A 1000 N load hangs symmetrically from two slings, each at \( 45^\circ \) above horizontal. Tension per sling:

  1. 500 N
  2. 707 N ✓
  3. 1000 N
  4. 1414 N

\( 2T\sin 45^\circ = 1000 \Rightarrow T = 1000/(2 \times 0.707) = 707\ \mathrm{N} \). Each sling carries more than half the load because only its vertical component supports weight. 500 N ignores the angle; 1414 N applies the factor twice.

MCQ 3

A body is in equilibrium under exactly three non-parallel forces. The three forces must be:

  1. Concurrent — their lines of action meet at one point ✓
  2. Equal in magnitude
  3. Perpendicular to each other
  4. Applied at the same point on the body

If two of the lines meet at a point, the third must pass through it too — otherwise it would create an unbalanced moment about that intersection. That is the three-force-member theorem, and it locates unknown reaction directions geometrically before any algebra. Equal magnitudes and right angles are special cases, not requirements; the forces act where they act — only their lines must concur.

MCQ 4

A rope runs over a frictionless pulley. The pulley changes the rope's:

  1. tension only
  2. direction only ✓
  3. both tension and direction
  4. neither

With no friction at the sheave, the same tension runs through the whole rope — the pulley redirects it, nothing more. Mechanical advantage in pulley systems comes from the number of rope parts sharing the load, never from a single sheave altering tension.

MCQ 5

A ring in equilibrium is pulled by exactly two cables. The two tensions must be:

  1. Equal, opposite, and collinear ✓
  2. Equal and perpendicular
  3. Proportional to the cable lengths
  4. Zero

Two-force equilibrium demands the forces be equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and share one line of action — otherwise either a net force or a net couple survives. This is the two-force-member principle that later turns truss members into pure tension/compression carriers. Cable length is irrelevant to statics; and nonzero loads are perfectly possible — they just must be collinear.

Lesson 05 · Equilibrium of rigid bodies

Lecture

§1 · The full toolkit

A rigid body in plane equilibrium obeys three independent equations:

\[ \Sigma F_x = 0, \qquad \Sigma F_y = 0, \qquad \Sigma M_O = 0 \tag{1} \]

with the moment point \( O \) yours to choose — and choosing it on an unknown force's line of action removes that unknown from the equation. The craft of rigid-body statics is mostly the craft of choosing moment points that make equations fall apart into one-unknown pieces.

§2 · Two- and three-force members

A member loaded at exactly two points carries force only along the line joining them — anything else would leave an unbalanced moment. Recognizing a two-force member converts a two-component unknown into a single magnitude along a known direction, which is a large discount. A three-force member's forces must be concurrent (or all parallel); that fact locates unknown directions geometrically. Both shortcuts come from \( \Sigma M = 0 \) applied with intent.

§3 · Determinate or not

Three equations solve three unknowns. A beam on a pin and a roller (3 unknowns) is statically determinate: statics alone finishes it. Add a second pin (4 unknowns) and equilibrium no longer decides how the supports share — the structure is statically indeterminate, and the answer depends on stiffness, which is Strength of Materials' opening business. Recognizing indeterminacy early saves you from hunting for a fourth equation that does not exist.

§4 · The rigid-body recipe

Bodies with size add one resource and one obligation: moments. The sequence is fixed. FBD with the support table: replace each support by its reaction set — pin two, roller one, fixed three — and count; a planar body offers exactly three equations, so more than three unknowns stops the problem before it starts. Take moments first, and tactically: choose the moment center where the most unknown reaction lines intersect — usually a support — so one \( \Sigma M = 0 \) line yields one unknown immediately. Finish with the force sums: \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \), \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \) deliver the remaining reactions. Audit with a spare moment equation: a moment sum about a different point, computed with the solved numbers, must close to zero — the free self-check almost nobody uses and every professional should.

Two readings matter on the way out. Reactions must add up to carry the total applied load — vertically and horizontally — or something is lost. And the load-sharing should make physical sense: a point load near one support loads that support proportionally harder, in inverse ratio of the distances — lever arms doing exactly what levers do.

One caution for the field: supports in drawings are idealizations of hardware. A "pin" is a bolted cleat that also resists a little moment; a "roller" is a slotted hole or a bearing pad that can bind. The three-equation arithmetic stands on those idealizations, so when a measured reaction disagrees stubbornly with the calculation, inspect the support before doubting the algebra — the hardware may have quietly changed its type.

Closing. Three equations, moment points chosen to kill unknowns, two-force members spotted on sight, and the determinacy count run before solving. That is rigid-body statics in one paragraph; the rest is practice.

SOLVE 1

Beam with mixed loading

Problem. A 6 m beam, pin at A (left), roller at B (right), carries a uniform load of 2 kN/m across its full length plus a 5 kN point load 2 m from A. Find: the reactions.

Solution. (1) Replace the UDL by its resultant: \( 2 \times 6 = 12\ \mathrm{kN} \) at midspan (3 m). (2) \( \Sigma M_A = 0 \): \( 6R_B - 12(3) - 5(2) = 0 \Rightarrow R_B = 46/6 = 7.67\ \mathrm{kN} \). (3) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \): \( R_A = 12 + 5 - 7.67 = 9.33\ \mathrm{kN} \). (4) Check with \( \Sigma M_B \): \( 9.33 \times 6 - 12 \times 3 - 5 \times 4 = 56 - 36 - 20 = 0 \). ✓

Answer: \( R_A = 9.33\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( R_B = 7.67\ \mathrm{kN} \). Sanity check: the point load sits toward A, so A carries the larger share; totals sum to 17 kN, the whole applied load. ✓

SOLVE 2

The two-force strut discount

Problem. A horizontal arm is pinned to a wall at A and carries 4 kN down at its 2 m free end. A strut, pinned at the arm 1.2 m from A and to the wall below, props it at \( 45^\circ \). Find: the strut force and pin reactions at A.

Solution. (1) The strut is a two-force member: its force \( T \) acts along its \( 45^\circ \) axis — one unknown, not two. (2) \( \Sigma M_A = 0 \): \( T\sin 45^\circ \times 1.2 = 4 \times 2 \Rightarrow T = 9.43\ \mathrm{kN} \) (compression — it props). (3) \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \): \( A_x = T\cos 45^\circ = 6.67\ \mathrm{kN} \), directed toward the wall. (4) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \): \( A_y = 4 - T\sin 45^\circ = 4 - 6.67 = -2.67\ \mathrm{kN} \) — the pin actually pulls the arm down.

Answer: strut 9.43 kN (compression); at A: 6.67 kN horizontal, 2.67 kN downward. Sanity check: the strut's vertical push (6.67 kN) exceeds the 4 kN load, so the pin must hold the arm down — a sign the arithmetic predicted and the negative \( A_y \) confirmed.

SOLVE 3

Reactions of a simply supported beam

Problem. A 6 m beam is pinned at A and rests on a roller at B. A 4 kN load acts 2 m from A. Find: both support reactions.

Solution. (1) FBD: \( A_x, A_y \) at the pin, \( B_y \) at the roller; no horizontal loads → \( A_x = 0 \). (2) Moments about A (killing both pin unknowns): \( \Sigma M_A = B_y \times 6 - 4 \times 2 = 0 \Rightarrow B_y = 8/6 = 1.33\ \mathrm{kN} \). (3) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \): \( A_y = 4 - 1.33 = 2.67\ \mathrm{kN} \).

Answer: \( A_y = 2.67\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( B_y = 1.33\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( A_x = 0 \). Sanity check: the load sits twice as close to A as to B, and A carries twice B's share \( (2.67 = 2\times1.33) \) — the lever ratio exactly; audit moment about B: \( 2.67\times6 - 4\times4 = 16 - 16 = 0 \) ✓.

MCQ 1

  2. perpendicular to its axis

  4. only if it is straight

MCQ 2

  2. 5.0 kN
  3. 7.5 kN
  4. 10 kN

MCQ 3

How many independent equilibrium equations does a single rigid body offer in a planar problem?

  1. 3 ✓
  2. 2
  3. 4
  4. 6

Two force sums and one moment sum: \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \), \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \), \( \Sigma M = 0 \). A plane structure has four unknown reactions. Equilibrium alone:

"Vertically upward" is the flat-ground habit misapplied; "any direction" describes a pin, not a roller. Lesson 06 · Trusses

Lecture

§1 · A truss is a set of two-force members

The truss idealization: straight members, frictionless pin joints, loads applied only at joints. Every member is then a two-force member — pure tension or pure compression along its axis. That single assumption converts a structure into bookkeeping: find each member's axial force and its sense. Real gusset-plated, bolted trusses are not pinned, but the idealization prices member forces remarkably well and is the standard first analysis of any roof truss or braced rack.

§2 · Method of joints, method of sections

The method of joints isolates one pin at a time and applies particle equilibrium — two equations per joint, so start at a joint with at most two unknowns (usually a support) and walk the structure. The method of sections cuts clean through up to three members and treats one whole side as a rigid body; a moment taken about the intersection of two cut members isolates the third in a single line. Joints when you want everything; sections when you want one member deep inside the span without solving half the truss to reach it.

§3 · Zero-force members and reading the load path

At an unloaded joint of two non-collinear members, both are zero-force; at an unloaded joint of three members where two are collinear, the third is zero-force. Spotting these by inspection prunes the work — and teaches the deeper habit: a truss is a load path. Follow the applied load through diagonals and chords to the supports and the force pattern (top chord compression, bottom chord tension in a simply supported truss under gravity load) stops being memorized and starts being seen.

§4 · Choosing the attack: joints or sections

Truss problems reward a decision made before any arithmetic. Sweep for zero-force members first — the two-collinear-plus-one pattern at unloaded joints — and strike them out; trusses routinely shrink by a quarter before any equation is written. Ask what the problem actually wants. Every member force? Method of joints: find a starting joint with at most two unknowns (support ends and loaded tips qualify), solve it, and let each solved member unlock the next joint. Three specific member forces in a big truss? Method of sections: cut through at most three members including the targets, treat either half as a rigid body, and take a moment about the point where the two unwanted cut members intersect — the target force falls out in one line.

Keep the bookkeeping uniform: assume every member in tension, let negative answers declare compression, and record T or C beside every result — downstream design cares about the difference more than the magnitude, because compression members buckle and tension members don't. Final audit: at any solved joint, the component sums must close; thirty seconds of checking buys back the whole truss.

Closing. Two-force members, joints for the census, sections for the surgical strike, zero-force members pruned on sight. A truss drawing is a picture of forces waiting to be read.

SOLVE 1

Method of joints on a triangular truss

Problem. A symmetric triangular truss spans 4 m with apex height 1.5 m; a 10 kN load hangs at the apex. Find: the forces in a sloping member and the bottom chord.

Solution. (1) By symmetry each support carries 5 kN. (2) Slope angle: \( \theta = \tan^{-1}(1.5/2) = 36.87^\circ \). (3) Joint at left support — vertical: \( F_{slope}\sin\theta = 5 \Rightarrow F_{slope} = 5/0.6 = 8.33\ \mathrm{kN} \), pushing into the joint: compression. (4) Horizontal: \( F_{chord} = F_{slope}\cos\theta = 8.33 \times 0.8 = 6.67\ \mathrm{kN} \), pulling: tension.

Answer: sloping members 8.33 kN (C); bottom chord 6.67 kN (T). Sanity check: the sloped member must exceed the 5 kN vertical it carries (only its sine works vertically): 8.33 > 5. ✓ Flatter trusses drive both numbers up — why shallow roofs need heavier members.

SOLVE 2

One member by sections

Problem. A parallel-chord truss: span 8 m, height 2 m, four 2 m panels, 4 kN at each of the three interior bottom-chord joints. Find: the bottom-chord force in the second panel.

Solution. (1) Reactions: total 12 kN, symmetric, \( R = 6\ \mathrm{kN} \) each. (2) Cut vertically through the second panel (between \( x = 2 \) and \( x = 4 \) m); keep the left portion. (3) Moment about the top joint at \( x = 2\ \mathrm{m} \) (height 2 m) — the cut top chord and diagonal pass through it, vanishing from the equation. The 4 kN load at \( x = 2 \) also has zero arm. (4) \( \Sigma M = 0 \): \( F_{BC} \times 2 = 6 \times 2 \Rightarrow F_{BC} = 6\ \mathrm{kN} \) (tension).

Answer: 6 kN, tension. Sanity check: one cut, one moment equation, one member — against walking three joints to reach the same number. Sections earn their keep exactly here.

SOLVE 3

First joint of a roof truss

Problem. At a truss joint, a diagonal member rises at 45° and a bottom chord runs horizontally; the joint carries a 10 kN downward load. Find: both member forces and their sense (T or C).

Solution. (1) Assume both members in tension (pulling away from the joint). (2) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \): \( F_d\sin 45° - 10 = 0 \Rightarrow F_d = 14.14\ \mathrm{kN} \)… but the diagonal must push up to hold the load, so with the tension assumption the sign works out via geometry: taking the diagonal rising away from the joint, \( F_d = +14.14\ \mathrm{kN} \) along the member toward the joint = compression when the diagonal sits above the load. (3) \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \): \( F_c = F_d\cos 45° = 10\ \mathrm{kN} \), pulling away — tension.

Answer: diagonal ≈ 14.1 kN (C), chord = 10.0 kN (T). Sanity check: the diagonal's vertical component \( 14.14\sin45° = 10 \) exactly balances the load, and its horizontal component \( 14.14\cos45° = 10 \) is exactly what the chord resists — the classic strut-and-tie pair every roof truss repeats.

  4. indeterminate

  4. It is only a convention

Cut four and the section alone cannot finish (though a clever moment point may still extract one target). The limit is the equation budget, not the saw: nothing is physically cut at all.

Lesson 07 · Frames and machines

Lecture

§1 · Frames are not trusses

A frame contains at least one multi-force member — a member loaded at three or more points, or loaded between its pins. Its members carry shear and bending, not just axial force, so the truss shortcut (force along the member) is off the table except for any genuine two-force members hiding inside. Identify those first: every one you spot replaces two unknowns with one.

§2 · Dismember at the pins — and pay Newton his due

The method is systematic: draw an FBD of the whole frame for the external reactions, then dismember — separate the members at their connecting pins and draw each member alone. At every pin, the force one member exerts on the other appears on both diagrams, equal and opposite. The classic error is drawing pin components in inconsistent directions on the two sides, which silently violates \( \Sigma F = 0 \) for the pin itself. Adopt a convention, draw the action–reaction pair explicitly, and let negative answers correct your guesses.

§3 · Machines: frames that multiply force

A machine is a frame with moving parts designed to transmit and transform force — pliers, clamps, linkages, toggles. The same dismemberment analysis yields the mechanical advantage: output force per input force, set by the lever arms. A toggle clamp near its dead-center position multiplies force enormously because the geometry sends the output arm toward zero — the reason a modest hand force can hold a workpiece against machining loads. §Worked Examples runs a gripping tool and a loaded two-member frame.

§4 · The disassembly working method

Frames surrender to a fixed sequence, and improvising it is how pin forces get lost. Survey for two-force members first — straight, loaded only at their two pins — because each one found replaces two unknown components with a single force along the member line. Solve the assembly as one rigid body where possible, harvesting the external reactions before any disassembly. Then pull the pins: draw a separate FBD for every member, and at each shared pin write the force ONCE, applying it to the two members as an equal-and-opposite pair — the bookkeeping backbone of the whole method. Choose which member's diagram to solve first by counting unknowns: there is almost always one member with only two or three, and solving it feeds the others like the truss's joint order did.

For machines, the same disassembly answers the practical question — what does the jaw, blade, or clamp deliver for a given hand force? Moments about the pivot of the handle member give the multiplication directly: output over input equals input arm over output arm. When the answer looks too good, check whether friction and pin clearances were ignored — they were, and real tools deliver a little less than the statics promises.

Closing. Spot the two-force members, dismember at pins with honest action–reaction pairs, and read machines as frames whose geometry is doing force arithmetic. Statics of frames is Newton's third law made procedural.

Gripping tool

Problem. A gripping tool's handles are squeezed with 150 N at 200 mm from the pivot pin; the jaws grip at 40 mm on the other side of the pivot. Find: the jaw force and mechanical advantage.

Solution. (1) FBD of one handle-jaw lever about the pivot. (2) \( \Sigma M_{pivot} = 0 \): \( 150 \times 200 = F_{jaw} \times 40 \). (3) \( F_{jaw} = 30000/40 = 750\ \mathrm{N} \). (4) \( MA = 750/150 = 5 \) — equal to the arm ratio \( 200/40 \).

Answer: 750 N grip; mechanical advantage 5. Sanity check: arms ratio 5:1 must give force ratio 5:1 — the lever law in its plainest form; the pivot pin itself carries \( 150 + 750 = 900\ \mathrm{N} \), the largest force in the tool, which is why pivot pins wear first.

SOLVE 2

Two-member frame under a side load

Problem. Members AC and BC are pinned together at C \( (2, 3)\ \mathrm{m} \), with A \( (0,0) \) pinned and B \( (4,0) \) on a roller. A 6 kN horizontal force pushes at C. Find: reactions and member forces.

Solution. (1) Whole frame, \( \Sigma M_A = 0 \): \( B_y \times 4 - 6 \times 3 = 0 \Rightarrow B_y = 4.5\ \mathrm{kN} \) up. (2) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \Rightarrow A_y = -4.5\ \mathrm{kN} \) (i.e., 4.5 kN down); \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \Rightarrow A_x = -6\ \mathrm{kN} \) (opposing the push). (4) Solving joint C: \( F_{AC} = 5.41\ \mathrm{kN} \) tension, \( F_{BC} = 5.41\ \mathrm{kN} \) compression.

SOLVE 3

Problem. A pair of pliers has handles gripped 180 mm from the pivot pin and jaws biting 45 mm from it, on the other side. A technician squeezes with 120 N per handle. Find: the jaw clamping force, treating each half as a lever about the pin.

Lesson 08 · Internal forces: shear and bending moment

Lecture

§1 · Cut the beam, expose the invisible

External reactions are only half the story; design needs the forces inside the member. Cut a beam at any section and, on the exposed face, three internal resultants restore equilibrium: normal force \( N \), shear force \( V \), and bending moment \( M \). Sign convention (keep it for life): positive shear rotates the element clockwise; positive moment sags the beam — compression on top, tension on the bottom. The convention is arbitrary; consistency is not.

§2 · Diagrams, and the calculus that draws them

Shear and moment vary along the span, so we plot them. Two differential relations do most of the drawing:

\[ \frac{dV}{dx} = -w(x), \qquad \frac{dM}{dx} = V \tag{1} \]

Between point loads, \( V \) is constant and \( M \) runs straight; under a uniform load, \( V \) slopes linearly and \( M \) curves parabolically. A point load steps \( V \); a couple steps \( M \). And the operational headline: the bending moment is extremal where the shear crosses zero — because \( dM/dx = V = 0 \) there. That is where beams break, and where you look first.

§3 · Why these diagrams are the product

Strength of Materials will convert \( M \) into bending stress and \( V \) into shear stress; every beam, shaft, and rack girder calculation starts from these two plots. Drawing them is not an academic exercise before the real work — for the structural side of plant engineering, the diagram is the deliverable that the stress check consumes. §Worked Examples builds both canonical cases: the central point load and the uniform load.

§4 · Building the diagrams without tears

Internal-force diagrams are constructed, not guessed, and the construction has a grammar. Reactions first — no diagram survives wrong supports. Walk the beam from the left, cutting just after each load or support: shear jumps by each point force, slopes down under distributed load at exactly the load intensity, and the moment's slope at every station IS the shear there — \( dM/dx = V \). That one relation is the whole craft: where shear crosses zero, the moment peaks; where shear is constant, the moment runs straight; where the load is uniform, the moment curves as a parabola. Close the audit at the far end: both diagrams must return to zero (or to the end reaction/moment) — a diagram that doesn't close is announcing an arithmetic error at the exact spot it diverges.

Keep the benchmark cases in your head as calibration: a central point load peaks at \( PL/4 \); a uniform load at \( wL^2/8 \). Any computed maximum should be sanity-checked against the nearest benchmark before it goes near a design table — the courses on strength and machine design will consume these diagrams, and they inherit every error made here.

Closing. Cut, expose \( N, V, M \), plot with the two differential relations, and read the maximum moment where shear crosses zero. Beams are analyzed exactly once you can draw these two pictures without hesitation.

Central point load

Problem. Simply supported beam, span \( L = 4\ \mathrm{m} \), point load \( P = 8\ \mathrm{kN} \) at midspan. Find: the shear and moment diagrams and the maximum moment.

Solution. (1) Reactions: symmetric, \( R = 4\ \mathrm{kN} \) each. (2) Shear: \( V = +4\ \mathrm{kN} \) from A to midspan, stepping down by 8 to \( -4\ \mathrm{kN} \) to B. SOLVE 2

Uniform load

Problem. Simply supported beam, \( L = 6\ \mathrm{m} \), UDL \( w = 3\ \mathrm{kN/m} \). Find: diagrams and \( M_{max} \).

Answer: \( V_{max} = 9\ \mathrm{kN} \) at the supports; \( M_{max} = 13.5\ \mathrm{kN\cdot m} \) at midspan. Sanity check: shear crosses zero at midspan by symmetry, and the parabola peaks there. Shear and moment at a section

Problem. An 8 m simply supported beam carries a 6 kN point load at midspan. Find: the reactions, the internal shear and moment 2 m from the left support, and the maximum bending moment.

Solution. (1) Symmetry: \( R_A = R_B = 3\ \mathrm{kN} \). (2) Cut at 2 m (left of the load), keep the left piece: \( V = +3\ \mathrm{kN} \); \( M = 3 \times 2 = 6\ \mathrm{kN\,m} \). (3) Maximum at midspan where V crosses zero: \( M_{max} = PL/4 = 6 \times 8/4 = 12\ \mathrm{kN\,m} \).

Lesson 09 · Friction

Lecture

§1 · Dry friction and the threshold

A stationary block resists sliding with whatever friction force equilibrium demands — up to a ceiling:

\[ F \le \mu_s N \tag{1} \]

Equality holds only at impending motion; below it, friction is found from equilibrium, not from (1). Once sliding starts, kinetic friction \( \mu_k N \) (with \( \mu_k < \mu_s \)) takes over — which is why things lurch. The resultant of \( N \) and limiting \( F \) leans at the friction angle \( \phi = \tan^{-1}\mu_s \) from the normal; the cone it sweeps contains every possible contact reaction. If the applied load's line falls inside the cone, no sliding — geometry answering a force question.

§2 · Wedges and screws

A wedge is friction's canonical machine: driving it must beat friction on both faces, and the friction directions always oppose the impending relative motion — set them by asking "which way does each surface slide?" before writing equations. A power screw is a wedge wrapped around a cylinder: the thread's helix angle plays the wedge angle, and self-locking (the jack that holds under load) occurs when the friction angle exceeds the helix angle. Every jack, clamp, and threaded fastener trades on this.

§3 · Belt friction: the exponential surprise

A belt or rope wrapped on a drum can hold a large tension with a small one. Integrating the friction on each element of wrap gives the capstan equation:

\[ \frac{T_2}{T_1} = e^{\mu \beta} \tag{2} \]

with \( \beta \) the wrap angle in radians. The ratio is exponential in wrap — each added turn multiplies. Three turns of rope on a bollard hold a ship; the same law sets how much power a conveyor drive pulley can transmit before the belt slips.

§4 · The friction working method

Friction problems have one trap and one discipline. The trap: writing \( F = \mu_s N \) by reflex. Static friction is adaptive — it supplies exactly what equilibrium demands, UP TO the ceiling \( \mu_s N \), and equals the ceiling only at impending motion. So the method is: assume equilibrium, solve for the friction force required, then compare with the available maximum. Required below available — the body holds, and the actual friction is the required value, not the ceiling. Required above available — motion begins, and kinetic friction \( \mu_k N \) takes over at its lower, fixed value.

The discipline: recompute \( N \) from equilibrium every time the loading changes. A downward push on a crate raises N and buys holding capacity; an upward-angled pull robs it — the same pull that helps horizontally hurts vertically, and the trade is decided by the numbers, not intuition. Complete the method by checking the second failure mode: tall narrow bodies may TIP before they slide, so compare the moment of the push about the tipping edge with the restoring moment of the weight. Slip-or-tip is answered by whichever threshold arrives first, and rigging, racking, and machine-skid problems ask it weekly.

Treat published coefficients as estimates, not constants. Surface condition, moisture, paint, and dust move \( \mu \) by tens of percent, which is why lift plans and skid calculations carry margin on top of the friction arithmetic. When a job genuinely hinges on friction, the professional move is to measure — the tilt test that reads \( \mu_s \) straight off the angle of repose — and then still design as if the measured number were optimistic.

Closing. Friction is an inequality until motion impends; wedges and screws are friction geometrized; and wrap buys holding power exponentially. Get the impending-motion direction right and the rest is bookkeeping.

Lesson 10 · Centroids and centers of gravity

Lecture

§1 · The average position of area or weight

The centroid is the area-weighted average position of a shape:

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{\int x\, dA}{\int dA}, \qquad \bar{y} = \frac{\int y\, dA}{\int dA} \tag{1} \]

The center of gravity is the same construction weighted by weight; for uniform density and gravity they coincide. Integration handles curved boundaries once; engineering practice handles everything else by composite parts — split the shape into rectangles, triangles, and circles whose centroids are known, then take the weighted average. Holes enter the sum as negative areas: same formula, signed.

§2 · The tabular method

Composite work is done in a table: one row per part, columns for \( A \), \( \bar{x}_i \), \( \bar{y}_i \), \( A\bar{x}_i \), \( A\bar{y}_i \); sum the columns, divide. The discipline matters more than it looks — a consistent datum (pick one corner, state it) and signed areas prevent the classic errors: measuring one part from a different origin, or forgetting that a cut-out subtracts moment as well as area. The same table extends without modification to weights: replace \( A \) with \( W \) and the result is a center of gravity.

§3 · Where the hook must sit

Lift any rigid load from a single hook and it hangs with its center of gravity vertically below the hook. If the CG is off the hook line, the load tilts until they align — swinging tools, spilling contents, and shifting sling angles on the way. A lifting plan therefore starts with a CG calculation: composite masses of pump, motor, and baseplate, exactly the table of §2 with kilograms in the area column. The hook, or the sling spread, is then placed to put the CG under the crane's line of pull. §Worked Examples runs one steel section and one equipment skid.

§4 · The tabular working method

Centroid calculations live or die by bookkeeping, so industrialize it. Decompose the section into shapes whose centroids are known — rectangles, triangles, circles. Fix one reference corner for the whole table and never move it mid-problem; half of all centroid errors are two shapes measured from two different origins. Tabulate \( A_i \), \( \bar{x}_i \), \( \bar{y}_i \), \( A_i\bar{x}_i \), \( A_i\bar{y}_i \) — holes entering as negative areas with negative first moments — sum the columns, divide. The table is not scaffolding to discard later: the moment-of-inertia lesson extends the same table by one column, and CAD packages print the same quantities under the same names, so fluency here reads straight into the tools.

Audit the answer geometrically before accepting it: the centroid must sit inside the convex hull of the section (though possibly outside the material, as in an L or a channel — that is legal and common), and symmetry must be honored — any axis of symmetry the section has, the centroid sits on. An answer violating either rule is wrong with certainty, no recomputation required.

Closing. One averaging formula, executed by table, with signed areas and a declared datum. From steel sections to lifting plans, it is the same arithmetic wearing different units.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Centroid of an L-section

Problem. An L-section: vertical leg 20 mm wide × 100 mm tall; horizontal leg 80 mm × 20 mm extending right from the vertical leg's base. Datum: outer bottom-left corner. Find: the centroid.

Solution. (1) Part 1 (vertical): \( A_1 = 20 \times 100 = 2000\ \mathrm{mm^2} \), centroid (10, 50). (2) Part 2 (horizontal): \( A_2 = 80 \times 20 = 1600\ \mathrm{mm^2} \), centroid (60, 10). (3) \( \bar{x} = \dfrac{2000(10) + 1600(60)}{3600} = \dfrac{116000}{3600} = 32.2\ \mathrm{mm} \). (4) \( \bar{y} = \dfrac{2000(50) + 1600(10)}{3600} = \dfrac{116000}{3600} = 32.2\ \mathrm{mm} \).

Answer: \( (\bar{x}, \bar{y}) = (32.2, 32.2)\ \mathrm{mm} \). Sanity check: the centroid lies between the two part centroids, pulled toward the larger leg — and lands outside the metal, in the corner notch, which is perfectly legal: centroids are properties of the area, not addresses in it.

SOLVE 2

Skid center of gravity for a lift

Problem. A skid (masses representative): pump 320 kg centered at \( x = 0.5\ \mathrm{m} \), motor 260 kg at \( x = 1.6\ \mathrm{m} \), baseplate 140 kg at \( x = 1.1\ \mathrm{m} \), from the skid's left edge. Find: the CG and the single-hook pick point.

Solution. (1) Total mass: \( 320 + 260 + 140 = 720\ \mathrm{kg} \). (2) \( \bar{x} = \dfrac{320(0.5) + 260(1.6) + 140(1.1)}{720} = \dfrac{160 + 416 + 154}{720} = \dfrac{730}{720} = 1.014\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: CG at 1.01 m from the left edge; hook (or sling resultant) goes there. Sanity check: the CG sits between pump and motor, slightly motor-side of the midpoint — the motor's greater distance nearly offsets the pump's greater mass. Hook it at midspan (≈0.8–0.9 m for this layout) and the motor end dips on pick.

SOLVE 3

Centroid of an L-section

Problem. An L-shaped bracket section: a 100 × 20 mm horizontal base plate with a 20 × 80 mm vertical leg standing on its left end. Reference origin at the bottom-left corner. Find: the centroid.

Solution. (1) Parts: base \( A_1 = 2000\ \mathrm{mm^2} \) at \( (50, 10) \); leg \( A_2 = 1600\ \mathrm{mm^2} \) at \( (10, 60) \). (2) \( \bar{x} = (2000 \times 50 + 1600 \times 10)/3600 = 116{,}000/3600 = 32.2\ \mathrm{mm} \). (3) \( \bar{y} = (2000 \times 10 + 1600 \times 60)/3600 = 116{,}000/3600 = 32.2\ \mathrm{mm} \).

Answer: \( (\bar{x}, \bar{y}) \approx (32.2, 32.2)\ \mathrm{mm} \). Sanity check: the centroid lands between the two part centroids, pulled toward the larger base — and it sits just off the material in the L's inside corner, which is legal and typical for L-sections; a hanging bracket would tilt about exactly this point.

MCQ 1

A load is lifted from a single hook placed away from its center of gravity. On pick-up, the load:

  1. rises level
  2. tilts until the CG hangs below the hook ✓
  3. rotates continuously
  4. cannot be lifted

A suspended rigid body settles with its CG vertically below the support point — the only orientation with zero net moment. The tilt is the field's free CG-finder (the trial pick), and the hazard when tall equipment swings as it seeks that alignment.

MCQ 2

Masses of 100 kg at \( x = 0 \) and 300 kg at \( x = 2\ \mathrm{m} \) share a skid. The CG is at:

  1. 0.5 m
  2. 1.0 m
  3. 1.5 m ✓
  4. 2.0 m

\( \bar{x} = (100 \times 0 + 300 \times 2)/400 = 1.5\ \mathrm{m} \) — three-quarters of the way toward the heavier mass, matching the 3:1 mass ratio. 1.0 m is the unweighted midpoint, the error the weighted average exists to prevent.

MCQ 3

A section has one axis of symmetry. Its centroid:

  1. Lies somewhere on that axis ✓
  2. Lies at the section's geometric center
  3. Must lie inside the material
  4. Cannot be found without integration

Symmetry forces the first moment across the axis to cancel, pinning the centroid to the axis — one coordinate for free. The geometric center requires TWO axes of symmetry; and the centroid can sit outside the material entirely (an L-section's inside corner, a channel's mouth) — legal and common. Composite tables handle the rest without any integral.

MCQ 4

Can a shape's centroid lie outside its material?

  1. never
  2. only for curved shapes
  3. yes — an L-section's does ✓
  4. only if density varies

The centroid is an area-weighted average position, not an address in the metal — an L-section's centroid sits in the empty corner notch. Rings, channels, and angles all put it in air; slings and fixtures must still act through it.

MCQ 5

In a composite-area centroid table, a drilled hole is handled by entering:

  1. Its area and first moments with negative signs ✓
  2. Its area as positive but located at the origin
  3. Nothing — holes are ignored
  4. Half its area

A hole is anti-material: it removes area AND first moment, so it rides the same table with \( A < 0 \) and correspondingly negative \( A\bar{x} \), \( A\bar{y} \) — computed at the HOLE's own centroid. Ignoring holes biases the centroid toward the removed metal; the negative-entry convention keeps one uniform procedure for any section a drawing can throw at you.

Lesson 11 · Moments of inertia

Lecture

§1 · The second moment: area times distance squared

The second moment of area about the x-axis is

\[ I_x = \int y^2\, dA \tag{1} \]

Distance enters squared: area far from the axis counts disproportionately. For the workhorse rectangle about its own centroid, \( I = bh^3/12 \) — the cubed depth is the single most consequential exponent in structural engineering. \( I \) is not inertia in the dynamic sense; it is a purely geometric measure of how a section's area is deployed against bending.

§2 · The parallel-axis theorem

Centroidal values are tabulated; real composite parts sit away from the composite centroid. The transfer rule:

\[ I = \bar{I} + A d^2 \tag{2} \]

adds \( Ad^2 \) for a part whose own centroid lies distance \( d \) from the axis in question. Two habits keep it honest: the theorem transfers only from a centroidal axis (never between two non-centroidal axes directly), and in composite work the \( Ad^2 \) terms usually dwarf the parts' own \( \bar{I} \) — flanges of an I-beam earn their keep almost entirely through \( Ad^2 \).

§3 · Why sections look the way they do

An I-beam is the parallel-axis theorem drawn in steel: put most of the area in two flanges far from the neutral axis (huge \( d \)), keep a thin web to hold them apart and carry shear. §Worked Examples computes a small I-section at \( I = 4.49 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \) — then shows a solid square of the same area manages only \( 0.65 \times 10^6 \): the shape, not the steel, is doing seven times the work. Strength of Materials will divide bending moment by exactly this \( I \) to get stress; every section table you will ever open is a catalog of this one integral.

§4 · The transfer working method

Composite moments of inertia extend the centroid table by exactly one discipline: the parallel-axis transfer. The sequence never varies. Find the composite centroid first — Lesson 10's table verbatim. For each part, write its own centroidal \( \bar{I}_i \) from the standard formulas, measure the distance \( d_i \) from the part's centroid to the COMPOSITE centroid, and transfer: \( I_i = \bar{I}_i + A_i d_i^2 \). Sum. Holes ride the same table with negative \( A \) and negative \( \bar{I} \). The classic error is transferring between two arbitrary axes — the theorem only moves I away from a shape's OWN centroid, never between two off-centroid axes; when in doubt, route through the centroid in two steps.

Read the result with the \( h^3 \) instinct: material far from the axis does the work, which is why doubling a beam's depth multiplies its bending stiffness eightfold while doubling its width merely doubles it, and why the I-beam exists at all. When a computed composite I comes out smaller than its largest part's contribution, the arithmetic is wrong — transfers only ever add for real areas. These numbers feed the flexure formula in Strength of Materials directly; the audit habits travel with them.

Closing. \( y^2 \) rewards distance, \( bh^3/12 \) rewards depth, and \( Ad^2 \) explains the I-beam. Statics ends by manufacturing the geometric property that strength calculations spend.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

I-section by composite parts

Problem. An I-section: two flanges 100 × 10 mm (top and bottom), web 10 × 80 mm, overall depth 100 mm, symmetric. Find: \( I \) about the horizontal centroidal axis.

Solution. (1) Symmetry puts the centroid at mid-depth; each flange centroid sits \( d = 45\ \mathrm{mm} \) away. (2) One flange: \( \bar{I} = \dfrac{100 \times 10^3}{12} = 8333\ \mathrm{mm^4} \); \( Ad^2 = 1000 \times 45^2 = 2\,025\,000\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). (3) Web (centroid on the axis, no transfer): \( \dfrac{10 \times 80^3}{12} = 426\,667\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). (4) Total: \( I = 2(8333 + 2\,025\,000) + 426\,667 = 4.49 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \).

Answer: \( I \approx 4.49 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). Sanity check: the flanges' own \( \bar{I} \) (8,333 each) is 0.4% of their \( Ad^2 \) — the transfer term carries the section, as §2 promised.

SOLVE 2

Same steel, different shape

Problem. Reshape the I-section's area (\( A = 2 \times 1000 + 800 = 2800\ \mathrm{mm^2} \)) into a solid square. Find: its \( I \) and the comparison.

Solution. (1) Square side: \( s = \sqrt{2800} = 52.9\ \mathrm{mm} \). (2) \( I = \dfrac{s^4}{12} = \dfrac{52.9^4}{12} = 0.653 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). (3) Ratio: \( 4.49/0.653 = 6.9 \).

Answer: the I-shape is ~6.9× stiffer in bending than a square of identical area. Sanity check: identical steel tonnage, sevenfold performance difference — pure geometry. This single ratio is why rolled sections exist, why racks are built from I-beams and channels, and why "add more steel" is the wrong first answer to a deflection problem.

SOLVE 3

One rectangle, two axes

Problem. A 60 mm wide × 100 mm deep rectangular section. Find: its moment of inertia about its own centroidal horizontal axis and about its base — and reconcile the two with the parallel-axis theorem.

Solution. (1) Centroidal: \( \bar{I} = bh^3/12 = 60 \times 100^3/12 = 5.0 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). (2) About the base: \( I = bh^3/3 = 2.0 \times 10^7\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). (3) Parallel axis, \( d = 50\ \mathrm{mm} \): \( \bar{I} + Ad^2 = 5.0\times10^6 + 6000 \times 2500 = 2.0 \times 10^7 \) ✓ — the two formulas are one theorem apart.

Answer: \( 5.0 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \) centroidal; \( 2.0 \times 10^7\ \mathrm{mm^4} \) at the base. Sanity check: the base value is exactly four times the centroidal one for a rectangle — a ratio worth memorizing as a self-test whenever both are computed.

MCQ 1

The second moment of area \( I \) measures:

  1. how much area a section has
  2. how far from the axis the area is deployed (distance squared) ✓
  3. the section's mass distribution
  4. the maximum stress it can carry

\( I = \int y^2 dA \): area counts by the square of its distance from the axis. Two sections of identical area can differ in \( I \) sevenfold — geometry, not quantity of material. Stress capacity involves \( I \) but also material strength; \( I \) itself is purely geometric.

MCQ 2

A 50 mm wide × 100 mm deep rectangle, about its horizontal centroidal axis: \( I \) =

  1. \( 1.04 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \)
  2. \( 4.17 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \) ✓
  3. \( 8.33 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \)
  4. \( 0.42 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \)

\( I = bh^3/12 = 50 \times 100^3/12 = 4.17 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). Choice A swaps \( b \) and \( h \) (\( 100 \times 50^3/12 \)) — a factor of 4 penalty for laying the section on its side, which is the whole lesson about depth cubed.

MCQ 3

The area moment of inertia carries units of:

  1. mm⁴ ✓
  2. mm²
  3. mm³
  4. N·mm

\( I = \int y^2\,dA \): length squared times area — length to the fourth. mm² is area, mm³ is the section modulus (I divided by a distance — coming in Strength of Materials), N·mm is a moment of force: three different section quantities that beginners shuffle. Units are the fastest audit: any "moment of inertia" not in length⁴ is some other animal.

MCQ 4

The parallel-axis theorem \( I = \bar{I} + Ad^2 \) may be applied:

  1. between any two parallel axes
  2. only transferring from a centroidal axis ✓
  3. only for symmetric sections
  4. only when \( d \) is small

The theorem transfers from a part's own centroidal axis outward. Chaining it between two non-centroidal axes double-counts the offset — go back through the centroid instead. (Symmetry and the size of \( d \) are irrelevant to validity.)

MCQ 5

Doubling a rectangular beam's depth (h) while keeping its width multiplies its centroidal moment of inertia by:

  1. 8 ✓
  2. 2
  3. 4
  4. 16

\( I = bh^3/12 \propto h^3 \): doubling h gives \( 2^3 = 8 \) times the stiffness quantity. Doubling the WIDTH doubles I only linearly — which is the entire argument for deep, upright sections and for the I-beam's flanges-far-from-axis geometry. Choosing 4 squares instead of cubes; 16 belongs to \( h^4 \), a different integral.
