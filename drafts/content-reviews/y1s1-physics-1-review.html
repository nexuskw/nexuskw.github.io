Lesson 01 · Measurement, units, and vectors

Lecture

§1 · Measurement is a contract

Physics begins where opinion ends: at measurement. A physical quantity is a number, a unit, and an uncertainty, and all three are part of the value. The SI system supplies seven base units, of which mechanics uses three — the metre, the kilogram, and the second — and builds everything else from them: newtons are \( \mathrm{kg\,m/s^2} \), joules are newton-metres, watts are joules per second. Because derived units are built by algebra, they can be CHECKED by algebra — both sides of any physical equation must carry identical units, and an equation that fails this test is wrong before any numbers enter. Significant figures carry the honesty further: a value written as 4.80 m promises more than 4.8 m does, and the professional habit is to promise no more than the instrument delivered.

§2 · Vectors carry direction

Some quantities are complete as bare numbers — temperature, mass, energy. Mechanics' central quantities are not: force, velocity, acceleration, and displacement all point, and a number without its direction is half a fact. A vector is specified either by magnitude and angle or by components on chosen axes, and the two descriptions convert through the triangle: \( A_x = A\cos\theta \), \( A_y = A\sin\theta \), with \( A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2} \) returning the magnitude. Addition happens component by component — never magnitude by magnitude — which is the single most consequential sentence in this lesson: two 100 N forces can sum to anything from 0 to 200 N depending on their directions.

§3 · Two products, two meanings

Vectors multiply in two distinct ways, each answering a physical question. The dot product, \( \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B} = AB\cos\theta = A_xB_x + A_yB_y + A_zB_z \), measures alignment and produces a scalar — work is force dotted with displacement, and a force at right angles to motion does none. The cross product, \( |\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B}| = AB\sin\theta \), measures turning capacity and produces a vector along the axis of turn — torque is position crossed with force, the right-hand rule assigning its sense. Choosing the wrong product is not a small error; it is answering a different question.

§4 · The component working method

Every vector calculation in this course runs one procedure. Choose axes deliberately and draw them. Resolve every vector into components with a small sign table, committing each sine and cosine once, visibly. Do all arithmetic on components. Recompose at the end — magnitude from Pythagoras, direction from the arctangent WITH a quadrant check from the component signs, because the calculator's arctangent is wrong half the time on its own. Then run the two audits: units on both sides of every line, and magnitude inside the possible band (never more than the sum of the parts, never less than their difference). The method is mechanical on purpose — statics, dynamics, and every field theory after them will run it thousands of times, and a procedure executed identically is a procedure whose errors are findable. Speed comes later and automatically; correctness is the habit being installed here.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Resolving a pull

Problem. A cable pulls a skid with 100 N at 37° above the horizontal. Find: the horizontal and vertical components, and verify by recomposition.

Solution. (1) \( F_x = 100\cos 37° = 79.9\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) \( F_y = 100\sin 37° = 60.2\ \mathrm{N} \). (3) Recompose: \( \sqrt{79.9^2 + 60.2^2} = 100.0\ \mathrm{N} \) ✓ and \( \tan^{-1}(60.2/79.9) = 37.0° \) ✓.

Answer: \( (79.9,\ 60.2)\ \mathrm{N} \). Sanity check: below 45°, the horizontal component must be the larger of the two — it is; and the recomposition returning exactly the inputs certifies the arithmetic loop closed.

SOLVE 2

Work as a dot product

Problem. A force \( \mathbf{F} = (300, 150)\ \mathrm{N} \) acts on a trolley that displaces \( \mathbf{d} = (2, -1)\ \mathrm{m} \). Find: the work done.

Solution. (1) \( W = \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{d} = F_xd_x + F_yd_y = 300\times2 + 150\times(-1) \). (2) \( W = 600 - 150 = 450\ \mathrm{J} \).

Answer: 450 J. Sanity check: the negative contribution is physical — the vertical force component points up while the vertical displacement is down, so that pair FIGHTS the motion and subtracts. The dot product bookkeeps help and hindrance automatically; that is its job.

SOLVE 3

Torque as a cross product

Problem. A force of 250 N acts vertically (+y) at the end of a horizontal arm \( \mathbf{r} = (0.4, 0, 0)\ \mathrm{m} \) from a pivot. Find: the torque vector.

Solution. (1) \( \boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F} \); with \( \mathbf{r} \) along x and \( \mathbf{F} \) along y, the result points along z (right-hand rule). (2) Magnitude: \( rF\sin 90° = 0.4 \times 250 = 100\ \mathrm{N\,m} \). (3) \( \boldsymbol{\tau} = (0, 0, 100)\ \mathrm{N\,m} \) — counterclockwise seen from +z.

Answer: 100 N·m about +z. Sanity check: force perpendicular to arm is the maximum-torque geometry \( (\sin 90° = 1) \); any other angle would deliver less, and a force ALONG the arm would deliver zero — the cross product encodes the wrench-handle intuition exactly.

MCQ 1

Which of these quantities is a scalar?

  1. Work ✓
  2. Force
  3. Velocity
  4. Torque

Work is energy — a magnitude with no direction, born of a dot product, which always outputs scalars. Force and velocity point by definition, and torque points along its axis of turn (a cross-product output). The scalar/vector sorting matters operationally: scalars add like numbers; vectors must go through components.

MCQ 2

The unit vector in the direction of \( (3, 4) \) is:

  1. \( (0.6, 0.8) \) ✓
  2. \( (3, 4) \)
  3. \( (0.75, 1.0) \)
  4. \( (1, 1) \)

The magnitude is \( \sqrt{3^2+4^2} = 5 \); dividing each component by it gives \( (0.6, 0.8) \), whose own magnitude is exactly 1 — the definition of a unit vector. Leaving the vector undivided keeps its length 5; dividing by the wrong number (4) or normalizing by eye produces the other traps. Unit vectors carry pure direction, ready to be scaled by any magnitude.

MCQ 3

Two vectors are perpendicular. Their dot product is:

  1. Zero ✓
  2. The product of their magnitudes
  3. Negative, always
  4. Undefined

\( \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B} = AB\cos 90° = 0 \): the dot product measures alignment, and perpendicular vectors share none. The product of magnitudes would require full alignment \( (\cos 0° = 1) \). Physically this is why a force at right angles to motion does no work — the centripetal force of Lesson 3's curves, working hard and doing zero joules.

MCQ 4

A motor is labeled "10 kg". Its weight — the force the crane feels — is about:

  1. 98 N ✓
  2. 10 N
  3. 10 kg-force is a force already
  4. 1 N

\( W = mg = 10 \times 9.81 = 98\ \mathrm{N} \). Kilograms measure mass; newtons measure force, and the two differ by \( g \). The colloquial "kilogram-force" survives on old gauges but is not an SI unit and invites exactly this confusion — rigging calculations, spring selections, and load cells all live in newtons, and the conversion habit starts here.

MCQ 5

The magnitude of the vector \( (6, 8) \) is:

  1. 10 ✓
  2. 14
  3. 7
  4. 48

\( \sqrt{6^2 + 8^2} = \sqrt{100} = 10 \) — the 3-4-5 triangle scaled by two. Adding components (14) treats perpendicular parts as collinear — the cardinal vector sin; multiplying them (48) computes an area, not a length. The Pythagorean recomposition is half of the component method's closing audit, worth making reflexive now.

Lesson 02 · Kinematics in one dimension

Lecture

§1 · The three siblings: position, velocity, acceleration

Kinematics describes motion without asking why it happens, and its entire vocabulary is three quantities chained by calculus. Position \( x(t) \) says where; velocity \( v = dx/dt \) says how fast where is changing; acceleration \( a = dv/dt \) says how fast how-fast is changing. The chain runs both directions: differentiate to go from position toward acceleration, integrate to come back — and the mathematics course's slogan that "slopes and areas are inverses" becomes physical here. On a velocity–time graph, the slope at any instant is the acceleration and the area under the curve is the displacement; an engineer who can read those two facts off a trend owns most of kinematics.

§2 · The constant-acceleration toolkit

When acceleration is constant — winch starts, braking runs, free fall — integration yields the standard kit: \( v = v_0 + at \), \( x = v_0t + \tfrac12 at^2 \), and the time-free combination \( v^2 = v_0^2 + 2ax \). Derive them once from the definitions and they stop being formulas to memorize and become consequences to trust. Each answers its own question — the first about speed at a time, the second about distance in a time, the third about speed after a distance — and choosing the one whose variables match the givens is the entire selection skill. Free fall is the same kit with \( a = g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) downward, air resistance ignored until a later course chooses to price it.

§3 · Signs carry the physics

One axis, declared positive in one direction, governs every sign in the problem. Velocity opposite the axis is negative; acceleration opposite the VELOCITY is braking, but acceleration opposite the AXIS is just negative — the two statements differ, and conflating them is the classic kinematics error. A car moving in +x while slowing has negative acceleration; the same car reversing and speeding up ALSO has negative acceleration. The sign belongs to the axis, not to the feeling of speeding up or slowing down.

§4 · The kinematics working method

Solve every problem the same way. Draw the axis and mark positive. List the knowns and the wanted symbolically — \( v_0, v, a, t, x \) — and strike what the problem doesn't mention. Pick the ONE equation containing exactly the knowns and the wanted; if none fits, the problem has two phases, so split it where the acceleration changes and chain the phases, the final state of one becoming the initial state of the next. Solve symbolically first, substitute numbers last, and audit twice: units through every line, and magnitude against experience — a forklift does not brake at \( 40\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), and an answer claiming so is a decimal slip announcing itself. The two-phase discipline matters most: most real motions — lift, travel, lower; accelerate, cruise, brake — are chains of simple segments, and the method scales to any chain length without changing shape.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

The hoist start

Problem. A hoist accelerates a load from rest at a constant \( 0.5\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) for 6 s. Find: the final speed and the distance risen.

Solution. (1) \( v = v_0 + at = 0 + 0.5 \times 6 = 3.0\ \mathrm{m/s} \). (2) \( x = v_0t + \tfrac12 at^2 = 0 + 0.5 \times 0.5 \times 36 = 9.0\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: 3 m/s after 9 m of travel. Sanity check: average velocity from rest to 3 m/s is 1.5 m/s; over 6 s that is 9 m — the area-under-the-graph reading (a triangle: ½ × 6 × 3) confirming the formula exactly.

SOLVE 2

Braking distance

Problem. A vehicle traveling at 20 m/s brakes at a constant \( 4\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). Find: the time to stop and the stopping distance.

Solution. (1) Time: \( 0 = 20 - 4t \Rightarrow t = 5\ \mathrm{s} \). (2) Distance (time-free check): \( 0 = 20^2 - 2 \times 4 \times x \Rightarrow x = 400/8 = 50\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: 5 s; 50 m. Sanity check: average speed while braking is 10 m/s for 5 s → 50 m ✓. Note the scaling buried here: stopping distance goes as \( v^2 \) — doubling speed quadruples the distance, the single most safety-relevant equation in kinematics.

SOLVE 3

A two-phase travel

Problem. An automated cart accelerates from rest at \( 2\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) for 5 s, then holds its speed for 10 s. Find: the total distance covered.

Solution. (1) Phase 1: \( v = 2 \times 5 = 10\ \mathrm{m/s} \); \( x_1 = \tfrac12 \times 2 \times 25 = 25\ \mathrm{m} \). (2) Phase 2 (constant speed): \( x_2 = 10 \times 10 = 100\ \mathrm{m} \). (3) Total: \( 125\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: 125 m. Sanity check: the v–t graph is a triangle (area 25) followed by a rectangle (area 100) — the areas ARE the distances, and chaining phases at the speed handoff (10 m/s ends phase 1, begins phase 2) is the working method in miniature.

MCQ 1

On a velocity–time graph, the area under the curve represents:

  1. Displacement ✓
  2. Acceleration
  3. Average speed
  4. Jerk

Integrating velocity over time accumulates displacement — area under v–t is distance traveled (signed). Acceleration is the SLOPE of the same graph, one derivative up; average speed is total area over total time. Slope-versus-area on the v–t graph is the compact summary of all of kinematics, and this question is its passport check.

MCQ 2

A machine slide starts from rest and accelerates at \( 2\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) over 16 m. Its final speed is:

  1. 8 m/s ✓
  2. 32 m/s
  3. 5.7 m/s
  4. 16 m/s

No time is given, so the time-free equation applies: \( v^2 = 0 + 2 \times 2 \times 16 = 64 \Rightarrow v = 8\ \mathrm{m/s} \). Multiplying a by distance (32) or halving things by instinct produces the distractors. Equation selection by matching knowns — here \( a, x \), no \( t \) — is the entire skill this item drills.

MCQ 3

A wrench is dropped from a platform and falls for 2 s. Ignoring air resistance, it has fallen about:

  1. 19.6 m ✓
  2. 9.8 m
  3. 39.2 m
  4. 4.9 m

\( x = \tfrac12 g t^2 = 0.5 \times 9.81 \times 4 = 19.6\ \mathrm{m} \) — roughly a six-story drop, which is why dropped-object prevention is a permit topic. Using \( gt \) (19.6 m/s) computes the SPEED, not the distance — the classic mix; 4.9 m is one second's fall. The square in \( t^2 \) means the second second does three times the damage of the first.

MCQ 4

A cart moves in the +x direction while slowing down. Its acceleration is:

  1. Directed in −x ✓
  2. Directed in +x
  3. Zero
  4. Vertical

Slowing means the velocity (in +x) is being eaten away — the rate of change of velocity points against it: −x. "Deceleration" is not a separate quantity with its own rules; it is ordinary acceleration whose direction opposes the velocity. Attaching the sign to the axis rather than to the sensation of slowing is the discipline that keeps two-phase problems honest.

MCQ 5

The slope of a position–time graph at an instant gives:

  1. The velocity at that instant ✓
  2. The acceleration
  3. The average speed for the trip
  4. The distance so far

\( v = dx/dt \): slope of position–time is instantaneous velocity — steep means fast, flat means stopped, downhill means moving backward. Acceleration lives one graph over (slope of v–t), and averages need whole intervals, not instants. Reading motion graphs at sight transfers directly to reading machine-axis traces and historian trends later.

Lesson 03 · Kinematics in two dimensions

Lecture

§1 · Two motions sharing one clock

The deepest fact of two-dimensional motion is independence: the horizontal and vertical motions of a projectile do not consult each other. Gravity acts only vertically, so the horizontal velocity is untouched — constant from launch to landing — while the vertical motion is ordinary free fall. The only thing the two share is the clock. Every projectile problem therefore splits into two one-dimensional problems solved with Lesson 2's toolkit, joined by the time variable: usually the vertical motion sets the time (how long until it lands?) and the horizontal motion spends it (how far did it get?).

§2 · The projectile's arithmetic

Launched horizontally from height \( h \) with speed \( v_0 \): the fall time is \( t = \sqrt{2h/g} \) — identical to a dropped object, regardless of \( v_0 \) — and the reach is \( x = v_0 t \). Launched at angle \( \theta \): the initial velocity splits into \( v_0\cos\theta \) horizontal and \( v_0\sin\theta \) vertical, the apex arrives when the vertical velocity runs out, and the level-ground range is \( R = v_0^2 \sin 2\theta / g \), maximized at 45°. At the apex the vertical velocity is zero but the horizontal velocity is untouched — the projectile is still moving, a fact the classic "zero velocity at the top" error forgets.

§3 · Relative motion: velocities add like vectors

Velocity is always measured relative to a frame, and frames stack by vector addition: the velocity of A relative to ground equals A relative to B plus B relative to ground. A worker walking at 1.0 m/s against a conveyor doing 1.5 m/s moves at 0.5 m/s over the floor; a part carried on a moving belt leaves the belt end with the belt's velocity as its inheritance. In one dimension this is signed addition; in two, components — nothing new, just Lesson 1's machinery applied to velocities.

§4 · The two-column working method

Solve every 2-D motion with two columns and one clock. Write the horizontal column: constant velocity, \( x = v_x t \), nothing else. Write the vertical column: free fall with the full constant-acceleration kit. Identify which column the question interrogates and which column supplies the time, and pass \( t \) between them explicitly. Keep signs by one declared axis pair (right and up positive is conventional), and audit at the seams: the landing condition is a VERTICAL statement (height reaches zero), the reach is a HORIZONTAL one, and mixing the columns — using the launch speed where a component belongs — is the error the layout exists to prevent. The same two-column habit will return, upgraded, as normal-and-tangential components in the dynamics course; learning its discipline now on projectiles is the cheap rehearsal.

The applications are nearer than they look: discharge trajectories off conveyor heads set guard and bin positions, falling-object times set interlock reaction budgets, and every "how far can it reach" safety question — spray, jet, or dropped tool — is a projectile problem wearing overalls. The two columns answer all of them.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Off the end of the conveyor

Problem. A part leaves a horizontal conveyor at 2.0 m/s, 1.25 m above the floor. Find: the flight time and where it lands, measured from the conveyor end.

Solution. (1) Vertical column sets the clock: \( t = \sqrt{2h/g} = \sqrt{2 \times 1.25/9.81} = 0.505\ \mathrm{s} \). (2) Horizontal column spends it: \( x = v_x t = 2.0 \times 0.505 = 1.01\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: ≈0.5 s; ≈1.0 m out. Sanity check: the fall time is exactly a dropped part's — belt speed cannot buy hang time, only reach. Doubling the belt speed doubles the landing distance but changes the flight time not at all: the independence principle, priced in numbers a guard designer can use.

SOLVE 2

The 45° maximum

Problem. A material-testing launcher fires at 20 m/s on level ground. Find: the maximum range and the angle that achieves it.

Solution. (1) \( R = v_0^2\sin 2\theta/g \), maximized where \( \sin 2\theta = 1 \), i.e. \( \theta = 45° \). (2) \( R_{max} = 20^2/9.81 = 40.8\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: ≈41 m at 45°. Sanity check: range scales as \( v_0^2 \) — a 10% speed increase buys 21% more reach — and the \( \sin 2\theta \) symmetry means 30° and 60° land in the same place, a fact worth knowing whenever a spray, jet, or thrown object's reach is being bounded for safety.

SOLVE 3

Walking against the belt

Problem. A maintenance worker walks at 1.0 m/s toward the tail of a conveyor that carries them forward at 1.5 m/s. Find: the worker's velocity over the floor, and their displacement after 8 s.

Solution. (1) Frames stack: \( v_{worker/ground} = v_{worker/belt} + v_{belt/ground} = -1.0 + 1.5 = +0.5\ \mathrm{m/s} \). (2) Displacement: \( 0.5 \times 8 = 4.0\ \mathrm{m} \) forward.

Answer: 0.5 m/s forward; 4 m in 8 s. Sanity check: walking exactly at belt speed would hold position (0 m/s), and walking slower than the belt must still drift forward — the signed addition delivers both limits correctly, which is the entire content of relative velocity in one dimension.

MCQ 1

During projectile flight (no air resistance), the horizontal velocity:

  1. Stays constant ✓
  2. Decreases steadily
  3. Increases steadily
  4. Falls to zero at the apex

No horizontal force acts, so no horizontal acceleration exists: \( v_x \) at landing equals \( v_x \) at launch. Gravity spends itself entirely on the vertical column. The apex-stop intuition confuses the vertical component (which does reach zero there) with the whole velocity — the projectile is moving fastest horizontally precisely when it looks momentarily 'paused' at the top.

MCQ 2

Two identical parts leave a table edge at the same instant — one dropped, one sliding off horizontally at 3 m/s. Which lands first?

  1. They land together ✓
  2. The dropped one
  3. The sliding one
  4. Depends on their masses

Both fall the same height under the same vertical physics; the slider's horizontal speed buys distance, not time. This is the independence principle in its purest test form — and the classic demonstration that the vertical clock cannot be bribed horizontally. Mass cancels out of free fall entirely, a fact Newton's laws will formalize next lesson.

MCQ 3

A part must fall 4.9 m from a chute to a bin. Its flight time is about:

  1. 1.0 s ✓
  2. 0.5 s
  3. 2.0 s
  4. 4.9 s

\( t = \sqrt{2h/g} = \sqrt{2 \times 4.9/9.81} = 1.0\ \mathrm{s} \). The square root is the working surprise: quadrupling the height only doubles the time. Numbers like this size catch-guards and interlock timings — one second of flight is one second for a light curtain to react, and the calculation is exactly this one line.

MCQ 4

At the apex of its arc, a projectile launched at an angle has:

  1. Zero vertical velocity and unchanged horizontal velocity ✓
  2. Zero total velocity
  3. Zero acceleration
  4. Maximum speed

The vertical component has been spent \( (v_y = 0) \) while the horizontal component never changes — so the projectile moves horizontally at the top, at its MINIMUM (not zero, not maximum) speed. Gravity still acts: acceleration at the apex is the same \( g \) as everywhere else. Three classic errors, one question.

MCQ 5

A box rides a conveyor moving 2 m/s and slides off its end. At the instant it leaves, the box's horizontal velocity is:

  1. 2 m/s — it inherits the belt's velocity ✓
  2. Zero — it left the belt
  3. Depends on its weight
  4. Equal to g

Velocity belongs to the object, not the surface: leaving the belt removes the belt's FORCE, not the velocity already possessed. The box begins its projectile life at the belt's speed — which is why discharge trajectories, guard distances, and bin placements are computed from belt speed. Weight decides nothing here, and \( g \) is an acceleration, not a velocity.

Lesson 04 · Newton's laws of motion

Lecture

§1 · The three laws, stated for work

Newton's laws are the constitution of mechanics, and each earns a working translation. First law: a body's velocity is constant unless a net force acts — so anything at rest OR cruising steadily has balanced forces, and the interesting engineering question is always "balanced by what?" Second law: \( \mathbf{F}_{net} = m\mathbf{a} \) — the net force, the vector sum of everything acting, produces acceleration in its own direction, scaled inversely by the mass. Third law: forces come in pairs, equal and opposite, acting on DIFFERENT bodies — the floor pushes the crate up exactly as hard as the crate pushes the floor down, and the pair never meets on one free-body diagram. The third law is bookkeeping; the second is the engine; the first is the second at \( \mathbf{a} = 0 \), which is the entire statics course in four symbols.

§2 · Mass versus weight, permanently

Mass measures inertia — the reluctance to accelerate — in kilograms, identical everywhere in the universe. Weight is the gravitational FORCE on that mass, \( W = mg \), in newtons, local to the gravity that produces it. The distinction stops being pedantry the moment anything accelerates vertically: in a lift accelerating upward, the floor must push harder than \( mg \) — the scale reads more — and in a dropping lift it reads less, while the mass never changes. Load cells, crane scales, and accelerating machinery all live on this distinction.

§3 · Using the second law on systems

\( F = ma \) applies to any body you choose to isolate — a whole train of carts or any single cart within it. The professional trick is choosing wisely: the whole system hides internal forces and yields the acceleration cheaply; a single member exposes the internal force you actually want. Solve the system for \( a \), then isolate one piece and let its own second law reveal the coupling force. Connected bodies share the acceleration magnitude whenever their connection is inextensible — the rope constraint that makes multi-body problems solvable at all.

§4 · The second-law working method

The procedure is Lesson 3's FBD checklist with one upgrade: the right side of the equation is no longer zero. Draw the free body. Choose axes — align one with the acceleration when its direction is known, and the algebra thanks you. Write \( \Sigma F_x = ma_x \) and \( \Sigma F_y = ma_y \) with the sign table. Solve symbolically, substitute, and audit: units (newtons throughout), limits (set \( a = 0 \) and the answer must collapse to the statics result), and magnitude sense (accelerations of industrial machinery are typically fractions of \( g \); an answer of \( 40\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) for a forklift is a flag, not a finding). When several bodies connect, write one second law per body plus the constraint, and count equations against unknowns before solving — exactly the bookkeeping the statics course taught, now with motion.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Force to accelerate a load

Problem. A tug moves a 1200 kg trailer from rest with a required acceleration of \( 1.5\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) on level ground (rolling resistance neglected). Find: the required drawbar force, and the speed after 4 s.

Solution. (1) \( F = ma = 1200 \times 1.5 = 1800\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) Kinematics inherits the a: \( v = at = 1.5 \times 4 = 6.0\ \mathrm{m/s} \).

Answer: 1.8 kN; 6 m/s. Sanity check: the force is about 15% of the trailer's 11.8 kN weight — a plausible drawbar figure; and the audit limit works: at \( a = 0 \) the required force collapses to zero, exactly as a frictionless statics problem demands.

SOLVE 2

The scale in the lift

Problem. An 80 kg technician stands on a scale in a service lift accelerating upward at \( 2.0\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). Find: the scale reading (the normal force), and the reading in free fall.

Solution. (1) Second law, up positive: \( N - mg = ma \Rightarrow N = m(g + a) = 80 \times 11.81 = 945\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) Free fall \( (a = -g) \): \( N = m(g - g) = 0 \) — the scale reads nothing.

Answer: ≈945 N (as if 96 kg); zero in free fall. Sanity check: the reading exceeds the true 785 N weight by exactly \( ma = 160\ \mathrm{N} \) — the extra push the floor must supply to accelerate the technician. Load cells under moving loads misread for precisely this reason; the correction is this equation.

SOLVE 3

The coupling force in a two-cart train

Problem. A 10 kg cart towing a 4 kg cart is pulled by 60 N on a smooth floor. Find: the acceleration of the train and the tension in the coupling.

Solution. (1) Whole system: \( a = F/m_{total} = 60/14 = 4.29\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). (2) Isolate the trailing cart — the coupling is its only horizontal force: \( T = m_2 a = 4 \times 4.29 = 17.1\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: \( 4.3\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \); ≈17 N in the coupling. Sanity check: the coupling carries \( 4/14 = 28.6\% \) of the applied force — exactly the trailing cart's share of the total mass. The system view found a cheaply; the member view exposed T: the two-step method in its standard form.

MCQ 1

A pallet slides across a smooth floor at constant velocity. The net force on it is:

  1. Zero ✓
  2. In the direction of motion
  3. Opposite the motion
  4. Equal to its weight

Constant velocity IS the first-law condition: net force zero — motion needs no force to continue, only to change. The push-to-keep-moving intuition (Aristotle's) survives in everyday friction-filled life but fails the moment friction leaves. Vertically, weight and the normal force cancel as part of that zero. This question separates physics from folk physics in one line.

MCQ 2

A crate weighs 981 N on Earth. On a moon base where \( g = 1.62\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), its mass is:

  1. 100 kg — mass does not change ✓
  2. 16.2 kg
  3. 981 kg
  4. 162 N

Mass is the invariant: \( m = W/g = 981/9.81 = 100\ \mathrm{kg} \) everywhere in the universe. Its moon WEIGHT would be \( 100 \times 1.62 = 162\ \mathrm{N} \) — a force, hence not an answer to a mass question (and kilograms, not newtons, is the mass unit). Inertia travels with the crate; the scale reading stays home with the gravity.

MCQ 3

A net force of 500 N acts on a 250 kg trolley. Its acceleration is:

  1. \( 2\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) ✓
  2. \( 0.5\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \)
  3. \( 125{,}000\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \)
  4. \( 250\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \)

\( a = F/m = 500/250 = 2\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). Inverting the division (0.5) and multiplying instead (125,000) are the arithmetic traps; the physics trap is forgetting that F must be the NET force — if friction claimed 100 N of the 500, the answer would drop accordingly. Second-law arithmetic is trivial; assembling the net force is the actual work.

MCQ 4

A tractor pulls a loaded sled. The sled pulls back on the tractor (third law). The pair can still accelerate because:

  1. The paired forces act on different bodies — each body answers only to the forces on itself ✓
  2. The tractor's force is slightly larger
  3. The third law fails during acceleration
  4. Friction cancels the reaction

Third-law partners never meet on one free body: the sled's pull-back acts on the TRACTOR, whose own budget also includes ground traction — and the tractor accelerates if traction beats pull-back. The sled, on ITS ledger, feels the forward pull and accelerates too. The 'equal forces should cancel' paradox dissolves the moment each body keeps its own books — which is what FBDs are for.

MCQ 5

The same 30 N force is applied in turn to a 3 kg part and a 6 kg part. The accelerations compare as:

  1. 10 vs 5 m/s² — halved mass ratio, doubled acceleration ✓
  2. Equal — same force, same acceleration
  3. 5 vs 10 m/s²
  4. Cannot compare without weights

\( a = F/m \): 30/3 = 10 and 30/6 = 5 m/s² — acceleration inversely proportional to mass at fixed force. This inverse relationship IS inertia quantified, and it scales to every sizing decision where a drive must accelerate a load: double the tooling mass and the same motor delivers half the acceleration, before friction says a word.

Lesson 05 · Applying Newton's laws

Lecture

§1 · Friction joins the diagram

Newton's laws meet real surfaces through friction, and the rules carry over from statics with one upgrade: motion. Static friction adapts — it supplies exactly what equilibrium demands, up to the ceiling \( \mu_s N \) — while kinetic friction, once sliding begins, holds the steady lower value \( \mu_k N \). The normal force is never assumed: on an incline it is \( mg\cos\theta \), under a downward push it grows, under an angled pull it shrinks, and every friction calculation begins by solving for \( N \) from the perpendicular equation. The incline is the standard stage: gravity resolves into \( mg\sin\theta \) along the slope and \( mg\cos\theta \) into it, and the contest between \( mg\sin\theta \) and \( \mu_s mg\cos\theta \) — that is, between \( \tan\theta \) and \( \mu_s \) — decides whether anything moves at all.

§2 · Ropes, pulleys, and shared fates

An ideal rope is inextensible and massless: it transmits one tension throughout its length, and it forces connected bodies to share one acceleration magnitude. Those two facts turn multi-body problems into systems of simple equations — one second law per body, plus the constraint. An ideal pulley redirects the rope without changing its tension; the hanging-mass-drags-sliding-mass arrangement is the archetype, and its logic reappears in every counterweight, hoist, and tensioning system built since.

§3 · Friction as the transmitter of motion

Friction does not only resist motion — it CAUSES most of it. A box accelerates with its conveyor belt only because static friction drags it along; the maximum acceleration friction can transmit is \( a_{max} = \mu_s g \), and a belt that accelerates harder leaves its cargo sliding behind. The same ceiling governs tires on tarmac and shoes on floors: traction is static friction employed, and the μ-times-g budget is the hard limit on every start and every stop that relies on it.

§4 · The applying-Newton working method

The procedure composes everything so far. Draw the free body; solve the perpendicular axis first to capture \( N \); decide the friction regime honestly — compute the force equilibrium would require, compare with \( \mu_s N \), and only then choose static (adaptive, at the required value) or kinetic (fixed, at \( \mu_k N \), opposing the sliding). Align one axis with the acceleration, write the second law per axis, add the rope constraint when bodies connect, and count equations against unknowns before solving. Audit with limits: set \( \mu = 0 \) and the answer must collapse to the frictionless case; set \( a = 0 \) and it must collapse to statics. The regime decision is where the marks — and the real machines — are won or lost: assuming sliding when the part actually grips, or grip when it actually slides, produces answers that are precisely and confidently wrong. When in doubt, solve both regimes and let the comparison with the ceiling name the truth — two short calculations beat one plausible guess every time.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Holds or slides?

Problem. A 60 kg crate rests on a 20° ramp with \( \mu_s = 0.50 \). Find: whether it holds, the actual friction force, and the margin against sliding.

Solution. (1) Required to hold: \( mg\sin 20° = 588.6 \times 0.342 = 201\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) Available ceiling: \( \mu_s mg\cos 20° = 0.5 \times 588.6 \times 0.940 = 277\ \mathrm{N} \). (3) 201 < 277 → holds; actual friction = 201 N (the required value); margin = 277/201 = 1.38.

Answer: holds, with friction at 201 N and a 38% reserve. Sanity check: the comparison reduces to \( \tan 20° = 0.364 \) versus \( \mu_s = 0.50 \) — same verdict in one line; the ramp would release this crate at \( \tan^{-1}(0.5) = 26.6° \), a number worth computing before anyone tilts anything further.

SOLVE 2

The table-and-hanger system

Problem. A 12 kg block on a smooth table is tied by a cord over an ideal pulley to a hanging 6 kg block. Find: the system acceleration and the cord tension.

Solution. (1) System view (both bodies, one rope): driving force = hanging weight = \( 6 \times 9.81 = 58.9\ \mathrm{N} \); total mass 18 kg → \( a = 58.9/18 = 3.27\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). (2) Isolate the table block — tension is its only horizontal force: \( T = 12 \times 3.27 = 39.2\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: \( 3.27\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \); \( T \approx 39\ \mathrm{N} \). Sanity check: \( T \) must sit between zero and the hanging weight (58.9 N) — it does; and the hanging block's own ledger closes: \( 58.9 - 39.2 = 19.7 = 6 \times 3.27 \) ✓. The counterweight logic of every hoist starts exactly here.

SOLVE 3

Will the box ride the belt?

Problem. A conveyor accelerates at \( 3.0\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). The box it carries has \( \mu_s = 0.25 \) against the belt. Find: whether the box keeps up, and the friction coefficient that would be needed.

Solution. (1) Friction is the only horizontal force ON the box; its ceiling delivers at most \( a_{max} = \mu_s g = 0.25 \times 9.81 = 2.45\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). (2) \( 2.45 < 3.0 \) → the box cannot keep up; it slides backward relative to the belt. (3) Required: \( \mu_s \ge a/g = 3.0/9.81 = 0.31 \).

Answer: it slips; the belt-box pair would need \( \mu_s \ge 0.31 \). Sanity check: once slipping, kinetic friction (lower still) takes over and the lag worsens — which is why belt ramps and indexing conveyors publish acceleration limits, and why the fix is gentler ramps or grippier belting, not wishful thinking.

MCQ 1

A crate sits on a 15° ramp. The normal force from the ramp is:

  1. \( mg\cos 15° \) — less than the weight ✓
  2. \( mg \) — always
  3. \( mg\sin 15° \)
  4. Zero unless it slides

The ramp pushes perpendicular to its own surface, and only the into-slope component of gravity loads it: \( N = mg\cos\theta \). Assuming \( N = mg \) off the horizontal quietly inflates every friction ceiling computed from it — the most common incline error. The along-slope component \( mg\sin\theta \) is the sliding drive, a different actor entirely.

MCQ 2

Once an object breaks free and slides, the friction force typically:

  1. Drops to the lower kinetic value \( \mu_k N \) ✓
  2. Rises above the static ceiling
  3. Stays exactly at \( \mu_s N \)
  4. Vanishes

\( \mu_k < \mu_s \) for nearly all dry pairs, so breaking free brings a force DROP — the lurch when a stuck skid finally moves, and the reason wheels that lock (sliding) stop cars worse than wheels that keep rolling (static contact). The static value is a ceiling that applies only up to the instant of release, not during the slide.

MCQ 3

In an ideal rope over an ideal pulley, the tension is:

  1. The same everywhere along the rope ✓
  2. Larger on the heavier side
  3. Zero at the pulley
  4. Proportional to rope length

Massless rope + frictionless pulley = one tension throughout; the pulley redirects the force without taxing it. A tension difference across a real pulley belongs to pulley friction and rope mass — refinements later courses price. The single-tension idealization is what lets one symbol T close a two-body problem, and it is accurate to a few percent on decent hardware.

MCQ 4

An indexing table must accelerate parts at \( 4\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) using friction alone. The minimum \( \mu_s \) is about:

  1. 0.41 ✓
  2. 0.25
  3. 4.0
  4. 0.10

Friction must supply \( ma \), and its ceiling is \( \mu_s mg \): \( \mu_s \ge a/g = 4/9.81 = 0.41 \). Mass cancels — heavier parts need no more coefficient, only more force, which friction automatically scales. Answering 4.0 confuses the acceleration with the coefficient; 0.25 would cap the table at 2.45 m/s². This one-line check sizes every friction-driven transfer in automation.

MCQ 5

Anti-lock braking keeps wheels rolling rather than skidding because:

  1. Rolling contact uses the higher static friction; skidding drops to kinetic ✓
  2. Rolling tires have no friction
  3. Kinetic friction exceeds static
  4. It reduces the car's weight

A rolling tire's contact patch is momentarily stationary on the road — static friction, the higher ceiling. Lock the wheel and the patch slides — kinetic, lower, and steering vanishes with the grip differential. The same physics governs machine spindles, clutch engagement, and web handling: keep the contact static and you keep both the force ceiling and the control.

Lesson 06 · Work and energy

Lecture

§1 · Work: force through distance, honestly counted

Work is energy in transit by force: \( W = Fd\cos\theta \) — only the component of force ALONG the motion counts, and the dot product of Lesson 1 was built for exactly this accounting. Force perpendicular to motion does nothing (the normal force on a rolling cart, the centripetal pull of a curve); force opposing motion does negative work, withdrawing energy (friction, always); and a force with no motion under it does no work at all, however hard it strains — the crane holding a load stationary spends money, not joules. The unit is the joule, one newton-metre, and keeping work's sign honest is keeping the energy books balanced.

§2 · Kinetic energy and the theorem that shortcuts dynamics

A moving mass carries kinetic energy \( KE = \tfrac12 mv^2 \), and the work–energy theorem states the exchange rate: the NET work done on a body equals its change in kinetic energy, \( W_{net} = \Delta KE \). The theorem is a derived consequence of \( F = ma \), but as a tool it routinely beats its parent: it needs no timeline, no acceleration bookkeeping — just states, before and after. The \( v^2 \) matters more than any other exponent in safety engineering: doubling speed quadruples the energy to be absorbed, which is why impact severity, braking distances, and guard designs all scale on the square of speed.

§3 · Power: the rate that sizes machines

Machines are bought by the rate they can move energy: power, \( P = W/t \), in watts — joules per second. For a force moving at speed, \( P = Fv \); for a hoist lifting steadily, \( P = mgv \); and the same energy delivered faster demands proportionally more machine. The kilowatt-hour on the invoice is a unit of ENERGY (a kilowatt sustained for an hour, 3.6 MJ), and keeping power and energy distinct — the rate versus the total — is the first literacy test of every utilities conversation.

§4 · The energy-accounting working method

Solve energy problems as an audit, not a story. Choose the system and the interval — initial state, final state. List every force that acts, and classify: does it do positive work, negative work, or none (perpendicular or unmoving)? Write the theorem, \( W_{net} = \Delta KE \), with each work term signed, and solve for the unknown. Audit by units (joules throughout, watts only when time enters) and by sign sense: a body that sped up must have received positive net work; one that slowed must have paid energy out somewhere the ledger can point to. The method's power is its indifference to detail — it never asks HOW the velocity varied in between — and its discipline is completeness: a force forgotten is energy silently created or destroyed, which nature forbids and examiners notice. Run the classification aloud on every new problem — works, opposes, ignores — until the sorting is reflex; the next lesson adds stored energy to the same ledger, and the audit habit carries over unchanged.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Work at an angle

Problem. A tow rope pulls a skid 12 m across a floor with 250 N at 30° above horizontal. Find: the work done by the rope.

Solution. (1) Only the along-floor component works: \( W = Fd\cos\theta = 250 \times 12 \times \cos 30° \). (2) \( W = 250 \times 12 \times 0.866 = 2600\ \mathrm{J} \).

Answer: ≈2.6 kJ. Sanity check: the vertical component (125 N) does no work on the horizontal travel — it lightens the skid on the floor instead, quietly reducing friction's negative work: the same decomposition doing two different jobs in one pull.

SOLVE 2

Energy to speed up a vehicle

Problem. A 1500 kg utility vehicle accelerates from 15 m/s to 25 m/s. Find: the net work required, by the work–energy theorem.

Solution. (1) \( W_{net} = \Delta KE = \tfrac12 m(v^2 - v_0^2) \). (2) \( = 0.5 \times 1500 \times (625 - 225) = 750 \times 400 = 300{,}000\ \mathrm{J} = 300\ \mathrm{kJ} \).

Answer: 300 kJ. Sanity check: note the asymmetry the square creates — the same vehicle going 0→10 m/s needs only 75 kJ; the higher the speed, the costlier each further m/s. No timeline was needed: the theorem prices the change of state and ignores the journey, which is exactly its advantage over \( F = ma \) here.

SOLVE 3

Sizing the hoist motor

Problem. A hoist raises a 400 kg load at a steady 0.8 m/s. Find: the power delivered to the load, and the motor size with an overall efficiency of 75%.

Solution. (1) Steady lift: \( P = mgv = 400 \times 9.81 \times 0.8 = 3.14\ \mathrm{kW} \). (2) Motor input: \( 3.14/0.75 = 4.2\ \mathrm{kW} \) — a 4-to-5 kW frame.

Answer: ≈3.1 kW to the load; ≈4.2 kW at the motor. Sanity check: steady speed means zero net work on the load's kinetic energy — the motor's entire output goes into potential energy at rate \( mgv \) plus losses; double the speed and the same load demands double the motor, which is why hoist speed is a purchased quantity.

MCQ 1

Friction acting on a sliding crate does:

  1. Negative work — it withdraws kinetic energy ✓

  3. No work
  4. Work only if the crate accelerates

Friction on a slider points opposite the motion: \( \cos 180° = -1 \), so its work is negative — energy leaves the crate's motion budget (becoming heat, Lesson 7's accounting). The sign is the physics: negative work is how the ledger records energy withdrawn. Only static friction that PREVENTS sliding under a rolling or gripped contact can do zero or even positive work — a subtlety the belt problems of Lesson 5 already met.

MCQ 2

Doubling a vehicle's speed multiplies its kinetic energy by:

  1. 4 ✓
  2. 2
  3. 8
  4. \( \sqrt{2} \)

This square is the most consequential exponent in industrial safety, and it is why speed limits on forklifts and cranes are energy statements wearing traffic clothing. Eight belongs to \( v^3 \) (a POWER scaling, for fans and pumps — a different law for a different quantity).

MCQ 3

One watt equals:

  1. One joule per second ✓
  2. One newton per metre
  3. One joule
  4. One kilogram-metre

The distinction runs the plant's economics: motors are sized in kW (rate capability), the utility bills kWh (energy delivered) — and conflating the two is the classic first error of every energy meeting this course inoculates against.

MCQ 4

A cart rolls at constant speed across a level floor. The work done by the normal force from the floor is:

  1. Zero — it acts perpendicular to the motion ✓
  2. Positive
  3. Negative
  4. Equal to the weight times distance

The normal force points vertically while the motion runs horizontally: \( \cos 90° = 0 \), no work — however far the cart travels and however heavy it is. Weight-times-distance would apply to LIFTING through that distance, a different geometry. Perpendicular forces steer and support but never pay into or out of the kinetic-energy account.

MCQ 5

The kinetic energy of a 2 kg part moving at 3 m/s is:

  1. 9 J ✓
  2. 6 J
  3. 18 J
  4. 3 J

Forgetting the half (18) and forgetting the square (6, from \( \tfrac12 \times 2 \times 3 \)) are the two standard slips, and both are unit-invisible — the audit that catches them is recomputing with the formula spoken aloud. Nine joules is also a dropped-from-half-a-metre energy: small numbers, but the same arithmetic prices crane loads.

Lesson 07 · Potential energy and conservation

Lecture

§1 · Stored work: potential energy

Some forces give back what work puts in. Lift a mass and gravity stores the effort: potential energy \( PE = mgh \), recoverable in full on the way down. Compress or stretch a spring and it banks \( PE = \tfrac12 kx^2 \). Forces with this repayment property — gravity, springs — are conservative: the work they do depends only on endpoints, not on the path, and that is what makes a stored-energy bookkeeping possible at all. The reference height for \( mgh \) is yours to choose; only DIFFERENCES in potential energy ever enter the physics, so the floor, the bench, or sea level serve equally, provided the choice is made once and kept.

§2 · Conservation: the ledger balances

When only conservative forces do work, mechanical energy is conserved: \( KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2 \). Height converts to speed and back without loss — the falling pallet, the swinging hammer, the cam follower riding its profile. The equation answers speed-from-height questions in one line: \( v = \sqrt{2gh} \) for a drop from rest, with mass canceling entirely — a heavy tool and a light one arrive at the same speed, though not, as the momentum lesson will insist, with the same consequences.

§3 · When friction joins: the ledger still balances

Friction does not break energy accounting; it opens a new account. With friction acting, \( KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2 + E_{lost} \), where the loss term — friction force times sliding distance — leaves the mechanical books and arrives as heat. The equation turns observed shortfalls into measurements: a crate that arrives slower than the frictionless prediction has told you exactly how much energy friction collected en route. Nothing disappears; it changes accounts, and the engineer's job is reading which account.

§4 · The conservation working method

Choose two states — where the question starts and where it asks. Set the height reference and declare it. Write the full ledger for each state: kinetic, gravitational, spring if present. Ask the gatekeeper question — does anything non-conservative do work between the states? If no, equate the ledgers; if yes, add the loss term and route it honestly. Solve, then audit: mass should cancel in pure gravity problems (if it doesn't, a term is misplaced); speeds must come from energy DIFFERENCES, never absolute heights; and the frictionless answer should always bound the frictional one. The method's reach is its reward: trajectory shape, path curvature, and time all drop out of the accounting, which is why energy methods solve in one line what force methods solve in a page — whenever the question asks about states rather than histories.

The ledger habit is also a diagnostic instrument in the field: measure the arrival speed a machine element actually achieves, compare with the frictionless prediction, and the shortfall prices the losses without a single coefficient measured. Energy accounting reads machines the way the trend-reading of the mathematics course read signals — by what the books say is missing.

SOLVE 1

Speed at the bottom of a drop

Problem. A pallet slips from a rack and falls 2.5 m. Find: its impact speed, and show the mass does not matter.

Solution. (1) Conservation, drop from rest: \( mgh = \tfrac12 mv^2 \). (2) Mass cancels: \( v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.81 \times 2.5} = 7.0\ \mathrm{m/s} \).

SOLVE 2

The drop hammer's exchange

Problem. A 5 kg hammer head is raised 1.2 m and released to swing to its lowest point. Find: its speed and kinetic energy at the bottom, ignoring pivot losses.

Solution. (1) \( v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.81 \times 1.2} = 4.85\ \mathrm{m/s} \). (2) \( KE = \tfrac12 \times 5 \times 4.85^2 = 58.9\ \mathrm{J} \) — exactly the \( mgh = 5 \times 9.81 \times 1.2 \) invested.

Answer: ≈4.9 m/s carrying ≈59 J. Sanity check: the path — arc, ramp, or free drop — never entered the calculation; conservative forces pay by endpoints only. That path-independence is why energy methods dispatch swinging and sliding problems that force methods turn into geometry exercises.

SOLVE 3

What friction collected on the ramp

Problem. A 20 kg crate leaves the top of a 3.0 m-high ramp from rest and arrives at the bottom at 5.0 m/s. Find: the energy lost to friction and the fraction of the invested energy it represents.

Solution. (1) Invested: \( mgh = 20 \times 9.81 \times 3.0 = 589\ \mathrm{J} \). (2) Delivered: \( \tfrac12 mv^2 = 0.5 \times 20 \times 25 = 250\ \mathrm{J} \). (3) Lost: \( 589 - 250 = 339\ \mathrm{J} \) — 58% of the investment, now heat in the ramp surface.

Answer: ≈339 J (58%) to friction. Sanity check: the frictionless arrival would have been \( \sqrt{2 \times 9.81 \times 3} = 7.7\ \mathrm{m/s} \); the observed 5.0 m/s < 7.7 respects the bound, and the shortfall MEASURED the friction without a single coefficient — the ledger used as an instrument.

MCQ 2

  1. Only conservative forces (gravity, springs) do work ✓
  2. The object moves slowly
  3. No forces act at all
  4. The path is straight

MCQ 5

Lesson 08 · Momentum, impulse, and collisions

Lecture

§1 · Momentum: motion's currency

Momentum, \( \mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v} \), is the quantity of motion — a vector, in kilogram-metres per second. Newton's second law in its original and most general form says force is the RATE of change of momentum: \( \mathbf{F} = d\mathbf{p}/dt \). Integrated over a contact, this becomes the impulse–momentum theorem: \( \mathbf{F}\Delta t = \Delta\mathbf{p} \) — the product of force and its duration equals the momentum change it produces. The product is the point: the same momentum change can be bought with a large force briefly or a small force at leisure, and nearly all impact engineering — packaging, bumpers, crash padding, soft-landing programs — is the deliberate purchase of more \( \Delta t \) to shrink \( F \).

§2 · Conservation: the system keeps its total

When no net EXTERNAL force acts on a system, its total momentum cannot change — the internal pushes and pulls cancel in third-law pairs. Collisions, couplings, and separations all obey it: \( m_1v_1 + m_2v_2 \) before equals the same sum after, signs carried by direction. The law holds regardless of how violent, brief, or lossy the interaction is — which is what makes it the collision analyst's first equation, written before anything is known about the forces themselves.

§3 · Collisions: what momentum keeps, energy may lose

Momentum is conserved in every collision; kinetic energy is not. A perfectly INELASTIC collision — the bodies couple and move together — conserves momentum while converting the maximum possible kinetic energy to deformation and heat. An ELASTIC collision conserves both, an idealization that hard steel balls and billiards approach. Real impacts live between the limits. The two conservation questions are therefore always asked separately, and the energy shortfall in a sticking collision is not an error — it is the crumple, the dent, and the noise, priced exactly.

§4 · The impulse–momentum working method

Define the system so the interesting forces become internal — both carts, hammer plus pile — and check that external forces are absent, negligible, or perpendicular during the brief event. Declare the positive direction and carry every velocity's sign. Write momentum conservation across the event; solve; then, if energies matter, audit the kinetic-energy books separately and route any loss honestly to deformation and heat. For contact-force questions, use the theorem in its \( F = \Delta p/\Delta t \) form and respect its sensitivity: halving the stopping time doubles the average force, which is why hard surfaces break what padded ones spare. The method's discipline is the system boundary — drawn well, the messy contact forces never need computing at all; drawn badly, nothing conserves and everything confuses.

Momentum and energy together complete the collision toolkit: momentum answers the velocities, energy names the damage, and the pair of audits — run separately, never merged — resolves every impact question this course will pose. The dynamics course will add rotation to both ledgers; the bookkeeping discipline installed here is the part that does not change.

SOLVE 1

The price of a hard stop

Problem. A 0.5 kg part arrives at 4 m/s and must be stopped. Find: the average force if it stops in 0.02 s (rigid stop) versus 0.2 s (cushioned stop).

Solution. (1) Momentum to remove: \( \Delta p = 0.5 \times 4 = 2.0\ \mathrm{kg\,m/s} \). (2) Rigid: \( F = 2.0/0.02 = 100\ \mathrm{N} \). (3) Cushioned: \( F = 2.0/0.2 = 10\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: 100 N versus 10 N — tenfold softer for tenfold longer. Sanity check: the product \( F\Delta t \) is identical (2.0 N·s) in both cases — the momentum bill must be paid in full; only the installment plan changes. Every bumper, dashpot, and packaging foam is this calculation cast in hardware.

SOLVE 2

Coupling on the line

Problem. A 5 kg carrier moving at 4 m/s couples into an identical stationary carrier. Find: the common speed after coupling, and the kinetic energy lost.

Solution. (1) Momentum: \( 5 \times 4 + 5 \times 0 = 10\,v \Rightarrow v = 2.0\ \mathrm{m/s} \). (2) KE before: \( \tfrac12 \times 5 \times 16 = 40\ \mathrm{J} \); after: \( \tfrac12 \times 10 \times 4 = 20\ \mathrm{J} \). (3) Lost: 20 J — exactly half, to the coupling's clunk, flex, and heat.

Answer: 2 m/s; 50% of the kinetic energy gone. Sanity check: equal masses coupling always halve the speed and the kinetic energy — momentum conserved, energy honestly not. The energy did not err; it bought the permanent coupling, and the accounting names its price.

SOLVE 3

Recoil of the free trolley

Problem. A 200 kg unbraked trolley carries a 20 kg component. The component is ejected horizontally at 3 m/s (relative to ground). Find: the trolley's recoil velocity.

Solution. (1) System momentum before: zero. (2) After: \( 20 \times 3 + 180 \times v = 0 \Rightarrow v = -60/180 = -0.33\ \mathrm{m/s} \). (3) The 180 kg remainder recoils at 0.33 m/s opposite the ejection.

Answer: ≈0.33 m/s backward. Sanity check: momentum magnitudes match — \( 20 \times 3 = 60 = 180 \times 0.33 \) — and the mass ratio (9:1) appears inverted in the speed ratio, as conservation demands. Every launcher, ejector, and pusher mechanism on an unsecured base rehearses this arithmetic; brakes exist because of it.

MCQ 1

Total momentum of a system is conserved when:

  1. No net external force acts on the system ✓
  2. No friction exists anywhere
  3. The collision is elastic
  4. The bodies are rigid

Conservation follows from Newton's laws whenever external forces sum to zero — internal forces, however violent, cancel in third-law pairs. Elasticity governs the ENERGY question, not the momentum one; rigidity and friction are red herrings at the system level (friction internal to the system is internal). Drawing the boundary so the big forces are internal is the entire craft.

MCQ 2

Impulse equals:

  1. The area under the force–time curve ✓
  2. Force divided by time
  3. The slope of the momentum curve
  4. Mass times acceleration

Force-over-time is a rate, not an accumulation; slope-of-momentum IS force (the differential form); and \( ma \) is instantaneous force, one integral short of impulse. MCQ 3

Padded surfaces reduce impact damage because they:

  1. Extend the stopping time, lowering the force for the same momentum change ✓
  2. Reduce the momentum to be removed
  3. Absorb momentum permanently
  4. Make the object lighter

The momentum bill \( \Delta p \) is fixed by mass and speed; padding stretches \( \Delta t \), and \( F = \Delta p/\Delta t \) falls proportionally. Momentum is not absorbed or shrunk — it is transferred at a gentler rate. Crumple zones, dock bumpers, and foam packaging are all \( \Delta t \)-merchants, selling time to buy down force.

MCQ 4

  1. Momentum only — kinetic energy takes its maximum loss ✓
  2. Both momentum and kinetic energy
  3. Kinetic energy only
  4. Neither

Sticking together is the maximum-KE-loss outcome compatible with momentum conservation; the missing energy became deformation, heat, and sound.   2. Equal kinetic energies
  3. Equal momenta and energies

Lesson 09 · Rotation I: kinematics and torque

Lecture

§1 · The angular vocabulary

Rotation replays kinematics with angular characters. Angular position \( \theta \) is measured in radians — the natural unit, because it makes arc length \( s = \theta r \) with no conversion factor. Angular velocity \( \omega = d\theta/dt \) in rad/s, and angular acceleration \( \alpha = d\omega/dt \) in rad/s², chain exactly as their linear siblings did, and the constant-\( \alpha \) toolkit is Lesson 2's kit with letters swapped: \( \omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t \), \( \theta = \omega_0 t + \tfrac12\alpha t^2 \), \( \omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\theta \). The machine floor speaks rpm; physics speaks rad/s; the exchange rate \( \omega = 2\pi N/60 \) is crossed so often it should be reflex — 1450 rpm, the standard 4-pole motor speed, is 152 rad/s.

§2 · The rolling connection

Rotation meets translation at the rim: a point at radius \( r \) moves at \( v = \omega r \) and, under angular acceleration, gains speed at \( a_t = \alpha r \). The relation runs every belt, chain, and traction drive: pulleys sharing a belt share rim speed, so their angular velocities trade inversely with radius — the speed-ratio law of every pulley pair and gear mesh. Radians earn their keep here: the formulas hold ONLY with angles in radians, and feeding degrees or rpm into \( v = \omega r \) is the classic rotational unit error.

§3 · Torque: force's rotational agent

What force is to translation, torque is to rotation: \( \tau = Fr\sin\theta \), the force times its perpendicular lever arm, in newton-metres. The statics course built moments for equilibrium; dynamics now lets them cause angular acceleration. On a belt drive, the torque delivered is the TENSION DIFFERENCE times radius — \( (T_1 - T_2)r \) — because both belt runs pull, in opposition; on a bolted coupling, torque is what the wrench and the torque spec are negotiating. The moment of inertia \( I \), rotation's mass, waits one lesson ahead as torque's partner.

§4 · The rotational working method

Run rotation problems with a currency-exchange discipline. Convert everything to SI angular units at the border — rpm to rad/s, revolutions to radians — work entirely inside, and convert back only for the final answer if the floor wants rpm. Choose the angular toolkit by the same known-versus-wanted matching as Lesson 2, and pass between the angular and linear worlds only through the radius: \( v = \omega r \), \( a_t = \alpha r \), \( s = \theta r \). Audit with the two standard checks — units (a radian is dimensionless, so \( \omega r \) lands in m/s cleanly) and magnitude sense (industrial machines mostly live between 0.5 and 300 rad/s; an answer of 15,000 rad/s is a units error announcing itself). The kinematics is deliberately familiar; fluency in the conversion discipline is what this lesson actually installs, because every machine after it — pumps, gearboxes, turbines — speaks both languages at once.

Lesson 10 · Rotation II: dynamics and angular momentum

Lecture

§1 · Moment of inertia: rotation's mass

Newton's second law rotates into \( \tau = I\alpha \), and the new character \( I = \int r^2\,dm \) — moment of inertia — is mass weighted by the SQUARE of its distance from the axis. The square is everything: the same kilograms placed twice as far resist spin-up four times harder. A solid disc carries \( I = \tfrac12 mr^2 \); a thin hoop, with all mass at the rim, carries \( I = mr^2 \) — double, at equal mass and radius. Distribution, not just quantity, decides rotational reluctance, which is why the statics course's area moment (resisting bending) and this mass moment (resisting angular acceleration) share a name and a square: same mathematics, different physics.

§2 · Rotational energy and the flywheel

A spinning body banks kinetic energy \( KE = \tfrac12 I\omega^2 \), and the \( \omega^2 \) makes speed the cheap lever: doubling spin quadruples storage. That is the flywheel's entire business plan — presses and punches draw their violent, brief demands from a wheel kept spinning by a modest motor, smoothing the load the way the machinery course will later formalize. A body that rolls carries both accounts at once, translational \( \tfrac12 mv^2 \) plus rotational \( \tfrac12 I\omega^2 \), which is why rolling objects arrive at ramp bottoms slower than sliding ones: part of the height budget went into spin.

§3 · Angular momentum and its conservation

Momentum's rotational sibling is \( L = I\omega \), conserved whenever no external torque acts. Because \( I \) depends on mass placement, a system that redistributes its own mass trades \( I \) against \( \omega \) at constant product — the figure skater's spin-up, and equally the reason a machine that throws a mass outward slows its own rotation. Torque is the rate of change of angular momentum, \( \tau = dL/dt \), completing the rotational translation dictionary: every linear law has now crossed over.

§4 · The rotational dynamics working method

Solve rotating problems by the same audit sequence as linear ones, with the dictionary open. Identify the axis and get \( I \) about THAT axis — from the standard shapes, or by the parallel-axis transfer when the spin axis is offset. Sum torques about the axis, signs by a declared positive sense, and apply \( \tau_{net} = I\alpha \). Chain to angular kinematics for speeds and angles, and to \( \tfrac12 I\omega^2 \) when the question is energetic. Audit with limits — at \( \alpha = 0 \) the torque balance must collapse to statics — and with the distribution instinct: between two candidates of equal mass, the one with mass farther out always spins up slower, coasts longer, and stores more at speed. The machines this feeds are immediate: every motor start is \( \tau = I\alpha \) against the driven train's inertia, and every coast-down time is the same equation read backward — which is exactly how the condition-monitoring course will one day read it.

The flywheel's bank balance

Problem. A press flywheel has \( I = 2.5\ \mathrm{kg\,m^2} \) and spins at 150 rad/s. Find: its stored kinetic energy, and the energy released if a punch stroke drops it to 140 rad/s.

  3. Both equally
  4. Depends on the material

\( I_{disc} = \tfrac12 mr^2 \) versus \( I_{hoop} = mr^2 \): the disc's \( \alpha = \tau/I \) is double. Distribution, not just mass, sets rotational reluctance — the \( r^2 \) weighting means rim mass counts fourfold per doubling of distance. Material is irrelevant at fixed m and geometry. This one comparison is the mental model for every rotor, coupling, and flywheel decision after it.

MCQ 2

Doubling a flywheel's speed multiplies its stored energy by:

  1. 4 ✓
  2. 2
  3. 8
  4. 16

\( KE = \tfrac12 I\omega^2 \propto \omega^2 \): double speed, quadruple storage — the same square that governed vehicle kinetic energy, now in rotation. This is why energy-storage flywheels chase speed (and why burst containment becomes their design driver). Eight belongs to \( \omega^3 \), a fan-power law; sixteen to \( \omega^4 \), nothing in this chapter.

MCQ 3

Angular momentum of a rotating system is conserved when:

  1. No external torque acts about the axis ✓
  2. No forces act at all
  3. The rotation is slow
  4. The body is rigid

\( \tau_{ext} = dL/dt \): zero external torque freezes L, whatever internal rearrangements occur — that is precisely how the skater and the rebalancing rotor trade I against ω. External FORCES through the axis exert no torque and are harmless; rigidity is not required (the skater isn't rigid). The conservation is the rotational twin of Lesson 8's, with torque replacing force at the gate.

MCQ 4

A net torque of 24 N·m acts on a rotor with \( I = 1.5\ \mathrm{kg\,m^2} \). Its angular acceleration is:

  1. 16 rad/s² ✓
  2. 36 rad/s²
  3. 0.0625 rad/s²
  4. 24 rad/s²

\( \alpha = \tau/I = 24/1.5 = 16\ \mathrm{rad/s^2} \). Multiplying (36) and inverting (0.0625) are the arithmetic traps; forgetting I entirely (24) treats torque as acceleration. The equation is the second law wearing rotational clothes, and the same discipline applies: τ must be the NET torque, friction and load subtracted before dividing.

MCQ 5

A cylinder rolls (without slipping) down a ramp while an identical cylinder slides down frictionlessly. At the bottom:

  1. The slider is faster — the roller spent part of its height budget on spin ✓
  2. The roller is faster
  3. They tie
  4. The heavier one wins

Both convert \( mgh \), but the roller must fund two accounts — \( \tfrac12 mv^2 \) AND \( \tfrac12 I\omega^2 \) — so less lands in translation: the slider arrives faster. Mass cancels in both cases. The split depends only on the shape's \( I/mr^2 \) ratio, which is why rolling races between hoops and discs are decided at the starting line by geometry.

Lesson 11 · Oscillations first contact

Lecture

§1 · The restoring recipe

Oscillation needs exactly two ingredients: a restoring force that pulls toward equilibrium in proportion to displacement, and inertia that overshoots it. The spring–mass system is the archetype — \( F = -kx \) feeding \( m\ddot{x} \) — and its motion is simple harmonic: \( x(t) = A\sin(\omega_n t) \), a sine forever, with amplitude \( A \) set by how the motion started and angular frequency set by the system itself: \( \omega_n = \sqrt{k/m} \). Stiffness accelerates the cycle; mass slows it; amplitude, remarkably, does not enter — small or large swings keep the same beat, which is what makes natural frequency a property of the MACHINE, not of the disturbance.

§2 · The pendulum and the family of oscillators

The pendulum repeats the recipe with gravity as the spring: \( T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g} \) for small swings — mass absent, length and gravity only. Every oscillator in the family carries the same signature quantities: period \( T = 2\pi/\omega_n \), frequency \( f = 1/T \) in hertz, and the energy sloshing between kinetic (maximum at equilibrium, where speed peaks at \( v_{max} = A\omega_n \)) and potential (maximum at the extremes, where motion pauses). Reading \( x(t) = A\sin(\omega t) \) fluently — amplitude from the coefficient, frequency from the argument — is reading the native language of every vibration trace the later courses will put on screen.

§3 · Why machines care: resonance previewed

Every machine element has natural frequencies, and rotating machinery supplies periodic forcing at running speed and its multiples. When forcing frequency approaches natural frequency, amplitude grows dramatically — resonance — and structures that seemed rigid begin to breathe. This lesson only introduces the actors; the vibrations course will quantify their meeting. But the design instinct starts now: know your \( \omega_n \), know your running speeds, and keep them apart.

§4 · The oscillation working method

Identify the stiffness and the inertia — every oscillator has both, whatever it looks like — and form \( \omega_n = \sqrt{k/m} \) (or the pendulum's \( \sqrt{g/L} \)). Convert consciously among \( \omega_n \), \( f \), and \( T \): the \( 2\pi \) that separates rad/s from Hz is this chapter's border crossing, as rpm's was the last one's. Extract amplitude and frequency from any given \( x(t) \) by inspection, and derive the speed and acceleration peaks as \( A\omega \) and \( A\omega^2 \). Audit with the scaling instincts: quadrupling stiffness only doubles frequency (the square root taxes enthusiasm), adding mass always slows the beat, and amplitude never changes the clock. Where a real trace disagrees with these instincts — frequency shifting with load, beat patterns, growth over time — the disagreement is information, and the vibrations course will teach you to read it. Here, the assignment is fluency with the clean case that all the diagnostics measure against — the sine every analyzer decomposes real signals into, and the natural frequency every balancing report quotes. First courses end where careers begin; this one ends on the exact frequency.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

The isolator's beat

Problem. A 2.0 kg instrument sits on a mount of stiffness 800 N/m. Find: the natural frequency in rad/s and Hz, and the period.

Solution. (1) \( \omega_n = \sqrt{k/m} = \sqrt{800/2} = 20\ \mathrm{rad/s} \). (2) \( f = \omega_n/2\pi = 3.18\ \mathrm{Hz} \); \( T = 1/f = 0.314\ \mathrm{s} \).

Answer: 20 rad/s = 3.2 Hz; period ≈0.31 s. Sanity check: quadrupling the stiffness would only double the frequency (√), and doubling the mass would divide it by √2 — the square root damping every intuition of proportionality. A 3 Hz mount under a 24 Hz (1450 rpm) machine is comfortably clear of resonance: the design arithmetic in miniature.

SOLVE 2

The inspection pendulum

Problem. A 0.70 m lanyard with a small tool swings as a pendulum. Find: its period, and what changes if the tool is twice as heavy.

Solution. (1) \( T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.70/9.81} = 1.68\ \mathrm{s} \). (2) Mass does not appear: the heavier tool swings with exactly the same period.

Answer: ≈1.7 s, at any mass. Sanity check: gravity provides both the restoring pull AND acts on the inertia, so mass cancels — the same cancellation as free fall. Only length and gravity remain: to slow a pendulum, lengthen it (fourfold length for double period, the square root again).

SOLVE 3

Reading a vibration trace

Problem. A bearing housing's displacement follows \( x(t) = 0.030\sin(12t) \) (metres, seconds). Find: the amplitude, frequency in Hz, and peak velocity.

Solution. (1) Amplitude by inspection: \( A = 0.030\ \mathrm{m} = 30\ \mathrm{mm} \). (2) \( \omega = 12\ \mathrm{rad/s} \Rightarrow f = 12/2\pi = 1.9\ \mathrm{Hz} \). (3) \( v_{max} = A\omega = 0.030 \times 12 = 0.36\ \mathrm{m/s} \).

Answer: 30 mm at 1.9 Hz; peak speed 0.36 m/s. Sanity check: peak velocity occurs at mid-swing where the trace crosses zero — displacement and velocity peaks alternate, a quarter-cycle apart. Reading A and ω off a sine by inspection is the exact skill vibration analyzers automate; learning it manually first is what makes their output meaningful.

MCQ 1

For small oscillations, increasing a pendulum's swing amplitude:

  1. Leaves the period unchanged ✓
  2. Increases the period
  3. Decreases the period
  4. Stops the swing

Amplitude independence is SHM's defining courtesy: larger swings travel farther but proportionally faster, and the clock holds — which is why pendulums could keep time. (At LARGE angles the approximation frays and the period does grow, but that correction belongs to a later course.) The natural frequency is the system's property; the amplitude merely reports how hard it was disturbed.

MCQ 2

To raise a machine mount's natural frequency, you should:

  1. Stiffen the mount or lighten the machine ✓
  2. Soften the mount
  3. Add mass
  4. Increase the operating amplitude

\( \omega_n = \sqrt{k/m} \): frequency rises with stiffness and falls with mass. Softening and adding mass LOWER it — which is itself a design tool (isolation mounts deliberately sit far below forcing frequencies). Amplitude has no vote. The k-and-m tuning of natural frequencies against running speeds is the first design instinct of vibration engineering.

MCQ 3

In SHM, the speed of the mass is greatest:

  1. Passing through equilibrium ✓
  2. At the extremes of travel
  3. Constant throughout
  4. At the quarter points

At the extremes the motion pauses (all energy potential); at equilibrium the restoring force has finished accelerating it and the energy is all kinetic: \( v_{max} = A\omega_n \). The energy slosh of Lesson 7 sets this geography — and vibration instruments exploit it, velocity sensors reading peak values a quarter-cycle from displacement peaks.

MCQ 4

Quadrupling a spring's stiffness multiplies the oscillation frequency by:

  1. 2 ✓
  2. 4
  3. 16
  4. \( \sqrt{2} \)

\( \omega_n \propto \sqrt{k} \): four times the stiffness, twice the frequency — the square root taxing the investment, exactly as it taxed grain refinement and case depth in other courses. Linear thinking (×4) overpays; the lesson's scaling instincts exist to prevent precisely that procurement conversation.

MCQ 5

A machine's natural frequency is 24 Hz and its rotor runs at 1440 rpm (24 Hz). The expected behavior is:

  1. Resonant amplitude growth — forcing sits on the natural frequency ✓
  2. Perfect smoothness
  3. The machine cannot rotate
  4. Nothing observable

Forcing at the natural frequency is the resonance condition: each cycle's push arrives in step with the motion, and amplitude climbs until damping or damage caps it. The numbers were chosen to collide — 1440 rpm IS 24 Hz. Real design keeps margins between running speeds and natural frequencies precisely to avoid this meeting; the vibrations course quantifies how much margin buys how much peace.
