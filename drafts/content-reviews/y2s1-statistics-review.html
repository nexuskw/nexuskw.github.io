Lesson 01 · Describing data

Lecture

§1 · Why engineers need statistics

No two manufactured parts are identical, no measurement is exact, and no process is perfectly steady. Statistics is the mathematics of that variation — the discipline that turns a scatter of numbers into a defensible statement. It has two halves: descriptive statistics, which summarise data in hand, and inferential statistics, which reason from a sample to the population it came from. This lesson builds the descriptive toolkit, the honest summary that every later inference rests on. Get the summary wrong and every test, interval, and control chart built on it inherits the error.

§2 · Centre: mean, median, mode

Three numbers locate the centre of a data set, and they answer different questions. The mean \( \bar x=\frac{1}{n}\sum x_i \) is the balance point; for the set \( \{2,4,4,4,5,5,7,9\} \) it is \( 40/8=5 \). The median is the middle value once ordered — here the average of the 4th and 5th values, \( (4+5)/2=4.5 \) — and it ignores outliers, which is why incomes and house prices are reported as medians. The mode is the most frequent value, here \(4\) (appearing three times), the only measure that works for categorical data. When a distribution is skewed the three separate, and the gap between mean and median is itself a signal of skew.

§3 · Spread: variance and standard deviation

Centre alone is half the story; spread is the other half. The variance averages squared deviations from the mean, and the standard deviation is its square root, back in the data's own units. For the set above the squared deviations sum to \(32\); the population variance is \( 32/8=4 \) (so \( \sigma=2 \)), while the sample variance divides by \(n-1\): \( 32/7\approx4.57 \) (so \( s\approx2.14 \)). The \(n-1\) — Bessel's correction — is not a quirk: a sample's own mean sits closer to its data than the true mean does, so dividing by \(n\) would systematically underestimate the spread. Use \(n-1\) whenever the data are a sample, which in engineering is almost always.

§4 · The describing-data working method

Before any test, summarise honestly: report a measure of centre and a measure of spread — a mean with no standard deviation hides exactly the variation that matters to an engineer. Choose the measure to fit the data: median and interquartile range for skewed or outlier-prone data, mean and standard deviation for roughly symmetric data, mode for categories. Plot before you compute — a histogram or box plot reveals skew, gaps, and outliers that no single number shows, and it is the cheapest guard against summarising a bimodal process as if it were one. These honest summaries are the raw material for the whole course: the mean becomes an estimate (Lesson 6), its variation becomes a test statistic (Lesson 7), and its spread becomes a control limit in the metrology and quality courses. Two further summaries round out the descriptive toolkit and appear throughout the curriculum: the range and interquartile range (IQR), which capture spread without squaring, and the coefficient of variation \( s/\bar x \), a dimensionless spread that lets you compare the relative scatter of two processes measured in different units. Together with a histogram, these turn a column of raw numbers into a picture an engineer can defend in a review.

Lesson 02 · Probability rules

Lecture

§1 · Probability as the language of chance

Inference runs on probability, so the rules must be exact. A probability is a number between 0 and 1 measuring how likely an event is; for equally likely outcomes it is favourable outcomes over total outcomes. A fair die shows an even number with probability \( 3/6=0.5 \). Two ideas organise everything else: how probabilities combine across events (this lesson), and how they build into distributions (Lessons 3–4). Getting the combination rules right is what separates a sound reliability estimate from a plausible-sounding wrong one.

§2 · The addition and multiplication rules

The addition rule gives the probability that A or B occurs: \( P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B) \), subtracting the overlap so it is not counted twice. For \( P(A)=0.5,\ P(B)=0.4,\ P(A\cap B)=0.2 \), \( P(A\cup B)=0.5+0.4-0.2=0.7 \). The multiplication rule gives the probability that A and B both occur; when the events are independent — one does not affect the other — it is simply the product \( P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B) \). Two fair coins both landing heads is \( 0.5\times0.5=0.25 \). Independence is an assumption to be checked, not assumed: correlated failures are exactly where naive multiplication underestimates risk.

§3 · Conditional probability and Bayes

When events are not independent, the conditional probability of A given B is \( P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} \) — the chance of A restricted to the world where B has happened. With \( P(A\cap B)=0.2 \) and \( P(B)=0.4 \), \( P(A\mid B)=0.2/0.4=0.5 \). Reversing the condition is the job of Bayes' theorem, \( P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{P(B\mid A)P(A)}{P(B)} \), which updates a prior belief with new evidence. Bayes is the reason a positive result on a rare-fault test can still, correctly, mean the fault is probably absent — the base rate dominates — and it underpins modern diagnostics and reliability updating.

§4 · The probability working method

Translate carefully: "or" invokes the addition rule (watch the overlap), "and" the multiplication rule (check independence first), "given that" a conditional. Draw the sample space or a tree for anything with more than two events, because intuition fails fast — the birthday and false-positive paradoxes are famous precisely because the correct rules override the gut. Keep every probability in \([0,1]\) and confirm a partition sums to 1 as an arithmetic check. These rules are the grammar of the distributions in the next lessons and of every reliability, quality, and risk calculation the curriculum returns to, so precision here pays compound interest. Two structural ideas make large problems tractable. Complementary events — \( P(\text{not }A)=1-P(A) \) — often turn a hard "at least one" calculation into an easy "none": the probability that at least one of several independent components fails is one minus the probability that all survive, the backbone of system-reliability analysis. And the law of total probability assembles an overall chance from mutually exclusive cases, weighting each conditional by how often its case occurs. Both are just the addition and multiplication rules applied with care, and both recur constantly once systems have more than a couple of parts.

Lesson 03 · Discrete distributions

Lecture

§1 · Random variables and distributions

A random variable assigns a number to each outcome — the count of defects in a batch, the number of arrivals in an hour — and its probability distribution lists how likely each value is. For counts, the distribution is discrete: a probability mass on each integer, summing to 1. Two discrete distributions cover most engineering counting problems, and recognising which one applies is the whole skill: the binomial for a fixed number of yes/no trials, and the Poisson for events occurring at a steady rate.

§2 · The binomial distribution

The binomial models \(n\) independent trials each with success probability \(p\): \( P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k} \). Its mean is \( \mu=np \) and its variance \( \sigma^2=np(1-p) \). For \( n=10 \) parts with a \( p=0.2 \) defect rate, the expected number of defects is \( np=2 \), the variance is \( 10\times0.2\times0.8=1.6 \), and the standard deviation \( \sqrt{1.6}\approx1.26 \). The chance of zero defects is \( P(X=0)=0.8^{10}\approx0.107 \) — about an 11% chance a batch of ten passes clean, a number that surprises people who expect a 20% defect rate to hit every batch.

§3 · The Poisson distribution

The Poisson models the count of events in a fixed interval when they occur independently at an average rate \( \lambda \): \( P(X=k)=\dfrac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!} \). Uniquely, its mean and variance are both \( \lambda \). For an average of \( \lambda=3 \) breakdowns per week, the probability of a quiet week with zero breakdowns is \( P(X=0)=e^{-3}\approx0.0498 \), about 5%. The Poisson also arises as the limit of a binomial with large \(n\) and small \(p\) (rare events in many trials), which is why it models flaws per metre of weld, calls per hour, or particles per sample — any "how many in a span" question with a steady rate.

§4 · The discrete-distribution working method

Pick the model from the physical situation, not the formula: a fixed number of independent yes/no trials is binomial; a count of events over a continuous span at a constant rate is Poisson. Check the assumptions — binomial needs constant \(p\) and independence; Poisson needs a steady rate and no clustering — because a violated assumption (say, defects that come in bursts) breaks the model quietly. Then read the mean and variance directly (\(np\) and \(np(1-p)\); \(\lambda\) and \(\lambda\)) to sanity-check any probability you compute. These two distributions feed straight into quality sampling plans and reliability counts later in the curriculum, and their means are the expectations every acceptance criterion is written against. A third discrete model, the geometric distribution, answers a different question — how many trials until the first success (or failure) — with mean \( 1/p \); it is the natural model for "how long until the next breakdown" in trial terms and the discrete cousin of the exponential distribution met in Lesson 4. Knowing which of these three a situation calls for — a fixed count, a rate over a span, or a waiting time — is the practical payoff of this lesson, because choosing the wrong family produces answers that are precise and wrong.

Lesson 04 · Continuous distributions

Lecture

§1 · From counts to measurements

Where discrete distributions count, continuous distributions measure — length, temperature, strength, time. A continuous variable takes any value in a range, so probability attaches to intervals, not points, through a probability density function whose area gives probability and integrates to 1. One continuous distribution dominates engineering: the normal, or Gaussian, distribution — the symmetric bell curve that describes measurement error, natural variation, and, through the next lesson's central limit theorem, the behaviour of averages themselves.

§2 · The normal distribution and the z-score

The normal distribution is fixed by two parameters, its mean \( \mu \) and standard deviation \( \sigma \). Any value is located by its z-score \( z=\dfrac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \), the number of standard deviations from the mean, which converts any normal to the standard normal (mean 0, SD 1) so one table serves all. A test score of \(x=130\) from a distribution with \( \mu=100,\ \sigma=15 \) has \( z=(130-100)/15=2 \); the fraction of the population below it is \( \Phi(2)\approx0.977 \), so it beats about 97.7% of the population. The z-score is the universal ruler of the normal world.

§3 · The empirical rule and other continuous models

The normal's spread follows the empirical (68–95–99.7) rule: about 68% of values lie within \( \pm1\sigma \) of the mean, 95% within \( \pm2\sigma \), and 99.7% within \( \pm3\sigma \). This is why "six-sigma" quality — keeping the process spread far inside the tolerance — corresponds to a vanishingly small defect fraction. Two other continuous models recur: the uniform, flat over a range (rounding error, a first guess at ignorance), and the exponential, describing the time between random events with mean \( 1/\lambda \) — the Poisson's continuous partner, and the basic model of memoryless failure times in reliability.

§4 · The continuous-distribution working method

To use a normal model: standardise with the z-score, then read the tail or interval probability from the standard-normal table (or software), remembering that the table gives area to the left, so a right tail is one minus that and a central band is a difference of two. Check the model before trusting it — a histogram that is skewed or bounded (times, counts near zero) is not normal, and forcing it there gives wrong tail probabilities exactly where decisions are made. The normal earns its dominance in the next lesson, where the central limit theorem shows that sample means become normal almost regardless of the parent distribution, which is why so much of inference is built on this one curve. It is worth carrying one honest caution about the normal: it is unbounded and symmetric, so it assigns nonzero probability to impossible values — a negative length, a strength below zero. For quantities physically bounded at zero or strongly one-sided, distributions such as the lognormal (for multiplicative processes and many strength and lifetime data) or the Weibull (the reliability workhorse of Lesson 10) fit better and keep their tails on the physically possible side. The normal is the default, not the law — reach for it because the data support it, not out of habit.

Lesson 05 · Sampling distributions and the CLT

Lecture

§1 · The leap from data to inference

Every earlier lesson described data in hand. Inference asks the harder question: what does a sample tell us about the population it came from? The bridge is the sampling distribution — the distribution not of raw data but of a statistic, such as the sample mean \( \bar x \), computed over many possible samples. Because a different sample gives a different \( \bar x \), the sample mean is itself a random variable with its own centre and spread, and understanding that spread is what makes an estimate trustworthy rather than a lucky guess.

§2 · The standard error

The sample mean has the same expected value as the population, \( \mu \), but a smaller spread. Its standard deviation — called the standard error to distinguish it from the data's — is \( \text{SE}=\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n} \). For a population with \( \sigma=10 \) and a sample of \( n=25 \), \( \text{SE}=10/\sqrt{25}=2 \); quadruple the sample to \( n=100 \) and the SE halves to 1. The \( \sqrt n \) is the central economic fact of sampling: precision improves only with the square root of effort, so halving the uncertainty costs four times the data. Every sample-size decision in the curriculum trades against this square-root wall.

§3 · The central limit theorem

The central limit theorem (CLT) is the reason statistics works at all: for a sufficiently large sample, the distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal — with mean \( \mu \) and standard error \( \sigma/\sqrt n \) — regardless of the shape of the parent population. Skewed, uniform, even bimodal parents all yield normal sample means as \(n\) grows (a rule of thumb is \( n\ge30 \)). This is why the normal distribution dominates inference: even when the raw data are not normal, the averages we test and interval-estimate almost always are. The CLT converts the messy variety of real data into one predictable, tabulated shape.

§4 · The sampling-distribution working method

To reason from a sample: identify the statistic (usually the mean), compute its standard error \( \sigma/\sqrt n \) — or \( s/\sqrt n \) when \( \sigma \) is unknown — and, if \(n\) is large enough, treat the statistic as normal by the CLT. Then a z-score of the statistic, \( z=(\bar x-\mu)/\text{SE} \), places any observed sample mean on the standard normal, exactly as Lesson 4 did for raw values. Two cautions: the CLT is about the mean, not individual data points, and it needs independence — clustered or autocorrelated samples inflate the true error beyond \( \sigma/\sqrt n \). With those in hand, the sampling distribution becomes the engine of the confidence intervals and hypothesis tests that follow. It is worth naming the finite-population and proportion cases you will meet. When sampling a large fraction of a finite lot, a finite-population correction shrinks the standard error, because sampling without replacement removes uncertainty as the sample approaches the whole. And for a proportion — a defect fraction rather than a mean — the same logic gives a standard error \( \sqrt{p(1-p)/n} \), which is why acceptance sampling can quote a confidence band on a scrap rate. Both are the sampling distribution applied to a new statistic, not new theory.

Lesson 06 · Estimation and confidence intervals

Lecture

§1 · From point estimate to interval

A single sample mean is a point estimate — the best single guess for the population mean — but on its own it hides its own uncertainty. A confidence interval reports the estimate together with a range that, with stated confidence, contains the true value. It answers the engineer's real question: not "what is the mean?" but "how sure are we, and within what band?" The interval is built directly on the sampling distribution of the previous lesson.

§2 · Building a confidence interval

For a mean with known \( \sigma \) and a large sample, the interval is \( \bar x\pm z^*\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n} \), where \( z^* \) is the standard-normal value cutting off the desired confidence — \( z^*=1.96 \) for 95%. With \( \bar x=50 \), \( \sigma=10 \), \( n=25 \) (so \( \text{SE}=2 \)), the margin of error is \( 1.96\times2=3.92 \) and the 95% interval is \( 50\pm3.92=(46.08,\ 53.92) \). The margin of error \( z^*\text{SE} \) is the interval's half-width, and it shrinks with \( \sqrt n \) and grows with the confidence demanded — you buy certainty with width.

§3 · Small samples and the t-distribution

When \( \sigma \) is unknown (the usual case) it is estimated by the sample \( s \), and for small samples that extra uncertainty widens the interval: the normal \( z^* \) is replaced by a slightly larger \( t^* \) from Student's t-distribution, whose fatter tails depend on the degrees of freedom \( n-1 \). As \(n\) grows the t-distribution converges to the normal, so for large samples the two agree. The interpretation of the interval is subtle and worth stating precisely: "95% confidence" means the procedure captures the true mean 95% of the time over many samples — not that this particular interval has a 95% probability of containing it, which is a common and consequential misreading.

§4 · The estimation working method

To report an estimate honestly: compute the point estimate, its standard error, and the margin \( z^* \) (or \( t^* \)) \( \text{SE} \); state the interval and the confidence level together. Choose the confidence to fit the stakes — 95% is conventional, 99% for safety-critical claims (wider), 90% where a rough band suffices. Use \(t\) whenever \( \sigma \) is estimated from a small sample; do not pretend to know \( \sigma \) you had to estimate. And resist the temptation to report a point estimate alone: a mean strength quoted without an interval invites the reader to treat a noisy guess as a fact, which is exactly the malpractice Lesson 11 catalogues. The confidence interval is the discipline that keeps an estimate honest. One design use deserves mention: the margin formula can be inverted to plan a sample size before collecting data. Setting \( z^*\sigma/\sqrt n \) equal to a target margin \(E\) and solving gives \( n=(z^*\sigma/E)^2 \) — so halving the acceptable margin quadruples the required sample, the same square-root wall met in Lesson 5, now driving a budget. Deciding the interval you need first, then computing the sample it costs, is far cheaper than collecting data blindly and discovering the band is too wide to support any decision.

Lesson 07 · Hypothesis testing

Lecture

§1 · Testing a claim against data

A hypothesis test is a formal procedure for deciding whether data are consistent with a claim. It sets up two competing statements: the null hypothesis \( H_0 \), the default or "no effect" claim (a process is on target, a new method is no better), and the alternative \( H_1 \), what we would conclude if the null fails. The logic is that of proof by contradiction: assume \( H_0 \), compute how surprising the data would be under it, and reject \( H_0 \) only if the data are surprising enough. The test never proves the null — it only fails, or fails to fail, to reject it.

§2 · The test statistic and the p-value

Surprise is measured by a test statistic — for a mean, \( z=\dfrac{\bar x-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt n} \), the number of standard errors the sample mean sits from the null value \( \mu_0 \). Testing \( \mu_0=50 \) with \( \bar x=52 \), \( \sigma=10 \), \( n=25 \) (so \( \text{SE}=2 \)) gives \( z=(52-50)/2=1 \). The p-value is the probability, if \( H_0 \) were true, of a result at least this extreme: here a two-tailed \( p\approx0.317 \), so a result this far out happens about a third of the time by chance alone. That is not surprising, so \( H_0 \) is not rejected.

§3 · Significance, errors, and their trade-off

The decision threshold is the significance level \( \alpha \), commonly 0.05: reject \( H_0 \) when \( p<\alpha \). Two errors are possible. A Type I error rejects a true null (a false alarm), with probability \( \alpha \); a Type II error fails to reject a false null (a missed effect), with probability \( \beta \), and \( 1-\beta \) is the test's power. The two trade off: lowering \( \alpha \) to avoid false alarms raises \( \beta \) and misses real effects, and the only way to reduce both at once is more data. Choosing \( \alpha \) is therefore an engineering judgement about which error is costlier — a false alarm that halts a line, or a missed defect that ships.

§4 · The hypothesis-testing working method

State \( H_0 \) and \( H_1 \) before seeing the data; choose \( \alpha \) by the cost of each error; compute the test statistic and its p-value; reject or retain \( H_0 \), and report the p-value, not just the verdict. Two disciplines guard against abuse. Fix the hypotheses and \( \alpha \) in advance — choosing them after seeing the data (p-hacking) manufactures false significance, the malpractice of Lesson 11. And separate statistical from practical significance: with a huge sample even a trivial, meaningless difference becomes "significant," so always report the effect size and its confidence interval alongside the p-value. A test answers "is the effect real?"; the interval answers "is it big enough to matter?" — and engineers need both. There is also a direct bridge between the two: a two-sided test at level \( \alpha \) rejects \( H_0 \) exactly when the null value falls outside the \( (1-\alpha) \) confidence interval, so the interval already contains the test's verdict plus the effect size the bare p-value hides — a good reason to report intervals by default.

Lesson 08 · Regression

Lecture

§1 · Relating one variable to another

Much of engineering is about relationships: how strength depends on carbon content, wear on load, yield on temperature. Regression fits a model to that relationship so it can be summarised, tested, and used to predict. The simplest and most important is simple linear regression, which fits a straight line \( \hat y=a+bx \) to paired data — \(x\) the predictor, \(y\) the response — capturing the trend in two numbers, a slope and an intercept.

§2 · Least squares

The line is chosen by least squares: minimise the sum of squared vertical distances (residuals) from the data to the line. This gives the slope \( b=\dfrac{\sum(x-\bar x)(y-\bar y)}{\sum(x-\bar x)^2} \) and the intercept \( a=\bar y-b\bar x \). For the points \( (1,2),(2,4),(3,5),(4,4),(5,5) \) with \( \bar x=3,\ \bar y=4 \): the numerator \( \sum(x-\bar x)(y-\bar y)=6 \), the denominator \( \sum(x-\bar x)^2=10 \), so \( b=0.6 \) and \( a=4-0.6\times3=2.2 \). The fitted line is \( \hat y=2.2+0.6x \). The slope is the physical takeaway: on average \(y\) rises 0.6 per unit of \(x\).

§3 · Goodness of fit and its limits

How well the line fits is measured by the coefficient of determination \( R^2 \), the fraction of the response's variation the model explains, between 0 and 1; for this data \( R^2=0.6 \), so 60% of the scatter in \(y\) is accounted for by \(x\). But \( R^2 \) is not the whole story, and two cautions are essential. First, correlation is not causation: a strong fit shows association, not that \(x\) causes \(y\) — a lurking third variable may drive both. Second, do not extrapolate: the line is evidence only across the range of the data, and predicting far outside it assumes a linearity nothing has tested. Always plot the residuals; a pattern in them means the straight line is the wrong model.

§4 · The regression working method

To fit and use a regression: plot the data first to confirm a roughly linear trend; compute the slope and intercept by least squares; report \( R^2 \) and, crucially, inspect a residual plot for curvature, funnelling variance, or outliers that a single number hides. Use the line to interpolate within the data range, never to extrapolate beyond it, and never state a causal conclusion the design of the study cannot support. Regression generalises to many predictors (multiple regression) and underlies calibration curves, empirical models, and the design-of-experiments analysis of the next lesson — but every one of those inherits these same three disciplines: plot, check residuals, and resist reading causation into a correlation. A closely related number is the correlation coefficient \(r\), the square root of \( R^2 \) carrying the sign of the slope, which measures the strength and direction of a linear association on a \(-1\) to \(+1\) scale; \(r=0\) means no linear relationship, though a strong curved one can still hide behind it — another reason the scatter plot is not optional. In practice the slope answers "how much," \(R^2\) and \(r\) answer "how tightly," and the residual plot answers "is a line even the right shape" — three questions every fitted relationship must survive before it is trusted for prediction.

Lesson 09 · Design of experiments first contact

Lecture

§1 · Experimenting on purpose

Design of experiments (DOE) is the discipline of planning tests so that the data answer the question with the fewest runs and the clearest conclusions. Its target is the relationship between input factors (temperature, pressure, feed rate) and an output response (yield, strength). The naive approach — change one factor at a time (OFAT), holding the rest fixed — is intuitive but wasteful and, worse, blind: it cannot detect interactions, where the effect of one factor depends on the level of another. Factorial design fixes both problems at once.

§2 · Factorial designs

A full factorial tests every combination of factor levels. With \(k\) factors each at two levels (low and high), the design has \( 2^k \) runs: three factors need \( 2^3=8 \) runs, four need \( 2^4=16 \). From these runs the analysis extracts two things OFAT cannot. The main effect of a factor is the average change in response as it moves from low to high — computed across all the other factors' settings, so it is more reliable than a single OFAT sweep. The interaction effect measures how one factor's influence shifts with another's level, which is often where the real engineering lives: a catalyst that helps at high temperature but hurts at low is an interaction no one-factor study would ever reveal.

§3 · Efficiency and fractional designs

Factorial designs are efficient because every run contributes to estimating every effect — the data are reused, not spent one factor at a time. When \(k\) is large, \( 2^k \) grows fast (seven factors would need 128 runs), so a fractional factorial runs a carefully chosen subset — a half-fraction \( 2^{k-1} \) uses 8 runs for four factors instead of 16 — trading the ability to resolve high-order interactions (usually negligible) for a large saving in runs. Good DOE also uses randomisation (to protect against unknown drifts), replication (to estimate noise), and blocking (to remove known nuisance variation), the three safeguards that separate a designed experiment from a mere collection of trials.

§4 · The DOE working method

To design an experiment: list the factors and the response; choose two levels (or more for curvature); pick a full or fractional factorial sized to the run budget; and randomise the run order. Analyse by computing main effects and interactions, testing each against the noise estimated from replication — the hypothesis-testing logic of Lesson 7 applied to effects. Report the significant effects and, above all, the interactions, because those are what OFAT and intuition miss. DOE is the bridge from passive statistics (describing data that happened) to active engineering (arranging data to be maximally informative), and it recurs across the quality, process, and reliability courses wherever a process must be optimised rather than merely observed. A final note on scope: two levels per factor reveal linear effects and interactions but cannot see curvature — a response that peaks in the middle looks flat if only the ends are tested. Detecting an optimum needs a centre point or a three-level (response-surface) design, the natural next step once a factorial has identified which few factors actually matter. This screen-then-optimise sequence — a cheap factorial to find the vital factors, a richer design to tune them — is the standard industrial recipe, and it is far more efficient than either exhaustive testing or one-factor guesswork.

Lesson 10 · Reliability statistics preview

Lecture

§1 · The statistics of failure

Reliability is the probability that a component or system performs its function for a stated time under stated conditions — statistics applied to failure. Its central function is the reliability function \( R(t) \), the probability of surviving to time \(t\), which falls from 1 at \(t=0\) toward 0. Its complement \( F(t)=1-R(t) \) is the cumulative failure probability. This lesson is a preview of the dedicated reliability courses, connecting the distributions already met to the language of survival, failure rate, and mean life.

§2 · The exponential model and MTBF

The simplest and most-used life model is the exponential, which assumes a constant failure rate \( \lambda \): \( R(t)=e^{-\lambda t} \). It is memoryless — a survivor is statistically as good as new — which fits random, non-wearout failures. Its mean time between failures is \( \text{MTBF}=1/\lambda \). A component with \( \lambda=0.001 \) failures per hour has \( \text{MTBF}=1000 \) hours, and its reliability at that mean life is \( R(1000)=e^{-0.001\times1000}=e^{-1}\approx0.368 \) — a much-misunderstood fact: only 37% survive to the MTBF, because the MTBF is a mean, not a guarantee, and the exponential is heavily right-skewed.

§3 · Wearout, the bathtub, and systems

Constant rate is not universal. The Weibull distribution generalises the exponential with a shape parameter that captures infant mortality (falling rate, early defects), random failures (constant rate), and wearout (rising rate, ageing) — the three regions of the classic bathtub curve of failure rate over life. For systems, reliability combines by structure exactly like the probability rules of Lesson 2. Components in series (all must work) multiply: two at \(0.9\) give \( 0.9\times0.9=0.81 \), and reliability only falls as parts are added. Components in parallel (redundant, one suffices) combine through their failure probabilities: two at \(0.9\) give \( 1-(0.1)(0.1)=0.99 \) — redundancy is how a system is made more reliable than any of its parts.

§4 · The reliability working method

To reason about reliability: pick the life model from the failure physics — exponential for random failures, Weibull when the rate changes with age — and estimate its parameters from failure data (the project pattern of the computing course). Compute \( R(t) \), MTBF, or a survival probability as the question demands, and never quote MTBF as a lifetime: report \( R(t) \) at the actual mission time. Build system reliability from component reliability using series (multiply) and parallel (redundancy) rules, and use them to find the weak link — in series, the least reliable component dominates. This preview hands directly to the reliability-engineering stream, where Weibull analysis, redundancy design, and maintenance strategy are developed in full, all resting on the survival function and the probability rules established here. One more quantity ties the pieces together: the failure rate or hazard \( h(t) \), the instantaneous rate of failure among survivors, which is constant for the exponential, falling then rising for the bathtub, and monotonic for many Weibull shapes. It is the hazard, not the raw failure count, that tells maintenance when to intervene — a rising hazard justifies scheduled replacement, while a constant one means age-based replacement buys nothing and condition monitoring is the better strategy. Reading the hazard shape from failure data is thus the hinge between the statistics of this lesson and the maintenance decisions of the reliability courses.

Lesson 11 · Statistical malpractice and honest reporting

Lecture

§1 · How statistics goes wrong

Statistics is as easily used to mislead as to inform, usually without outright lying. This closing lesson catalogues the common failures — some honest mistakes, some motivated — so that an engineer can produce and demand honest analysis. The theme is that every technique in this course has an integrity condition attached, and violating it quietly produces numbers that are precise, confident, and wrong. Recognising the failure modes is as much a professional skill as computing the statistics themselves.

§2 · P-hacking and multiple comparisons

The most corrosive abuse is p-hacking: trying many analyses and reporting only the one that reached significance. Its engine is the multiple-comparisons problem. A single test at \( \alpha=0.05 \) has a 5% false-alarm rate, but run many and false positives become nearly certain: with 20 independent tests, the chance of at least one spurious "significant" result is \( 1-0.95^{20}\approx0.64 \) — about a two-in-three chance of a false discovery even when nothing is real. The honest defences are to fix the hypothesis before the data, to correct the threshold for the number of tests, and to report all analyses run, not just the flattering one.

§3 · Base rates, confounding, and Simpson's paradox

Ignoring the base rate is the classic probability error. A test that is 99% sensitive and 95% specific for a fault present in 1% of parts, applied to a positive result, gives \( P(\text{fault}\mid\text{positive})=\dfrac{0.01\times0.99}{0.01\times0.99+0.99\times0.05}\approx0.167 \) — only a 17% chance the flagged part is actually faulty, because the rare condition is swamped by false positives. Confounding — a lurking variable driving both an apparent cause and effect — turns correlation into false causation, and its dramatic form is Simpson's paradox, where a trend that holds in every subgroup reverses when the groups are pooled. All three are failures to condition on the right information, and all three are invisible to anyone who trusts a single summary number.

§4 · The honest-reporting working method

Report to inform, not to persuade. State the hypothesis and analysis plan before collecting data; report the sample size, the effect size, and a confidence interval, not just a p-value; and disclose every analysis performed. Draw graphs honestly — a truncated or inverted axis, a cherry-picked time window, or a mismatched scale can make a trivial change look dramatic or hide a real one, and misleading graphs are the most common statistical deception precisely because they bypass the arithmetic. Distinguish statistical from practical significance, correlation from causation, and the sample from the population. This is the integrity floor of the whole discipline, and it is the same floor this platform is built on: every number verified, every claim sourced, nothing precise-but-false. An engineer who can compute a statistic and also refuse to misuse it is the one this course aims to produce. A short checklist captures the defences: was the hypothesis fixed before the data, or fished from them; is the significance corrected for how many things were tested; does the claimed cause survive the obvious confounders; is the effect large enough to matter, not merely detectable; and does every graph use an honest scale. A result that passes all five is worth acting on; one that fails any of them is, at best, a lead to investigate rather than a conclusion to ship. Carrying that checklist is the mark of a professional, and it is the note this statistics course deliberately ends on.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Multiple comparisons

Problem. With 20 independent tests each at \( \alpha=0.05 \), find the probability of at least one false positive when nothing is real.

Solution. \( P=1-(1-0.05)^{20}=1-0.95^{20}\approx1-0.358=0.64 \). Answer: \(\approx0.64\) — false discovery is likely, the reason to correct for multiplicity.

SOLVE 2

Base-rate fallacy

Problem. A test 99% sensitive, 95% specific, for a fault in 1% of parts, returns positive. Find the probability the part is actually faulty.

Solution. \( P(\text{fault}\mid+)=\frac{0.01\times0.99}{0.01\times0.99+0.99\times0.05}=\frac{0.0099}{0.0594}\approx0.167 \). Answer: \(\approx0.167\) — only 17%, because the fault is rare.

SOLVE 3

Reading a p-hacked study

Problem. A team ran 20 analyses and reports the one with \(p=0.03\). Why is this weak evidence?

Solution. Across 20 tests a false positive is ~64% likely by chance, so a single \(p=0.03\) chosen from many is not the 3% it appears — it must be corrected for the number of comparisons. Answer: selective reporting inflates significance; correct for multiplicity or pre-register.
