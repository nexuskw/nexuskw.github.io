Lesson 01 · Why materials fail the plant

Lecture

§1 · Four families, four bargains

Every engineering material belongs to one of four families, and each family is a bargain with physics. Metals: stiff, strong, tough, conductive — but dense and corrodible. Ceramics: hard, refractory, chemically inert — but brittle, and brittle without apology. Polymers: light, cheap, formable, corrosion-immune — but soft, creep-prone, and temperature-limited. Composites: property combinations the families cannot reach alone — bought with cost and joining headaches. No family is best; each is best at something, and the selection question is always "what does this application refuse to forgive?"

§2 · The spine of the course

Materials science stands on one chain:

\[ \text{processing} \rightarrow \text{structure} \rightarrow \text{properties} \rightarrow \text{performance} \tag{1} \]

Processing sets the internal structure — from atomic bonding through crystal defects to microstructure; structure sets the properties; properties decide whether the part survives service. The chain runs both directions: read it left-to-right to design a material, right-to-left to diagnose a failure. Every lesson in this course is one link examined closely.

§3 · The failed part is a materials question

A snapped bolt, a cracked impeller, a swollen seal — the maintenance floor sees these as events; this course teaches you to see them as structure speaking. Was the fracture surface woody and deformed (ductile, overload) or flat and crystalline (brittle, or fatigue)? Did the polymer harden and craze (thermal aging) or soften and smear (chemical attack)? Chain (1) run backwards is failure analysis, and it begins with knowing which family the part belongs to and what that family's bargain was.

§4 · The failure-reading working method

Materials thinking has a procedure, and it starts at the broken part, not the catalog. Name the failure mode first — overload, fatigue, corrosion, wear, creep — because each mode names its own culprit property and its own fix; a fatigue failure is not solved by a stronger material if the problem is a sharp shoulder. Walk the causal chain backward: performance failed → which property fell short → which structure produced that property → which processing produced that structure. The chain runs in both directions, and every lesson in this course adds one link. Quantify before judging: \( \sigma = F/A \) turns "the bolt looked thin" into a number — a 20% section loss is a 25% stress rise at constant load, because the divisor shrank — and numbers survive meetings that adjectives do not.

Close every reading with the honest questions: what environment, what load spectrum, what life, what cost? A material is never good or bad — it is suited or unsuited to those four declared inputs. The professional habit this course builds is refusing to discuss materials until the four inputs are on the table, and refusing to blame a material until the failure mode has a name.

Keep one more discipline from day one: data provenance. Handbook numbers are typical values with real scatter; a delivered batch's certificate is the number that actually matters, and downstream lessons will teach you to read one. Where the certificate and the handbook disagree, the certificate wins — the material in the rack outranks the material in the book.

Closing. Four families, one chain, two reading directions. Everything that follows — bonding, crystals, defects, strengthening — is the machinery inside the word "structure".

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Stiffness per kilogram

Problem. Compare steel (\( E = 200\ \mathrm{GPa} \), \( \rho = 7850\ \mathrm{kg/m^3} \)) and aluminum (\( E = 69\ \mathrm{GPa} \), \( \rho = 2700\ \mathrm{kg/m^3} \)) on specific stiffness \( E/\rho \).

Solution. (1) Steel: \( 200 \times 10^9 / 7850 = 25.5 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{m^2/s^2} \). (2) Aluminum: \( 69 \times 10^9 / 2700 = 25.6 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{m^2/s^2} \).

Answer: essentially identical — a classic and initially shocking result. Sanity check: aluminum is one-third as stiff but also roughly one-third as dense; the ratio cancels. The lesson: "aluminum saves weight" is true only when the design is not limited by simple tensile stiffness — geometry (deeper, thinner sections) is where aluminum actually wins. Property data from Callister & Rethwisch.

SOLVE 2

Same load, different family

Problem. A machine guard bracket in steel (\( E = 200\ \mathrm{GPa} \)) is proposed in glass-filled nylon (\( E \approx 2.8\ \mathrm{GPa} \), representative). Same geometry, same load. Find: the elastic deflection ratio.

Solution. (1) Elastic deflection at fixed geometry and load scales as \( 1/E \). (2) Ratio: \( 200/2.8 = 71 \).

Answer: the nylon bracket deflects ~71× more. Sanity check: that may be fine (a guard is not a bearing housing) or fatal (anything holding alignment). The number itself is neutral — the application's tolerance for deflection decides, which is §1's point: families are bargains, and you must know which clause of the bargain your part invokes.

SOLVE 3

The corroded bolt's rising stress

Problem. A bolt with 245 mm² of effective cross-section carries a steady 30 kN. Corrosion thins it to 80% of its original area. Find: the stress before and after, and the percentage rise.

Solution. (1) Before: \( \sigma = F/A = 30{,}000/245 = 122\ \mathrm{MPa} \). (2) After: \( A = 0.8 \times 245 = 196\ \mathrm{mm^2} \); \( \sigma = 30{,}000/196 = 153\ \mathrm{MPa} \). (3) Rise: \( 1/0.8 = 1.25 \) — exactly +25%, at perfectly constant load.

Answer: 122 → 153 MPa; +25%. Sanity check: stress scales as the reciprocal of area, so a 20% area loss must produce a 25% stress rise \( (0.8^{-1} = 1.25) \) — the part moved a quarter of the way toward failure without the load changing at all, which is the quiet arithmetic behind most corrosion-driven fractures.

MCQ 1

Ceramics buy their hardness and temperature resistance mainly at the price of:

  1. density
  2. toughness ✓
  3. corrosion resistance
  4. cost

Ionic/covalent bonding blocks the atomic-plane slide that lets metals deform, so ceramics fracture instead of flowing — low toughness is the family's standing bargain. They are typically less dense than metals and outstandingly corrosion-resistant, so the other choices invert the trade.

MCQ 3

A pump shaft snaps after two years of trouble-free service, with a flat, beach-marked fracture face. The first question a materials engineer asks is:

  1. Which failure mode does the fracture surface indicate? ✓
  2. What stronger alloy can replace it?
  3. Who installed it?
  4. What did the shaft cost?

Beach marks are fatigue's signature — and fatigue is fixed by attacking cyclic stress and stress raisers, not by raw strength; a stronger but more notch-sensitive alloy can fail sooner. Mode first, then property, then material: reversing the order is the classic upgrade-and-fail-again loop that keeps failure analysts employed.

MCQ 4

  1. steel wins by ~3×
  2. aluminum wins by ~3×
  3. they are essentially equal ✓
  4. neither has a defined ratio

\( E/\rho \) computes to \( 25.5 \) versus \( 25.6 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{m^2/s^2} \) — a dead heat. Aluminum's weight saving is real but comes from geometry (deeper, thinner sections at equal mass), not from raw specific stiffness. A classic selection-chart surprise.

MCQ 5

Of the four material families, which combination best describes ceramics?

  1. Stiff, hard, temperature-tolerant — and brittle ✓
  2. Ductile and conductive
  3. Light, tough, and cheap
  4. Soft but creep-resistant

Ionic/covalent bonding gives ceramics stiffness, hardness, and thermal standing — and denies them the dislocation plasticity that lets metals yield instead of shatter. That trade is the family's entire engineering identity: superb in compression and at temperature, unforgiving of tension and impact. The other option sets describe metals, polymers-in-a-good-mood, and nothing in nature respectively.

Lesson 02 · Atomic bonding

Lecture

§1 · Bonds are springs with a depth

Two atoms settle at the separation \( r_0 \) where attraction and repulsion balance — the bottom of the bond-energy well. Two features of that well govern macroscopic behavior. Its curvature at the bottom is the stiffness of the interatomic spring: deep, narrow wells give high elastic modulus. Its depth is the bond energy: deep wells mean high melting points and — because a deep well is also more symmetric near its floor — low thermal expansion. Modulus, melting point, and expansion coefficient travel together, and the bond-energy curve is why.

§2 · The four bonding types

Metallic: ion cores in a shared electron sea. The sea conducts (electrons free to drift) and the non-directional bonding lets planes of atoms slide without shattering — conductivity and ductility from one picture. Ionic: electron transfer, then Coulomb attraction between ions; strong but directionally rigid — slide a plane one spacing and like charges meet, so ceramics fracture rather than flow. Covalent: shared electron pairs, strongly directional; diamond's hardness and silicon's brittleness. Secondary (van der Waals / hydrogen): weak dipole attractions — the reason polymer chains, strongly covalent along their backbones, still slide past each other at modest temperature.

§3 · Mixed character, measured

Real compounds sit between poles. Electronegativity difference estimates the ionic fraction of a bond; Pauling's relation \( \%IC = [1 - e^{-0.25(X_A - X_B)^2}] \times 100 \) puts MgO at ~73% ionic while SiC is ~12% — one behaves as a classic ionic ceramic, the other as a covalent one, and their fracture and conduction behavior follow suit. §Worked Examples computes both, plus the raw Coulomb force inside an ion pair.

§4 · Reading properties off the bond

The bond-energy curve is a property-prediction machine if read with method. Well depth is cohesion: deeper wells mean higher melting points, higher stiffness, lower vapor pressure — which is why tungsten's deep metallic well melts at 3400 °C while polyethylene's van-der-Waals-held chains soften in boiling water. Well curvature at the bottom is elastic modulus: a steep, narrow well resists stretching hard. Well asymmetry is thermal expansion: shallow, lopsided wells let atoms ride up the soft side as temperature climbs — polymers expand roughly ten times more than metals per degree for exactly this reason.

Work the scale conversion fluently: table values arrive per mole, atoms bond one at a time, so divide by Avogadro's number — a 640 kJ/mol bond is about 1.1 aJ, or 6.6 eV, per bond. And keep the bonding families honest in memory: primary bonds (ionic, covalent, metallic) are hundreds of kJ/mol and set the ceiling properties; secondary bonds are tens and set the softening behavior of everything held together by them. When a datasheet surprises you, the bond picture is the first sanity check: no van-der-Waals solid will ever serve at 500 °C, whatever the brochure implies.

Closing. Stiffness, melting, and expansion live in the well's shape; ductility versus brittleness lives in whether the bonding forgives atomic-plane slide. Metals forgive; ceramics do not; polymers are covalent ropes held by weak handshakes.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

The force inside an ion pair

Problem. Estimate the attractive Coulomb force between a Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ion pair at separation \( r_0 = 0.276\ \mathrm{nm} \) (representative).

Solution. (1) \( F = \dfrac{k q_1 q_2}{r^2} \) with \( k = 8.99 \times 10^9\ \mathrm{N\,m^2/C^2} \), \( q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{C} \). (2) \( F = \dfrac{8.99 \times 10^9 \times (1.602 \times 10^{-19})^2}{(2.76 \times 10^{-10})^2} \). (3) \( F = 3.03 \times 10^{-9}\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: ~3 nN per ion pair. Sanity check: nanonewtons sound tiny, but a square millimetre of crystal face holds ~10¹³ such pairs — summed, that is the strength of salt crystals and the reason ionic solids melt at hundreds of degrees.

SOLVE 2

Percent ionic character

Problem. Using Pauling electronegativities (Callister & Rethwisch): Mg 1.2, O 3.5, Si 1.8, C 2.5. Find: the ionic character of MgO and SiC.

Solution. (1) MgO: \( \Delta X = 2.3 \); \( \%IC = [1 - e^{-0.25(2.3)^2}] \times 100 = [1 - e^{-1.32}] \times 100 = 73\% \). (2) SiC: \( \Delta X = 0.7 \); \( \%IC = [1 - e^{-0.25(0.7)^2}] \times 100 = [1 - e^{-0.123}] \times 100 = 12\% \).

Answer: MgO ≈ 73% ionic; SiC ≈ 12% ionic (88% covalent). Sanity check: oxygen sits far right of magnesium on the electronegativity scale, silicon and carbon are neighbors — the formula only quantifies what the periodic table already whispers. Both are "ceramics", but their bonding characters, and hence their oxidation and sintering behavior, differ usefully.

SOLVE 3

From table to atom

Problem. A handbook lists a bond energy of 640 kJ/mol for a primary bond. Find: the energy per single bond in joules and electron-volts, and compare with a van der Waals interaction of ~8 kJ/mol.

Solution. (1) Per bond: \( 640{,}000/6.022\times10^{23} = 1.06\times10^{-18}\ \mathrm{J} \). (2) In eV: \( 1.06\times10^{-18}/1.602\times10^{-19} = 6.6\ \mathrm{eV} \). (3) The van der Waals bond: \( 8/640 = 1/80 \) of the energy — about 0.08 eV.

Answer: ≈1.1 aJ = 6.6 eV per bond; the secondary bond is ~80× weaker. Sanity check: thermal energy at room temperature is ~0.025 eV — far below 6.6 eV (primary bonds shrug off heat) but only a factor of ~3 below the secondary bond's scale, which is why gentle warmth softens polymers while leaving metals untouched.

A deep, narrow bond-energy well predicts:

  2. low melting point, low stiffness, high expansion
  3. high melting point, low stiffness, high expansion
  4. no measurable consequences

  2. their bonding electrons are delocalized — an electron sea ✓
  3. they contain vacancies
  4. their bonds are directional

Metallic bonding pools valence electrons into a shared sea free to drift under a field — the same non-directional picture that lets atomic planes slide (ductility). Ions in a solid metal are locked in the lattice; vacancies matter for diffusion, not conduction.

MCQ 3

Which bond type explains a material that conducts electricity well and deforms plastically without shattering?

  1. Metallic ✓
  2. Ionic
  3. Covalent
  4. Van der Waals

The electron sea does both jobs: delocalized electrons carry charge, and non-directional bonding lets planes of ion cores slide to new positions without breaking the cohesion — plasticity. MCQ 5

Polymers soften near 100–200 °C while metals serve far hotter. The bond-level reason is:

  1. Chains are held to each other by weak secondary bonds that heat easily overcomes ✓
  2. Polymer covalent bonds are weak
  3. Polymers contain no crystals

Lesson 03 · Crystal structures

Lecture

§1 · Three cells carry most of metallurgy

Most engineering metals crystallize into one of three unit cells. FCC (face-centered cubic): atoms at corners and face centers — copper, aluminum, nickel, austenitic stainless. BCC (body-centered cubic): corners plus one center atom — ferritic iron, chromium, tungsten. HCP (hexagonal close-packed): zinc, magnesium, titanium. Geometry first: in FCC atoms touch along the face diagonal, so \( a = 2R\sqrt{2} \) with 4 atoms per cell; in BCC they touch along the body diagonal, \( a = 4R/\sqrt{3} \), 2 atoms per cell.

§2 · Packing predicts behavior

The atomic packing factor — fraction of cell volume occupied — is 0.74 for FCC and HCP against 0.68 for BCC. FCC's close packing comes with close-packed planes on which slip is easy in many directions at any temperature: FCC metals stay ductile from cryogenic cold to near melting. BCC's slightly open structure makes slip thermally assisted — cool a BCC steel enough and slip stalls before fracture does, the root of the ductile–brittle transition that Lesson 8 prices in joules. HCP's single close-packed plane family gives few slip systems and the notorious formability limits of magnesium and zinc.

§3 · Density from first principles

The unit cell converts crystallography into a checkable number:

\[ \rho = \frac{nA}{V_C N_A} \tag{1} \]

\( n \) atoms per cell, \( A \) the atomic weight, \( V_C \) the cell volume, \( N_A \) Avogadro's number. §Worked Examples runs copper and lands within 1% of the handbook value — a satisfying audit that the atomic picture is not a metaphor but a measurement.

§4 · The unit-cell bookkeeping method

Crystal arithmetic rewards the same tabular discipline as statics. Fix the cell geometry first: for FCC, atoms touch along the face diagonal, so \( a = 2R\sqrt{2} \); for BCC, along the body diagonal, \( a = 4R/\sqrt{3} \) — derive the relation from the touching direction rather than memorizing it, and it cannot betray you. Count atoms the honest way: corners contribute an eighth each, faces a half, body atoms a whole — 4 per FCC cell, 2 per BCC. Then let the density formula audit everything: \( \rho = nA/(V_C N_A) \) predicts copper at 8.90 g/cm³ from nothing but a radius and an atomic mass; agreement with the handbook to within a percent certifies the whole chain of geometry, counting, and units in one number.

Use the same audit in reverse on real parts: a measured density falling meaningfully below the theoretical value is porosity talking — castings and additive parts confess their voids this way, and the quality lab's densitometer is running this lesson's equation as a routine instrument. Structure is not an abstraction; it is a number you can weigh — and the first crystal-level measurement most engineers ever make is a bathroom-scale argument about atoms, which is precisely what makes it trustworthy on a shop floor.

Closing. Three cells, two touching rules, one packing-factor gap that explains why austenitic steel stays tough in liquid nitrogen while ferritic steel turns to glass in a cold snap. Crystal structure is destiny, slightly exaggerated — but only slightly.

Examples & Quiz (3 solved + 5 MCQ)

SOLVE 1

Packing factor of FCC

Problem. Derive the FCC atomic packing factor.

Solution. (1) Face-diagonal touching: \( 4R = a\sqrt{2} \Rightarrow a = 2R\sqrt{2} \). (2) Atoms per cell: \( 8 \times \tfrac{1}{8} + 6 \times \tfrac{1}{2} = 4 \). (3) \( APF = \dfrac{4 \times \tfrac{4}{3}\pi R^3}{(2R\sqrt{2})^3} = \dfrac{16.76\,R^3}{22.63\,R^3} = 0.740 \).

Answer: 0.74 — the maximum possible for equal spheres. Sanity check: the same computation for BCC (\( a = 4R/\sqrt{3} \), 2 atoms) gives 0.68; FCC is measurably tighter, consistent with its higher coordination (12 vs 8).

SOLVE 2

Density of copper from its lattice

Problem. Copper: FCC, \( R = 0.128\ \mathrm{nm} \), \( A = 63.55\ \mathrm{g/mol} \) (Callister & Rethwisch). Find: the theoretical density.

Solution. (1) \( a = 2R\sqrt{2} = 0.362\ \mathrm{nm} = 3.62 \times 10^{-8}\ \mathrm{cm} \). (2) \( V_C = a^3 = 4.75 \times 10^{-23}\ \mathrm{cm^3} \). (3) \( \rho = \dfrac{nA}{V_C N_A} = \dfrac{4 \times 63.55}{4.75 \times 10^{-23} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23}} = 8.90\ \mathrm{g/cm^3} \).

Answer: \( 8.90\ \mathrm{g/cm^3} \). Sanity check: the measured density of copper is 8.94 g/cm³ — agreement within half a percent, from nothing but an atomic radius, a molar mass, and geometry. The unit cell is bookkeeping that nature audits.

SOLVE 3

Predicting copper's density from its cell

Problem. Copper is FCC with atomic radius 0.128 nm and atomic mass 63.55 g/mol. Find: the lattice parameter and the theoretical density.

Solution. (1) FCC touching along the face diagonal: \( a = 2R\sqrt{2} = 2 \times 0.128 \times 1.414 = 0.362\ \mathrm{nm} \). (2) Cell volume: \( a^3 = (3.62\times10^{-8}\ \mathrm{cm})^3 = 4.74\times10^{-23}\ \mathrm{cm^3} \); FCC holds \( n = 4 \) atoms. (3) \( \rho = nA/(V_C N_A) = 4 \times 63.55/(4.74\times10^{-23} \times 6.022\times10^{23}) = 8.90\ \mathrm{g/cm^3} \).

Answer: \( a \approx 0.362\ \mathrm{nm} \); \( \rho \approx 8.90\ \mathrm{g/cm^3} \). Sanity check: handbook copper is 8.94 g/cm³ — agreement within half a percent from nothing but a radius, a mass, and geometry; a real part measuring well below this value is declaring porosity.

Lesson 04 · Crystallographic directions and planes

Lecture

§1 · Naming directions and planes

Crystals are anisotropic objects, so we need coordinates. Directions [uvw]: take the vector's components along the cell axes, clear fractions to smallest integers, wrap in square brackets, mark negatives with an overbar. Planes (hkl), the Miller indices: take the plane's intercepts on the axes in units of the lattice parameters, invert them, clear fractions, wrap in parentheses — an intercept at infinity (plane parallel to an axis) inverts to a clean zero. Families of equivalent directions and planes get angle and brace brackets: ⟨110⟩, {111}.

§2 · Why these labels earn their keep

Slip — plastic deformation — happens on the densest planes along the densest directions, because those are the paths where atoms ride over each other with the least protest. In FCC that means {111} planes and ⟨110⟩ directions: 12 combinations, the slip systems that make copper and aluminum so obligingly ductile. Linear and planar densities (atoms per length, per area) are the quantitative versions, and computing them is how the claim "close-packed = slip-prone" stops being a slogan.

§3 · Anisotropy leaves the lab

A single crystal's modulus can differ by a factor of two between directions. Polycrystals average this out — unless processing lines the grains up. Rolling does exactly that: plate develops a crystallographic texture plus elongated grains and aligned inclusions, so strength, ductility, and especially toughness differ between the rolling direction and the transverse. Standards respond by specifying test-bar orientation, and fabricators respond — or should — by orienting bends relative to the rolling direction. The Miller machinery of §1 is the language in which all of this is written.

§4 · The indexing working method

Miller indices are a procedure, and running it identically every time is the entire skill. Planes: read the intercepts in cell-edge units, take reciprocals, clear fractions, bracket as \( (hkl) \) — a plane parallel to an axis intercepts at infinity and earns a zero. Directions: head coordinates minus tail coordinates, clear to smallest integers, bracket as \( [uvw] \). Negative components wear an overbar, and families collect all symmetry-equivalent members: \( \{111\} \) means every close-packed plane in FCC at once. The classic error is mixing conventions mid-problem — intercepts for directions or vector components for planes produce plausible-looking nonsense, which is why the two recipes should be written at the top of the page until they are reflex.

Then put the indices to work, because they exist for the packing arithmetic: linear density along a close-packed direction is \( 1/(2R) \) — atoms touching in a row — and the densest planes with the densest directions form the slip systems where plasticity lives. FCC's twelve well-fed slip systems are the geometric reason copper draws into wire without complaint; HCP's grudging three are why some alloys crack where others flow. The indices are the language; slip is the sentence they were invented to write.

Closing. Directions in brackets, planes in parentheses, slip on the close-packed combinations, and rolled products carrying their processing history as directionality. The labels are dry; what they index is not.

Lesson 05 · Defects in solids

Lecture

§1 · The perfect crystal is a useful fiction

Every real crystal is defective, and the defects are not blemishes on the theory — they are the theory of real behavior. By dimensionality: point defects — vacancies (missing atoms), interstitials (atoms squeezed into gaps), and substitutional or interstitial solute atoms; line defects — dislocations, edge and screw; planar defects — grain boundaries, twin boundaries, free surfaces. Each class controls a different family of properties, and the rest of this course is largely the story of engineering their populations.

§2 · Vacancies obey thermodynamics

Vacancies are not accidents; equilibrium demands them. Their fraction rises exponentially with temperature:

\[ \frac{N_v}{N} = \exp\!\left(-\frac{Q_v}{kT}\right) \tag{1} \]

Near the melting point roughly one site in ten thousand is empty; at room temperature the fraction is astronomically smaller. This matters because vacancies are diffusion's vehicle — atoms move mainly by hopping into adjacent empty sites — so (1) is the deep reason every diffusion-controlled process in Lesson 6 accelerates exponentially with temperature.

§3 · Dislocations: why metals are a thousand times weaker than they should be

Shearing a perfect crystal means breaking every bond across a plane simultaneously — the theoretical strength, orders of magnitude above anything measured. A dislocation breaks the bonds one row at a time, like moving a carpet by running a wrinkle across it rather than dragging the whole rug. Metals deform at realistic stresses because dislocations exist; they strengthen when we obstruct dislocation motion — with solutes, boundaries, and other dislocations, the entire agenda of Lesson 9. Grain boundaries, meanwhile, are both obstacles (strength) and fast-diffusion highways (creep, corrosion sensitivity): planar defects always charge rent both ways.

§4 · Reading the defect inventory

Perfection is not an option in real crystals, so the working method is inventory management. Know each defect's dimension: point defects (vacancies, interstitials, substitutionals), line defects (dislocations), planar defects (grain boundaries, surfaces) — and match each to the property it governs: vacancies to diffusion, dislocations to plasticity, boundaries to strength and to creep. Respect the exponential: the vacancy fraction \( e^{-Q_v/kT} \) is astronomically sensitive to temperature — around \( 3\times10^{-5} \) at 1000 K for a typical metal and essentially zero at room temperature — so processes that need vacancies (diffusion, annealing, creep) switch on with heat and freeze out without it. That single exponential explains why heat treatment works at temperature and why parts are stable at service conditions.

The professional reading: defects are not flaws to lament but levers to pull. Carbon interstitials ARE steel; substitutional chromium IS stainless; grain boundaries ARE the strengthening in fine-grained plate. The materials engineer's job is rarely to remove defects — it is to choose which defects, at what population, on purpose. The next two lessons pull exactly those levers.

A note on scale keeps the inventory honest: even a heavily defective crystal is mostly perfect. At the 1000 K figure computed in this lesson's examples, fewer than three sites in a hundred thousand stand vacant — and yet that trace population carries all of diffusion. Materials behavior is routinely governed by its rarest constituents, which is why counting them, rather than rounding them to zero, is a professional skill.

Closing. Point defects carry diffusion, line defects carry plasticity, planar defects referee both. "Defect" is the field's least fortunate word — these are the control knobs.

Lesson 06 · Diffusion

Lecture

§1 · Fick's laws, with atoms attached

Diffusion is atoms hopping site to site, biased downhill in concentration. Steady state is Fick's first law, \( J = -D\, dC/dx \): flux proportional to gradient, \( D \) the diffusion coefficient. Transient cases — the plant's usual reality — obey Fick's second law:

\[ \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2} \tag{1} \]

whose solutions for a surface held at constant concentration follow the error function. The engineering headline hiding in those solutions: any characteristic penetration depth scales as

\[ x \sim \sqrt{Dt} \tag{2} \]

Double the depth costs four times the time. That square root governs carburizing, decarburizing, homogenization, and every soak-time argument you will ever have with a furnace schedule.

§2 · Temperature is the big lever

The diffusion coefficient is Arrhenius:

\[ D = D_0 \exp\!\left(-\frac{Q_d}{RT}\right) \tag{3} \]

with activation energy \( Q_d \) set by the migration mechanism — interstitials like carbon in iron dart between gaps cheaply; substitutional atoms must wait for vacancies and pay more. Because \( T \) sits in an exponential, modest temperature moves swamp heroic time extensions: in §Worked Examples, 200 K of extra furnace temperature buys a factor of ~19 in \( D \), which no affordable soak time matches.

§3 · Reading processes through (2) and (3)

Case-hardening a gear: carbon in from the surface, case depth by \( \sqrt{Dt} \), temperature chosen high enough for speed but below distortion limits. Homogenizing a segregated casting: the same mathematics with the dendrite arm spacing as the distance to erase. Even degradation reads this way — hydrogen charging into a weld, carburization of furnace tubes — diffusion is one mathematics wearing many overalls.

§4 · The diffusion estimating method

Most industrial diffusion questions are answered by one scaling law before any full solution: penetration grows as \( x \sim \sqrt{Dt} \). Run it as a ratio and the unknown constants cancel — if 4 hours of carburizing buys a 1 mm case, a 2 mm case costs \( 2^2 = 4 \) times as long: 16 hours, not 8. The square root is the story: doubling depth always quadruples time, which is why deep cases are expensive and why homogenizing a badly segregated casting can be economically hopeless. Temperature is the other lever, and it is exponential: \( D = D_0 e^{-Q_d/RT} \) — a modest temperature rise can buy what hours of extra soak cannot, which is why furnace setpoints, not timers, dominate diffusion process design.

Keep the mechanisms straight when judging feasibility: small interstitials like carbon hop between gaps and move fast; substitutional atoms must wait for a vacancy to swap with and move orders of magnitude slower at the same temperature. That is why case-hardening with carbon is an overnight job while chromizing is a specialist's slow art — and why "just diffuse it in" is a plan that needs this lesson's arithmetic before it becomes a work order.

The same estimating method audits vendors: a heat-treat certificate quoting an improbably deep case for the stated hours and temperature is either running hotter than declared or measuring generously — and \( \sqrt{Dt} \), evaluated on the back of the certificate itself, is enough to ask the question with numbers instead of suspicion.

Closing. Gradient drives, \( \sqrt{Dt} \) paces, Arrhenius accelerates. Three relations, and most furnace-floor arithmetic is already in hand.

Lesson 07 · Mechanical properties I: elasticity and the tension test

Lecture

§1 · One test to rule them

Pull a standard bar to fracture, record load and elongation, and normalize away the geometry: engineering stress \( \sigma = F/A_0 \), engineering strain \( \epsilon = \Delta L/L_0 \), both referred to original dimensions. The resulting curve is the most quoted document in mechanical engineering, and four numbers summarize it: the elastic modulus \( E \) (initial slope, Hooke's law \( \sigma = E\epsilon \)); the yield strength \( \sigma_y \) (0.2% offset, where permanent set begins); the ultimate tensile strength (the curve's peak); and ductility, as percent elongation or reduction of area.

§2 · What each number is for

\( E \) is a bonding property — recall Lesson 2's well curvature — essentially untouchable by heat treatment: all steels share ~200 GPa. \( \sigma_y \) is the designer's boundary; machines are sized so working stress stays below it with margin. UTS matters for what happens beyond intent — overload margins and, through empirical correlations, hardness checks. Ductility is the forgiveness budget: the plastic reserve that lets a part yield locally, redistribute, and warn before it breaks.

§3 · True stress, and the necking illusion

Past the UTS the engineering curve droops — but the material is not softening. Necking has begun: area shrinks locally, and dividing load by the original area hides it. True stress \( \sigma_T = F/A \) (instantaneous area) keeps rising to fracture; with volume constancy the conversions in the plastic range are

\[ \sigma_T = \sigma(1+\epsilon), \qquad \epsilon_T = \ln(1+\epsilon) \tag{1} \]

Metal forming lives on true quantities — a rolling pass or a drawing die sees real areas, not test-report conventions. §Worked Examples extracts all four report numbers from raw data, then converts a point through (1).

§4 · Reading a tension test like a professional

The tensile curve is read in a fixed order, and each stop answers one design question. The elastic slope first: Young's modulus — and the professional expectation that it barely varies within an alloy family; every carbon steel runs near 200 GPa, so a certificate claiming otherwise indicts the test, not the steel. Yield next, by the 0.2% offset construction when the knee is gradual: draw the line parallel to the elastic slope from \( \varepsilon = 0.002 \) and read the intersection — this is the number design codes divide by safety factors. Ultimate strength marks where necking begins: beyond it, the ENGINEERING curve droops only because the true area is shrinking faster than the load — the material itself is still hardening, and knowing that resolves the classic "why does the curve fall?" confusion. Ductility last: percent elongation, the formability budget manufacturing will spend.

The working method for any calculation is unit discipline on \( \sigma = F/A_0 \) and \( \varepsilon = \Delta L/L_0 \): kilonewtons over square millimetres give megapascals directly, and strain is always dimensionless — a strain quoted in millimetres is a gauge length away from being a number. Verify computed moduli against the family expectation before reporting; the check costs nothing and catches decimal slips every time.

Closing. One bar, one pull, four numbers, and one conversion that keeps the story honest after necking. Every mill certificate you will ever read is this lesson in tabular form.

Engineering to true

Lesson 08 · Mechanical properties II: hardness and toughness

Lecture

§1 · Hardness: the field's favorite proxy

Hardness is resistance to localized plastic indentation — push a standard indenter with a standard load, measure the dent. Brinell (10 mm ball, the impression's diameter), Vickers (diamond pyramid, works from foils to carbides on one scale), Rockwell (depth-based, direct-reading, the production floor's default). Conversions between scales are empirical, tabulated, and alloy-specific — treat cross-scale comparisons as estimates, not measurements.

§2 · Why hardness tracks strength — and when it lies

An indentation is a small, constrained plasticity experiment, so hardness rides the same dislocation resistance as yield strength. For steels the workhorse correlation is

\[ UTS\ (\mathrm{MPa}) \approx 3.45 \times HB \tag{1} \]

good enough to audit a heat treatment without cutting the part — which is precisely how the field uses it. The proxy lies when the assumptions break: heavily cold-worked surfaces, decarburized skins, coarse multi-phase structures sampled by a small indenter, or non-ferrous alloys wearing a steel correlation. Hardness answers "what is the strength here, at this spot" — sometimes that locality is the feature, sometimes the trap.

§3 · Toughness, and the transition that sank ships

Strength says what stress a material bears; toughness says what energy it absorbs breaking — measured crudely and usefully by the Charpy impact test: a notched bar, a swinging pendulum, joules read off the follower. BCC steels hold an unpleasant surprise: impact energy collapses below a ductile–brittle transition temperature, Lesson 3's thermally-assisted slip arriving as fracture. Liberty ships cracked in cold seas on exactly this physics. FCC metals — austenitic stainless, aluminum, copper — have no transition, which is why cryogenic plant is built from them.

§4 · Choosing and converting hardness numbers honestly

Hardness testing is the plant's favorite mechanical test because it is fast, cheap, and almost nondestructive — but its numbers demand method. Choose the scale by the job: Brinell's big ball averages over coarse structures (castings, weld HAZ surveys); Vickers's diamond reads thin cases and small zones; Rockwell's direct-reading dial rules production floors. Convert with respect: scales measure different deformations, so conversions between them are empirical tables, not algebra — and the steel-only estimate \( UTS \approx 3.45 \times HB \) is a field tool for triage, never a substitute for a tensile certificate when the number matters contractually.

Toughness closes the lesson because it is the property strength tests miss: the area under the stress–strain curve — strength AND ductility multiplied into energy. Two materials of equal strength can differ tenfold in toughness, and the Charpy machine exists to catch the difference under shock, including the ductile-to-brittle transition that ambushes BCC steels in cold service. The working rule: specify strength for the load, hardness for the wear, toughness for the accident — and never let one number impersonate the other two. And when a service temperature dips below freezing, ask the Charpy question explicitly: transition behavior is found by testing across temperatures, never by extrapolating a single room-temperature result.

Closing. Hardness is portable strength, within its correlation's jurisdiction; Charpy is energy accounting with a notch. Between them they answer the floor's two commonest questions: is it hard enough, and will it shatter when the weather turns.

Lesson 09 · Dislocations and strengthening

Lecture

§1 · One enemy, many obstacles

Plastic flow is dislocation traffic (Lesson 5), so all strengthening is one strategy: obstruct the traffic. Every industrial method is a different obstacle wearing a different price tag — and each purchase of strength is paid for, almost always, in ductility. Four mechanisms cover the metallurgy you will meet in practice.

§2 · The four mechanisms

Grain refinement. A boundary forces slip to renegotiate direction; more boundaries, more negotiations. Quantitatively, the Hall–Petch relation:

\[ \sigma_y = \sigma_0 + k_y\, d^{-1/2} \tag{1} \]

— fine grains strengthen, and (uniquely among the four) usually improve toughness too, which is why fine grain is the closest metallurgy comes to a free lunch. Solid solution. Solute atoms strain the lattice locally; dislocations wade through the strain fields. Every alloying addition does some of this. Strain hardening. Deform the metal and dislocations multiply until they obstruct each other — quantified by percent cold work, spent by every rolling pass and drawing die. Precipitation. Fine second-phase particles force dislocations to cut or bow around them; the strongest aluminum alloys and nickel superalloys live here.

§3 · Choosing among them

The mechanisms stack — a drawn, alloyed, fine-grained wire uses three at once — but they differ in temperature stability (strain hardening anneals away; precipitates coarsen), in cost, and in what they do to toughness and conductivity. A copper conductor is the cautionary tale: solutes that strengthen it also scatter electrons, so cable copper is strengthened by drawing, not doping. §Worked Examples prices mechanism one with real Hall–Petch constants and mechanism three on a drawn rod.

§4 · The strengthening decision method

Every strengthening mechanism is the same sentence with a different subject: something obstructs dislocations. The engineering skill is choosing WHICH something, and the choice runs on three questions. What temperature will the part see? Strain hardening anneals away — it cannot serve hot; grain-boundary strengthening fades as boundaries begin to slide at creep temperatures; solid-solution and precipitation strengthening hold higher. What process route is available? Cold work arrives free with rolling and drawing; fine grain arrives through controlled rolling and normalizing; precipitates demand the full solution-quench-age cycle of the heat-treatment lessons ahead. What does the trade cost? Every mechanism buys strength with ductility — the strengthened metal forms less willingly, and a drawing schedule that ignores this snaps wire.

Run Hall–Petch as the worked example of quantitative thinking: \( \sigma_y = \sigma_0 + k\,d^{-1/2} \) turns a micrograph's grain size into a yield prediction, and its inverse square root explains why halving grain size buys a meaningful but diminishing return. The mechanisms stack — commercial alloys routinely deploy three at once — and reading a datasheet is recognizing which levers its producer pulled. A dual-phase automotive steel, for instance, runs fine grains, solute, and a hard second phase simultaneously; its certificate is a three-lever chord, and hearing the chord is what this lesson trains.

Closing. Strength is obstructed dislocation motion — by boundaries, solutes, tangles, or particles. Name the obstacle in any datasheet's "condition" column and you have read the alloy's biography.

Lesson 10 · Recovery, recrystallization, and grain growth

Lecture

§1 · What cold work leaves behind

A cold-worked metal is a loaded spring at the microscale: dislocation density up a thousandfold, grains flattened and rotated, a few percent of the deformation energy locked in as internal strain. That stored energy is the driving force for everything in this lesson — heat the metal and it will spend that energy reorganizing, in three acts.

§2 · Recovery, recrystallization, grain growth

Recovery (lowest temperatures): dislocations climb, annihilate, and tidy into low-energy walls. Residual stress falls and conductivity returns, but strength barely moves — the stress-relief anneal lives here. Recrystallization: new, strain-free grains nucleate in the deformed matrix and consume it wholesale; strength drops toward the annealed baseline and ductility floods back. The temperature where this completes in about an hour — the recrystallization temperature — runs roughly

\[ T_R \approx 0.4\, T_m \quad (\mathrm{in\ kelvins}) \tag{1} \]

and falls with heavier prior cold work (more stored energy, more driving force). Grain growth: left hot after recrystallization finishes, boundaries migrate to cut boundary area, grains coarsen, and Hall–Petch (Lesson 9) runs in reverse. Annealing schedules end when recrystallization does, not later, for exactly this reason.

§3 · Hot working, defined properly

Hot working is deformation above the recrystallization temperature — the metal recrystallizes as fast as the rolls harden it, so it never accumulates cold work. Cold working is deformation below \( T_R \). The boundary is homologous, not absolute: by (1), lead recrystallizes below room temperature — a fitter bending lead sheet is hot-working it at 25 °C — while tungsten at a red-hot 800 °C can still be cold-working. "Hot" belongs to the metal's melting point, not the thermometer.

§4 · The annealing decision method

Cold work and annealing form a cycle the mill walks deliberately, and the walking has rules. Track the stored energy: each cold pass banks dislocations, raising strength and dropping ductility toward the cracking threshold; the annealing furnace spends that stored energy on new, strain-free grains. Locate the temperature honestly: recrystallization begins near \( 0.4\,T_m \) in kelvins — the boundary that separates cold work from hot work by definition, not by whether the shop felt warm. Below it, recovery alone relaxes residual stresses while keeping most of the strength: the stress-relief anneal that fabricators order by that exact name. Respect the clock after the reset: grain growth never stops at temperature — \( d^2 - d_0^2 = Kt \) keeps consuming boundaries — so an over-soaked anneal trades the fine grain the previous lesson prized for coarse, weak structure. Schedules end on time for a reason.

The method in practice: choose the cold-work total for the final strength, insert intermediate anneals where cumulative reduction approaches the alloy's formability limit, and finish either work-hardened (springy temper) or annealed (soft temper) to order. Temper designations on certificates are precisely this cycle's bookkeeping.

Closing. Stored strain energy drives three acts — tidy, replace, coarsen — and \( 0.4\,T_m \) marks the stage door. Every anneal on every line is a decision about which act to stop after.

Problem. An annealed sheet enters a soak with 20 µm grains. Grain growth follows \( d^2 - d_0^2 = Kt \) with \( K = 0.12\ \mathrm{\mu m^2/s} \) at the soak temperature. Find: the grain size after a 1-hour hold, and comment via Hall–Petch.

Solution. (1) \( d^2 = 20^2 + 0.12 \times 3600 = 400 + 432 = 832\ \mathrm{\mu m^2} \). (2) \( d = \sqrt{832} = 28.8\ \mathrm{\mu m} \). (3) Hall–Petch: \( d^{-1/2} \) falls from 224 to 186 \( \mathrm{m^{-1/2}} \) — roughly a sixth of the grain-boundary strengthening quietly gone in one hour.

Answer: ≈29 µm after the hour — the fine grain is dissolving into the soak. Sanity check: the parabolic law decelerates (coarser structures have less boundary energy to spend), but it never stops at temperature — which is why annealing schedules end on time, not on convenience, and why "we left it in over the break" is a metallurgical event, not a scheduling footnote.

MCQ 2

A stress-relief anneal that must not soften the part should stop at:

  1. recovery ✓
  2. recrystallization
  3. grain growth
  4. melting

Recovery lets dislocations tidy into low-energy arrangements — residual stress falls, conductivity returns, but strength barely moves. Recrystallization replaces the strengthened structure wholesale and erases cold work; grain growth then makes things worse. Anneal schedules are decisions about which act to stop after.

MCQ 3

The practical rule-of-thumb for where recrystallization begins is:

  1. About 0.4 of the melting point in kelvins ✓
  2. Exactly 500 °C for all metals
  3. Just below the melting point
  4. Room temperature, given time

\( T_{rx} \approx 0.4\,T_m \) (absolute) — which is why lead recrystallizes on the bench (its 0.4 Tm is below room temperature) while steel needs a furnace. MCQ 4

A fitter bends lead sheet at room temperature. Metallurgically, this is:

  1. cold working
  2. hot working — room temperature is above lead's \( T_R \) ✓
  3. impossible without heating
  4. precipitation hardening

MCQ 5

What drives grain growth during a long high-temperature soak?

  1. Reduction of total grain-boundary energy ✓
  2. Gravity settling of grains
  3. Chemical reaction with the furnace air
  4. Leftover casting stresses

Boundaries carry energy per area; coarsening trades many small grains for fewer large ones and shrinks the total boundary area — thermodynamics collecting its due. That is why growth never stops at temperature, only slows as the driving energy dwindles (the parabolic law), and why time at temperature is a controlled quantity on every annealing schedule rather than a rounding detail.

Lesson 11 · Solidification and cast structure

Lecture

§1 · Nucleation: the reluctant start

A melt below its freezing point does not freeze at once — a solid nucleus must first reach a critical size before growing is cheaper than redissolving. The balance of surface energy paid against volume energy won sets the critical radius:

\[ r^* = \frac{2\gamma T_m}{\Delta H_f\, \Delta T} \tag{1} \]

— inversely proportional to the undercooling \( \Delta T \). Homogeneous nucleation needs implausible undercoolings; real melts nucleate heterogeneously on mold walls and inoculant particles, which is why foundries seed melts deliberately: more nuclei, finer grains, better castings.

§2 · The three zones of a casting

Read a sectioned billet or ingot and solidification history is written in its grains. Chill zone: a skin of fine equiaxed grains, nucleated in a rush against the cold mold. Columnar zone: long grains growing inward along the heat-flow direction, competing until the best-aligned survive. Central equiaxed zone: grains nucleated in the cooling interior liquid. The proportions are process knobs — pouring superheat, mold cooling, stirring — and they matter because columnar structures are anisotropic and their convergence planes are weak.

§3 · Segregation: solidification's chemical fingerprint

Solids accept less solute than liquids, so a growing crystal rejects solute into the melt ahead of it. At dendrite scale this leaves microsegregation — solute-lean dendrite cores, solute-rich interdendritic pockets, quantified by the lever rule on the phase diagram and erasable only by homogenization (Lesson 6's \( \sqrt{Dt} \), with the arm spacing as the distance). At full-ingot scale, macrosegregation — centerline enrichment in continuously cast products — cannot be diffused away at any industrial timescale; it must be managed in the caster. Coring survives because solid-state diffusion is slow: the fingerprint outlives the process that made it.

§4 · Reading a casting's history from its structure

A sectioned casting is a recording of its own solidification, and the working method is to read it zone by zone. The chill zone at the surface — fine random grains — says the mold quenched the first metal fast. The columnar zone — long grains aimed at the center — says heat left through the walls, and its aligned boundaries are weak paths that matter for load direction. The equiaxed center says nucleation won the race in the last, slowest-cooling liquid. Alongside the zones, read the defects as verbs: centerline shrinkage means feeding failed; segregation bands mean solute was pushed ahead of the fronts; gas porosity's round voids mean dissolved gas came out of solution.

Then run the arithmetic that controls all of it: Chvorinov's rule, \( t = B(V/A)^2 \), prices each region's solidification time by its modulus \( V/A \) — and the whole craft of risering is making the riser's modulus larger than the casting's, so the riser freezes LAST and feeds shrinkage instead of hosting it. The same square law says thin sections freeze fast and fine, heavy bosses freeze slow and coarse — so properties vary across one casting by design necessity, a fact the machine-design courses will inherit and respect.

Closing. Nucleation sets the grain count, growth writes the three zones, rejection writes the chemistry. A casting's cross-section is a recording — this lesson teaches you to play it back.

SOLVE 1

The critical nucleus for iron

Problem. For pure iron (representative values, Callister & Rethwisch): \( \gamma = 0.255\ \mathrm{J/m^2} \), \( \Delta H_f = 1.85 \times 10^9\ \mathrm{J/m^3} \), \( T_m = 1811\ \mathrm{K} \), homogeneous undercooling \( \Delta T = 295\ \mathrm{K} \). Find: \( r^* \).

Solution. (1) \( r^* = \dfrac{2 \times 0.255 \times 1811}{1.85 \times 10^9 \times 295} \). (2) Numerator: 923.6; denominator: \( 5.46 \times 10^{11} \). (3) \( r^* = 1.69 \times 10^{-9}\ \mathrm{m} \approx 1.7\ \mathrm{nm} \).

Answer: ≈1.7 nm — a cluster of only a few thousand atoms. Sanity check: the required undercooling, ~295 K, is enormous — real ladles never see it, confirming §1: industrial solidification is heterogeneous, nucleating on walls and particles at a few degrees of undercooling instead.

SOLVE 2

Lever rule on a freezing alloy

Problem. A Cu–35 wt% Ni alloy sits in the two-phase field where the liquid contains 32 wt% Ni and the solid 43 wt% Ni (compositions from the Cu–Ni diagram, Callister & Rethwisch). Find: the phase fractions.

Solution. (1) Lever rule, liquid fraction: \( W_L = \dfrac{C_s - C_0}{C_s - C_L} = \dfrac{43 - 35}{43 - 32} = \dfrac{8}{11} = 0.73 \). (2) Solid: \( W_S = \dfrac{35 - 32}{11} = 0.27 \). Sum: 1.00. ✓

Answer: 73% liquid, 27% solid. Sanity check: the first solid (43% Ni) is markedly richer than the average (35%) — the rejection of §3 in numbers. Freeze fast and that difference is buried as coring in every dendrite; the lever rule is segregation's arithmetic before it becomes homogenization's problem.

SOLVE 3

Sizing the freeze with Chvorinov

Problem. A plate casting has volume-to-area modulus \( V/A = 0.02\ \mathrm{m} \), and the mold's Chvorinov constant is \( B = 2.5\times10^{6}\ \mathrm{s/m^2} \). Find: the solidification time, and the modulus a riser needs to freeze after the casting.

Solution. (1) \( t = B\,(V/A)^2 = 2.5\times10^{6} \times (0.02)^2 = 2.5\times10^{6} \times 4\times10^{-4} = 1000\ \mathrm{s} \approx 17\ \mathrm{min} \). (2) A riser must freeze later: its modulus must exceed the casting's — practice sizes risers at \( (V/A)_{riser} \approx 1.2 \times (V/A)_{casting} = 0.024\ \mathrm{m} \), giving \( t \approx 1440\ \mathrm{s} \), a 44% margin.

Answer: ≈1000 s (17 min); riser modulus ≥ ~0.024 m. Sanity check: the square law means the 20% modulus margin becomes a 44% time margin \( (1.2^2 = 1.44) \) — geometry's gift to the riser designer, and the reason modulus, not volume, is the quantity risering tables tabulate.

MCQ 3

Per Chvorinov's rule, a riser feeds a casting successfully when the riser has:

  1. A larger volume-to-area modulus than the casting ✓
  2. A larger volume, regardless of shape

MCQ 5

The long, aligned grains found just inside a casting's surface are the:

  2. Chill zone
