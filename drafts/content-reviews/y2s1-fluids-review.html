Lesson 01 · Fluid properties

Lecture

§1 · What a fluid is, and the properties that matter

A fluid is a substance that cannot resist a shear stress at rest — it deforms continuously under any tangential force, however small. That single fact separates fluid mechanics from solid mechanics and forces a different toolkit. Before any flow is analysed, four bulk properties must be pinned down. Density \( \rho \) (mass per unit volume, kg/m³) sets inertia and weight; specific weight \( \gamma=\rho g \) converts it to force per volume; specific gravity \( SG=\rho/\rho_{\text{water}} \) is the dimensionless ratio to water at 4°C; and viscosity \( \mu \) measures a fluid's resistance to shearing. Water has \( \rho\approx1000 \) kg/m³, so \( \gamma\approx9810 \) N/m³; an oil of \( \rho=850 \) has \( SG=0.85 \).

§2 · Viscosity, defined by Newton's law

Viscosity is defined operationally. Place a fluid between two plates and slide the top one: the fluid develops a velocity gradient \( du/dy \), and the shear stress needed is \( \tau=\mu\,\dfrac{du}{dy} \) — Newton's law of viscosity. Fluids obeying it with constant \( \mu \) are Newtonian (water, air, light oils); those whose apparent viscosity changes with shear rate (paint, slurry, blood) are non-Newtonian. For \( \mu=0.1 \) Pa·s and a gradient of \( 50\ \text{s}^{-1} \), the shear stress is \( \tau=0.1\times50=5 \) Pa. Viscosity is the property that makes real flow lose energy; an idealised inviscid fluid (\( \mu=0 \)) would flow forever without friction, a useful fiction for some problems and a dangerous one for others.

§3 · Kinematic viscosity and the role of temperature

Two viscosities appear in practice. The dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) (Pa·s) enters force balances; the kinematic viscosity \( \nu=\mu/\rho \) (m²/s) enters the Reynolds number and diffusion of momentum. For the oil above, \( \nu=0.1/850\approx1.18\times10^{-4} \) m²/s. Temperature dependence runs opposite ways for the two states of matter that concern us: liquids thin dramatically as they warm (their molecules escape each other's attraction), while gases thicken slightly as they warm (faster molecules transfer more momentum across streamlines). An engineer sizing a pump for cold-start oil and hot running oil is really sizing for two different fluids.

§4 · The property-first working method

The discipline this lesson installs is to fix properties, with units, before touching a flow equation. Read \( \rho \) at the operating temperature, not a handbook default; compute \( \gamma=\rho g \) only when a weight or pressure is wanted; carry \( \mu \) and \( \nu \) separately and know which one a formula expects. Units are the audit: \( \tau=\mu\,du/dy \) must come out in pascals, and if it does not, a property is in the wrong units. Every later lesson — statics through compressible flow — takes these four numbers as given, so an error here propagates through the whole course. A further property, bulk modulus \( K=-V\,dp/dV \), measures resistance to compression and decides whether a fluid may be treated as incompressible: liquids have enormous \(K\) and are effectively incompressible below high pressures, while gases are compressible whenever speeds approach the speed of sound (Lesson 11). Get the fluid right first — its density, its viscosity, and whether it compresses — then analyse its motion.

Lesson 02 · Pressure and fluid statics

Lecture

§1 · Pressure and the hydrostatic law

In a fluid at rest there is no shear, so the only stress is pressure, acting equally in all directions at a point (Pascal's principle). The one equation that governs a static fluid is the hydrostatic law: pressure increases with depth as \( \dfrac{dp}{dz}=-\rho g \), which for a constant-density fluid integrates to \( p=p_0+\rho g h \), where \(h\) is depth below the reference surface. Ten metres of water therefore adds \( \rho g h=1000\times9.81\times10=98{,}100 \) Pa \(=98.1\) kPa — close to one atmosphere for every ten metres, a number worth memorising.

§2 · Gauge, absolute, and vacuum

Pressure is quoted against a reference. Absolute pressure is measured from a perfect vacuum; gauge pressure is measured from local atmospheric, \( p_{\text{abs}}=p_{\text{atm}}+p_{\text{gauge}} \). A depth reading of 98.1 kPa gauge is \( 101.325+98.1=199.4 \) kPa absolute. Confusing the two is a classic and consequential error — pump suction limits, boiling points, and gas-law calculations all demand absolute pressure, while structural loads on a wall care only about the gauge difference across it. State which you mean, every time.

§3 · Manometers and pressure measurement

The hydrostatic law turns a column of liquid into a pressure gauge. In a manometer, a pressure difference is read as a height difference: \( \Delta p=\rho g\,\Delta h \). Because \(\rho\) sits in the formula, a dense fluid gives a short, readable column — mercury (\( \rho=13{,}600 \) kg/m³) reads 50 kPa as only \( \Delta h=50{,}000/(13{,}600\times9.81)\approx0.375 \) m, whereas water would need over five metres. The same law gives the force and centre of pressure on a submerged gate: pressure grows linearly with depth, so the resultant acts below the centroid, a fact that decides how dam gates and tank walls are hinged.

§4 · The statics working method

The procedure is uniform. Choose a reference surface where the pressure is known (often the free surface at atmospheric). Move to the point of interest along a connected body of the same fluid, adding \( \rho g h \) for every descent and subtracting it for every rise. Switch \(\rho\) whenever the fluid changes (oil over water, gas over liquid). Keep gauge and absolute straight, and let units confirm the arithmetic — \( \rho g h \) must land in pascals. This "walk the column" method solves every manometer and submerged-surface problem without new equations, and it is the same energy-accounting habit that Bernoulli's equation extends to moving fluids in the next lessons. Two extensions of the static law are worth flagging now. Buoyancy — Archimedes' principle — is nothing more than the net of hydrostatic pressure over a submerged body: the upward force equals the weight of displaced fluid, \( F_B=\rho g V_{\text{disp}} \), which is why steel ships float and why a partly filled tank's contents shift the centre of buoyancy. And the force on a plane submerged surface is the pressure at its centroid times its area, applied at the centre of pressure below the centroid — the calculation behind every tank wall, lock gate, and dam face. Both follow from \( p=p_0+\rho g h \) with no new physics, only careful bookkeeping of where the pressure acts.

Lesson 03 · Flow description, Reynolds number, and continuity

Lecture

§1 · Describing flow, and the master number

Once a fluid moves, the first questions are how fast, how much, and in what regime. Flow rate comes in two forms: volume flow \( Q=AV \) (m³/s) and mass flow \( \dot m=\rho Q=\rho AV \) (kg/s), where \(V\) is the average velocity over cross-section \(A\). The regime — smooth laminar layers or chaotic turbulent mixing — is set by the single most important dimensionless group in the subject, the Reynolds number \( Re=\dfrac{\rho V D}{\mu}=\dfrac{V D}{\nu} \), the ratio of inertial to viscous forces. In a pipe, \( Re<2300 \) is laminar, \( Re>4000 \) turbulent, with a transitional band between.

§2 · Reading the Reynolds number

For water (\( \nu\approx10^{-6} \) m²/s) at \( V=2 \) m/s in a \( D=0.05 \) m pipe, \( Re=VD/\nu=2\times0.05/10^{-6}=100{,}000 \) — firmly turbulent, as almost all practical water pipes are. The power of \(Re\) is that it collapses geometry, speed, and fluid into one number: two completely different flows with the same \(Re\) behave alike, which is the basis of model testing (Lesson 6). Low \(Re\) means viscosity dominates and disturbances are damped; high \(Re\) means inertia dominates and small disturbances grow into turbulence.

§3 · Continuity: mass is conserved

Mass cannot accumulate in a rigid pipe running full and steady, so what enters must leave: \( \rho_1 A_1 V_1=\rho_2 A_2 V_2 \), and for an incompressible fluid \( A_1 V_1=A_2 V_2 \) — the continuity equation. A pipe of \( D=0.05 \) m carrying \( V=2 \) m/s passes \( Q=AV=\frac{\pi}{4}(0.05)^2\times2\approx3.93\times10^{-3} \) m³/s. Halve the diameter and the area falls fourfold, so continuity forces the velocity to quadruple: \( V_2=V_1(D_1/D_2)^2=2\times4=8 \) m/s. Continuity is why a nozzle speeds flow up and a diffuser slows it down, with no energy added — purely geometry conserving mass.

§4 · The flow-description working method

Given any internal flow: first compute \( Re \) to fix the regime, because laminar and turbulent flows obey different friction and profile laws and mixing them is a common error. Then apply continuity to relate velocities at different sections — it is an accounting identity that holds regardless of losses, so it is always available and always exact for incompressible steady flow. Keep \( Q \) (what a meter reads) and \( V \) (what the physics uses) distinct, linked by \(Q=AV\). These two tools — a regime number and a mass balance — precede every energy analysis, and they are exactly what Bernoulli's equation in Lesson 4 builds upon. It also helps to distinguish the flow's character in time and space: a steady flow has properties that do not change with time at a fixed point (most pipe systems at their operating point), while an unsteady flow does (a valve slamming, a pump starting). A flow is uniform if velocity does not change along the path and developing if it does, as near a pipe entrance where the velocity profile is still forming. These distinctions decide which simplified equations are legitimate: the clean continuity and Bernoulli forms used throughout this course assume steady, and usually fully-developed, conditions, and applying them to a genuinely unsteady flow is a category error, not a small approximation.

Lesson 04 · Bernoulli, energy equation, pipe flow, and pump curves

Lecture

§1 · Energy along a streamline

Bernoulli's equation is the energy balance of an ideal flow: for steady, incompressible, frictionless flow along a streamline, \( \dfrac{p}{\rho g}+\dfrac{V^2}{2g}+z=\text{constant} \). Each term is a head, an energy per unit weight measured in metres: pressure head \( p/\rho g \), velocity head \( V^2/2g \), and elevation head \(z\). The statement is that these three trade against one another but their sum is conserved — speed a fluid up and its pressure must fall, raise it and something else must give. It is Lesson 2's hydrostatic accounting extended to a moving fluid.

§2 · Reading the trades

The velocity head is where intuition often fails, so anchor it numerically: water at \( V=2 \) m/s carries a velocity head \( V^2/2g=4/(2\times9.81)\approx0.204 \) m and a dynamic pressure \( \tfrac12\rho V^2=\tfrac12\times1000\times2^2=2000 \) Pa. Torricelli's result — the speed of a jet draining under a head \(h\) — falls straight out by setting pressure and velocity heads equal: \( V=\sqrt{2gh} \), so a 5-metre head drives \( V=\sqrt{2\times9.81\times5}\approx9.9 \) m/s. The same balance explains why a venturi throat (small area, high speed) shows low pressure, the principle behind carburettors, flow meters, and aspirators.

§3 · The real energy equation: adding losses and machines

Real flow has friction and often a pump or turbine, so Bernoulli is generalised to the steady-flow energy equation: \( \dfrac{p_1}{\rho g}+\dfrac{V_1^2}{2g}+z_1+h_{\text{pump}}=\dfrac{p_2}{\rho g}+\dfrac{V_2^2}{2g}+z_2+h_{\text{turbine}}+h_{\text{loss}} \). The head loss \( h_{\text{loss}} \) collects pipe friction (major losses) and fittings (minor losses); \( h_{\text{pump}} \) is the head a pump adds. A pump's required head and the system's demand meet at the operating point where the pump curve (head falling as flow rises) crosses the system curve (head rising as flow rises) — the single most important reading in rotating-equipment work, developed fully in Lesson 10.

§4 · The Bernoulli working method

Apply it disciplined: pick two points on the same streamline where you know the most, write the full energy equation, and cancel what is genuinely zero — an open surface has gauge pressure zero, a large reservoir has negligible velocity, a horizontal run drops the \(z\) terms. Never drop friction on a long pipe or a machine that is present; ideal Bernoulli is a first estimate, not a licence to ignore losses. Confirm the trade makes physical sense — pressure should fall where the fluid speeds up — and check units, since every head is in metres. This energy accounting, together with continuity and the Reynolds number, closes the toolkit for the momentum, loss, and turbomachinery lessons that follow. One caution completes the picture: Bernoulli is an energy statement along a streamline and says nothing about forces on a bend or a vane — for those, the momentum equation of Lesson 5 is required, and the two are complementary rather than interchangeable. Nor does Bernoulli survive a hydraulic jump, a shock, or any abrupt loss, where mechanical energy is dissipated to heat and the constant on the right-hand side simply drops. Knowing exactly which assumptions are in force — steady, incompressible, frictionless, one streamline — is what separates a correct application from a plausible-looking wrong one, and it is worth restating those four words each time the equation is written.

Lesson 05 · Momentum analysis

Lecture

§1 · Why energy is not enough

Bernoulli's equation tracks energy, but it says nothing about the forces a moving fluid exerts on the hardware that guides it. To hold a bend in place, size the bolts on a nozzle, or find the thrust of a jet, a different conservation law is needed: linear momentum. Newton's second law for a control volume states that the net external force equals the rate at which momentum leaves minus the rate at which it enters: \( \sum\vec F=\dot m\,(\vec V_{\text{out}}-\vec V_{\text{in}}) \) for steady flow with one inlet and one outlet, where \( \dot m=\rho Q \). It is a vector equation, so direction is everything.

§2 · The control-volume method

The procedure is disciplined. Draw a control volume enclosing the device, mark every force crossing its boundary — pressure on the cut faces, the reaction from the wall, and weight — and mark the momentum flux \( \dot m\vec V \) at each opening. Then write \( \sum F_x=\dot m(V_{2x}-V_{1x}) \) in each direction separately. For a jet of water at \( V=20 \) m/s carrying \( Q=0.01 \) m³/s (so \( \dot m=\rho Q=10 \) kg/s) striking a fixed flat plate normally and spreading sideways, the plate must supply a force \( F=\dot m\,V=10\times20=200 \) N to destroy the incoming axial momentum. The plate feels an equal and opposite push — the physics behind a Pelton turbine bucket and a firefighter's hose reaction.

§3 · Bends, nozzles, and reversals

Direction changes multiply the force. A pipe bend that turns the flow through 180°, reversing the velocity from \(+V\) to \(-V\), changes momentum by \( \dot m(-V-V)=-2\dot m V \), so the anchor must resist \( 2\dot m V=2\times10\times20=400 \) N — twice the flat-plate value, because the fluid's momentum is not merely stopped but reversed. Pressure forces on the cut faces add to this and often dominate in a pressurised line, which is why large pipe bends sit on massive concrete thrust blocks. The momentum equation, not Bernoulli, sizes those blocks.

§4 · The momentum working method

Momentum and energy are complementary, never interchangeable: energy (Bernoulli) gives pressures and velocities; momentum gives forces. Solve them in that order — use continuity and Bernoulli first to find the velocities at each opening, then feed those into the momentum equation for the force. Keep the sign convention fixed by choosing a positive direction and holding it; a dropped sign turns a 400 N restraint into a 0 N one. Include every force on the control surface, especially the pressure terms that beginners forget. Done carefully, one control-volume sketch replaces a great deal of intuition, and it is the same accounting the rotating-equipment and turbomachinery lessons use to find blade and impeller loads. A subtlety worth naming: if the control volume itself moves — a vane on a turbine wheel travelling at speed \(U\) — the momentum flux is computed with the relative velocity \(V-U\), not the absolute jet speed, because it is the relative approach that the moving surface actually turns. This single correction is the difference between a stationary-plate calculation and a working turbine analysis, and forgetting it overstates the force on every moving blade. Fixed surface: use \(V\); moving surface: use \(V-U\). With that distinction in hand, the control-volume method scales from a garden hose to a hydro-turbine without any new equation.

Lesson 06 · Dimensional analysis and similarity

Lecture

§1 · Why make things dimensionless

Experiments are expensive and variables are many. Dimensional analysis cuts both by grouping physical variables into a smaller set of dimensionless numbers, so a single curve replaces a family of tests. The tool is the Buckingham Pi theorem: a relationship among \(n\) physical variables involving \(k\) independent base dimensions (mass, length, time…) reduces to \( n-k \) independent dimensionless groups, the "Pi" groups. Fewer knobs means fewer experiments and a result that transfers across scales and fluids.

§2 · Buckingham Pi in practice

Take the drag force \(F\) on a sphere, which depends on velocity \(V\), diameter \(D\), density \( \rho \), and viscosity \( \mu \): that is \(n=5\) variables in \(k=3\) dimensions (M, L, T), so \( n-k=2 \) dimensionless groups. Choosing repeating variables and combining, they come out as the drag coefficient \( C_D=\dfrac{F}{\tfrac12\rho V^2 A} \) and the Reynolds number \( Re=\rho VD/\mu \). The entire physics collapses to one relationship \( C_D=f(Re) \) — a single experimental curve valid for any sphere in any Newtonian fluid, which is exactly the drag chart every design handbook prints.

§3 · Similarity and model testing

The same theorem underwrites scale-model testing. A model reproduces the prototype's behaviour when three similarities hold: geometric (same shape, scaled lengths), kinematic (same velocity-pattern shape), and dynamic (matching the governing dimensionless groups). For a fully submerged flow dominated by viscosity, dynamic similarity means matching the Reynolds number: \( Re_{\text{model}}=Re_{\text{prototype}} \). A quarter-scale model in the same fluid must therefore run four times faster (\( V_m=V_p\,D_p/D_m=4V_p \)) to keep \(Re\) equal — a demand that often forces a bigger tunnel or a different fluid, and explains why some flows are tested in pressurised or cryogenic wind tunnels.

§4 · The similarity working method

To plan a test: list the variables, count the base dimensions, and read off \( n-k \) groups; then identify which group governs the physics (Reynolds for viscous flows, Froude for free-surface flows, Mach for compressible ones) and match it between model and prototype. Report results as dimensionless curves, not raw numbers, so they generalise. The discipline pays twice: it slashes the experimental matrix, and it is the only rigorous way to carry a wind-tunnel or water-channel result onto a full-scale machine. Where two groups cannot be matched at once — the classic ship-model conflict between Reynolds and Froude — the engineer must know which one dominates and correct for the other, a judgement this method makes explicit rather than hidden. It is worth naming the common groups, because each encodes a force ratio and each rules a different flow: Reynolds (inertia vs viscosity) for internal and submerged flows, Froude (inertia vs gravity) for open channels and ship hulls, Mach (speed vs sound speed) for compressible gas flow, Weber (inertia vs surface tension) for droplets and sprays, and Euler or the pressure coefficient for cavitation and pressure-driven problems. Recognising which of these governs a given situation is the real skill — the algebra of forming the groups is mechanical, but choosing the one to match is engineering judgement, and it decides whether a scale test means anything at all.

Lesson 07 · Laminar and turbulent pipe flow in depth

Lecture

§1 · Head loss in a real pipe

Real pipe flow loses mechanical energy to friction, and that loss is the major head loss, given by the Darcy–Weisbach equation \( h_f=f\,\dfrac{L}{D}\,\dfrac{V^2}{2g} \). Here \(L/D\) is the pipe's length-to-diameter ratio, \(V^2/2g\) is the velocity head, and \(f\) is the dimensionless Darcy friction factor that carries all the physics of the wall and the regime. Everything in sizing a pump against a pipe run reduces to finding \(f\) and reading off \( h_f \).

§2 · Laminar versus turbulent friction

In laminar flow (\( Re<2300 \)) the friction factor is exact and simple: \( f=64/Re \). At \( Re=1000 \), \( f=64/1000=0.064 \) — friction falls as flow speeds up, because a faster laminar flow is proportionally less retarded. In turbulent flow the wall's roughness matters and \(f\) no longer has a tidy formula; it is read from the Moody chart or computed from the Colebrook equation as a function of \(Re\) and relative roughness \( \varepsilon/D \). The transition between them is abrupt and the two laws are genuinely different — using the laminar \(64/Re\) in a turbulent pipe understates friction badly.

§3 · A worked head loss

Take water at \( V=2 \) m/s in a \( D=0.1 \) m, \( L=100 \) m pipe with a turbulent friction factor \( f=0.02 \). The velocity head is \( V^2/2g=4/19.62\approx0.204 \) m and \( L/D=1000 \), so \( h_f=0.02\times1000\times0.204\approx4.08 \) m. That head — over four metres of water — is what a pump must supply just to overcome friction in this single run, before lifting the fluid or pressurising anything. The strong dependence on velocity (\( h_f\propto V^2 \)) is why oversizing pipe diameter, which cuts velocity, is the cheapest way to save pumping energy over a plant's life. Because velocity itself scales as \(Q/D^2\), the head loss actually falls with roughly the fifth power of diameter for a fixed flow — a modest increase in pipe size yields a dramatic drop in friction and pumping cost, the reason distribution mains are so much larger than a naive velocity limit would suggest.

§4 · The pipe-friction working method

Given a pipe run: compute \( Re \) to fix the regime; if laminar, use \( f=64/Re \); if turbulent, read \(f\) from the Moody chart using \(Re\) and \( \varepsilon/D \). Then apply Darcy–Weisbach for \( h_f \). Add the minor losses of Lesson 8 and feed the total head loss into the energy equation to size the pump. Two habits prevent most errors: never carry the laminar formula across the transition, and keep \(f\) (Darcy) distinct from the Fanning friction factor (four times smaller) that some texts use. The velocity-squared law is the design lever — halving velocity quarters friction — and it is the single most useful piece of intuition in piping-system economics. It helps to read the Moody chart as three territories. At low \(Re\) the laminar line \(f=64/Re\) falls steeply and roughness is irrelevant, because a smooth viscous sublayer buries the wall texture. In the transitional-turbulent band \(f\) depends on both \(Re\) and \( \varepsilon/D \), the curves fanning out by roughness. At high \(Re\) the curves flatten into the fully rough zone, where \(f\) depends on \( \varepsilon/D \) alone and no longer on \(Re\) — friction has become purely a matter of wall texture, so a rougher pipe simply costs more head forever regardless of speed. Knowing which zone a design sits in tells you immediately whether polishing the pipe, or slowing the flow, is the lever that actually helps.

Lesson 08 · Piping networks and valves

Lecture

§1 · Minor losses at fittings

Beyond wall friction, every fitting — entrance, bend, contraction, and especially every valve — dissipates energy as the flow separates and re-forms. These are the minor losses, written \( h_m=K\,\dfrac{V^2}{2g} \), where \(K\) is a tabulated loss coefficient for the component. "Minor" is a misnomer in a compact, fitting-heavy system, where they can exceed the pipe friction. A sharp pipe entrance has \(K\approx0.5\); a submerged exit has \(K=1.0\) (all the velocity head is lost); a partly closed valve can reach \(K\) of tens or hundreds.

§2 · A worked minor loss, and equivalent length

For water at \( V=3 \) m/s through a sharp entrance (\(K=0.5\)): \( h_m=0.5\times\dfrac{9}{19.62}\approx0.229 \) m; the exit of the same line (\(K=1.0\)) loses \( \approx0.459 \) m. A convenient way to fold fittings into pipe calculations is the equivalent length \(L_e\), the length of straight pipe giving the same loss: from \( K=f\,L_e/D \), \( L_e=KD/f \). For the entrance with \(D=0.1\) m and \(f=0.02\), \( L_e=0.5\times0.1/0.02=2.5 \) m of pipe. Summed over many fittings, equivalent lengths turn a messy network into one friction calculation.

§3 · Networks: series and parallel

Real systems are networks. Pipes in series carry the same flow \(Q\) and their head losses add: \( h_{\text{total}}=\sum h_i \). Pipes in parallel share the same end-to-end head loss and their flows add: \( Q_{\text{total}}=\sum Q_i \). These are the fluid analogues of resistors in series and parallel, and they let a branched system be reduced to an equivalent single path. Valves are the network's control elements: a gate valve is a low-loss on/off device, a globe valve throttles well but at high loss, a check valve prevents reverse flow, and a ball or butterfly valve trades compactness against controllability — the choice sets both the \(K\) in the loss sum and the plant's operability.

§4 · The network working method

To analyse a system: sum major losses (Darcy–Weisbach) and minor losses (\(KV^2/2g\), or equivalent lengths) along each path; reduce series and parallel sections to a single equivalent loss versus flow — the system curve; then intersect it with the pump curve for the operating point (Lesson 10). Choose valves for duty, not habit: throttling with a globe valve wastes energy deliberately as heat, so on a variable-flow line a variable-speed pump is usually cheaper over its life. Keep the loss bookkeeping explicit and units consistent — every term a head in metres — and the whole network, however branched, resolves to one head-versus-flow relationship the pump must satisfy. One phenomenon deserves a place in every valve discussion: cavitation. Where a valve or a pump inlet drops the local pressure below the fluid's vapour pressure, vapour bubbles form and then collapse violently as pressure recovers, pitting metal and roaring like gravel in the line. It is not a friction loss but a limit — it caps how far a valve may be throttled and how low a pump's suction may fall (the NPSH margin of Lesson 10). Designing a network is therefore not only summing losses but keeping every local pressure comfortably above vapour pressure, so the tidy head-versus-flow curve stays physically valid across the whole operating range.

Lesson 09 · Flow measurement

Lecture

§1 · Measuring flow by measuring pressure

Most flow meters do not measure velocity directly — they measure a pressure difference and infer the flow from Bernoulli. Force the fluid through a known area change and it must speed up (continuity), so its pressure must drop (Bernoulli); read that drop and you have the flow. The venturi, orifice plate, and flow nozzle are all differential-pressure meters built on exactly this idea, differing only in how gracefully they make the fluid accelerate and how much pressure they permanently lose.

§2 · The Pitot tube and stagnation

The simplest velocity probe is the Pitot tube, which brings the flow to rest at its tip. The pressure rise from moving to stagnant is the dynamic pressure \( \tfrac12\rho V^2 \), so measuring the difference between stagnation and static pressure gives \( V=\sqrt{2\,\Delta p/\rho} \). For water with \( \Delta p=2000 \) Pa, \( V=\sqrt{2\times2000/1000}=\sqrt4=2 \) m/s. This is the same dynamic pressure met in Lesson 4, now put to work: an aircraft's airspeed indicator is a Pitot-static system reading exactly this difference.

§3 · Venturi and orifice meters

For pipe flow, a venturi smoothly contracts to a throat and expands back. Combining continuity and Bernoulli, the ideal flow is \( Q=A_2\sqrt{\dfrac{2\,\Delta p}{\rho\,(1-(A_2/A_1)^2)}} \). A meter with \(D_1=0.1\) m contracting to \(D_2=0.05\) m (so \(A_2/A_1=0.25\)) reading \( \Delta p=2000 \) Pa passes an ideal \( Q\approx4.06\times10^{-3} \) m³/s. Real meters multiply this by a discharge coefficient \(C_d\) that corrects for friction and the vena contracta: a venturi's is near 0.98 (gentle, low permanent loss), while a cheap sharp-edged orifice plate runs about \(C_d\approx0.6\) (abrupt, high permanent loss but simple and cheap). The engineer trades installed cost against pumping cost across that difference.

§4 · The measurement working method

To size or read a DP meter: pick the device by the loss the system can afford — venturi where pumping energy matters, orifice where cheapness wins; apply the venturi/orifice equation with the right \(C_d\); and always work in absolute, consistent units, keeping \( \Delta p \) as the meter's differential, not a gauge reading against atmosphere. Remember that these meters read \( \sqrt{\Delta p} \), so flow resolution is poor at low flow and the turndown is limited — a real constraint on metering a widely varying line. Calibrate against a known flow when accuracy matters, because \(C_d\) drifts with Reynolds number and wear. The same Bernoulli-plus-continuity reasoning that sizes the meter also predicts its permanent pressure loss, closing the loop with the network losses of Lesson 8. It is worth knowing that these DP meters are only one family. Positive-displacement meters count fixed volumes and excel at low flows and viscous fluids; turbine meters spin a rotor whose speed tracks velocity; electromagnetic meters read the voltage a conductive fluid induces crossing a field, with no obstruction and no pressure loss at all; and ultrasonic meters time sound pulses with or against the flow. Each trades cost, accuracy, pressure loss, and fluid compatibility differently, but the DP meter remains the workhorse because it needs no moving parts and rests on the same Bernoulli physics the rest of this course has built.

Lesson 10 · Turbomachinery fundamentals

Lecture

§1 · What a pump does, in heads

A pump adds mechanical energy to a fluid, and that energy is measured as head \(H\) — metres of fluid, the same currency as every loss in the system. The useful (hydraulic) power delivered is \( P_{\text{hyd}}=\rho g Q H \). A pump raising \( Q=0.05 \) m³/s of water by \( H=30 \) m delivers \( P_{\text{hyd}}=1000\times9.81\times0.05\times30=14{,}715 \) W \(\approx14.7\) kW. No pump is perfect, so the shaft (brake) power it draws is larger: \( P_{\text{shaft}}=P_{\text{hyd}}/\eta \). At \( \eta=0.75 \) the shaft needs \( 14{,}715/0.75=19{,}620 \) W \(\approx19.6\) kW — the difference is lost to internal friction and turbulence as heat.

§2 · The pump curve meets the system curve

A centrifugal pump does not deliver a fixed flow; it delivers whatever the system demands along its pump curve, on which head falls as flow rises. The system curve — static lift plus friction that grows as \(Q^2\) — rises with flow. They cross at exactly one operating point, the flow and head the installation will actually run at. This single intersection, introduced in Lesson 4 and completed here, is the master diagram of rotating-equipment engineering: move either curve (throttle a valve, change speed, foul a pipe) and the operating point slides along the other, which is how every pumped system responds to a change.

§3 · Affinity laws and cavitation

Change a pump's speed \(N\) and its curve scales by the affinity laws: flow \( Q\propto N \), head \( H\propto N^2 \), power \( P\propto N^3 \). Double the speed and flow doubles, head quadruples, and power rises eightfold — the cubic power law is why variable-speed drives save so much energy at reduced flow, and why they are the modern alternative to throttling. The hard limit on the suction side is cavitation: if the pressure at the impeller eye falls below the fluid's vapour pressure, vapour bubbles form and collapse, eroding the impeller and collapsing head. The margin against it is the net positive suction head, and keeping the available NPSH above the pump's required NPSH is a non-negotiable installation constraint.

§4 · The turbomachinery working method

To specify a pump: build the system curve from static lift plus total losses (Lessons 7–8); overlay candidate pump curves and pick the one whose operating point sits near its best-efficiency flow, not far out on either side where efficiency collapses and vibration rises. Check NPSH available against required at the design flow, with margin. Use the affinity laws to trim speed rather than throttle where flow must vary, and size the motor from the shaft power \( P_{\text{hyd}}/\eta \) at the worst-case operating point. Turbines invert the same accounting — extracting head instead of adding it — so this one framework, head and efficiency about an operating point, serves pumps, fans, compressors, and hydro-turbines alike, which is why it anchors the whole rotating-equipment stream of the curriculum. Running two pumps in parallel adds their flows at a common head, while in series they add head at a common flow — the same series/parallel logic as pipe networks, applied to the machines instead of the pipes.

Lesson 11 · Compressible flow first contact

Lecture

§1 · When density stops being constant

Every lesson so far assumed constant density. That holds for liquids always and for gases at low speed, but as a gas approaches the speed of sound its density changes appreciably and a new regime — compressible flow — begins. The gatekeeper is the Mach number \( M=V/a \), the ratio of flow speed to the local speed of sound. Below about \( M=0.3 \) density changes are under a few percent and the incompressible tools of this course apply; above it, they do not, and the physics changes character. The change is not gradual bookkeeping but a genuine shift in behaviour: in subsonic flow, pressure signals travel upstream faster than the flow itself, so the fluid "feels" an obstacle coming and adjusts smoothly; once the flow is supersonic, no signal can outrun it, disturbances pile up, and the fluid meets changes abruptly through shocks. That single fact — whether information can travel upstream — is the deep reason compressible flow is a different subject rather than a corrected version of the incompressible one.

§2 · The speed of sound

Sound is a small pressure disturbance, and it travels at \( a=\sqrt{\gamma R T} \) for an ideal gas, where \( \gamma \) is the specific-heat ratio (1.4 for air), \(R\) the specific gas constant (287 J/kg·K for air), and \(T\) the absolute temperature. In air at \( 288 \) K, \( a=\sqrt{1.4\times287\times288}\approx340 \) m/s. Crucially \(a\) depends on temperature alone for a given gas, so the speed of sound — and therefore the Mach number at a fixed speed — falls as a gas cools, which is why a jet at fixed airspeed has a higher Mach number in the cold upper atmosphere. The temperature dependence also warns against a common shortcut: the speed of sound is not a fixed 340 m/s but a local property that changes wherever the gas heats or cools along its path, so in a flow with large temperature swings — a nozzle, a combustor — the Mach number must be evaluated with the local \(a\), not a single free-stream value. Getting the reference temperature right is half of getting the Mach number right.

§3 · Regimes and the shock

Mach number sorts compressible flow into regimes: subsonic (\(M<1\)), transonic (near 1), supersonic (\(M>1\)), and hypersonic beyond. A flow at \( V=170 \) m/s in that air has \( M=170/340\approx0.5 \) — subsonic but well into compressible territory. The defining feature of supersonic flow is the shock wave: a near-discontinuous jump in pressure, temperature, and density across which flow decelerates abruptly and mechanical energy is lost to heat. Shocks explain why a converging nozzle can accelerate a gas only to \(M=1\) at its throat, and why reaching supersonic speeds requires the converging–diverging (de Laval) nozzle of every rocket engine.

§4 · The compressible working method

The practical first step is always to compute the Mach number and decide whether compressibility even matters: if \( M<0.3 \), keep the incompressible Bernoulli and continuity of the earlier lessons and move on. If not, switch to compressible relations, carry temperature as a variable (it now changes with speed), and use absolute pressures and temperatures throughout. Watch for choking — a flow that has reached \(M=1\) at a restriction cannot be sped up by lowering downstream pressure further, a hard limit with no incompressible analogue that governs relief valves, gas pipelines, and nozzles. This lesson is a first contact, not a full treatment, but the discipline it installs — check the Mach number before choosing your equations — is exactly what keeps a thermal-systems or propulsion engineer from applying incompressible intuition where the physics has quietly changed. Two everyday consequences make the point concrete. In a high-pressure gas pipeline, the flow can choke at a valve or a rupture, capping the escape rate no matter how low the downstream pressure falls — a fact that safety-relief sizing depends on absolutely. And in a steam or gas turbine, the nozzles that accelerate the working fluid operate deep in the compressible regime, so their design is governed by Mach-number and choking limits that the incompressible relations of this course simply cannot express. Compressible flow is where fluid mechanics hands off to thermodynamics and propulsion, and recognising the handoff is itself the skill.
