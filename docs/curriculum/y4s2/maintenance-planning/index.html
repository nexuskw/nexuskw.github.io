Syllabus

01

The work management flowCore 60 · in production

Identify, plan, schedule, execute, close, analyze — the loop drawn as the maintenance department's operating system, with role boundaries (requester, planner, scheduler, supervisor) made explicit. Where the loop breaks in real plants: unplanned work injection, closure without data. The map every other planning lesson locates itself on.

Taught from — CORE 60 · F1 · SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Sketch the work-management loop and mark where unplanned work usually leaks in.
  • Why must planning and scheduling be distinct activities even when one person does both?
  • Emergency work consumes 30% of crew hours: trace what that does to every later stage of the loop.
02

Work identification and prioritization

Notification quality standards; screening and duplicate control. Priority set by criticality and consequence, not by who shouts. The gatekeeping function that protects the schedule.

Taught from — SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • What does a quality notification contain that a shout does not?
  • Why does screening protect the schedule?
03

Job planningCore 60 · in production

The planned job package: scoped steps, labor and duration estimates, parts, tools, permits, and drawings — assembled before the job needs them. Estimating methods and the feedback loop from actuals. The condensed example: a pump seal-replacement package built line by line, the planner interview's favorite question.

Taught from — CORE 60 · F2 · SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • List six things a complete job package contains before it may be scheduled.
  • Why must actual hours feed back into the estimate library?
  • What specific wastes does kitting remove from a technician's day?
04

SchedulingCore 60 · in production

Weekly scheduling against capacity: crew hours netted for leave and standing work, jobs committed by priority and readiness. Schedule compliance measured without gaming. The coordination meeting run properly — operations equipment release as the binding constraint.

Taught from — CORE 60 · F3 · SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • A crew of 8 × 40 h carries 25% PM load and 15% reactive reserve: how many hours are schedulable?
  • Schedule compliance hits 95% by scheduling less: name this gaming and the audit that catches it.
  • Why is the weekly schedule frozen by agreement with operations rather than issued by maintenance?
05

Backlog and maintenance KPIsCore 60 · in production

Backlog in crew-weeks: healthy ranges, aging analysis, and ready-versus-total distinction. The planning KPI set — schedule compliance, planned-work ratio, PM compliance, wrench time — each defined with its known manipulations. Reading a maintenance department's health from five numbers.

Taught from — CORE 60 · F4 · SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Backlog is 6 crew-weeks total but only 2 ready: which number drives next week's schedule, and is 6 healthy?
  • Ready work sitting 90 days old: what does aging analysis say is broken?
  • Which KPIs must be read as a set to prevent each from being gamed alone?
06

Spare parts and BOMsCore 60 · in production

Equipment bills of materials as the planner's index; criticality-driven stocking policies, min/max arithmetic from the inventory course, and the insurance-spare decision. Storeroom discipline: bin accuracy, preservation, kitting. The spares lesson EQUATE-scale plants staff whole teams for.

Taught from — CORE 60 · F5 · SMRP CMRP framework; PPC 356 foundations

Checkpoint questions
  • A gearbox has 26-week lead time: name the three inputs that decide the insurance-spare question.
  • A bearing consumes 6/year with 8-week lead: set a defensible min level and show the arithmetic.
  • How does a missing equipment BOM sabotage both planning and procurement?
07

CMMS and SAP PMCore 60 · in production

The CMMS data model: functional locations, equipment, task lists, work orders, notifications — SAP PM's names used since the Gulf runs on them. Master-data quality as the system's foundation; history codes that make analysis possible. What 'we have SAP but no data' means and how it happened.

Taught from — CORE 60 · F6 · SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Functional location versus equipment record: why does conflating them kill failure history?
  • Which work-order fields make later reliability analysis possible, and who fills them?
  • 'We have SAP but no data': give two root causes and their fixes.
08

TurnaroundsCore 60 · in production

The turnaround as a project: scope freeze, worklist challenge, long-lead procurement, and the CPM schedule at thousands of activities. Execution control: progress, extra work, and the discovery-work process. Why turnarounds overrun — scope, always scope — and the premium the Gulf pays planners who prevent it.

Taught from — CORE 60 · F7 · SMRP CMRP framework; NPTEL: Project Management

Checkpoint questions
  • Why is late scope the leading turnaround overrun cause, and what does a scope freeze actually freeze?
  • Exchanger bundle path 10 days versus column path 12 days: which drives the TA, and what buys float?
  • How do you budget for discovery work you cannot see until things open?
09

Maintenance budgetingCore 60 · in production

Zero-based versus historical budgeting; the budget built from PM programs, predicted corrective load, and turnaround accruals. Cost tracking by equipment and failure code — where the reliability engineer's Pareto comes from. Defending a budget with failure data instead of adjectives.

Taught from — CORE 60 · F8 · SMRP CMRP framework; ECO 406 foundations

Checkpoint questions
  • Assemble next year's budget from PM plan, corrective history, and TA accrual: what belongs in each line?
  • Why must costs be coded to assets rather than departments if the budget is to be defensible?
  • You get ten minutes with the plant manager to defend the budget: which single chart do you bring?
10

Shutdown execution and contractor management

Daily battle rhythm: progress capture, lookaheads, and constraint clearing, with handback and startup sequencing agreed with operations. Scoping service contracts — unit-rate versus lump-sum trade-offs — and field coordination of contractors at the HEISCO-scale interface. Contractor performance measured like internal crews: the owner's-engineer skill of buying maintenance work well.

Taught from — SMRP framework; NPTEL: Project Management

Checkpoint questions
  • What does the daily battle rhythm capture that weekly reports miss?
  • Unit-rate versus lump-sum: which risk sits where?
11

Planning simulation capstone

A four-week simulated plant: notifications arrive, you plan, schedule, and report KPIs; then a mini-turnaround is planned end to end. The full course exercised as one continuous role-play. Portfolio output: a complete planner's package.

Taught from — Course synthesis

Checkpoint questions
  • In the simulation, what broke your first weekly schedule?
  • What did the mini-turnaround teach about scope freeze?
  • SMRP CMRP framework (practitioner curriculum; framework, not a textbook)
  • NPTEL: operations and project management content (nptel.ac.in)
