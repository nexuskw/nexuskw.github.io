PAI 453 · YEAR 4 · SEMESTER 2

Pressure Equipment & Asset Integrity

Static equipment from design intent to end of life: vessels, piping, joints, and relief systems, then the integrity discipline that keeps them safe — damage mechanisms, inspection programs, risk-based inspection, and fitness-for-service.

Syllabus

01

Pressure equipment and the integrity discipline

Vessels, exchangers, boilers, piping, and tanks as regulated equipment classes. Design-code logic in outline: allowable stress and joint efficiency concepts. Why codes exist — written after explosions. Integrity as the discipline over static equipment: vessels, piping, tanks, relief systems. Loss-of-containment consequence logic. The role's boundaries against maintenance and inspection functions.

Taught from — NPTEL pressure vessel content; SOM 201 foundations; NPTEL reliability content; industry framework survey

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do pressure codes exist — what pattern preceded them?
  • What equipment classes does integrity own, and what consequence unites them?
02

Vessel shells and heads

Thin-wall theory recalled, then thickness logic with corrosion allowance. Head types and their stress behavior. Nozzles and reinforcement in concept.

Taught from — SOM 201; NPTEL pressure vessel content

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do heads and shells meet at a discontinuity stress?
  • What does corrosion allowance buy, and for how long?
03

Piping systems and their integrity

Pipe schedules, fittings, and specs; flexibility and thermal expansion loops from the thermal-stress lesson. Supports: shoes, guides, hangers, springs. Reading a piping isometric fluently. Piping classes, circuits, and injection-point programs; dead legs and small-bore vulnerability. Support and vibration walkdowns. Piping as the plant's largest and least-loved asset.

Taught from — NPTEL piping content; NPTEL content; Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 17

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do expansion loops exist, and what do they trade?
  • Why are dead legs disproportionately dangerous?
04

Flanged joints, gaskets, and valves

Flange classes and facings; gasket selection and seating stress from the bolted-joint lesson. Controlled bolting: sequence, lubrication, and torque versus tension. Why flanges leak in January and how to stop it. Gate, globe, check, ball, and butterfly internals; stem sealing and packing. Valve selection by service. Maintenance-relevant failure modes: passing, seizing, packing leaks.

Taught from — MDN 251 foundations; NPTEL piping content; NPTEL piping content

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does bolt-up sequence matter on a flange?
  • Gate versus globe: which throttles, which isolates, and why?
05

Damage mechanisms inventory

The degradation bestiary organized for static equipment: thinning, cracking, embrittlement, mechanical damage. Susceptibility mapped from process conditions. The corrosion course operationalized into an integrity register.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 17

Checkpoint questions
  • Sort thinning, cracking, and embrittlement by inspectability.
  • Why does susceptibility mapping precede any inspection plan?
06

Overpressure protection and relief integrity

Relief scenarios and device types: spring PSVs, pilot-operated, rupture discs. Set pressure, accumulation, and backpressure concepts. Installation sins: inlet losses and isolated relief paths. Relief philosophy: every credible overpressure case owns a device. PSV testing intervals and pre-test capture of as-found condition. The protective systems audit trail regulators read first.

Taught from — NPTEL process safety content

Checkpoint questions
  • Set pressure, accumulation, backpressure: define each in one line.
  • Why must every credible overpressure case map to a device?
07

Inspection: methods and in-service programs

Visual, UT thickness, shear-wave, and profile radiography deployed on vessels and piping. CML selection — where to measure so the data means something. Inspection effectiveness graded honestly. Thickness surveys, CML programs, and inspection intervals from the asset-integrity course specialized to vessels and piping. Internal inspection findings and their dispositions. The inspector–engineer interface in practice.

Taught from — NPTEL NDT-related content; AIM 402 foundations

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does CML selection decide whether thickness data means anything?
  • Why do CMLs live where they live on a piping circuit?
08

Risk-based inspection

Probability and consequence of failure combined into inspection priority. Interval setting from remaining life and confidence. RBI as budget allocation defended with arithmetic.

Taught from — NPTEL reliability content

Checkpoint questions
  • What two axes drive RBI priority, and what data feeds each?
  • How does RBI defend an interval extension to a regulator?
09

Fitness for service, repairs, and alterations

The engineering question behind FFS: is the flawed equipment safe to run, and for how long? Corrosion allowance and minimum thickness logic; crack-like flaws flagged to specialists. When to call the assessment versus replace. Temporary repairs — clamps, wraps — with expiry dates that must be honored; weld repairs and their qualification. Rerating logic in outline. Management of change for pressure equipment.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 8; NPTEL content; AIM 402 foundations

Checkpoint questions
  • What question does FFS answer that inspection cannot?
  • Why do temporary repairs carry expiry dates?
10

Storage tanks and corrosion under insulation

Atmospheric tank anatomy: shell, bottom, roof; settlement and corrosion patterns. Internal inspection logic and intervals. Floor scanning and the repair decisions it triggers. CUI's mechanism, temperature bands, and moisture pathways. Inspection strategies from stripping to screening. The Gulf coastal plant's standing CUI battle, budgeted properly.

Taught from — NPTEL content; Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 17

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does tank bottom corrosion hide from external inspection?
  • Name CUI's moisture pathways and the design details that feed them.
11

Static equipment dossier project

For one vessel and its piping circuit: equipment dossier with design data, damage mechanisms, CMLs, relief basis, and repair history template. The record-keeping backbone of integrity work, built once by hand.

Taught from — Course synthesis

Checkpoint questions
  • What belongs in an equipment dossier before you inherit it?
  • Which record gap would worry you most, and why?
  • Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 8, 17 (failure & corrosion foundations)
  • NPTEL: pressure vessel and piping-related courses (nptel.ac.in)
  • NPTEL: reliability and corrosion course content (nptel.ac.in)
  • Strength of Materials (SOM 201) foundations
