PEP 453 · YEAR 4 · SEMESTER 2

Pressure Equipment & Piping

The hardware that holds the pressure: vessel and piping construction logic, materials and joints revisited at code level, flanges and gaskets, relief devices, and inspection/repair thinking for static equipment.

11 lessons0 of 11 at full depth
Start lesson 01 Full curriculum

What you'll learn

✓Vessels, exchangers, boilers, piping, and tanks as regulated equipment classes.
✓Thin-wall theory recalled, then thickness logic with corrosion allowance.
✓Pipe schedules, fittings, and specs; flexibility and thermal expansion loops from the thermal-stress lesson.
✓Flange classes and facings; gasket selection and seating stress from the bolted-joint lesson.
✓Gate, globe, check, ball, and butterfly internals; stem sealing and packing.
✓Relief scenarios and device types: spring PSVs, pilot-operated, rupture discs.

Syllabus

01

Pressure equipment taxonomy

Vessels, exchangers, boilers, piping, and tanks as regulated equipment classes. Design-code logic in outline: allowable stress and joint efficiency concepts. Why codes exist — written after explosions.

Taught from — NPTEL pressure vessel content; SOM 201 foundations

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do pressure codes exist — what pattern preceded them?
  • What does joint efficiency discount, and why?
02

Vessel shells and heads

Thin-wall theory recalled, then thickness logic with corrosion allowance. Head types and their stress behavior. Nozzles and reinforcement in concept.

Taught from — SOM 201; NPTEL pressure vessel content

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do heads and shells meet at a discontinuity stress?
  • What does corrosion allowance buy, and for how long?
03

Piping systems and layout

Pipe schedules, fittings, and specs; flexibility and thermal expansion loops from the thermal-stress lesson. Supports: shoes, guides, hangers, springs. Reading a piping isometric fluently.

Taught from — NPTEL piping content

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do expansion loops exist, and what do they trade?
  • Read a piping isometric: what three things do you check first?
04

Flanged joints and gaskets

Flange classes and facings; gasket selection and seating stress from the bolted-joint lesson. Controlled bolting: sequence, lubrication, and torque versus tension. Why flanges leak in January and how to stop it.

Taught from — MDN 251 foundations; NPTEL piping content

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does bolt-up sequence matter on a flange?
  • What does gasket seating stress require of the bolts?
05

Valves as pressure equipment

Gate, globe, check, ball, and butterfly internals; stem sealing and packing. Valve selection by service. Maintenance-relevant failure modes: passing, seizing, packing leaks.

Taught from — NPTEL piping content

Checkpoint questions
  • Gate versus globe: which throttles, which isolates, and why?
  • What does packing seal, and what tells you it's failing?
06

Overpressure protection

Relief scenarios and device types: spring PSVs, pilot-operated, rupture discs. Set pressure, accumulation, and backpressure concepts. Installation sins: inlet losses and isolated relief paths.

Taught from — NPTEL process safety content

Checkpoint questions
  • Set pressure, accumulation, backpressure: define each in one line.
  • Name two installation sins that disable a healthy PSV.
07

Fabrication and welding at code level

The WPS/PQR system recalled under vessel and piping rules; NDT extents and hydrotest logic. Traceability from mill certificate to weld map. The fabrication paper trail HEISCO-scale shops live by.

Taught from — WLD 305 foundations

Checkpoint questions
  • What traceability chain links a weld to its mill certificate?
  • What does hydrotest prove, and what does it not?
08

In-service inspection

Thickness surveys, CML programs, and inspection intervals from the asset-integrity course specialized to vessels and piping. Internal inspection findings and their dispositions. The inspector–engineer interface in practice.

Taught from — AIM 402 foundations

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do CMLs live where they live on a piping circuit?
  • What disposition options follow an internal inspection finding?
09

Repairs and alterations

Temporary repairs — clamps, wraps — with expiry dates that must be honored; weld repairs and their qualification. Rerating logic in outline. Management of change for pressure equipment.

Taught from — NPTEL content; AIM 402 foundations

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do temporary repairs carry expiry dates?
  • What does MOC review before a rerate?
10

Failure case studies

Classic vessel and piping failures dissected: brittle fracture, CUI ruptures, dead-leg corrosion, relief-path isolation. Each traced through the lessons that would have prevented it. Institutional memory as curriculum.

Taught from — Course synthesis

Checkpoint questions
  • Pick one classic failure and name the lesson that would have prevented it.
  • What made brittle fracture disasters a temperature story?
11

Static equipment dossier project

For one vessel and its piping circuit: equipment dossier with design data, damage mechanisms, CMLs, relief basis, and repair history template. The record-keeping backbone of integrity work, built once by hand.

Taught from — Course synthesis

Checkpoint questions
  • What belongs in an equipment dossier before you inherit it?
  • Which record gap would worry you most, and why?
  • Strength of Materials (SOM 201) foundations
  • NPTEL: pressure vessel and piping-related courses (nptel.ac.in)
