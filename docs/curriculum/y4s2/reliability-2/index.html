Reliability Engineering II

From analysis to institution: RCM implementation and root cause analysis in practice, bad-actor elimination, reliability KPIs, and the design of a plant reliability program that survives management changes.

Syllabus

01

RCM implementation and RCACore 60 · in production

Running a real RCM study: team composition, facilitation discipline, and the information the analysis eats. Then root cause analysis as its operational partner — evidence preservation, causal-factor charting, and the 5-why's honest limits. The condensed example: an RCA on a repeat seal failure, from timeline to verified systemic cause.

Taught from — CORE 60 · C6 · NPTEL: Reliability Engineering alternative (enable captions); SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Name three pieces of evidence to preserve before a failed seal is torn down.
  • What class of causes does five-why analysis systematically miss?
  • What evidence standard closes an RCA — when may you say 'the fix worked'?
02

Bad-actor programs and reliability KPIsCore 60 · in production

Pareto analysis of failure records to find the vital few; bad-actor teams with closure discipline. The reliability KPI set — MTBF by asset class, top-loss trending, PM effectiveness — built from CMMS data with its coding sins acknowledged. One bad-actor case tracked from data to eliminated failure mode.

Taught from — CORE 60 · C7 · SMRP CMRP framework; NPTEL: Reliability Engineering alternative (enable captions)

Checkpoint questions
  • Ten assets drive 60% of maintenance cost: which analysis showed this, and who closes it out?
  • Overall uptime is flat while MTBF of one asset class falls: how can both be true?
  • Which single KPI proves a bad-actor program delivered, and over what horizon?
03

Building a plant reliability programCore 60 · in production

The program architecture: criticality analysis feeding strategy development, PdM routes, planning integration, and a living KPI system — sequenced as a realistic multi-year roadmap with early wins funding later phases. Organizational design: where reliability engineers report and why it matters. The lesson that turns this curriculum into a job plan.

Taught from — CORE 60 · C8 · SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Sequence the first three moves of a multi-year plant reliability roadmap and justify the order.
  • Where should reliability engineering report, and what goes wrong under the alternative?
  • Why fund later program phases from the early phases' verified savings?
04

Failure reporting and analysis systems

FRACAS logic: capture, code, analyze, act. Failure coding taxonomies that analysts can actually use. The data pipeline that makes Weibull analysis possible years later.

Taught from — NPTEL: Reliability Engineering alternative (enable captions)

Checkpoint questions
  • What does FRACAS close that work orders leave open?
  • Why do failure codes fail, and what fixes them?
05

Life-cycle reliability: design for maintainability

Reliability input to procurement specs; maintainability reviews of vendor drawings. Access, isolation, and monitoring provisions bought cheaply at design time. The capital-project seat reliability engineers must claim.

Taught from — SMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Name three maintainability features to demand on vendor drawings.
  • Why is design-stage reliability input so cheap?
06

Reliability testing and growth

Life testing and accelerated testing in outline; reliability growth tracking during commissioning. Burn-in's logic and limits. Evidence standards for 'the fix worked'.

Taught from — NPTEL: Reliability Engineering alternative (enable captions)

Checkpoint questions
  • What does reliability growth tracking during commissioning reveal?
  • Burn-in: what does it remove, and from which bathtub region?
07

Spares optimization with reliability data

Failure distributions driving stocking levels; Monte Carlo thinking for insurance spares. Pooling and vendor-held options. The reliability–inventory interface quantified.

Taught from — NPTEL: Reliability Engineering alternative (enable captions); MPL 452 foundations

Checkpoint questions
  • What does Monte Carlo add to an insurance-spare decision?
  • When does vendor-held stock beat owning?
08

Operational reliability: procedures and precision

Operating-envelope discipline and startup/shutdown stress; precision-maintenance standards as failure prevention. Operator care integrated with PdM findings. Reliability as everyone's output, engineered.

Taught from — SMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do startups and shutdowns concentrate failures?
  • What does an operating envelope discipline protect?
09

Management of change for reliability

MOC review of modifications for reliability impact; temporary equipment and its expiry. Documentation updates as part of the change, not afterthought. The quiet failures MOC exists to prevent.

Taught from — SMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Which changes sneak past MOC most often?
  • Why must documentation updates ride inside the change?
10

Economics of reliability programs

Program costs against avoided losses; the credibility problem of counting non-events. Baseline construction and honest attribution. Selling year three of a program in year one's budget cycle.

Taught from — SMRP framework; ECO 406 foundations

Checkpoint questions
  • Why is counting non-events the program's credibility problem?
  • What baseline construction makes avoided losses believable?
11

Reliability program capstone

Design the complete reliability program for a described plant: assessment, roadmap, organization, KPIs, and first-year plan with budget. Presented as to a plant manager. The portfolio piece for reliability-engineer interviews.

Taught from — Course synthesis

Checkpoint questions
  • Which first-year move funds year two in your capstone program?
  • What did you put in the plant manager's one-page summary?
