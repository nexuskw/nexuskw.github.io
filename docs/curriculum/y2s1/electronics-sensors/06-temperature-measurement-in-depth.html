01 · RTD classes and self-heating

A resistance temperature detector (RTD) measures temperature by its own resistance change — the most common, a platinum Pt100, reads \(100\ \Omega\) at \(0^\circ\)C with a well-defined, highly linear resistance-versus-temperature relationship, giving RTDs excellent accuracy and repeatability compared to a thermocouple. But reading that resistance requires passing an excitation current through the RTD, and that current dissipates power, \(P=I^2R\), heating the element slightly above the true process temperature — self-heating. Because power scales with the square of current, a small increase in excitation current produces a disproportionately larger heating error: doubling excitation current quadruples self-heating, the identical relationship behind a loose electrical connection's dangerous heating. Self-heating is managed by keeping excitation current low and, where the process allows it, by good thermal contact between the RTD and a moving process fluid that carries the excess heat away.

02 · Thermocouple types, cold junctions, and extension wire

A thermocouple generates a small voltage from the junction of two dissimilar metals, with the voltage depending on the temperature difference between the measuring junction and a reference — the cold junction. Because the thermocouple only ever reports a temperature difference, the instrument reading it must independently know the cold-junction temperature (measured separately, at the terminal block) and add it back in — cold-junction compensation — to recover the actual process temperature. Different thermocouple types (J, K, T, and others) use different metal pairs, each suited to a different temperature range and environment; extension wire — running from the cold junction back to the instrument — must be made of the same thermocouple alloy pair as the sensor itself, not ordinary copper wire, or a second, unaccounted-for junction is created partway along the run, corrupting the reading in a way that is easy to overlook and hard to diagnose from the symptom alone.

03 · Thermowells and response lag

A thermowell is a closed-end tube inserted into a process, protecting the sensor inside it from direct contact with the process fluid — allowing sensor replacement without draining or depressurizing the process, and protecting against corrosion or erosion. That protection comes at a real cost: the thermowell's own thermal mass and the air gap (or fill fluid) between it and the sensor both slow the sensor's response to a genuine temperature change — response lag. A fast process upset can move faster than a thermowell-protected sensor can follow, a real limitation to weigh against the thermowell's real safety and maintainability benefits.

04 · Choosing and installing a temperature point — the working method

(1) Match sensor type to the temperature range and required accuracy: RTDs for higher accuracy and stability at moderate temperatures, thermocouples for very high temperatures or where ruggedness matters more than ultimate precision. (2) Keep RTD excitation current low, and verify the specific installation's self-heating error against its actual accuracy requirement. (3) Use only matched extension wire for thermocouples, all the way to the cold-junction reference point. (4) Weigh a thermowell's protection and maintainability against its response-lag cost for the specific application — a slow-moving tank temperature can easily tolerate the lag; a fast-cycling process control loop may not.

Key relation — self-heating

\[ P = I^2R \Rightarrow \Delta T \propto I^2. \]

Same I²R relationship as a loose electrical connection — small current changes, large error changes.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Self-heating scales with current squared

Problem. An RTD self-heats by about 0.005 K at 1 mA excitation current. Estimate the self-heating error at 5 mA, and confirm the relationship with power.

Solution. Since \( \Delta T\propto I^2 \), increasing current by a factor of 5 increases self-heating by \(5^2=25\): \(0.005\times25=0.125\ \text{K}\). This matches \(P=I^2R\) directly — a 5× current increase means 25× the dissipated power, and therefore 25× the temperature rise for the same thermal resistance.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Diagnosing an extension-wire error

Problem. A type-K thermocouple is connected back to its instrument using ordinary copper extension wire instead of type-K extension wire. Explain what this does to the reading, and why the error is easy to overlook.

Solution. The junction between the type-K thermocouple wire and the copper extension wire forms an unintended second thermocouple junction, at whatever temperature that connection happens to be — an error the instrument's cold-junction compensation, applied only at the true terminal block, does not account for. It is easy to overlook because the wiring may look correct at a glance, and the resulting error is often small enough to seem like ordinary measurement drift rather than an obvious fault.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

RTD or thermocouple — and why

Problem. A 90°C bearing housing needs a temperature measurement for a condition-monitoring program valuing accuracy and stability over the housing’s expected life. Which sensor type fits better, and on what grounds?

Solution. An RTD fits better. At 90°C, well within an RTD's comfortable range, its superior accuracy, linearity, and long-term stability outweigh a thermocouple's advantages (wider range, ruggedness at very high temperature) that are not actually needed here — the RTD trades a modest self-heating concern, easily managed at low excitation current, for meaningfully better measurement quality at this specific, moderate temperature.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A maintenance shop stocking generic "thermocouple extension wire" without checking the type against each specific thermocouple in service is a quiet, ongoing source of small temperature-reading errors across a plant — 2's mismatched-junction problem does not announce itself loudly, it just makes affected loops read consistently a little off, in a way that is easy to blame on drift or calibration rather than trace to a wiring stock mix-up.

A bearing-temperature RTD reading that seems to run a fraction of a degree high compared to a calibrated reference, consistently across every unit on a given excitation-current setting, is a textbook 1 self-heating signature worth checking against the transmitter's excitation-current setting before assuming a calibration problem — sometimes simply lowering the excitation current, where the transmitter allows it, resolves the offset directly.
