01 · Analog-to-digital conversion

An ADC converts a continuous analog voltage into a discrete digital number, at a moment in time it is triggered to sample. Two independent specifications govern how faithfully that conversion represents the original signal: resolution — how many discrete levels the ADC can distinguish, set by its bit depth (a 12-bit ADC resolves \(2^{12}=4096\) levels across its input range) — and sampling rate — how often, in samples per second (Hz), the ADC takes a new reading. Resolution limits how finely a single reading is known; sampling rate limits how fast a changing signal can be tracked at all.

02 · The Nyquist criterion

The Nyquist criterion states that a signal must be sampled at a rate at least twice its highest frequency component to be reconstructed unambiguously — \(F_s>2f_{max}\). Half the sampling rate, \(F_s/2\), is the Nyquist frequency: the highest frequency that can be faithfully captured at that sampling rate. A signal containing energy above the Nyquist frequency is not simply lost — it reappears, corrupted, as described next.

03 · Aliasing — a false frequency, not a missing one

When a signal above the Nyquist frequency is sampled anyway, it does not vanish — it aliases, reappearing in the digitized data as a false signal at a lower frequency, indistinguishable from a genuine signal at that lower frequency. A signal at \(700\ \text{Hz}\), sampled at \(F_s=1000\ \text{Hz}\) (Nyquist frequency \(500\ \text{Hz}\)), aliases to \(|700-1000|=300\ \text{Hz}\) — the digitized data looks exactly as if a real \(300\ \text{Hz}\) signal had been sampled, and no processing performed after the fact on the digital samples alone can tell the two apart, because the samples themselves are identical either way.

04 · Anti-aliasing filters — fixing it before, not after

Because aliasing is unrecoverable after digitization — the information distinguishing "real 300 Hz" from "aliased 700 Hz" is simply gone from the samples — the only correct fix is prevention: an anti-aliasing filter, an analog low-pass filter placed before the ADC, removing any signal content above the Nyquist frequency before sampling ever occurs. Choosing a sampling rate for a new measurement therefore means identifying the highest genuine frequency of interest first (a bearing defect frequency, a fast pressure transient), setting \(F_s\) comfortably above twice that value, and fitting an anti-aliasing filter tuned to the resulting Nyquist frequency — sampling rate and filter are chosen together, not independently.

Key relation — Nyquist and aliasing

\[ F_s > 2f_{max}, \qquad f_{alias}=|f_{true}-F_s| \ \text{(when } f_{true}>F_s/2 \text{)} \]

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Computing an alias frequency

Problem. A 700 Hz vibration signal is sampled at 1000 Hz, with no anti-aliasing filter fitted. What frequency will it falsely appear as in the digitized data?

Solution. Nyquist frequency \(=1000/2=500\ \text{Hz}\); since 700 Hz exceeds this, it aliases to \(|700-1000|=300\ \text{Hz}\) — the digitized data is indistinguishable from a genuine 300 Hz signal.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Choosing a sampling rate

Problem. A bearing defect frequency of interest is expected up to 4 kHz. Propose a minimum sampling rate satisfying the Nyquist criterion, and explain why choosing exactly \(2\times4\ \text{kHz}=8\ \text{kHz}\) would be a poor practical choice even though it technically satisfies the criterion.

Solution. The Nyquist criterion requires \(F_s>2f_{max}\), strictly greater than, not equal to — sampling at exactly 8 kHz leaves no margin, and any real anti-aliasing filter has a gradual roll-off rather than a perfectly sharp cutoff, so some content just above 4 kHz could still leak through and alias. A practical choice would sample comfortably above 8 kHz (commonly 2.5–5× the frequency of interest) to give the anti-aliasing filter real margin to work with.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Why filtering after sampling cannot fix aliasing

Problem. Explain why a digital low-pass filter applied to already-sampled data cannot undo aliasing that occurred during sampling.

Solution. Once sampled, a false 300 Hz alias and a genuine 300 Hz signal produce numerically identical sample sequences — the information that would distinguish them was never captured in the first place. A digital filter can only operate on the samples it is given, and has no way to recover a distinction that does not exist in that data; the only correct fix is an analog anti-aliasing filter applied before sampling, removing the offending frequency content before it can alias.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A vibration spectrum showing an unexpected, physically implausible peak at a frequency that does not correspond to any known machine component or fault is worth checking against 3 before treating it as real: an inadequately filtered high-frequency vibration component aliasing down into the analyzed range can produce a peak that looks meaningful but is actually a sampling artifact, not a genuine mechanical signature.

A data acquisition system being specified for a new high-speed process measurement documents both the sampling rate and the anti-aliasing filter's cutoff together, as a matched pair, in the same specification sheet — per 4, quoting one without the other leaves the measurement's actual frequency validity unstated, a gap a careful commissioning review should catch before the system is accepted.
