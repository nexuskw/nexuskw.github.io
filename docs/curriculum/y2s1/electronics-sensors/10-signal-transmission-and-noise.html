Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What causes a ground loop, and what breaks it?
  • Why route signal and power cables apart?

01 · Ground is not one number everywhere

Ground symbols on a schematic suggest a single, shared \(0\ \text{V}\) reference — in practice, a real plant's grounding grid has resistance, and different equipment grounded at physically distant points can sit at slightly different actual potentials, especially when other equipment (large motors, welding, switching drives) injects current into that same grid. This ground potential difference, often just a volt or two, is normally harmless — until a cable's shield connects two such points directly together.

02 · How a ground loop forms

A cable shield grounded at both its field end and its control-room end forms a closed electrical path between those two ground points. If those two points sit at even a small potential difference, that difference drives a real current through the shield — a ground loop current — entirely independent of, and in addition to, the shield's intended job of blocking external electromagnetic interference. That circulating current can couple into the actual signal conductors, appearing as noise, drift, or an offset error that has nothing to do with the process being measured.

03 · The fix — single-point grounding

Grounding the shield at only one end — conventionally the source or control-room end, with the far end left floating (not connected to local ground) — leaves the loop open. No closed path exists, so no current can flow through the shield regardless of how different the two ground potentials are, while the shield still performs its intended job of intercepting external interference and carrying it away at the single grounded end. This is why a shielded cable correctly landed at one end, and correctly left open at the other, is not a wiring shortcut — it is the deliberate fix for a real, measurable failure mode.

04 · When isolation is worth the added cost

Sometimes both ends genuinely need a local ground connection for their own independent safety or EMI reasons, or the expected potential difference is too large to leave to single-point grounding alone. A signal isolator (or isolation amplifier) uses a transformer or optical coupling to pass the signal's information across a gap with no direct electrical (galvanic) connection between the two sides — each side can be grounded locally as needed, with no possible path for a loop current to form between them, at the cost of an added component in the signal path. Choosing between single-point grounding and full galvanic isolation is a judgment call based on the expected potential difference, the distance involved, and whether both ends have an independent reason to be locally grounded.

Key rule — shield grounding

Ground a cable shield at ONE end only. Grounding both ends turns any ground potential difference into a driving voltage for a loop current.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Diagnosing a ground loop

Problem. A long analog signal cable is found with its shield grounded at both the field device and the control-room instrument. The signal shows a small, persistent noise component correlated with nearby heavy equipment cycling on and off. Explain the likely cause and the standard fix.

Solution. Grounding the shield at both ends forms a closed loop between the two ground points. If heavy equipment cycling on and off shifts the local ground potential at one end relative to the other, that shifting potential difference drives a varying loop current through the shield, coupling correlated noise into the signal. The standard fix is to ground the shield at one end only, opening the loop so no current can flow regardless of the potential difference.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Single-point grounding vs. isolation

Problem. A signal run between two buildings on a large site is expected to have a ground potential difference of several volts, and each building’s local electrical code requires its own instrument to be locally grounded for safety. Would single-point shield grounding alone be an adequate fix here? Explain.

Solution. Not necessarily. Single-point shield grounding addresses the shield specifically, but if each instrument itself must be locally grounded at both buildings for independent safety reasons, a large potential difference between those two safety grounds can still drive problematic current through other paths, or make the single-point shield choice itself difficult to apply consistently. A galvanic signal isolator, breaking the electrical connection between the two sides entirely while still passing the signal, is the more robust solution when both ends have an independent, non-negotiable local grounding requirement.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Why the shield still works when grounded once

Problem. Explain why a shield grounded at only one end still protects the signal from external electromagnetic interference, even though it no longer forms a closed loop.

Solution. The shield's interference-blocking function works by intercepting external electric fields and providing a path for the induced currents they create to drain away at the single grounded end — this does not require a closed loop through both ends. What the closed loop specifically enables is the additional, unwanted ground-potential-difference-driven current; removing that closed path removes the ground loop risk while leaving the shield's intended EMI-interception function intact.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A newly commissioned long cable run that tests perfectly on the bench but develops mysterious, intermittent noise once installed and connected at both ends in the field is a textbook 2 ground-loop signature worth checking before suspecting the sensor or the instrument itself — the bench test never had two separated, independently-grounded end points, so a loop that only exists in the field installation would never have shown up during that earlier bench check.

A site standard requiring "shield grounded at the control-room end only, taped back and insulated at the field end" on every analog cable drawing — rather than leaving the grounding choice to whoever happens to land each individual cable — is applying 3 as a documented, repeatable rule precisely because a single inconsistent cable in an otherwise correctly wired plant can reintroduce exactly the noise problem the standard exists to prevent.
