01 · The transistor as a switch

Driven by a small base (or gate) current or voltage, a transistor can control a much larger current through its collector (or drain) — the basis of both amplification and switching. Used as a switch, the goal is to avoid the linear amplifying region entirely and drive the device hard into one of two extremes: cutoff, where base drive is insufficient and essentially no collector current flows — the switch is open; or saturation, where base drive is large enough that the transistor is fully on, collector current is set by the external circuit, and the voltage drop across the transistor itself is small — the switch is closed.

02 · Why lingering in between matters — heat

Power dissipated in the transistor is (approximately) collector current times the voltage across it. In cutoff, current is near zero — negligible power, regardless of voltage. In saturation, voltage is near zero — negligible power, regardless of current. In the linear region between them, both current and voltage can be substantial simultaneously, and their product — real dissipated power — can be large. A switching circuit is designed specifically to cross that middle region quickly, not to idle there, which is why switching transistor circuits stay cool while linear amplifier stages, deliberately biased into that middle region, need real heat-sinking.

03 · Driving relays and indicator loads

A transistor switching an inductive load — a relay coil, a solenoid — faces the same interruption hazard from Electrical Fundamentals: opening that current path abruptly induces a large voltage spike (\(V=L\,dI/dt\)) that can exceed the transistor's rated breakdown voltage and destroy it instantly. A flyback diode across the coil gives that stored energy a safe path to decay through, exactly as it does for any mechanical contact switching an inductive load — the transistor needs the identical protection a relay's own contacts would.

04 · Solid-state relays versus electromechanical ones

A solid-state relay (SSR) uses a transistor (or triac, for AC) as the switching element, optically isolated from the control side — no moving parts, extremely fast switching, no contact bounce or wear, essentially unlimited switching life for a given current rating. An electromechanical relay (EMR) uses a physical armature and contacts — genuinely isolated by an air gap, tolerant of large inrush and surge currents an SSR of similar rating often cannot survive, and visibly, audibly confirmable as open or closed. An SSR wins where switching is frequent, fast, or silent operation matters (many cycles per minute, PWM-style control); an EMR wins where isolation certainty, surge tolerance, or simple visual/audible confirmation of state matters more than switching speed.

Key rule — switching, not amplifying

A switching transistor sits at cutoff (off) or saturation (on) — the linear region between them is crossed quickly, since that is where real power dissipation (and heat) occurs.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Where does the heat come from?

Problem. A switching transistor carries 2 A at a 0.2 V saturation drop when on, and blocks 24 V with negligible leakage current when off. Estimate the power dissipated in each state, and explain why a transistor lingering halfway between them would dissipate far more.

Solution. Saturation: \(P=2\ \text{A}\times0.2\ \text{V}=0.4\ \text{W}\). Cutoff: current is negligible, so \(P\approx0\ \text{W}\) regardless of the 24 V blocked. Halfway between, both current and voltage could be substantial simultaneously — for example 1 A at 12 V gives 12 W, thirty times the saturation dissipation — which is exactly why a switching circuit is designed to cross that region quickly rather than idle there.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Why the flyback diode is not optional

Problem. A transistor switches a relay coil, \(L=0.3\ \text{H}\), carrying 0.5 A, and turns off in about 2 microseconds with no flyback diode fitted. Estimate the induced voltage spike, and explain the consequence.

Solution. \( V\approx L\,dI/dt=0.3\times(0.5/2\times10^{-6})=75{,}000\ \text{V} \) (an extreme, idealized estimate assuming the current is forced to zero that fast) — in practice the transistor breaks down and conducts destructively well before reaching that number, since its rated breakdown voltage is typically well under 100 V. Without a flyback diode, the coil’s stored energy has nowhere to go except through the transistor itself, destroying it.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

SSR or EMR?

Problem. A control system must switch a heater load a few times per hour, with occasional large inrush current at turn-on, and the maintenance team wants to be able to visually confirm the switch state from across the panel. Would a solid-state relay or an electromechanical relay be the better fit, and why?

Solution. An electromechanical relay fits better: infrequent switching means SSR speed is not needed, the inrush tolerance favors mechanical contacts, and a visible/audible armature gives the maintenance team the confirmation an SSR (with no moving parts) cannot provide directly.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A control-panel output card running warm to the touch, with individual output transistors noticeably hotter than their neighbors switching identical loads, is a 2 diagnosis waiting to happen: a transistor stuck lingering in its linear region — from a marginal drive signal, a failing base resistor, or a load drawing more current than the output was rated for — dissipates real power as heat exactly where a healthy switching output would not, well before it fails outright.

A plant standardizing on solid-state relays for high-cycle-count applications (frequent starts/stops on a filling line, for instance) while keeping electromechanical relays for infrequent, high-inrush loads like large motor contactors is applying 4's trade-off deliberately, not by habit — mixing the two relay types intentionally across a panel, rather than defaulting to one type everywhere, is a sign of a design that actually thought about the application.
