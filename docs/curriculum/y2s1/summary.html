MTH 207 — Engineering Mathematics III — Differential Equations & Linear Algebra

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · First-order equations revisited: separable and linear

01 · Two shapes of first-order equation

A first-order ordinary differential equation relates a function to its first derivative. It is the mathematics of anything whose rate of change depends on its present state, which is most of engineering: a cooling casting, a draining tank, a charging capacitor. Two shapes cover the great majority of models. The separable form \( \frac{dy}{dx}=g(x)\,h(y) \) can be split so each variable sits with its own differential: \( \frac{dy}{h(y)}=g(x)\,dx \), after which both sides are integrated independently. The linear form \( y'+p(x)y=q(x) \) cannot generally be separated — the unknown appears both differentiated and undifferentiated, entangled — and needs the integrating-factor method of 3. Recognising which shape you hold is the first and most important decision, because it selects the entire solution route; misreading it wastes effort on a method that cannot close.

02 · Separable equations, worked as a method

Take \( \frac{dy}{dx}=xy \): divide by \(y\), multiply by \(dx\), and integrate each side to get \( \ln|y|=\tfrac12 x^2+C \), so \( y=Ae^{x^2/2} \). The constant \(A\) is then fixed by one initial condition — with \( y(0)=1 \), \(A=1\). The pattern is invariant across every separable problem: isolate the variables, integrate both sides, and apply the condition last, after the general solution is in hand. It is cleaner to carry a single constant \(C\) through the integration and resolve it once than to guess its final algebraic form; and it pays to keep the absolute value in \( \ln|y| \) until the sign is settled by the data, because a dropped sign is the commonest error in the whole method.

03 · The linear equation and its integrating factor

For \( y'+p(x)y=q(x) \), multiply through by \( \mu(x)=e^{\int p\,dx} \). The left side then collapses, by the product rule, into a single exact derivative \( \frac{d}{dx}(\mu y) \). This is the whole trick, and it is derived, not asserted: \( \frac{d}{dx}(\mu y)=\mu y'+\mu' y \), and because \( \mu'=p\mu \) by construction, \( \mu y'+p\mu y=\mu q \) is exactly \(\mu\) times the original equation. Integrating once gives \( \mu y=\int \mu q\,dx+C \). For \( y'+2y=4 \), \( \mu=e^{2x} \), so \( (e^{2x}y)'=4e^{2x} \), giving \( e^{2x}y=2e^{2x}+C \) and \( y=2+Ce^{-2x} \). The condition \( y(0)=5 \) fixes \( C=3 \). The factor is not magic — it is precisely the function that makes the product rule run backwards.

04 · Reading the answer: transient and steady state

Every solution above splits the same way — a part that decays and a part that remains. In \( y=2+3e^{-2x} \) the \( 3e^{-2x} \) is the transient, vanishing as \(x\) grows, and the \(2\) is the steady state, the value the system settles to. The steady state of \( y'+py=q \) with constant coefficients is simply \( q/p \), found by setting \(y'=0\); it is worth computing before any integration, because it names the destination, so a full solution that heads anywhere else is wrong and can be caught immediately. This transient-plus-steady decomposition is not a curiosity of these examples — it is the structural signature of every stable first-order system, and it returns in every modelling lesson that follows, which is why first-order linear equations are treated as the backbone of the entire course.

Lesson 02 · Modeling with first-order equations

01 · One balance, three plants

First-order models almost always arise the same way: write a balance for how a quantity accumulates. The statement is always rate of change = rate in − rate out, and everything downstream is bookkeeping. A mixing tank balances the mass of dissolved solute; a cooling body balances thermal energy; an RC circuit balances electric charge. Strikingly, all three collapse to \( \tau y'+y=y_{\text{in}} \) — the same equation with only the names on the symbols changed. That is why one piece of mathematics serves the thermal, fluid, and electrical courses at once, and why an engineer who reads this form fluently can size a response in any of those domains without re-deriving anything.

02 · Newton cooling, end to end

A body at temperature \(T\) sitting in surroundings at \(T_a\) loses heat at a rate proportional to the temperature difference: \( T'=-k(T-T_a) \). This is a linear first-order equation, and its solution (by the integrating factor of Lesson 1) is \( T(t)=T_a+(T_0-T_a)e^{-kt} \). For a part starting at \(T_0=90^\circ\)C in \(T_a=20^\circ\)C air with \(k=0.05\ \text{min}^{-1}\): \( T(10)=20+70e^{-0.5}\approx62.5^\circ\)C. The body forgets its starting temperature exponentially and approaches the ambient value; the excess \( (T_0-T_a) \) is what decays, never the absolute temperature, which is why cooling and heating problems share one formula with only a sign difference in the initial excess.

03 · The time constant, read from the coefficient

Rewrite any of these balances as \( \tau y'+y=y_{\text{in}} \). The coefficient \( \tau \) is the time constant — the time to cover \(1-1/e\), about 63%, of the distance to steady state. It is read straight off the physics: for cooling \( \tau=1/k \); for an RC circuit \( \tau=RC \), so \(R=1000\,\Omega\) with \(C=100\,\mu\text{F}\) gives \( \tau=0.1 \) s and a circuit essentially settled after \(5\tau=0.5\) s; for a stirred tank \( \tau=V/Q \), so a 200-L vessel at 5 L/min has \( \tau=40 \) min. The full time history is rarely needed to answer the question a plant actually asks — "how fast does it respond?" — because that answer is the coefficient itself.

04 · The modelling method

The reusable procedure has three steps. First, name the accumulating quantity and write its balance honestly, accounting for every stream in and out. Second, put the equation into \( \tau y'+y=y_{\text{in}} \) form and read \( \tau \) and the steady state directly, before solving. Third, integrate for a full time history only if one is genuinely required. Units audit the whole model: every term in a balance must carry the same units, and \( \tau \) must emerge with units of time. A time constant that comes out in the wrong units is proof of a wrong or missing term, and it is caught on inspection, before a single number is computed — the cheapest error check in modelling and the habit worth building first. A last discipline ties the modelling together: always state the assumptions the balance rests on — well mixed, constant properties, single dominant stream — because a first-order model is only as trustworthy as those assumptions, and naming them tells the next engineer exactly when the model may be reused and when it must be rebuilt. The same equation that describes a cooling casting will misdescribe a stratified tank, and the difference lives entirely in the assumptions, not the algebra.

05 · One reading, checked in seconds

Bring the three plants back to a single question and answer it without integrating. A 200-L tank stirred at 5 L/min has \( \tau=V/Q=40 \) min, so it is practically settled after \( 5\tau=200 \) min; the RC pair \( R=1000\,\Omega,\ C=100\,\mu\text{F} \) has \( \tau=RC=0.1 \) s and is done in half a second; the cooling part with \( k=0.05\ \text{min}^{-1} \) has \( \tau=1/k=20 \) min. Notice that the shape of the answer — an exponential approach governed by one time constant — is identical in every case, and the only work was reading a coefficient. This is the payoff of the balance-then-normalise method: response speed is available before, and independently of, any full solution, which is exactly the number a plant engineer is usually paid to know.

Lesson 03 · Second-order equations: the homogeneous story

01 · From calculus to algebra

The homogeneous second-order equation with constant coefficients, \( m x''+c x'+k x=0 \), models every mass–spring–damper, and by direct analogy every RLC circuit and every simple oscillator in the curriculum. Its solution is found by a guess that converts calculus into algebra: try \( x=e^{rt} \). Substituting, \( x'=re^{rt} \) and \( x''=r^2e^{rt} \), so the equation becomes \( (mr^2+cr+k)e^{rt}=0 \); and because the exponential never vanishes, the bracket must — the characteristic equation \( mr^2+cr+k=0 \). A differential problem has become a quadratic, which is exactly why the quadratic formula is the working tool of second-order dynamics.

02 · Three regimes, set by the discriminant

The discriminant \( \Delta=c^2-4mk \) sorts the behaviour completely. If \( \Delta>0 \), two distinct real negative roots give \( x=Ae^{r_1t}+Be^{r_2t} \), a pure decay with no oscillation — overdamped. If \( \Delta=0 \), a repeated root gives \( x=(A+Bt)e^{rt} \), the fastest non-oscillating return — critically damped. If \( \Delta<0 \), complex roots \( -\sigma\pm i\omega_d \) give \( x=e^{-\sigma t}(A\cos\omega_d t+B\sin\omega_d t) \), a decaying oscillation — underdamped. For \( x''+5x'+6x=0 \), \( \Delta=25-24=1>0 \) with roots \(-2,-3\): overdamped. For \( x''+2x'+5x=0 \), \( \Delta=-16<0 \) with roots \(-1\pm2i\): underdamped, oscillating at \( \omega_d=2 \) while decaying at rate 1. One number, the discriminant, decides which of three physically distinct worlds you are in.

03 · Natural frequency and damping ratio

Two derived numbers name the system independently of its absolute scale. The natural frequency \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) is the rate it would oscillate with no damping; with \(m=1,k=100\), \( \omega_n=10 \) rad/s. The damping ratio \( \zeta=\frac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}} \) measures the actual damping against the critical amount \( c_c=2\sqrt{mk} \). Here \( c_c=20 \), so a damper \(c=8\) gives \( \zeta=0.4 \) — underdamped, since \( \zeta<1 \). The three regimes restate cleanly and memorably as \( \zeta<1 \) (under), \( \zeta=1 \) (critical), and \( \zeta>1 \) (over), and this pair — \( \omega_n,\zeta \) — is scale-free, so two systems with the same \( \zeta \) behave alike no matter their mass.

04 · The reading method

Given \( m x''+cx'+kx=0 \), the disciplined order is: compute \( \Delta=c^2-4mk \) first, because its sign is the entire qualitative story before a single root is found; then read \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) and \( \zeta=c/(2\sqrt{mk}) \) to put numbers on it. Sign, then magnitudes. This is precisely how vibration and control engineers diagnose a system from its coefficients alone, and it is why \( \zeta \) and \( \omega_n \) are the two numbers every actuator, damper, and structural datasheet reports. The forced version in Lesson 4 adds a particular solution on top of exactly this homogeneous skeleton — so mastering the skeleton first is not optional. It is worth carrying one physical picture for each regime: the overdamped system creeps back to rest like a door with a strong closer, the critically damped one returns as fast as possible without overshooting, and the underdamped one rings before settling like a struck bell. Engineers tune damping toward critical for positioning systems that must not overshoot, and deliberately underdamp instruments meant to respond quickly, so the abstract discriminant maps directly onto a design choice made on real hardware every day. Two systems with identical damping ratio behave alike no matter their absolute size or speed, which is why the datasheet pair — natural frequency and damping ratio — is preferred over the raw coefficients: it is the scale-free fingerprint of the dynamics, portable from a benchtop model to a full machine.

05 · Why two numbers are enough

The pair \( (\omega_n,\zeta) \) is not merely convenient — it is complete for a second-order system, in the sense that every qualitative and quantitative feature of the free response follows from it. The decay rate is \( \sigma=\zeta\omega_n \); the damped oscillation frequency is \( \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2} \); the number of visible cycles before the motion dies scales with \( 1/\zeta \). Two machines with different masses, stiffnesses, and dampers but the same \( \zeta \) trace the same curve after rescaling time by \( \omega_n \), which is why datasheets and control textbooks quote \( \zeta \) rather than the raw \( m,c,k \). For our worked case \( m=1,k=100,c=8 \): \( \omega_n=10 \), \( \zeta=0.4 \), so \( \omega_d=10\sqrt{1-0.16}\approx9.17 \) rad/s and \( \sigma=4 \). Coefficients in, behaviour out — no integration required to see the whole story.

Lesson 04 · Nonhomogeneous equations and forced response

01 · The structure theorem

A nonhomogeneous equation \( m x''+cx'+kx=f(t) \) carries forcing on the right-hand side. Its general solution is always \( x=x_h+x_p \): the homogeneous solution \(x_h\) from Lesson 3, which carries the two arbitrary constants, plus any single particular solution \(x_p\) that matches the forcing. This is a theorem, not a convenience — the difference of any two full solutions satisfies the homogeneous equation, so every solution differs from any one particular solution only by some \(x_h\). Practically it splits the work cleanly: solve the homogeneous problem for the transient, find one \(x_p\) for the steady behaviour, and add them. The two halves answer two different questions — how the system settles, and where.

02 · Undetermined coefficients

When \(f(t)\) is a constant, polynomial, exponential, or sinusoid, guess an \(x_p\) of the same shape with unknown coefficients and solve for them. Constant forcing \( x''+3x'+2x=10 \): try a constant, so \(x''=x'=0\) and \(2x_p=10\Rightarrow x_p=5\) — the steady state. Ramp forcing \( x''+3x'+2x=2t \): try \(x_p=at+b\); then \(x'_p=a,\ x''_p=0\), giving \(3a+2(at+b)=2t\). Matching powers of \(t\): \(2a=2\Rightarrow a=1\); matching constants: \(3a+2b=0\Rightarrow b=-1.5\). So \(x_p=t-1.5\). The method is entirely mechanical once the trial shape is chosen correctly, which is its great virtue for hand computation.

03 · Transient versus steady state

For \( x''+3x'+2x=10 \) the homogeneous roots are \(-1\) and \(-2\), both stable, so the full solution is \( x=Ae^{-t}+Be^{-2t}+5 \). The exponentials are the transient — the record of how the system approaches its destination — and the \(5\) is the steady state, that destination itself. After a few time constants only the \(5\) survives, regardless of the initial conditions, which is precisely why a steady-state calculation predicts the long-run answer without ever solving the full transient. Controls engineers exploit this split constantly: the steady-state value fixes accuracy, while the transient fixes speed and overshoot, and the two are designed almost separately.

04 · Sinusoidal forcing and the working method

Sinusoidal forcing \(f(t)=F_0\cos\omega t\) produces a sinusoidal \(x_p\) of the same frequency but with shifted phase and scaled amplitude, and that amplitude depends on how close the forcing frequency \(\omega\) sits to the natural frequency \(\omega_n\) — the seed of resonance, developed in Lesson 5. The working method is uniform: (1) solve the homogeneous equation for \(x_h\); (2) guess an \(x_p\) matching the form of \(f\), and if that trial already solves the homogeneous equation, multiply it by \(t\) (or \(t^2\) for a double root); (3) fix the constants from the initial conditions applied to the full \(x_h+x_p\), never to \(x_h\) alone. That last rule is the single most common error in the whole topic, and it is worth restating on every problem until it is automatic. A final caution on stability closes the topic: everything here assumed the homogeneous roots have negative real parts, so the transient decays and a steady state exists. If a root has a positive real part the transient grows without bound and there is no steady state to settle to — the system is unstable, and no particular solution rescues it. Checking that the characteristic roots are stable before trusting a steady-state answer is therefore not a formality but the precondition that makes the whole transient-plus-steady framework meaningful, and it is exactly the check the controls course formalises later.

05 · The resonance the constant hid

The steady part of a forced response is a genuine physical prediction, not an algebra by-product. For constant forcing it is a fixed offset — the \( x_p=5 \) of 2 is where the system sits once the transient dies. For sinusoidal forcing it is an oscillation whose amplitude depends sharply on the driving frequency \( \omega \), and that dependence is the entire subject of Lesson 5: as \( \omega \) approaches \( \omega_n \) the steady amplitude peaks, limited only by damping. The structural point to carry forward is that the split \( x=x_h+x_p \) survives every kind of forcing — the transient \( x_h \) always decays on its own timescale set by \( \zeta,\omega_n \), while \( x_p \) is dictated entirely by \( f(t) \). Keep the two questions — how it settles, and where — permanently separate, and forced problems stop being hard.

Lesson 05 · Free and forced oscillations

01 · The one equation dynamics keeps returning to

Free undamped motion strips the mass–spring–damper to \( m x''+kx=0 \), and its solution is pure oscillation: \( x=A\cos\omega_n t+B\sin\omega_n t \), where the natural frequency \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) is the rate the system rings at when nothing removes energy. For \( m=4 \) kg on \( k=10 \) kN/m, \( \omega_n=\sqrt{10000/4}=50 \) rad/s. This single number is the fingerprint of the system: it is set only by mass and stiffness, never by how hard you hit it, so the ringing pitch of a struck part is a direct read-out of its \( k/m \). Every rotating machine, every suspension, every building has one, and half of vibration engineering is keeping operating speeds away from it.

02 · Adding damping: the ring decays

Real systems lose energy, so restore the damper: \( m x''+cx'+kx=0 \). Using Lesson 3's machinery, the underdamped case (\( \zeta<1 \)) gives \( x=e^{-\sigma t}(A\cos\omega_d t+B\sin\omega_d t) \), a decaying oscillation. Two numbers govern it: the decay rate \( \sigma=\zeta\omega_n \) and the damped frequency \( \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2} \), always a little below the undamped \( \omega_n \). For \( m=1,k=100,c=4 \): \( \omega_n=10 \), \( \zeta=c/(2\sqrt{mk})=0.2 \), so \( \omega_d=10\sqrt{1-0.04}\approx9.80 \) rad/s. Light damping barely shifts the frequency but steadily bleeds the amplitude — which is why a lightly damped structure rings at almost its natural pitch, just fading.

03 · Forcing and resonance

Drive the system with \( f(t)=F_0\cos\omega t \) and, after the transient dies, it responds at the driving frequency with a steady amplitude \( X(\omega)=\dfrac{F_0}{\sqrt{(k-m\omega^2)^2+(c\omega)^2}} \). At low frequency this reduces to the static deflection \( F_0/k \); as \( \omega \) climbs toward \( \omega_n \), the term \( (k-m\omega^2) \) collapses toward zero and the amplitude peaks — resonance. With no damping the peak is infinite; with damping it is finite but can still be large. For \( m=1,k=100,c=4,F_0=10 \) driven exactly at \( \omega=\omega_n=10 \): \( k-m\omega^2=0 \), so \( X=F_0/(c\omega)=10/40=0.25 \) m — twenty-five times the static \( F_0/k=0.1 \). That multiplier is why resonance destroys machines.

04 · Beats, and the working method

When two undamped oscillations of near-equal frequency add, their sum is a fast oscillation at the average frequency inside a slow amplitude envelope at half the difference — beats. Two tones at 11 and 9 rad/s beat at \( |11-9|=2 \) rad/s, the envelope pulsing as the components drift in and out of phase; it is the same mathematics as an unforced system driven just off resonance. The working method for any oscillation problem is fixed: (1) read \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) and \( \zeta \) from the coefficients; (2) if forced, compute the frequency ratio \( \omega/\omega_n \) and evaluate \( X(\omega) \) to see how near resonance you sit; (3) add the decaying transient only if the startup matters. Sit far from \( \omega_n \), or add damping — those are the only two defences resonance respects.

05 · The two defences, made concrete

Resonance respects exactly two remedies, and both are quantitative. Detune: keep the operating frequency \( \omega \) away from \( \omega_n \), because the amplitude \( X(\omega) \) only spikes where \( \omega\approx\omega_n \); doubling the separation shrinks the response fast. Damp: raise \( c \), since at the peak \( X\approx F_0/(c\omega) \) falls in direct proportion. A rotor with \( \omega_n=50 \) rad/s run at \( 50 \) rad/s is at the worst possible point; shifting the speed or adding a damper is not optional tuning but the whole safety margin. This is why every vibration specification lists a natural frequency and a damping ratio — they are the two knobs, and there are no others.

Lesson 06 · Laplace transforms I

01 · What the transform is for

The Laplace transform \( \mathcal{L}\{f\}=F(s)=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-st}f(t)\,dt \) trades a function of time for a function of a new variable \( s \). Its point is not the integral but its consequence: differentiation in \( t \) becomes multiplication in \( s \). A differential equation, which relates a function to its derivatives, therefore turns into an algebraic equation in \( F(s) \) — one you solve by rearrangement, not integration. This is the same strategic move as Lesson 3's \( e^{rt} \) guess, generalised: convert calculus into algebra, work in the easy domain, then convert back. The whole payoff, realised in Lesson 7, is that even discontinuous forcing — a switch thrown, a step applied — is handled by the same bookkeeping.

02 · Building the table from the integral

A few transforms, derived once, cover most problems. For \( f=1 \): \( \int_0^\infty e^{-st}\,dt=1/s \). For \( f=e^{at} \): \( \int_0^\infty e^{-(s-a)t}\,dt=1/(s-a) \), valid for \( s>a \). For \( f=t \): integration by parts gives \( 1/s^2 \), and in general \( \mathcal{L}\{t^n\}=n!/s^{n+1} \), so \( \mathcal{L}\{t^2\}=2/s^3 \). The sinusoids follow from \( e^{i\omega t} \): \( \mathcal{L}\{\cos\omega t\}=s/(s^2+\omega^2) \) and \( \mathcal{L}\{\sin\omega t\}=\omega/(s^2+\omega^2) \), so \( \mathcal{L}\{\sin4t\}=4/(s^2+16) \). These half-dozen entries, plus the two rules below, are the entire working toolkit.

03 · Linearity and the derivative rule

Two properties turn the table into a solver. Linearity: \( \mathcal{L}\{af+bg\}=aF+bG \), so \( \mathcal{L}\{3+2t\}=3/s+2/s^2 \) term by term. The derivative rule is the engine: \( \mathcal{L}\{f'\}=sF-f(0) \), and applied twice, \( \mathcal{L}\{f''\}=s^2F-sf(0)-f'(0) \). Read what this does — each derivative becomes a power of \( s \), and the initial conditions \( f(0),f'(0) \) are folded in automatically, at the transform step, not bolted on at the end. That is the structural advantage over undetermined coefficients: the initial data is built into the algebra from the first line, so there is no separate constant-fixing stage to forget.

04 · Shifting, and the working method

The first shifting theorem, \( \mathcal{L}\{e^{at}f(t)\}=F(s-a) \), says that multiplying by an exponential in time simply shifts the transform's argument. So from \( \mathcal{L}\{t\}=1/s^2 \) we get \( \mathcal{L}\{e^{-2t}t\}=1/(s+2)^2 \) — no new integral. The method for this lesson is to transform, never integrate: (1) break \( f \) into table pieces by linearity; (2) apply the shift if an \( e^{at} \) multiplies a known transform; (3) collect the result as \( F(s) \). Building fluency with the table and these two rules now is what makes Lesson 7 — solving whole initial-value problems — a matter of algebra and a look-up, rather than calculus.

05 · One function, both rules at once

The table plus linearity plus the shift handle combinations directly, with no return to the integral. Take \( f(t)=e^{-2t}(3+t) \). Linearity inside the bracket gives \( \mathcal{L}\{3+t\}=3/s+1/s^2 \); the first shift then replaces \( s \) by \( s+2 \), so \( \mathcal{L}\{e^{-2t}(3+t)\}=\dfrac{3}{s+2}+\dfrac{1}{(s+2)^2} \). Every realistic transform is assembled this way — decompose by linearity, apply the shift to each piece, collect. The discipline to carry forward into Lesson 7 is that you never evaluate \( \int_0^\infty e^{-st}f\,dt \) again after this section; the table and its two rules replace the calculus entirely, which is the whole reason the method is fast.

Lesson 07 · Laplace transforms II: solving problems

01 · The three-step recipe

Laplace turns an initial-value problem into a fixed procedure. (1) Transform every term of the ODE, using \( \mathcal{L}\{y'\}=sY-y(0) \) and \( \mathcal{L}\{y''\}=s^2Y-sy(0)-y'(0) \), so the initial data enters immediately. (2) Solve algebraically for \( Y(s) \) — now just a rational function, obtained by rearrangement, not calculus. (3) Invert \( Y(s) \) back to \( y(t) \) using the table, after breaking it into recognisable pieces. Take \( y'+2y=0,\ y(0)=3 \): transforming gives \( sY-3+2Y=0 \), so \( Y=\dfrac{3}{s+2} \), and inverting the exponential pair gives \( y=3e^{-2t} \). Three mechanical steps replace the whole integrating-factor apparatus.

02 · Partial fractions: the inversion engine

Step 3 almost always needs \( Y(s) \) split into simple fractions the table recognises. For \( \dfrac{1}{s(s+2)} \), write \( \dfrac{A}{s}+\dfrac{B}{s+2} \); clearing denominators, \( 1=A(s+2)+Bs \). Setting \( s=0 \) gives \( A=\tfrac12 \); setting \( s=-2 \) gives \( B=-\tfrac12 \). So \( \dfrac{1}{s(s+2)}=\dfrac{1/2}{s}-\dfrac{1/2}{s+2} \), which inverts term by term to \( \tfrac12-\tfrac12 e^{-2t} \). The cover-up choice of convenient \( s \)-values is the fast route; the point is that every rational \( Y(s) \) decomposes into pieces that are already in the table.

03 · A full problem, start to finish

Solve \( y'+3y=6,\ y(0)=0 \). Transform: \( sY+3Y=6/s \), so \( Y=\dfrac{6}{s(s+3)} \). Partial fractions: \( \dfrac{6}{s(s+3)}=\dfrac{2}{s}-\dfrac{2}{s+3} \). Invert: \( y=2-2e^{-3t} \). Read the answer — the constant \( 2 \) is the steady state (which you could have found instantly as \( q/p=6/3 \)), and \( -2e^{-3t} \) is the transient that carries the solution up from the initial \( y(0)=0 \). For a second-order example \( y''+4y=0 \) with \( y(0)=1,\ y'(0)=0 \), transforming gives \( (s^2+4)Y=s \), so \( Y=s/(s^2+4) \) and \( y=\cos 2t \) — the undamped oscillator, straight from the cosine pair.

04 · Why this method earns its keep

For these smooth problems Laplace merely matches the earlier methods, so the natural question is what it buys. The answer is discontinuous and impulsive forcing: a step voltage switched on at \( t=2 \), a hammer blow modelled as an impulse. Such inputs break undetermined coefficients — there is no single smooth trial shape — but Laplace absorbs them through the unit-step and shifting machinery, transforming a switch into a clean algebraic factor. The working method stays exactly the three steps of 1; only the table grows. Master transform-solve-invert on the smooth cases here, and the same recipe handles the realistic forcing that controls and circuit engineering actually face.

05 · Reading the answer the transform hands back

Once \( y(t) \) is inverted, read it the same way as in the earlier lessons — split into transient and steady parts. In \( y=2-2e^{-3t} \) the \( 2 \) is where the system ends up and the \( -2e^{-3t} \) is how it gets there from \( y(0)=0 \); the pole of \( Y(s) \) at \( s=-3 \) is exactly the decay rate, and the pole at \( s=0 \) supplied the constant. That correspondence — each pole of \( Y(s) \) becoming one term of \( y(t) \) — is the deeper lesson: the location of the poles in the \( s \)-plane is the qualitative behaviour, negative-real poles meaning decay and imaginary poles meaning oscillation. Controls engineering is built on reading stability straight off those pole locations, and it starts here.

Lesson 08 · Systems of equations: the matrix view

01 · From a page of equations to \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \)

A linear system — several equations, several unknowns — is really one object. The pair \( 2x+y=5,\ x+3y=10 \) is the matrix statement \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \) with \( A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix} \), \( \mathbf{x}=\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix} \), \( \mathbf{b}=\begin{pmatrix}5\\10\end{pmatrix} \). The coefficients become the matrix \(A\), the unknowns a vector, and the right-hand sides another vector. This is not just shorthand: it lets a system of any size be reasoned about as a single equation \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \), the same shape as \( ax=b \) in one variable, and it is the language every later lesson — Gaussian elimination, eigenvalues, systems of differential equations — is written in. Learning to read a system as one matrix equation is the entry fee for all of linear algebra.

02 · What the matrix does

The product \( A\mathbf{x} \) combines the rows of \(A\) with \(\mathbf{x}\): row \(i\) dotted into \(\mathbf{x}\) gives entry \(i\). For our \(A\) and \( \mathbf{x}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\3\end{pmatrix} \), the first row gives \( 2(1)+1(3)=5 \) and the second \( 1(1)+3(3)=10 \) — exactly \(\mathbf{b}\), confirming \( (x,y)=(1,3) \) solves the system. The identity matrix \( I \), with ones on the diagonal, leaves any vector unchanged: \( I\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x} \). Matrix multiplication chains these actions, and its order matters — \( AB\neq BA \) in general — because applying two transformations in sequence depends on which comes first. That non-commutativity is a feature, not a quirk; it encodes that rotating then stretching differs from stretching then rotating.

03 · Determinant: does a unique solution exist?

For a \( 2\times2 \) matrix the determinant is \( \det A=ad-bc \). It answers the existence question in one number: if \( \det A\neq0 \), the system \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \) has exactly one solution for every \(\mathbf{b}\); if \( \det A=0 \), the matrix is singular and the system has either none or infinitely many. Our \( A \) has \( \det=2\cdot3-1\cdot1=5\neq0 \), so its solution \( (1,3) \) is unique. By contrast \( \begin{pmatrix}1&2\\2&4\end{pmatrix} \) has \( \det=1\cdot4-2\cdot2=0 \): its second row is twice the first, the equations are secretly the same line, and uniqueness is lost. The determinant is the single fastest test of a system's solvability.

04 · The inverse, and the working method

When \( \det A\neq0 \) the inverse exists and solves the system in closed form: \( \mathbf{x}=A^{-1}\mathbf{b} \). For \( 2\times2 \), \( A^{-1}=\dfrac{1}{\det A}\begin{pmatrix}d&-b\\-c&a\end{pmatrix} \), so here \( A^{-1}=\dfrac15\begin{pmatrix}3&-1\\-1&2\end{pmatrix} \); multiplying by \( \mathbf{b}=(5,10) \) returns \( (1,3) \). The disciplined method is: (1) write the system as \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \); (2) compute \( \det A \) — if zero, stop and analyse for no/infinite solutions; (3) for small systems apply the inverse or Cramer's rule, and for larger ones use the elimination of Lesson 9. Computing the determinant first is the cheap gate that tells you whether a unique answer is even on the table.

05 · Why the matrix view scales

Packaging a system as \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \) is not cosmetic — it is what makes the rest of the course possible. Written this way, a system of two equations and one of two hundred have the same form, so a single method (the elimination of Lesson 9) and a single test (the determinant, generalised to rank) cover both. The same \(A\) that solves a static system reappears as the coefficient matrix of \( \mathbf{x}'=A\mathbf{x} \) in the differential-equations lessons, where its eigenvalues decide stability. Reading a wall of coupled equations as one object \(A\) acting on one vector \(\mathbf{x}\) is the habit that turns an unmanageable page of algebra into a problem you can actually reason about.

Lesson 09 · Matrices and linear systems

01 · Elimination: the universal method

Cramer's rule and the inverse are elegant for \( 2\times2 \) systems but grow impossibly expensive with size. The method that scales is Gaussian elimination: use row operations to carve the system into an upper-triangular staircase, then read the unknowns off by back-substitution. Three operations are legal because none changes the solution set — swap two rows, scale a row by a nonzero constant, or add a multiple of one row to another. The strategy is to use the first equation to remove the first unknown from all the others, then the second to remove the second unknown from those below it, and so on, until the last equation involves a single unknown. Every computational linear-algebra library does exactly this.

02 · A 3×3 system, worked

Solve \( x+y+z=6,\ 2x+y-z=1,\ x-y+2z=5 \). Subtract \(2\times\)row 1 from row 2: \( -y-3z=-11 \). Subtract row 1 from row 3: \( -2y+z=-1 \). Now eliminate \(y\): subtract \(2\times\)the new row 2 from the new row 3, giving \( 7z=21 \), so \( z=3 \). Back-substitute into \( -y-3z=-11 \): \( -y-9=-11\Rightarrow y=2 \). Finally row 1: \( x+2+3=6\Rightarrow x=1 \). The solution is \( (x,y,z)=(1,2,3) \), and substituting back into all three originals confirms \( 6,1,5 \) exactly. The staircase turned a coupled system into three one-line reads.

03 · Pivots, rank, and when there is no unique answer

The nonzero leading entries the method produces are the pivots, and their count is the rank of the matrix — the number of genuinely independent equations. A \( 3\times3 \) system with three pivots has a unique solution. Fewer pivots means a row has collapsed to \( 0=0 \) (a redundant equation, giving infinitely many solutions) or to \( 0=c \) with \( c\neq0 \) (a contradiction, giving none). The matrix \( \begin{pmatrix}1&2\\2&4\end{pmatrix} \), with \( \det=0 \), has rank 1: elimination turns its second row into zeros, exposing that the two equations were one. Rank, read straight off the pivots, is how elimination reports the solvability that the determinant only summarised.

04 · The working method

The procedure is fixed and mechanical: (1) form the augmented matrix \( [A\,|\,\mathbf{b}] \); (2) sweep top-to-bottom, using each pivot to zero the entries below it, swapping rows if a pivot is zero; (3) once triangular, back-substitute bottom-to-top. Two habits guard accuracy — pick the largest available pivot at each step so you never divide by a tiny number (this is partial pivoting, the reason the method is numerically stable), and count pivots at the end to declare the rank before trusting a "unique solution." Elimination is not one trick among many; it is the single algorithm behind solving systems, inverting matrices, and computing determinants at any size.

05 · Elimination as the engine underneath

It is worth seeing how much rests on this one algorithm. The determinant of any square matrix is the product of the pivots elimination produces (with a sign for row swaps); a matrix inverse is found by running elimination on \( [A\,|\,I] \) until the left block becomes \(I\); and the rank that decides solvability is just the pivot count. So the three separate-looking operations of Lesson 8 — solve, invert, take a determinant — are one procedure seen from three angles. This is also why numerical libraries implement elimination once, carefully, and build everything else on it: get the sweep-and-back-substitute loop right, with partial pivoting for stability, and the whole of computational linear algebra follows.

Lesson 10 · Eigenvalues and eigenvectors

01 · The special directions of a matrix

Most vectors are turned when a matrix acts on them. A few special ones are only stretched, keeping their direction: these are the eigenvectors, and the stretch factor is the eigenvalue. The defining relation is \( A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v} \) with \( \mathbf{v}\neq\mathbf{0} \). It says the complicated action of \(A\) reduces, along \(\mathbf{v}\), to simple multiplication by the number \(\lambda\). Finding these directions diagonalises a problem — it picks the coordinates in which the matrix does nothing but scale — and that is why eigenvalues govern the stability of dynamic systems, the resonant modes of structures, and the principal axes of stress. The whole subject is the search for the directions a matrix leaves alone.

02 · The characteristic equation

Rewrite \( A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v} \) as \( (A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0} \). A nonzero \(\mathbf{v}\) can satisfy this only if \( A-\lambda I \) is singular, i.e. \( \det(A-\lambda I)=0 \) — the characteristic equation. For a \( 2\times2 \) matrix it is always \( \lambda^2-(\operatorname{tr}A)\lambda+\det A=0 \), using the trace (sum of diagonal) and determinant. Take \( A=\begin{pmatrix}4&1\\2&3\end{pmatrix} \): \( \operatorname{tr}=7,\ \det=10 \), so \( \lambda^2-7\lambda+10=0 \), giving \( \lambda=2 \) and \( \lambda=5 \). A quick sanity check is built in — the eigenvalues must sum to the trace (\(2+5=7\)) and multiply to the determinant (\(2\times5=10\)).

03 · Finding the eigenvectors

Each eigenvalue has its own direction, found by solving \( (A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0} \). For \( A=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\-2&-3\end{pmatrix} \) the characteristic equation is \( \lambda^2+3\lambda+2=0 \), so \( \lambda=-1,-2 \). For \( \lambda=-1 \), \( A-\lambda I=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\-2&-2\end{pmatrix} \); the equation \( v_1+v_2=0 \) gives the direction \( \mathbf{v}=(1,-1) \). Check: \( A(1,-1)^\top=(0\cdot1+1\cdot(-1),\,-2\cdot1-3\cdot(-1))=(-1,1)=-1\cdot(1,-1) \) ✓. Eigenvectors come as directions, not unique vectors — any nonzero multiple works — because the relation scales freely. What matters is the line the eigenvector picks out.

04 · Why engineers compute eigenvalues

The payoff is the coupled system of differential equations \( \mathbf{x}'=A\mathbf{x} \), the multi-variable successor to Lesson 3. Its solutions are built from \( e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v} \) — one exponential mode per eigenvalue, evolving along its eigenvector. The sign of each eigenvalue's real part then decides stability at a glance: all negative means every mode decays and the system is stable; any positive means a mode grows and it is unstable; imaginary parts set oscillation. This is the direct generalisation of "roots of the characteristic equation" from second-order scalar ODEs to systems, and it is why a controls or vibrations engineer reads a system's fate straight from the spectrum of its matrix.

05 · Reading a system's fate from its spectrum

The set of eigenvalues — the spectrum of \(A\) — is a compact summary of everything \( \mathbf{x}'=A\mathbf{x} \) will do. Real negative eigenvalues give decaying modes; a positive one gives a growing, unstable mode; a complex conjugate pair \( \sigma\pm i\omega \) gives an oscillation at frequency \(\omega\) whose envelope decays if \( \sigma<0 \) and grows if \( \sigma>0 \). This is the exact multi-variable echo of Lesson 3, where the roots of the scalar characteristic equation played the same role: negative real parts meant a settling system, imaginary parts meant ringing. An engineer handed a matrix therefore computes its eigenvalues first — the signs of their real parts answer the only question that usually matters, will it stay bounded, before any trajectory is drawn.

Lesson 11 · Numerical solution of differential equations

01 · When no formula exists

Most differential equations that arise in practice have no closed-form solution — the tidy exponentials and sinusoids of the earlier lessons are the exception, not the rule. When a formula is unavailable, the answer is computed numerically: step forward in small increments of the independent variable, using the differential equation itself to predict each next value. The simplest scheme is Euler's method. Given \( y'=f(x,y) \) and a starting point, take \( y_{n+1}=y_n+h\,f(x_n,y_n) \), where \(h\) is the step size. Geometrically it follows the tangent line for one short step, then re-reads the slope and repeats. It is crude but it is the seed of every professional solver.

02 · Euler worked, with its error

Solve \( y'=y,\ y(0)=1 \) (exact answer \( e^x \)) with \( h=0.1 \). First step: \( y_1=1+0.1\cdot1=1.1 \). The exact value is \( e^{0.1}\approx1.10517 \), so Euler is low by about \( 0.00517 \). Second step: \( y_2=1.1+0.1\cdot1.1=1.21 \), against \( e^{0.2}\approx1.2214 \). The error accumulates because each step starts from an already-approximate value and uses the slope only at the step's beginning, so any curvature over the step is missed. Euler is a first-order method: halve \(h\) and the global error roughly halves — reliable improvement, but slow, since ten-fold accuracy costs ten-fold work.

03 · Better slopes: Heun and Runge–Kutta

The fix is to sample the slope more than once per step. Improved Euler (Heun's method) predicts with Euler, then averages the slope at the start and the predicted end: for the same problem and step, \( y_1=1+\tfrac{0.1}{2}(1+1.1)=1.105 \), already far closer to \(1.10517\). The workhorse of real computation is the fourth-order Runge–Kutta (RK4), which blends four slope samples per step; on this problem one RK4 step gives \(1.105171\), matching \( e^{0.1} \) to six figures. RK4 is fourth-order — halving \(h\) cuts the error roughly sixteen-fold — so it buys accuracy far more cheaply than Euler, which is why it is the default in engineering solvers.

04 · Step size, stability, and the method

Smaller steps mean less truncation error but more arithmetic and more accumulated round-off, so there is a practical sweet spot rather than "smaller is always better." Some problems are additionally stiff — they contain very fast and very slow modes together — and force explicit methods like Euler and RK4 to take punishingly tiny steps for stability, which is where implicit solvers take over. The working method is: (1) write \( y'=f(x,y) \) and the initial value; (2) choose a scheme by the accuracy needed — Euler to understand, RK4 to compute; (3) pick \(h\), then halve it and re-run to confirm the answer has stopped moving. That convergence check — does the solution stabilise as \(h\) shrinks — is the numerical analyst's substitute for an exact answer to compare against.

05 · Trading accuracy against cost

The choice among Euler, Heun, and RK4 is a deliberate trade. Euler costs one slope evaluation per step but needs tiny steps for accuracy; RK4 costs four evaluations per step yet reaches a target accuracy in so many fewer steps that it is cheaper overall, which is why it is the default. The order of a method is exactly this exchange rate: first-order Euler must do ~10× the work for 10× the accuracy, while fourth-order RK4 gets ~16× the accuracy from merely halving the step. The professional habit is to trust neither a single run nor the smallest possible step blindly, but to halve \(h\), re-run, and watch the answer converge — the same "does it stop moving?" test that stands in for an exact solution whenever, as is usual, no formula exists.

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Stress and strain under axial load

01 · Stress: force made local

A load of a few kilonewtons means nothing to a material until it is spread over an area. Normal stress \( \sigma = P/A \) is that intensity — force per unit area, in pascals (N/m²), or in engineering practice megapascals. A 100 kN pull on a 25 mm round bar (\( A=\tfrac{\pi}{4}d^2=490.9\ \text{mm}^2 \)) is \( \sigma=100{,}000/490.9\times10^{-6}\approx204\ \text{MPa} \). The same force on a thicker bar is a gentler stress. Stress, not force, is what a material feels, and it is the quantity every allowable is written against.

02 · Strain: deformation made dimensionless

Normal strain \( \varepsilon = \delta/L \) is the stretch per unit length — a pure number. It divides out the specimen size so that a 1 mm stretch in a 1 m bar (\( \varepsilon=0.001 \)) and a 2 mm stretch in a 2 m bar describe the same material state. This normalisation is why stress and strain, not force and elongation, are the axes of the material's fingerprint: they belong to the material, not to the part.

03 · Hooke's law and the elastic contract

Over the initial straight portion of the tensile curve, stress is proportional to strain: \( \sigma = E\varepsilon \). The slope \(E\) is Young's modulus — steel's is about 200 GPa — a stiffness the material carries regardless of shape. Combining the three relations gives the workhorse deformation formula \( \delta = \dfrac{PL}{AE} \). An 80 kN load on a 2 m steel bar of 20 mm diameter stretches it \( \delta=\dfrac{80{,}000\times2}{314\times10^{-6}\times200\times10^9} \approx2.55\ \text{mm} \). Longer and softer stretches more; thicker and stiffer stretches less — all four factors sit in one fraction.

04 · Reading the tensile curve as a contract

The tensile test is the material's signed contract. The proportional limit ends Hooke's law; the yield strength marks the onset of permanent deformation — the number most designs are kept below; the ultimate strength is the peak the specimen can carry; and the strain at fracture measures ductility, the warning a part gives before it breaks. Design keeps the working stress a chosen margin below yield, expressed as a factor of safety \( \text{FoS}=\sigma_{\text{yield}}/\sigma_{\text{allow}} \): a 250 MPa yield held to a 160 MPa allowable is a FoS of about 1.56.

05 · Why this lesson is the foundation of the course

Every later topic — torsion, bending, combined loading, buckling, fatigue — is this same logic applied to a different stress: define the stress from the load and geometry, relate it to a strain through an elastic law, and compare against an allowable drawn from the tensile contract. The stress–strain diagram is therefore not one lesson among many but the template for all of them. Master reading it — where the straight part ends, how much warning the ductility gives, what margin the factor of safety buys — and the rest of Strength of Materials becomes variations on a theme you already understand. The habit of converting a raw force into a local stress, and only then judging safety, is the single most important reflex the course installs.

Lesson 02 · Axial members and thermal stress

01 · Bars in series: deformations add

Real members are rarely one uniform prism. A stepped bar — segments of different area or load — deforms by the sum of its parts: \( \delta=\sum \dfrac{P_iL_i}{A_iE_i} \). Each segment carries its own internal force (found by a free-body cut) over its own length and area. The method is bookkeeping: cut, find \(P_i\), compute each \( \delta_i \), add with sign. Tension lengthens (+), compression shortens (−); the signs must be respected or a stretch and a squeeze will wrongly cancel.

02 · Statically indeterminate axial problems

When a bar is held at both ends, statics alone cannot find the internal forces — there are more unknown reactions than equilibrium equations. The missing equation is compatibility: a geometric statement about how the deformations must fit, usually "the total length change is zero" or "the two segments share the same displacement." Pairing equilibrium with compatibility and Hooke's law closes the problem. This trio — equilibrium, compatibility, material law — is the general recipe for every indeterminate structure in the curriculum, first met here in its simplest axial form.

03 · Thermal strain and free expansion

Temperature changes strain a material even with no load: \( \varepsilon_T=\alpha\,\Delta T \), where \( \alpha \) is the coefficient of thermal expansion (steel \(\approx12\times10^{-6} /^\circ\text{C}\)). A 50 °C rise gives \( \varepsilon_T=600\times10^{-6} \) — a free bar simply grows by \( \alpha\,\Delta T\,L \) and feels no stress. Thermal strain is real deformation; it becomes a stress only when something stops it.

04 · Thermal stress: expansion against restraint

Restrain that bar between rigid walls so it cannot grow, and the thermal strain is converted entirely into mechanical strain of the opposite sign — a compressive stress \( \sigma=E\alpha\,\Delta T \). For steel over 50 °C: \( \sigma=200\times10^9\times12\times10^{-6}\times50=120\ \text{MPa} \), independent of the bar's length or area. That length-independence surprises students and explains real failures: a fully restrained pipe run heated 50 °C carries 120 MPa whether it is one metre or one hundred. Expansion joints, loops, and sliding supports exist precisely to give thermal strain somewhere to go before it becomes stress.

05 · The engineering lesson

The pattern to carry forward: deformation is additive over segments, indeterminate problems need compatibility, and temperature is just another source of strain that turns into stress only under restraint. The thermal case is the embryo of the entire piping- and pressure-stress world of later courses — where a plant's thermal cycling, not its pressure, is often the fatigue driver. An engineer who instinctively asks "is this free to expand, or restrained?" before worrying about the temperature itself has absorbed the core of this lesson. The arithmetic is easy; the modelling judgement — free versus restrained, determinate versus indeterminate — is the skill. It is worth stressing the counter-intuitive part once more: thermal stress is set by the temperature change, the material, and the degree of restraint alone, never by how long the member is. A designer who adds length hoping to relieve a restrained thermal load achieves nothing; only giving the expansion room — a loop, a joint, a sliding support — actually lowers the stress. That single insight prevents a whole class of piping and structural failures.

Lesson 03 · Torsion of shafts

01 · Twisting a shaft

A driveline transmits power by torque, and torque twists. For a circular shaft the torsion formula \( \tau=\dfrac{Tr}{J} \) gives the shear stress at radius \(r\), where \(J\) is the polar second moment of area — \( J=\dfrac{\pi d^4}{32} \) for a solid shaft. Stress is zero on the axis and maximum at the surface, so shafts are loaded most where they can be inspected. The surface stress can be written directly as \( \tau_{\max}=\dfrac{16T}{\pi d^3} \), the sizing formula an engineer reaches for first.

02 · Angle of twist

How much a shaft rotates end-to-end is \( \varphi=\dfrac{TL}{GJ} \), the torsional twin of \( \delta=PL/AE \): torque replaces force, the shear modulus \(G\) replaces \(E\), and \(J\) replaces \(A\). The parallel is exact and worth holding onto — axial and torsional members obey the same load–stiffness–geometry logic. Excess twist matters even when stress is safe: a shaft that winds up too much upsets timing, gear meshing, and control response.

03 · Power, torque, and speed

Rotating machines are rated in power, but shafts are sized by torque, so the link \( P=T\omega \) is used constantly. With speed in rev/min, \( \omega=\dfrac{2\pi N}{60} \). A 15 kW motor at 1500 rev/min turns at \( \omega=157.1\ \text{rad/s} \) and delivers \( T=P/\omega=15{,}000/157.1=95.5\ \text{N·m} \). Halving the speed for the same power doubles the torque — which is why low-speed drives need fat shafts, and why a gearbox that reduces speed must be built for the torque it raises.

04 · Sizing and reading failures

To size a solid shaft, invert the surface-stress formula: \( d=\left(\dfrac{16T}{\pi\tau_{\text{allow}}}\right)^{1/3} \) — the cube root is why a modest torque increase needs only a small diameter increase, and why hollow shafts (most material at large radius, where it works hardest) are so efficient. Torsional failures read their own history: a ductile shaft shears flat across the section (maximum shear on the transverse plane), while a brittle shaft breaks on a 45° helix (maximum tension on the diagonal). The fracture surface names the failure mode before any calculation. This stress-at-a-radius reasoning returns directly in the rotating-equipment and machine-design courses, where shaft sizing is a daily task.

05 · Why torsion generalises the axial story

Torsion is the second instance of the course's master pattern, and seeing it as such is the point: a load (torque) produces a stress (shear) that varies with geometry (\(r\) and \(J\)), relates to a deformation (twist) through a material stiffness (\(G\)), and is judged against an allowable. Where axial members spread force over an area, shafts spread torque over a radius distribution — but the modelling grammar is identical. An engineer who has internalised \( \delta=PL/AE \) already half-knows \( \varphi=TL/GJ \); the remaining work is recognising which geometry factor plays the role of \(A\). That transfer of structure, lesson to lesson, is what turns a list of formulas into a single competence. The same recognition makes the hollow-shaft insight obvious rather than memorised: because stress rises with radius, material near the axis barely earns its weight, so removing it costs little strength and saves much mass — the reason drive shafts and structural tubes are hollow by default.

Lesson 04 · Bending and transverse shear in beams

01 · Bending stress from plane sections

A beam resists transverse load by bending, and bending puts one face in tension and the other in compression, with a neutral axis of zero stress between. Assuming plane sections stay plane, strain varies linearly with distance \(y\) from the neutral axis, and so — through Hooke's law — does stress. Integrating that stress into a resisting moment gives the flexure formula \( \sigma=\dfrac{My}{I} \), where \(I\) is the second moment of area of the cross-section. Stress is largest at the extreme fibre, farthest from the neutral axis.

02 · Section modulus, the designer's currency

Evaluating the flexure formula at the extreme fibre \(c\) gives \( \sigma_{\max}=\dfrac{M}{S} \), where \( S=I/c \) is the section modulus — a single number that captures a section's bending efficiency. A rectangular section \(b\times h\) has \( I=\dfrac{bh^3}{12} \) and \( S=\dfrac{bh^2}{6} \). For \(b=50\), \(h=100\) mm: \( I=4.17\times10^6\ \text{mm}^4 \), \( S=83{,}333\ \text{mm}^3 \), so a 10 kN·m moment gives \( \sigma=M/S=10\times10^6/83{,}333=120\ \text{MPa} \). Because \(S\propto h^2\), depth matters far more than width — the reason beams are tall, not fat.

03 · Transverse shear and shear flow

Bending rarely comes alone; the transverse load also produces a shear stress \( \tau=\dfrac{VQ}{Ib} \), where \(V\) is the shear force, \(Q\) the first moment of the area beyond the level of interest, and \(b\) the width there. Shear stress is greatest at the neutral axis — opposite to bending stress — which is why an I-beam puts material in flanges (far out, for bending) and a thin web at the centre (to carry shear). The related idea of shear flow \( q=VQ/I \) sizes the fasteners or welds that hold a built-up section together.

04 · Designing a beam

The design task inverts the flexure formula: for a required moment and an allowable stress, the section must supply \( S\ge M/\sigma_{\text{allow}} \). Choosing the lightest section that meets this — a deep, thin-webbed shape — is the everyday work of structural and machine design, and \(S\) is the number tabulated for every standard beam. Bending stress usually governs, but the shear check at the neutral axis must not be skipped for short, heavily loaded beams, where shear can control. Reading which of the two governs — long slender beams by bending, short stubby ones by shear — is the judgement this lesson trains.

05 · Bending as the course's capstone stress

Bending combines everything before it: an internal moment (from statics), a linear stress distribution (from plane sections and Hooke's law), a geometry factor (\(I\) and \(S\)), and an allowable (from the tensile contract). It is the axial and torsional grammar applied to a stress that varies across the section rather than being uniform or radial. Mastering the section modulus — why depth is cubed into \(I\) but squared into \(S\), why beams are oriented tall — equips the machine-design and structures courses that follow, where beam selection is routine. The through-line of Strength of Materials is now complete: define the stress, relate it to strain, judge it against an allowable — for axial, torsional, and bending members alike.

Lesson 05 · Beam deflection

01 · The elastic curve, from curvature

Beam curvature relates directly to bending moment, \( 1/\rho=M/(EI) \), and for the small deflections this course works with, curvature is well approximated by the second derivative of the deflection curve, giving the governing equation \[ EIv''=M(x). \] Integrating once gives slope, \( EIv'=\int M\,dx+C_1 \); integrating again gives deflection, \( EIv=\iint M\,dx\,dx+C_1x+C_2 \) — with the constants fixed by the actual boundary conditions at the supports.

02 · A worked cantilever, and reading the shape

For a cantilever fixed at \(x=0\) with a point load \(P\) at the free tip \(x=L\): \[ v(x)=\frac{P}{6EI}\left(3Lx^2-x^3\right), \] with maximum deflection at the tip, \[ \delta=v(L)=\frac{PL^3}{3EI}. \] The shape reflects both boundary conditions used at the wall — zero deflection and zero slope — curving away smoothly and accelerating toward the free end.

03 · Superposition — why it works

Because \(EIv''=M(x)\) is linear in \(v\), the deflection from several loads acting together is simply the sum of the deflections each would cause acting alone — superposition. This turns most practical beam-deflection problems from re-deriving a new differential-equation solution every time into a lookup-and-add exercise: break the actual loading into pieces matching a table of standard cases (cantilever tip load, cantilever uniform load, simply-supported centre load, and so on), find each piece's deflection, and add them.

04 · Stiffness as a distinct failure mode

A beam can be entirely safe against stress failure — well below yield — and still fail the actual application because it deflects too much: a machinery baseplate that flexes enough to misalign a coupling, a walkway that feels unacceptably springy. Stiffness, governed by \(EI\) and geometry, is a separate design criterion from strength, governed by section modulus and allowable stress (Lesson 4) — a design can be strength-limited or stiffness-limited, and which one actually governs must be checked explicitly, never assumed. Working method: (1) identify loading and supports, matching or superposing standard cases; (2) check both bending stress and deflection against whatever serviceability limit applies, often a fraction of span such as \(L/360\); (3) whichever check is more restrictive governs the real design.

Key result — cantilever tip deflection

\[ \delta = \frac{PL^3}{3EI} \]

Stiffness (this) and strength (Lesson 4) are separate checks — either can govern.

Lesson 06 · Combined loading

01 · Superposing stress types at a point

When axial load, torsion, bending, and pressure act on the same member, their stresses superpose at any given point — but only because each formula (\(\sigma=P/A\), \(\sigma=My/I\), \(\tau=Tr/J\)) is itself linear in its load, the same linearity behind Lesson 5's deflection superposition, now applied to stress instead. The essential discipline: superposing stress means tracking not just magnitude but which component and which face — normal stresses add to normal stresses on the same face; shear stresses add to shear stresses on the same face; the two types are never simply summed together as if they were one quantity.

02 · Finding stresses at a point on a shaft

The classic case: a shaft transmitting torque \(T\) while also carrying a bending moment \(M\) — from an off-axis pulley belt load, say — at the same cross-section. At the outer-surface point where bending stress is greatest, \( \sigma=32M/(\pi d^3) \) (normal, from bending) coexists with \( \tau=16T/(\pi d^3) \) (shear, from torsion) — a genuine two-component stress state at one point, not yet reduced to a single number, ready for stress transformation (next lesson) or a failure theory (two lessons ahead). If the shaft also carries an axial load \(P\), the direct stress \(\sigma=P/A\) adds to the bending normal stress on that same face, while the torsional shear stays a separate component entirely.

03 · Hunting the critical point

The critical point — where the combined stress state is worst — is not always obvious, because the individual stress components do not necessarily peak at the same location. Bending stress peaks at the outer fibre at the section of maximum moment; torsional shear is uniform around a circular shaft's circumference wherever torque is constant. If a stress concentration — a keyway, a fillet — sits nearby, that location can dominate even where the nominal stresses are lower, since the concentration factor multiplies whatever nominal stress exists there. Working method: identify two or three candidate points — the maximum-moment location, any stress-concentration site, any point where multiple components peak together — and evaluate the full combined state at each, rather than assuming the answer.

04 · A shaft-with-pulley pattern

Given a shaft of diameter \(d\) transmitting torque \(T\) while carrying bending moment \(M\) from an off-axis load at a specific section: (1) compute \( \sigma=32M/(\pi d^3) \) and \( \tau=16T/(\pi d^3) \) at that section's outer surface, using \(I=\pi d^4/64\) and \(J=\pi d^4/32\) substituted into the flexure and torsion formulas; (2) add any axial or other normal stress present at that same point; (3) the resulting \((\sigma,\tau)\) state is the direct starting point for the next two lessons — transformation, then a failure criterion.

Key formulas — solid circular shaft

\[ \sigma=\frac{32M}{\pi d^3},\qquad \tau=\frac{16T}{\pi d^3}. \]

Lesson 07 · Stress transformation and Mohr's circle

01 · Plane-stress transformation, derived

Given a stress state \((\sigma_x,\sigma_y,\tau_{xy})\) on the standard faces, equilibrium of a wedge cut at angle \(\theta\) gives the stress on that rotated face directly: \[ \sigma_{\theta}=\frac{\sigma_x+\sigma_y}{2}+\frac{\sigma_x-\sigma_y}{2}\cos2\theta+\tau_{xy}\sin2\theta, \] with a parallel expression for \(\tau_\theta\). These follow from summing forces on a triangular element — genuinely derived, not merely asserted.

02 · Mohr’s circle as the graphical engine

The transformation equations are exactly the parametric equations of a circle in \((\sigma,\tau)\) space: centre at \(\left(\frac{\sigma_x+\sigma_y}{2},0\right)\), radius \[ R=\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_x-\sigma_y}{2}\right)^2+\tau_{xy}^2}. \] Every possible stress state at that point, for every possible face orientation, lies somewhere on this one circle. Reading it: plot \((\sigma_x,\tau_{xy})\) for the x-face and \((\sigma_y,-\tau_{xy})\) for the y-face — diametrically opposite points — and any other orientation is a rotation around the same circle, by \(2\theta\), not \(\theta\): the graphical angle doubles the physical one, a classic point of confusion worth fixing early.

03 · Principal stresses and maximum shear

Principal stresses — where shear is zero — occur where the circle crosses the \(\sigma\)-axis: \( \sigma_{1,2}=\text{centre}\pm R \). Maximum in-plane shear equals the radius \(R\) itself, occurring \(90^\circ\) around the circle — \(45^\circ\) physically — from the principal directions. This is exactly why fatigue cracks often pick planes offset \(45^\circ\) from the obvious loading direction: many failure mechanisms, especially in ductile materials, are shear-driven, so they follow the maximum-shear orientation, not the maximum-normal-stress one.

04 · Working method

(1) Identify \(\sigma_x\), \(\sigma_y\), \(\tau_{xy}\) at the point of interest, typically from a combined-loading analysis (Lesson 6). (2) Compute the centre and radius. (3) Principal stresses are centre \(\pm R\). (4) Maximum shear is \(R\), acting at \(45^\circ\) from the principal directions. This is the direct bridge from "here are the stresses on an arbitrary cut" to "here is the worst normal stress and the worst shear stress, and the planes they act on" — the necessary input before any failure theory (Lesson 9) can be applied.

Key results — Mohr’s circle

\[ \text{centre}=\frac{\sigma_x+\sigma_y}{2},\qquad R=\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_x-\sigma_y}{2}\right)^2+\tau_{xy}^2},\qquad \sigma_{1,2}=\text{centre}\pm R. \]

Lesson 08 · Pressure vessels: thin-walled theory

01 · Hoop and axial stress, derived from equilibrium

For a thin-walled cylinder (\(r/t\gtrsim10\)), cutting it longitudinally and balancing internal pressure pushing the two halves apart against the wall's tensile stress holding them together gives \[ \sigma_{hoop}=\frac{pr}{t}. \] Cutting it transversely instead, balancing pressure on an end cap against the wall's axial stress around the thin circumference, gives \[ \sigma_{axial}=\frac{pr}{2t}. \] The ratio \(\sigma_{hoop}/\sigma_{axial}=2\) always — the pressure, radius, and thickness all cancel out of that ratio, leaving a result that holds for any thin cylindrical vessel whatsoever.

02 · What the 2:1 ratio predicts

Since hoop stress is the larger of the two, a cylindrical vessel failing from pure overpressure is expected to crack longitudinally — along the axis — because a longitudinal crack is opened by the larger hoop stress, while a circumferential crack, driven only by the smaller axial stress, needs a higher pressure to propagate. This is a genuine, testable prediction that matches observed pipe and vessel failures, not just a bookkeeping result.

03 · Spherical vessels, briefly

A sphere has no preferred direction by symmetry — cutting it any way gives the identical equilibrium, \( \sigma=pr/2t \) — the same value as a cylinder's lower, axial stress, in every direction. This is exactly why spherical pressure vessels are the material-efficient choice for the highest-pressure applications: no direction is "wasted" carrying only half the stress the way a cylinder's axial direction is, so a sphere uses less material for the same pressure and volume than an equivalent cylinder.

04 · When thin-wall theory stops being honest

The \(r/t>10\) rule of thumb is the honesty boundary. Below it, stress varies significantly through the wall thickness — higher at the inner surface than the outer — and the simple average formulas above increasingly understate the true peak stress; thick-wall (Lamé) theory takes over from there. Working method: (1) check \(r/t\) before applying any formula; (2) if thin-wall applies, compute both stresses and remember the 2:1 relationship; (3) predict crack orientation from whichever stress is genuinely higher.

Key ratio — thin-walled cylinder

\[ \sigma_{hoop}=\frac{pr}{t},\qquad \sigma_{axial}=\frac{pr}{2t},\qquad \frac{\sigma_{hoop}}{\sigma_{axial}}=2\ \text{always}. \]

Lesson 09 · Failure theories

01 · Tresca and von Mises for ductile metals

Ductile metals yield primarily by shear — dislocation slip — and both theories are built around that mechanism, differing in how they combine multiple stress components into one comparable value. Tresca (maximum shear stress theory) predicts yield when \( \sigma_1-\sigma_3\geq\sigma_y \) (max minus min principal stress), since in a simple tension test at yield, \(\sigma_1-\sigma_3=\sigma_y-0=\sigma_y\) exactly. Geometrically, Tresca's yield locus in principal-stress space is a hexagon. Von Mises (distortion-energy theory) predicts yield when \( \sigma_1^2-\sigma_1\sigma_2+\sigma_2^2\geq\sigma_y^2 \) (plane stress). Geometrically this is an ellipse circumscribing Tresca's hexagon, touching it only at the six uniaxial-stress corners and bulging outward everywhere else — von Mises always predicts equal or higher allowable stress than Tresca for the same state.

02 · Why von Mises predicts yielding better

Experimental ductile-metal yield data sits between the two curves, but noticeably closer to the von Mises ellipse — von Mises is the more accurate predictor. Tresca remains popular anyway, precisely because it is simpler to compute and conservative: a deliberate engineering trade, accepting some extra safety margin for calculation simplicity and certainty of staying on the safe side. For brittle materials, neither shear-based theory applies well; maximum-normal-stress theory — yield or fracture when the largest principal stress alone reaches ultimate strength — is appropriate instead, because brittle materials fail by flaw-driven crack propagation under tension, not shear-driven slip.

03 · Pure shear — a clean test case

For pure shear (\(\sigma_1=\tau\), \(\sigma_2=-\tau\)): Tresca predicts yield at \(\sigma_1-\sigma_2=2\tau=\sigma_y\), so \(\tau_y=\sigma_y/2=0.50\sigma_y\). Von Mises predicts yield at \(\sigma_1^2-\sigma_1\sigma_2+\sigma_2^2=3\tau^2=\sigma_y^2\), so \(\tau_y=\sigma_y/\sqrt3\approx0.577\sigma_y\). Von Mises allows about 15% more shear stress before predicting yield than Tresca does for this exact case — a concrete illustration of Tresca's conservatism.

04 · Working method

(1) Determine whether the material is ductile (use Tresca or von Mises) or brittle (use maximum-normal-stress). (2) Find the principal stresses at the critical point, from Lesson 7's Mohr’s circle. (3) Apply the appropriate theory's comparison against the uniaxial yield or ultimate strength from a simple tension test. (4) Remember that "the stress" is not a single number once several components are present — these theories exist specifically to reduce a multi-component state to one comparable equivalent value.

Key criteria

Tresca: \(\sigma_1-\sigma_3=\sigma_y\). Von Mises: \(\sigma_1^2-\sigma_1\sigma_2+\sigma_2^2=\sigma_y^2\). Von Mises ≥ Tresca always (less conservative).

Lesson 10 · Buckling of columns

01 · Euler buckling, derived

A slender column under axial compression can fail by sideways deflection — buckling — at a load far below what would cause simple material yielding. Modeling the column with the same \(EIv''=M(x)\) logic as Lesson 5's beam, but now with \(M(x)=P\,v(x)\) — the moment caused by the deflection itself, growing as the deflection grows — leads to a solution where deflection stays exactly zero until the load reaches a critical value: \[ P_{cr}=\frac{\pi^2EI}{L_e^2}, \] the Euler buckling load, with \(L_e\) the effective length, not simply the physical length.

02 · Effective length — what end conditions encode

\( L_e=KL \), where \(K\) depends on end restraint: \(K=1\) for pinned-pinned (the baseline case), \(K=0.5\) for fixed-fixed (far stiffer against buckling, since \(P_{cr}\propto1/L_e^2\)), \(K=2\) for fixed-free (a cantilevered column, much weaker), \(K=0.7\) for fixed-pinned. Real end conditions rarely match these idealized cases exactly, so \(K\) is often an engineering judgment call, not a precisely derived number.

03 · Slenderness and the yield crossover

In terms of stress, \[ \sigma_{cr}=\frac{P_{cr}}{A}=\frac{\pi^2E}{(L_e/r)^2}, \] with \(r=\sqrt{I/A}\) the radius of gyration and \(L_e/r\) the slenderness ratio. Notice \(E\) — stiffness — appears, but the material's yield or ultimate strength does not: Euler buckling depends on stiffness and geometry alone. But the formula cannot be trusted forever — as slenderness falls, the predicted \(\sigma_{cr}\) would eventually exceed the material's actual yield strength, which is physically impossible. Below a critical slenderness ratio (where the Euler curve crosses the yield stress), the column simply yields in compression before it ever reaches the geometrically-predicted buckling load — short, stocky columns are a strength problem; long, slender ones are a stiffness problem, the same distinction from Lesson 5, now appearing in compression.

04 · Why buckling fails without warning

Unlike a ductile material's visible plastic elongation before tensile fracture, an idealized column near its buckling load shows almost no deflection right up to \(P_{cr}\), then diverges sharply — real imperfections (a slight initial crookedness, off-centre loading) soften this into a more gradual but still sudden-feeling transition. Working method: (1) compute the slenderness ratio \(L_e/r\); (2) compute the Euler critical stress; (3) compare against yield strength — whichever is lower actually governs; (4) always use effective length \(L_e=KL\), never raw physical length, since end conditions change the answer by a large factor.

Key result — Euler buckling

\[ P_{cr}=\frac{\pi^2EI}{L_e^2},\qquad \sigma_{cr}=\frac{\pi^2E}{(L_e/r)^2}. \]

Depends on stiffness (E) and geometry — never on the material's strength.

Lesson 11 · Stress concentration and fatigue preview

01 · Kt — the geometry tax on nominal stress

Any geometric discontinuity — a hole, fillet, keyway, or groove — locally disturbs smooth stress flow, concentrating stress well above the simple load-over-area nominal value right at the discontinuity: \[ \sigma_{actual}=K_t\,\sigma_{nominal}, \] where \(K_t\), the stress concentration factor, depends purely on geometry — the discontinuity's shape relative to the part's size — not on load magnitude or material. A small fillet radius relative to the part size gives a high \(K_t\); a generous fillet approaches \(K_t\approx1\).

02 · Why keyways matter so much

A keyway — a slot cut for a key transmitting torque between a shaft and a hub — combines a sharp internal corner geometry with sitting exactly in a highly-stressed region: a shaft's outer surface, already carrying peak bending or torsion stress. That combination — an inherently severe geometric discontinuity landing precisely where nominal stress is already highest — is why keyways are a classically dangerous fatigue location, with \(K_t\) often in the range of \(2\)–\(3\) for a sharp keyway, a reasonable order-of-magnitude starting point before consulting an actual chart for the specific geometry.

03 · Notch sensitivity — the metallurgy half of the story

Not every material "feels" the full geometric \(K_t\) equally. Notch sensitivity \(q\) (\(0\leq q\leq1\)) scales how much of the theoretical \(K_t\) actually translates into reduced fatigue strength: \[ K_f=1+q(K_t-1). \] \(q\approx0\) means a material is essentially notch-insensitive — often a ductile material with enough local ductility to blunt the stress peak through local yielding before a fatigue crack can start, giving \(K_f\approx1\) regardless of \(K_t\). \(q\approx1\) means the material takes the full geometric \(K_t\) at face value — typically harder, higher-strength, more brittle materials lacking that local ductility. The same geometric feature can be a minor concern in one material and a serious fatigue risk in another: geometry sets the ceiling, metallurgy decides how much of it the material actually experiences.

04 · Working method — the handshake with materials science

(1) Identify every geometric discontinuity — holes, fillets, keyways, grooves, threads — as a candidate stress-concentration site. (2) Look up or estimate \(K_t\) for each, from its specific geometry ratio. (3) For a fatigue assessment, find the material's notch sensitivity \(q\) and compute \(K_f=1+q(K_t-1)\) — the number that actually multiplies the nominal alternating stress in a fatigue-life estimate (Materials Science II's S-N curve, meeting this course's geometry). (4) For a static check on a ductile material, \(K_t\) is often reasonably ignored, since local yielding redistributes stress before the whole part fails — but never for a fatigue check, where localized, repeated stress well below yield can still eventually crack a part. This distinction — matters enormously for fatigue and for brittle materials, often legitimately ignored for static ductile loading — is easy to get backward and worth fixing firmly.

FLD 203 — Fluid Mechanics

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Fluid properties

01 · What a fluid is, and the properties that matter

A fluid is a substance that cannot resist a shear stress at rest — it deforms continuously under any tangential force, however small. That single fact separates fluid mechanics from solid mechanics and forces a different toolkit. Before any flow is analysed, four bulk properties must be pinned down. Density \( \rho \) (mass per unit volume, kg/m³) sets inertia and weight; specific weight \( \gamma=\rho g \) converts it to force per volume; specific gravity \( SG=\rho/\rho_{\text{water}} \) is the dimensionless ratio to water at 4°C; and viscosity \( \mu \) measures a fluid's resistance to shearing. Water has \( \rho\approx1000 \) kg/m³, so \( \gamma\approx9810 \) N/m³; an oil of \( \rho=850 \) has \( SG=0.85 \).

02 · Viscosity, defined by Newton's law

Viscosity is defined operationally. Place a fluid between two plates and slide the top one: the fluid develops a velocity gradient \( du/dy \), and the shear stress needed is \( \tau=\mu\,\dfrac{du}{dy} \) — Newton's law of viscosity. Fluids obeying it with constant \( \mu \) are Newtonian (water, air, light oils); those whose apparent viscosity changes with shear rate (paint, slurry, blood) are non-Newtonian. For \( \mu=0.1 \) Pa·s and a gradient of \( 50\ \text{s}^{-1} \), the shear stress is \( \tau=0.1\times50=5 \) Pa. Viscosity is the property that makes real flow lose energy; an idealised inviscid fluid (\( \mu=0 \)) would flow forever without friction, a useful fiction for some problems and a dangerous one for others.

03 · Kinematic viscosity and the role of temperature

Two viscosities appear in practice. The dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) (Pa·s) enters force balances; the kinematic viscosity \( \nu=\mu/\rho \) (m²/s) enters the Reynolds number and diffusion of momentum. For the oil above, \( \nu=0.1/850\approx1.18\times10^{-4} \) m²/s. Temperature dependence runs opposite ways for the two states of matter that concern us: liquids thin dramatically as they warm (their molecules escape each other's attraction), while gases thicken slightly as they warm (faster molecules transfer more momentum across streamlines). An engineer sizing a pump for cold-start oil and hot running oil is really sizing for two different fluids.

04 · The property-first working method

The discipline this lesson installs is to fix properties, with units, before touching a flow equation. Read \( \rho \) at the operating temperature, not a handbook default; compute \( \gamma=\rho g \) only when a weight or pressure is wanted; carry \( \mu \) and \( \nu \) separately and know which one a formula expects. Units are the audit: \( \tau=\mu\,du/dy \) must come out in pascals, and if it does not, a property is in the wrong units. Every later lesson — statics through compressible flow — takes these four numbers as given, so an error here propagates through the whole course. A further property, bulk modulus \( K=-V\,dp/dV \), measures resistance to compression and decides whether a fluid may be treated as incompressible: liquids have enormous \(K\) and are effectively incompressible below high pressures, while gases are compressible whenever speeds approach the speed of sound (Lesson 11). Get the fluid right first — its density, its viscosity, and whether it compresses — then analyse its motion.

Lesson 02 · Pressure and fluid statics

01 · Pressure and the hydrostatic law

In a fluid at rest there is no shear, so the only stress is pressure, acting equally in all directions at a point (Pascal's principle). The one equation that governs a static fluid is the hydrostatic law: pressure increases with depth as \( \dfrac{dp}{dz}=-\rho g \), which for a constant-density fluid integrates to \( p=p_0+\rho g h \), where \(h\) is depth below the reference surface. Ten metres of water therefore adds \( \rho g h=1000\times9.81\times10=98{,}100 \) Pa \(=98.1\) kPa — close to one atmosphere for every ten metres, a number worth memorising.

02 · Gauge, absolute, and vacuum

Pressure is quoted against a reference. Absolute pressure is measured from a perfect vacuum; gauge pressure is measured from local atmospheric, \( p_{\text{abs}}=p_{\text{atm}}+p_{\text{gauge}} \). A depth reading of 98.1 kPa gauge is \( 101.325+98.1=199.4 \) kPa absolute. Confusing the two is a classic and consequential error — pump suction limits, boiling points, and gas-law calculations all demand absolute pressure, while structural loads on a wall care only about the gauge difference across it. State which you mean, every time.

03 · Manometers and pressure measurement

The hydrostatic law turns a column of liquid into a pressure gauge. In a manometer, a pressure difference is read as a height difference: \( \Delta p=\rho g\,\Delta h \). Because \(\rho\) sits in the formula, a dense fluid gives a short, readable column — mercury (\( \rho=13{,}600 \) kg/m³) reads 50 kPa as only \( \Delta h=50{,}000/(13{,}600\times9.81)\approx0.375 \) m, whereas water would need over five metres. The same law gives the force and centre of pressure on a submerged gate: pressure grows linearly with depth, so the resultant acts below the centroid, a fact that decides how dam gates and tank walls are hinged.

04 · The statics working method

The procedure is uniform. Choose a reference surface where the pressure is known (often the free surface at atmospheric). Move to the point of interest along a connected body of the same fluid, adding \( \rho g h \) for every descent and subtracting it for every rise. Switch \(\rho\) whenever the fluid changes (oil over water, gas over liquid). Keep gauge and absolute straight, and let units confirm the arithmetic — \( \rho g h \) must land in pascals. This "walk the column" method solves every manometer and submerged-surface problem without new equations, and it is the same energy-accounting habit that Bernoulli's equation extends to moving fluids in the next lessons. Two extensions of the static law are worth flagging now. Buoyancy — Archimedes' principle — is nothing more than the net of hydrostatic pressure over a submerged body: the upward force equals the weight of displaced fluid, \( F_B=\rho g V_{\text{disp}} \), which is why steel ships float and why a partly filled tank's contents shift the centre of buoyancy. And the force on a plane submerged surface is the pressure at its centroid times its area, applied at the centre of pressure below the centroid — the calculation behind every tank wall, lock gate, and dam face. Both follow from \( p=p_0+\rho g h \) with no new physics, only careful bookkeeping of where the pressure acts.

Lesson 03 · Flow description, Reynolds number, and continuity

01 · Describing flow, and the master number

Once a fluid moves, the first questions are how fast, how much, and in what regime. Flow rate comes in two forms: volume flow \( Q=AV \) (m³/s) and mass flow \( \dot m=\rho Q=\rho AV \) (kg/s), where \(V\) is the average velocity over cross-section \(A\). The regime — smooth laminar layers or chaotic turbulent mixing — is set by the single most important dimensionless group in the subject, the Reynolds number \( Re=\dfrac{\rho V D}{\mu}=\dfrac{V D}{\nu} \), the ratio of inertial to viscous forces. In a pipe, \( Re<2300 \) is laminar, \( Re>4000 \) turbulent, with a transitional band between.

02 · Reading the Reynolds number

For water (\( \nu\approx10^{-6} \) m²/s) at \( V=2 \) m/s in a \( D=0.05 \) m pipe, \( Re=VD/\nu=2\times0.05/10^{-6}=100{,}000 \) — firmly turbulent, as almost all practical water pipes are. The power of \(Re\) is that it collapses geometry, speed, and fluid into one number: two completely different flows with the same \(Re\) behave alike, which is the basis of model testing (Lesson 6). Low \(Re\) means viscosity dominates and disturbances are damped; high \(Re\) means inertia dominates and small disturbances grow into turbulence.

03 · Continuity: mass is conserved

Mass cannot accumulate in a rigid pipe running full and steady, so what enters must leave: \( \rho_1 A_1 V_1=\rho_2 A_2 V_2 \), and for an incompressible fluid \( A_1 V_1=A_2 V_2 \) — the continuity equation. A pipe of \( D=0.05 \) m carrying \( V=2 \) m/s passes \( Q=AV=\frac{\pi}{4}(0.05)^2\times2\approx3.93\times10^{-3} \) m³/s. Halve the diameter and the area falls fourfold, so continuity forces the velocity to quadruple: \( V_2=V_1(D_1/D_2)^2=2\times4=8 \) m/s. Continuity is why a nozzle speeds flow up and a diffuser slows it down, with no energy added — purely geometry conserving mass.

04 · The flow-description working method

Given any internal flow: first compute \( Re \) to fix the regime, because laminar and turbulent flows obey different friction and profile laws and mixing them is a common error. Then apply continuity to relate velocities at different sections — it is an accounting identity that holds regardless of losses, so it is always available and always exact for incompressible steady flow. Keep \( Q \) (what a meter reads) and \( V \) (what the physics uses) distinct, linked by \(Q=AV\). These two tools — a regime number and a mass balance — precede every energy analysis, and they are exactly what Bernoulli's equation in Lesson 4 builds upon. It also helps to distinguish the flow's character in time and space: a steady flow has properties that do not change with time at a fixed point (most pipe systems at their operating point), while an unsteady flow does (a valve slamming, a pump starting). A flow is uniform if velocity does not change along the path and developing if it does, as near a pipe entrance where the velocity profile is still forming. These distinctions decide which simplified equations are legitimate: the clean continuity and Bernoulli forms used throughout this course assume steady, and usually fully-developed, conditions, and applying them to a genuinely unsteady flow is a category error, not a small approximation.

Lesson 04 · Bernoulli, energy equation, pipe flow, and pump curves

01 · Energy along a streamline

Bernoulli's equation is the energy balance of an ideal flow: for steady, incompressible, frictionless flow along a streamline, \( \dfrac{p}{\rho g}+\dfrac{V^2}{2g}+z=\text{constant} \). Each term is a head, an energy per unit weight measured in metres: pressure head \( p/\rho g \), velocity head \( V^2/2g \), and elevation head \(z\). The statement is that these three trade against one another but their sum is conserved — speed a fluid up and its pressure must fall, raise it and something else must give. It is Lesson 2's hydrostatic accounting extended to a moving fluid.

02 · Reading the trades

The velocity head is where intuition often fails, so anchor it numerically: water at \( V=2 \) m/s carries a velocity head \( V^2/2g=4/(2\times9.81)\approx0.204 \) m and a dynamic pressure \( \tfrac12\rho V^2=\tfrac12\times1000\times2^2=2000 \) Pa. Torricelli's result — the speed of a jet draining under a head \(h\) — falls straight out by setting pressure and velocity heads equal: \( V=\sqrt{2gh} \), so a 5-metre head drives \( V=\sqrt{2\times9.81\times5}\approx9.9 \) m/s. The same balance explains why a venturi throat (small area, high speed) shows low pressure, the principle behind carburettors, flow meters, and aspirators.

03 · The real energy equation: adding losses and machines

Real flow has friction and often a pump or turbine, so Bernoulli is generalised to the steady-flow energy equation: \( \dfrac{p_1}{\rho g}+\dfrac{V_1^2}{2g}+z_1+h_{\text{pump}}=\dfrac{p_2}{\rho g}+\dfrac{V_2^2}{2g}+z_2+h_{\text{turbine}}+h_{\text{loss}} \). The head loss \( h_{\text{loss}} \) collects pipe friction (major losses) and fittings (minor losses); \( h_{\text{pump}} \) is the head a pump adds. A pump's required head and the system's demand meet at the operating point where the pump curve (head falling as flow rises) crosses the system curve (head rising as flow rises) — the single most important reading in rotating-equipment work, developed fully in Lesson 10.

04 · The Bernoulli working method

Apply it disciplined: pick two points on the same streamline where you know the most, write the full energy equation, and cancel what is genuinely zero — an open surface has gauge pressure zero, a large reservoir has negligible velocity, a horizontal run drops the \(z\) terms. Never drop friction on a long pipe or a machine that is present; ideal Bernoulli is a first estimate, not a licence to ignore losses. Confirm the trade makes physical sense — pressure should fall where the fluid speeds up — and check units, since every head is in metres. This energy accounting, together with continuity and the Reynolds number, closes the toolkit for the momentum, loss, and turbomachinery lessons that follow. One caution completes the picture: Bernoulli is an energy statement along a streamline and says nothing about forces on a bend or a vane — for those, the momentum equation of Lesson 5 is required, and the two are complementary rather than interchangeable. Nor does Bernoulli survive a hydraulic jump, a shock, or any abrupt loss, where mechanical energy is dissipated to heat and the constant on the right-hand side simply drops. Knowing exactly which assumptions are in force — steady, incompressible, frictionless, one streamline — is what separates a correct application from a plausible-looking wrong one, and it is worth restating those four words each time the equation is written.

Lesson 05 · Momentum analysis

01 · Why energy is not enough

Bernoulli's equation tracks energy, but it says nothing about the forces a moving fluid exerts on the hardware that guides it. To hold a bend in place, size the bolts on a nozzle, or find the thrust of a jet, a different conservation law is needed: linear momentum. Newton's second law for a control volume states that the net external force equals the rate at which momentum leaves minus the rate at which it enters: \( \sum\vec F=\dot m\,(\vec V_{\text{out}}-\vec V_{\text{in}}) \) for steady flow with one inlet and one outlet, where \( \dot m=\rho Q \). It is a vector equation, so direction is everything.

02 · The control-volume method

The procedure is disciplined. Draw a control volume enclosing the device, mark every force crossing its boundary — pressure on the cut faces, the reaction from the wall, and weight — and mark the momentum flux \( \dot m\vec V \) at each opening. Then write \( \sum F_x=\dot m(V_{2x}-V_{1x}) \) in each direction separately. For a jet of water at \( V=20 \) m/s carrying \( Q=0.01 \) m³/s (so \( \dot m=\rho Q=10 \) kg/s) striking a fixed flat plate normally and spreading sideways, the plate must supply a force \( F=\dot m\,V=10\times20=200 \) N to destroy the incoming axial momentum. The plate feels an equal and opposite push — the physics behind a Pelton turbine bucket and a firefighter's hose reaction.

03 · Bends, nozzles, and reversals

Direction changes multiply the force. A pipe bend that turns the flow through 180°, reversing the velocity from \(+V\) to \(-V\), changes momentum by \( \dot m(-V-V)=-2\dot m V \), so the anchor must resist \( 2\dot m V=2\times10\times20=400 \) N — twice the flat-plate value, because the fluid's momentum is not merely stopped but reversed. Pressure forces on the cut faces add to this and often dominate in a pressurised line, which is why large pipe bends sit on massive concrete thrust blocks. The momentum equation, not Bernoulli, sizes those blocks.

04 · The momentum working method

Momentum and energy are complementary, never interchangeable: energy (Bernoulli) gives pressures and velocities; momentum gives forces. Solve them in that order — use continuity and Bernoulli first to find the velocities at each opening, then feed those into the momentum equation for the force. Keep the sign convention fixed by choosing a positive direction and holding it; a dropped sign turns a 400 N restraint into a 0 N one. Include every force on the control surface, especially the pressure terms that beginners forget. Done carefully, one control-volume sketch replaces a great deal of intuition, and it is the same accounting the rotating-equipment and turbomachinery lessons use to find blade and impeller loads. A subtlety worth naming: if the control volume itself moves — a vane on a turbine wheel travelling at speed \(U\) — the momentum flux is computed with the relative velocity \(V-U\), not the absolute jet speed, because it is the relative approach that the moving surface actually turns. This single correction is the difference between a stationary-plate calculation and a working turbine analysis, and forgetting it overstates the force on every moving blade. Fixed surface: use \(V\); moving surface: use \(V-U\). With that distinction in hand, the control-volume method scales from a garden hose to a hydro-turbine without any new equation.

Lesson 06 · Dimensional analysis and similarity

01 · Why make things dimensionless

Experiments are expensive and variables are many. Dimensional analysis cuts both by grouping physical variables into a smaller set of dimensionless numbers, so a single curve replaces a family of tests. The tool is the Buckingham Pi theorem: a relationship among \(n\) physical variables involving \(k\) independent base dimensions (mass, length, time…) reduces to \( n-k \) independent dimensionless groups, the "Pi" groups. Fewer knobs means fewer experiments and a result that transfers across scales and fluids.

02 · Buckingham Pi in practice

Take the drag force \(F\) on a sphere, which depends on velocity \(V\), diameter \(D\), density \( \rho \), and viscosity \( \mu \): that is \(n=5\) variables in \(k=3\) dimensions (M, L, T), so \( n-k=2 \) dimensionless groups. Choosing repeating variables and combining, they come out as the drag coefficient \( C_D=\dfrac{F}{\tfrac12\rho V^2 A} \) and the Reynolds number \( Re=\rho VD/\mu \). The entire physics collapses to one relationship \( C_D=f(Re) \) — a single experimental curve valid for any sphere in any Newtonian fluid, which is exactly the drag chart every design handbook prints.

03 · Similarity and model testing

The same theorem underwrites scale-model testing. A model reproduces the prototype's behaviour when three similarities hold: geometric (same shape, scaled lengths), kinematic (same velocity-pattern shape), and dynamic (matching the governing dimensionless groups). For a fully submerged flow dominated by viscosity, dynamic similarity means matching the Reynolds number: \( Re_{\text{model}}=Re_{\text{prototype}} \). A quarter-scale model in the same fluid must therefore run four times faster (\( V_m=V_p\,D_p/D_m=4V_p \)) to keep \(Re\) equal — a demand that often forces a bigger tunnel or a different fluid, and explains why some flows are tested in pressurised or cryogenic wind tunnels.

04 · The similarity working method

To plan a test: list the variables, count the base dimensions, and read off \( n-k \) groups; then identify which group governs the physics (Reynolds for viscous flows, Froude for free-surface flows, Mach for compressible ones) and match it between model and prototype. Report results as dimensionless curves, not raw numbers, so they generalise. The discipline pays twice: it slashes the experimental matrix, and it is the only rigorous way to carry a wind-tunnel or water-channel result onto a full-scale machine. Where two groups cannot be matched at once — the classic ship-model conflict between Reynolds and Froude — the engineer must know which one dominates and correct for the other, a judgement this method makes explicit rather than hidden. It is worth naming the common groups, because each encodes a force ratio and each rules a different flow: Reynolds (inertia vs viscosity) for internal and submerged flows, Froude (inertia vs gravity) for open channels and ship hulls, Mach (speed vs sound speed) for compressible gas flow, Weber (inertia vs surface tension) for droplets and sprays, and Euler or the pressure coefficient for cavitation and pressure-driven problems. Recognising which of these governs a given situation is the real skill — the algebra of forming the groups is mechanical, but choosing the one to match is engineering judgement, and it decides whether a scale test means anything at all.

Lesson 07 · Laminar and turbulent pipe flow in depth

01 · Head loss in a real pipe

Real pipe flow loses mechanical energy to friction, and that loss is the major head loss, given by the Darcy–Weisbach equation \( h_f=f\,\dfrac{L}{D}\,\dfrac{V^2}{2g} \). Here \(L/D\) is the pipe's length-to-diameter ratio, \(V^2/2g\) is the velocity head, and \(f\) is the dimensionless Darcy friction factor that carries all the physics of the wall and the regime. Everything in sizing a pump against a pipe run reduces to finding \(f\) and reading off \( h_f \).

02 · Laminar versus turbulent friction

In laminar flow (\( Re<2300 \)) the friction factor is exact and simple: \( f=64/Re \). At \( Re=1000 \), \( f=64/1000=0.064 \) — friction falls as flow speeds up, because a faster laminar flow is proportionally less retarded. In turbulent flow the wall's roughness matters and \(f\) no longer has a tidy formula; it is read from the Moody chart or computed from the Colebrook equation as a function of \(Re\) and relative roughness \( \varepsilon/D \). The transition between them is abrupt and the two laws are genuinely different — using the laminar \(64/Re\) in a turbulent pipe understates friction badly.

03 · A worked head loss

Take water at \( V=2 \) m/s in a \( D=0.1 \) m, \( L=100 \) m pipe with a turbulent friction factor \( f=0.02 \). The velocity head is \( V^2/2g=4/19.62\approx0.204 \) m and \( L/D=1000 \), so \( h_f=0.02\times1000\times0.204\approx4.08 \) m. That head — over four metres of water — is what a pump must supply just to overcome friction in this single run, before lifting the fluid or pressurising anything. The strong dependence on velocity (\( h_f\propto V^2 \)) is why oversizing pipe diameter, which cuts velocity, is the cheapest way to save pumping energy over a plant's life. Because velocity itself scales as \(Q/D^2\), the head loss actually falls with roughly the fifth power of diameter for a fixed flow — a modest increase in pipe size yields a dramatic drop in friction and pumping cost, the reason distribution mains are so much larger than a naive velocity limit would suggest.

04 · The pipe-friction working method

Given a pipe run: compute \( Re \) to fix the regime; if laminar, use \( f=64/Re \); if turbulent, read \(f\) from the Moody chart using \(Re\) and \( \varepsilon/D \). Then apply Darcy–Weisbach for \( h_f \). Add the minor losses of Lesson 8 and feed the total head loss into the energy equation to size the pump. Two habits prevent most errors: never carry the laminar formula across the transition, and keep \(f\) (Darcy) distinct from the Fanning friction factor (four times smaller) that some texts use. The velocity-squared law is the design lever — halving velocity quarters friction — and it is the single most useful piece of intuition in piping-system economics. It helps to read the Moody chart as three territories. At low \(Re\) the laminar line \(f=64/Re\) falls steeply and roughness is irrelevant, because a smooth viscous sublayer buries the wall texture. In the transitional-turbulent band \(f\) depends on both \(Re\) and \( \varepsilon/D \), the curves fanning out by roughness. At high \(Re\) the curves flatten into the fully rough zone, where \(f\) depends on \( \varepsilon/D \) alone and no longer on \(Re\) — friction has become purely a matter of wall texture, so a rougher pipe simply costs more head forever regardless of speed. Knowing which zone a design sits in tells you immediately whether polishing the pipe, or slowing the flow, is the lever that actually helps.

Lesson 08 · Piping networks and valves

01 · Minor losses at fittings

Beyond wall friction, every fitting — entrance, bend, contraction, and especially every valve — dissipates energy as the flow separates and re-forms. These are the minor losses, written \( h_m=K\,\dfrac{V^2}{2g} \), where \(K\) is a tabulated loss coefficient for the component. "Minor" is a misnomer in a compact, fitting-heavy system, where they can exceed the pipe friction. A sharp pipe entrance has \(K\approx0.5\); a submerged exit has \(K=1.0\) (all the velocity head is lost); a partly closed valve can reach \(K\) of tens or hundreds.

02 · A worked minor loss, and equivalent length

For water at \( V=3 \) m/s through a sharp entrance (\(K=0.5\)): \( h_m=0.5\times\dfrac{9}{19.62}\approx0.229 \) m; the exit of the same line (\(K=1.0\)) loses \( \approx0.459 \) m. A convenient way to fold fittings into pipe calculations is the equivalent length \(L_e\), the length of straight pipe giving the same loss: from \( K=f\,L_e/D \), \( L_e=KD/f \). For the entrance with \(D=0.1\) m and \(f=0.02\), \( L_e=0.5\times0.1/0.02=2.5 \) m of pipe. Summed over many fittings, equivalent lengths turn a messy network into one friction calculation.

03 · Networks: series and parallel

Real systems are networks. Pipes in series carry the same flow \(Q\) and their head losses add: \( h_{\text{total}}=\sum h_i \). Pipes in parallel share the same end-to-end head loss and their flows add: \( Q_{\text{total}}=\sum Q_i \). These are the fluid analogues of resistors in series and parallel, and they let a branched system be reduced to an equivalent single path. Valves are the network's control elements: a gate valve is a low-loss on/off device, a globe valve throttles well but at high loss, a check valve prevents reverse flow, and a ball or butterfly valve trades compactness against controllability — the choice sets both the \(K\) in the loss sum and the plant's operability.

04 · The network working method

To analyse a system: sum major losses (Darcy–Weisbach) and minor losses (\(KV^2/2g\), or equivalent lengths) along each path; reduce series and parallel sections to a single equivalent loss versus flow — the system curve; then intersect it with the pump curve for the operating point (Lesson 10). Choose valves for duty, not habit: throttling with a globe valve wastes energy deliberately as heat, so on a variable-flow line a variable-speed pump is usually cheaper over its life. Keep the loss bookkeeping explicit and units consistent — every term a head in metres — and the whole network, however branched, resolves to one head-versus-flow relationship the pump must satisfy. One phenomenon deserves a place in every valve discussion: cavitation. Where a valve or a pump inlet drops the local pressure below the fluid's vapour pressure, vapour bubbles form and then collapse violently as pressure recovers, pitting metal and roaring like gravel in the line. It is not a friction loss but a limit — it caps how far a valve may be throttled and how low a pump's suction may fall (the NPSH margin of Lesson 10). Designing a network is therefore not only summing losses but keeping every local pressure comfortably above vapour pressure, so the tidy head-versus-flow curve stays physically valid across the whole operating range.

Lesson 09 · Flow measurement

01 · Measuring flow by measuring pressure

Most flow meters do not measure velocity directly — they measure a pressure difference and infer the flow from Bernoulli. Force the fluid through a known area change and it must speed up (continuity), so its pressure must drop (Bernoulli); read that drop and you have the flow. The venturi, orifice plate, and flow nozzle are all differential-pressure meters built on exactly this idea, differing only in how gracefully they make the fluid accelerate and how much pressure they permanently lose.

02 · The Pitot tube and stagnation

The simplest velocity probe is the Pitot tube, which brings the flow to rest at its tip. The pressure rise from moving to stagnant is the dynamic pressure \( \tfrac12\rho V^2 \), so measuring the difference between stagnation and static pressure gives \( V=\sqrt{2\,\Delta p/\rho} \). For water with \( \Delta p=2000 \) Pa, \( V=\sqrt{2\times2000/1000}=\sqrt4=2 \) m/s. This is the same dynamic pressure met in Lesson 4, now put to work: an aircraft's airspeed indicator is a Pitot-static system reading exactly this difference.

03 · Venturi and orifice meters

For pipe flow, a venturi smoothly contracts to a throat and expands back. Combining continuity and Bernoulli, the ideal flow is \( Q=A_2\sqrt{\dfrac{2\,\Delta p}{\rho\,(1-(A_2/A_1)^2)}} \). A meter with \(D_1=0.1\) m contracting to \(D_2=0.05\) m (so \(A_2/A_1=0.25\)) reading \( \Delta p=2000 \) Pa passes an ideal \( Q\approx4.06\times10^{-3} \) m³/s. Real meters multiply this by a discharge coefficient \(C_d\) that corrects for friction and the vena contracta: a venturi's is near 0.98 (gentle, low permanent loss), while a cheap sharp-edged orifice plate runs about \(C_d\approx0.6\) (abrupt, high permanent loss but simple and cheap). The engineer trades installed cost against pumping cost across that difference.

04 · The measurement working method

To size or read a DP meter: pick the device by the loss the system can afford — venturi where pumping energy matters, orifice where cheapness wins; apply the venturi/orifice equation with the right \(C_d\); and always work in absolute, consistent units, keeping \( \Delta p \) as the meter's differential, not a gauge reading against atmosphere. Remember that these meters read \( \sqrt{\Delta p} \), so flow resolution is poor at low flow and the turndown is limited — a real constraint on metering a widely varying line. Calibrate against a known flow when accuracy matters, because \(C_d\) drifts with Reynolds number and wear. The same Bernoulli-plus-continuity reasoning that sizes the meter also predicts its permanent pressure loss, closing the loop with the network losses of Lesson 8. It is worth knowing that these DP meters are only one family. Positive-displacement meters count fixed volumes and excel at low flows and viscous fluids; turbine meters spin a rotor whose speed tracks velocity; electromagnetic meters read the voltage a conductive fluid induces crossing a field, with no obstruction and no pressure loss at all; and ultrasonic meters time sound pulses with or against the flow. Each trades cost, accuracy, pressure loss, and fluid compatibility differently, but the DP meter remains the workhorse because it needs no moving parts and rests on the same Bernoulli physics the rest of this course has built.

Lesson 10 · Turbomachinery fundamentals

01 · What a pump does, in heads

A pump adds mechanical energy to a fluid, and that energy is measured as head \(H\) — metres of fluid, the same currency as every loss in the system. The useful (hydraulic) power delivered is \( P_{\text{hyd}}=\rho g Q H \). A pump raising \( Q=0.05 \) m³/s of water by \( H=30 \) m delivers \( P_{\text{hyd}}=1000\times9.81\times0.05\times30=14{,}715 \) W \(\approx14.7\) kW. No pump is perfect, so the shaft (brake) power it draws is larger: \( P_{\text{shaft}}=P_{\text{hyd}}/\eta \). At \( \eta=0.75 \) the shaft needs \( 14{,}715/0.75=19{,}620 \) W \(\approx19.6\) kW — the difference is lost to internal friction and turbulence as heat.

02 · The pump curve meets the system curve

A centrifugal pump does not deliver a fixed flow; it delivers whatever the system demands along its pump curve, on which head falls as flow rises. The system curve — static lift plus friction that grows as \(Q^2\) — rises with flow. They cross at exactly one operating point, the flow and head the installation will actually run at. This single intersection, introduced in Lesson 4 and completed here, is the master diagram of rotating-equipment engineering: move either curve (throttle a valve, change speed, foul a pipe) and the operating point slides along the other, which is how every pumped system responds to a change.

03 · Affinity laws and cavitation

Change a pump's speed \(N\) and its curve scales by the affinity laws: flow \( Q\propto N \), head \( H\propto N^2 \), power \( P\propto N^3 \). Double the speed and flow doubles, head quadruples, and power rises eightfold — the cubic power law is why variable-speed drives save so much energy at reduced flow, and why they are the modern alternative to throttling. The hard limit on the suction side is cavitation: if the pressure at the impeller eye falls below the fluid's vapour pressure, vapour bubbles form and collapse, eroding the impeller and collapsing head. The margin against it is the net positive suction head, and keeping the available NPSH above the pump's required NPSH is a non-negotiable installation constraint.

04 · The turbomachinery working method

To specify a pump: build the system curve from static lift plus total losses (Lessons 7–8); overlay candidate pump curves and pick the one whose operating point sits near its best-efficiency flow, not far out on either side where efficiency collapses and vibration rises. Check NPSH available against required at the design flow, with margin. Use the affinity laws to trim speed rather than throttle where flow must vary, and size the motor from the shaft power \( P_{\text{hyd}}/\eta \) at the worst-case operating point. Turbines invert the same accounting — extracting head instead of adding it — so this one framework, head and efficiency about an operating point, serves pumps, fans, compressors, and hydro-turbines alike, which is why it anchors the whole rotating-equipment stream of the curriculum. Running two pumps in parallel adds their flows at a common head, while in series they add head at a common flow — the same series/parallel logic as pipe networks, applied to the machines instead of the pipes.

Lesson 11 · Compressible flow first contact

01 · When density stops being constant

Every lesson so far assumed constant density. That holds for liquids always and for gases at low speed, but as a gas approaches the speed of sound its density changes appreciably and a new regime — compressible flow — begins. The gatekeeper is the Mach number \( M=V/a \), the ratio of flow speed to the local speed of sound. Below about \( M=0.3 \) density changes are under a few percent and the incompressible tools of this course apply; above it, they do not, and the physics changes character. The change is not gradual bookkeeping but a genuine shift in behaviour: in subsonic flow, pressure signals travel upstream faster than the flow itself, so the fluid "feels" an obstacle coming and adjusts smoothly; once the flow is supersonic, no signal can outrun it, disturbances pile up, and the fluid meets changes abruptly through shocks. That single fact — whether information can travel upstream — is the deep reason compressible flow is a different subject rather than a corrected version of the incompressible one.

02 · The speed of sound

Sound is a small pressure disturbance, and it travels at \( a=\sqrt{\gamma R T} \) for an ideal gas, where \( \gamma \) is the specific-heat ratio (1.4 for air), \(R\) the specific gas constant (287 J/kg·K for air), and \(T\) the absolute temperature. In air at \( 288 \) K, \( a=\sqrt{1.4\times287\times288}\approx340 \) m/s. Crucially \(a\) depends on temperature alone for a given gas, so the speed of sound — and therefore the Mach number at a fixed speed — falls as a gas cools, which is why a jet at fixed airspeed has a higher Mach number in the cold upper atmosphere. The temperature dependence also warns against a common shortcut: the speed of sound is not a fixed 340 m/s but a local property that changes wherever the gas heats or cools along its path, so in a flow with large temperature swings — a nozzle, a combustor — the Mach number must be evaluated with the local \(a\), not a single free-stream value. Getting the reference temperature right is half of getting the Mach number right.

03 · Regimes and the shock

Mach number sorts compressible flow into regimes: subsonic (\(M<1\)), transonic (near 1), supersonic (\(M>1\)), and hypersonic beyond. A flow at \( V=170 \) m/s in that air has \( M=170/340\approx0.5 \) — subsonic but well into compressible territory. The defining feature of supersonic flow is the shock wave: a near-discontinuous jump in pressure, temperature, and density across which flow decelerates abruptly and mechanical energy is lost to heat. Shocks explain why a converging nozzle can accelerate a gas only to \(M=1\) at its throat, and why reaching supersonic speeds requires the converging–diverging (de Laval) nozzle of every rocket engine.

04 · The compressible working method

The practical first step is always to compute the Mach number and decide whether compressibility even matters: if \( M<0.3 \), keep the incompressible Bernoulli and continuity of the earlier lessons and move on. If not, switch to compressible relations, carry temperature as a variable (it now changes with speed), and use absolute pressures and temperatures throughout. Watch for choking — a flow that has reached \(M=1\) at a restriction cannot be sped up by lowering downstream pressure further, a hard limit with no incompressible analogue that governs relief valves, gas pipelines, and nozzles. This lesson is a first contact, not a full treatment, but the discipline it installs — check the Mach number before choosing your equations — is exactly what keeps a thermal-systems or propulsion engineer from applying incompressible intuition where the physics has quietly changed. Two everyday consequences make the point concrete. In a high-pressure gas pipeline, the flow can choke at a valve or a rupture, capping the escape rate no matter how low the downstream pressure falls — a fact that safety-relief sizing depends on absolutely. And in a steam or gas turbine, the nozzles that accelerate the working fluid operate deep in the compressible regime, so their design is governed by Mach-number and choking limits that the incompressible relations of this course simply cannot express. Compressible flow is where fluid mechanics hands off to thermodynamics and propulsion, and recognising the handoff is itself the skill.

STS 206 — Engineering Statistics

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Describing data

01 · Why engineers need statistics

No two manufactured parts are identical, no measurement is exact, and no process is perfectly steady. Statistics is the mathematics of that variation — the discipline that turns a scatter of numbers into a defensible statement. It has two halves: descriptive statistics, which summarise data in hand, and inferential statistics, which reason from a sample to the population it came from. This lesson builds the descriptive toolkit, the honest summary that every later inference rests on. Get the summary wrong and every test, interval, and control chart built on it inherits the error.

02 · Centre: mean, median, mode

Three numbers locate the centre of a data set, and they answer different questions. The mean \( \bar x=\frac{1}{n}\sum x_i \) is the balance point; for the set \( \{2,4,4,4,5,5,7,9\} \) it is \( 40/8=5 \). The median is the middle value once ordered — here the average of the 4th and 5th values, \( (4+5)/2=4.5 \) — and it ignores outliers, which is why incomes and house prices are reported as medians. The mode is the most frequent value, here \(4\) (appearing three times), the only measure that works for categorical data. When a distribution is skewed the three separate, and the gap between mean and median is itself a signal of skew.

03 · Spread: variance and standard deviation

Centre alone is half the story; spread is the other half. The variance averages squared deviations from the mean, and the standard deviation is its square root, back in the data's own units. For the set above the squared deviations sum to \(32\); the population variance is \( 32/8=4 \) (so \( \sigma=2 \)), while the sample variance divides by \(n-1\): \( 32/7\approx4.57 \) (so \( s\approx2.14 \)). The \(n-1\) — Bessel's correction — is not a quirk: a sample's own mean sits closer to its data than the true mean does, so dividing by \(n\) would systematically underestimate the spread. Use \(n-1\) whenever the data are a sample, which in engineering is almost always.

04 · The describing-data working method

Before any test, summarise honestly: report a measure of centre and a measure of spread — a mean with no standard deviation hides exactly the variation that matters to an engineer. Choose the measure to fit the data: median and interquartile range for skewed or outlier-prone data, mean and standard deviation for roughly symmetric data, mode for categories. Plot before you compute — a histogram or box plot reveals skew, gaps, and outliers that no single number shows, and it is the cheapest guard against summarising a bimodal process as if it were one. These honest summaries are the raw material for the whole course: the mean becomes an estimate (Lesson 6), its variation becomes a test statistic (Lesson 7), and its spread becomes a control limit in the metrology and quality courses. Two further summaries round out the descriptive toolkit and appear throughout the curriculum: the range and interquartile range (IQR), which capture spread without squaring, and the coefficient of variation \( s/\bar x \), a dimensionless spread that lets you compare the relative scatter of two processes measured in different units. Together with a histogram, these turn a column of raw numbers into a picture an engineer can defend in a review.

Lesson 02 · Probability rules

01 · Probability as the language of chance

Inference runs on probability, so the rules must be exact. A probability is a number between 0 and 1 measuring how likely an event is; for equally likely outcomes it is favourable outcomes over total outcomes. A fair die shows an even number with probability \( 3/6=0.5 \). Two ideas organise everything else: how probabilities combine across events (this lesson), and how they build into distributions (Lessons 3–4). Getting the combination rules right is what separates a sound reliability estimate from a plausible-sounding wrong one.

02 · The addition and multiplication rules

The addition rule gives the probability that A or B occurs: \( P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B) \), subtracting the overlap so it is not counted twice. For \( P(A)=0.5,\ P(B)=0.4,\ P(A\cap B)=0.2 \), \( P(A\cup B)=0.5+0.4-0.2=0.7 \). The multiplication rule gives the probability that A and B both occur; when the events are independent — one does not affect the other — it is simply the product \( P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B) \). Two fair coins both landing heads is \( 0.5\times0.5=0.25 \). Independence is an assumption to be checked, not assumed: correlated failures are exactly where naive multiplication underestimates risk.

03 · Conditional probability and Bayes

When events are not independent, the conditional probability of A given B is \( P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} \) — the chance of A restricted to the world where B has happened. With \( P(A\cap B)=0.2 \) and \( P(B)=0.4 \), \( P(A\mid B)=0.2/0.4=0.5 \). Reversing the condition is the job of Bayes' theorem, \( P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{P(B\mid A)P(A)}{P(B)} \), which updates a prior belief with new evidence. Bayes is the reason a positive result on a rare-fault test can still, correctly, mean the fault is probably absent — the base rate dominates — and it underpins modern diagnostics and reliability updating.

04 · The probability working method

Translate carefully: "or" invokes the addition rule (watch the overlap), "and" the multiplication rule (check independence first), "given that" a conditional. Draw the sample space or a tree for anything with more than two events, because intuition fails fast — the birthday and false-positive paradoxes are famous precisely because the correct rules override the gut. Keep every probability in \([0,1]\) and confirm a partition sums to 1 as an arithmetic check. These rules are the grammar of the distributions in the next lessons and of every reliability, quality, and risk calculation the curriculum returns to, so precision here pays compound interest. Two structural ideas make large problems tractable. Complementary events — \( P(\text{not }A)=1-P(A) \) — often turn a hard "at least one" calculation into an easy "none": the probability that at least one of several independent components fails is one minus the probability that all survive, the backbone of system-reliability analysis. And the law of total probability assembles an overall chance from mutually exclusive cases, weighting each conditional by how often its case occurs. Both are just the addition and multiplication rules applied with care, and both recur constantly once systems have more than a couple of parts.

Lesson 03 · Discrete distributions

01 · Random variables and distributions

A random variable assigns a number to each outcome — the count of defects in a batch, the number of arrivals in an hour — and its probability distribution lists how likely each value is. For counts, the distribution is discrete: a probability mass on each integer, summing to 1. Two discrete distributions cover most engineering counting problems, and recognising which one applies is the whole skill: the binomial for a fixed number of yes/no trials, and the Poisson for events occurring at a steady rate.

02 · The binomial distribution

The binomial models \(n\) independent trials each with success probability \(p\): \( P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k} \). Its mean is \( \mu=np \) and its variance \( \sigma^2=np(1-p) \). For \( n=10 \) parts with a \( p=0.2 \) defect rate, the expected number of defects is \( np=2 \), the variance is \( 10\times0.2\times0.8=1.6 \), and the standard deviation \( \sqrt{1.6}\approx1.26 \). The chance of zero defects is \( P(X=0)=0.8^{10}\approx0.107 \) — about an 11% chance a batch of ten passes clean, a number that surprises people who expect a 20% defect rate to hit every batch.

03 · The Poisson distribution

The Poisson models the count of events in a fixed interval when they occur independently at an average rate \( \lambda \): \( P(X=k)=\dfrac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!} \). Uniquely, its mean and variance are both \( \lambda \). For an average of \( \lambda=3 \) breakdowns per week, the probability of a quiet week with zero breakdowns is \( P(X=0)=e^{-3}\approx0.0498 \), about 5%. The Poisson also arises as the limit of a binomial with large \(n\) and small \(p\) (rare events in many trials), which is why it models flaws per metre of weld, calls per hour, or particles per sample — any "how many in a span" question with a steady rate.

04 · The discrete-distribution working method

Pick the model from the physical situation, not the formula: a fixed number of independent yes/no trials is binomial; a count of events over a continuous span at a constant rate is Poisson. Check the assumptions — binomial needs constant \(p\) and independence; Poisson needs a steady rate and no clustering — because a violated assumption (say, defects that come in bursts) breaks the model quietly. Then read the mean and variance directly (\(np\) and \(np(1-p)\); \(\lambda\) and \(\lambda\)) to sanity-check any probability you compute. These two distributions feed straight into quality sampling plans and reliability counts later in the curriculum, and their means are the expectations every acceptance criterion is written against. A third discrete model, the geometric distribution, answers a different question — how many trials until the first success (or failure) — with mean \( 1/p \); it is the natural model for "how long until the next breakdown" in trial terms and the discrete cousin of the exponential distribution met in Lesson 4. Knowing which of these three a situation calls for — a fixed count, a rate over a span, or a waiting time — is the practical payoff of this lesson, because choosing the wrong family produces answers that are precise and wrong.

Lesson 04 · Continuous distributions

01 · From counts to measurements

Where discrete distributions count, continuous distributions measure — length, temperature, strength, time. A continuous variable takes any value in a range, so probability attaches to intervals, not points, through a probability density function whose area gives probability and integrates to 1. One continuous distribution dominates engineering: the normal, or Gaussian, distribution — the symmetric bell curve that describes measurement error, natural variation, and, through the next lesson's central limit theorem, the behaviour of averages themselves.

02 · The normal distribution and the z-score

The normal distribution is fixed by two parameters, its mean \( \mu \) and standard deviation \( \sigma \). Any value is located by its z-score \( z=\dfrac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \), the number of standard deviations from the mean, which converts any normal to the standard normal (mean 0, SD 1) so one table serves all. A test score of \(x=130\) from a distribution with \( \mu=100,\ \sigma=15 \) has \( z=(130-100)/15=2 \); the fraction of the population below it is \( \Phi(2)\approx0.977 \), so it beats about 97.7% of the population. The z-score is the universal ruler of the normal world.

03 · The empirical rule and other continuous models

The normal's spread follows the empirical (68–95–99.7) rule: about 68% of values lie within \( \pm1\sigma \) of the mean, 95% within \( \pm2\sigma \), and 99.7% within \( \pm3\sigma \). This is why "six-sigma" quality — keeping the process spread far inside the tolerance — corresponds to a vanishingly small defect fraction. Two other continuous models recur: the uniform, flat over a range (rounding error, a first guess at ignorance), and the exponential, describing the time between random events with mean \( 1/\lambda \) — the Poisson's continuous partner, and the basic model of memoryless failure times in reliability.

04 · The continuous-distribution working method

To use a normal model: standardise with the z-score, then read the tail or interval probability from the standard-normal table (or software), remembering that the table gives area to the left, so a right tail is one minus that and a central band is a difference of two. Check the model before trusting it — a histogram that is skewed or bounded (times, counts near zero) is not normal, and forcing it there gives wrong tail probabilities exactly where decisions are made. The normal earns its dominance in the next lesson, where the central limit theorem shows that sample means become normal almost regardless of the parent distribution, which is why so much of inference is built on this one curve. It is worth carrying one honest caution about the normal: it is unbounded and symmetric, so it assigns nonzero probability to impossible values — a negative length, a strength below zero. For quantities physically bounded at zero or strongly one-sided, distributions such as the lognormal (for multiplicative processes and many strength and lifetime data) or the Weibull (the reliability workhorse of Lesson 10) fit better and keep their tails on the physically possible side. The normal is the default, not the law — reach for it because the data support it, not out of habit.

Lesson 05 · Sampling distributions and the CLT

01 · The leap from data to inference

Every earlier lesson described data in hand. Inference asks the harder question: what does a sample tell us about the population it came from? The bridge is the sampling distribution — the distribution not of raw data but of a statistic, such as the sample mean \( \bar x \), computed over many possible samples. Because a different sample gives a different \( \bar x \), the sample mean is itself a random variable with its own centre and spread, and understanding that spread is what makes an estimate trustworthy rather than a lucky guess.

02 · The standard error

The sample mean has the same expected value as the population, \( \mu \), but a smaller spread. Its standard deviation — called the standard error to distinguish it from the data's — is \( \text{SE}=\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n} \). For a population with \( \sigma=10 \) and a sample of \( n=25 \), \( \text{SE}=10/\sqrt{25}=2 \); quadruple the sample to \( n=100 \) and the SE halves to 1. The \( \sqrt n \) is the central economic fact of sampling: precision improves only with the square root of effort, so halving the uncertainty costs four times the data. Every sample-size decision in the curriculum trades against this square-root wall.

03 · The central limit theorem

The central limit theorem (CLT) is the reason statistics works at all: for a sufficiently large sample, the distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal — with mean \( \mu \) and standard error \( \sigma/\sqrt n \) — regardless of the shape of the parent population. Skewed, uniform, even bimodal parents all yield normal sample means as \(n\) grows (a rule of thumb is \( n\ge30 \)). This is why the normal distribution dominates inference: even when the raw data are not normal, the averages we test and interval-estimate almost always are. The CLT converts the messy variety of real data into one predictable, tabulated shape.

04 · The sampling-distribution working method

To reason from a sample: identify the statistic (usually the mean), compute its standard error \( \sigma/\sqrt n \) — or \( s/\sqrt n \) when \( \sigma \) is unknown — and, if \(n\) is large enough, treat the statistic as normal by the CLT. Then a z-score of the statistic, \( z=(\bar x-\mu)/\text{SE} \), places any observed sample mean on the standard normal, exactly as Lesson 4 did for raw values. Two cautions: the CLT is about the mean, not individual data points, and it needs independence — clustered or autocorrelated samples inflate the true error beyond \( \sigma/\sqrt n \). With those in hand, the sampling distribution becomes the engine of the confidence intervals and hypothesis tests that follow. It is worth naming the finite-population and proportion cases you will meet. When sampling a large fraction of a finite lot, a finite-population correction shrinks the standard error, because sampling without replacement removes uncertainty as the sample approaches the whole. And for a proportion — a defect fraction rather than a mean — the same logic gives a standard error \( \sqrt{p(1-p)/n} \), which is why acceptance sampling can quote a confidence band on a scrap rate. Both are the sampling distribution applied to a new statistic, not new theory.

Lesson 06 · Estimation and confidence intervals

01 · From point estimate to interval

A single sample mean is a point estimate — the best single guess for the population mean — but on its own it hides its own uncertainty. A confidence interval reports the estimate together with a range that, with stated confidence, contains the true value. It answers the engineer's real question: not "what is the mean?" but "how sure are we, and within what band?" The interval is built directly on the sampling distribution of the previous lesson.

02 · Building a confidence interval

For a mean with known \( \sigma \) and a large sample, the interval is \( \bar x\pm z^*\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n} \), where \( z^* \) is the standard-normal value cutting off the desired confidence — \( z^*=1.96 \) for 95%. With \( \bar x=50 \), \( \sigma=10 \), \( n=25 \) (so \( \text{SE}=2 \)), the margin of error is \( 1.96\times2=3.92 \) and the 95% interval is \( 50\pm3.92=(46.08,\ 53.92) \). The margin of error \( z^*\text{SE} \) is the interval's half-width, and it shrinks with \( \sqrt n \) and grows with the confidence demanded — you buy certainty with width.

03 · Small samples and the t-distribution

When \( \sigma \) is unknown (the usual case) it is estimated by the sample \( s \), and for small samples that extra uncertainty widens the interval: the normal \( z^* \) is replaced by a slightly larger \( t^* \) from Student's t-distribution, whose fatter tails depend on the degrees of freedom \( n-1 \). As \(n\) grows the t-distribution converges to the normal, so for large samples the two agree. The interpretation of the interval is subtle and worth stating precisely: "95% confidence" means the procedure captures the true mean 95% of the time over many samples — not that this particular interval has a 95% probability of containing it, which is a common and consequential misreading.

04 · The estimation working method

To report an estimate honestly: compute the point estimate, its standard error, and the margin \( z^* \) (or \( t^* \)) \( \text{SE} \); state the interval and the confidence level together. Choose the confidence to fit the stakes — 95% is conventional, 99% for safety-critical claims (wider), 90% where a rough band suffices. Use \(t\) whenever \( \sigma \) is estimated from a small sample; do not pretend to know \( \sigma \) you had to estimate. And resist the temptation to report a point estimate alone: a mean strength quoted without an interval invites the reader to treat a noisy guess as a fact, which is exactly the malpractice Lesson 11 catalogues. The confidence interval is the discipline that keeps an estimate honest. One design use deserves mention: the margin formula can be inverted to plan a sample size before collecting data. Setting \( z^*\sigma/\sqrt n \) equal to a target margin \(E\) and solving gives \( n=(z^*\sigma/E)^2 \) — so halving the acceptable margin quadruples the required sample, the same square-root wall met in Lesson 5, now driving a budget. Deciding the interval you need first, then computing the sample it costs, is far cheaper than collecting data blindly and discovering the band is too wide to support any decision.

Lesson 07 · Hypothesis testing

01 · Testing a claim against data

A hypothesis test is a formal procedure for deciding whether data are consistent with a claim. It sets up two competing statements: the null hypothesis \( H_0 \), the default or "no effect" claim (a process is on target, a new method is no better), and the alternative \( H_1 \), what we would conclude if the null fails. The logic is that of proof by contradiction: assume \( H_0 \), compute how surprising the data would be under it, and reject \( H_0 \) only if the data are surprising enough. The test never proves the null — it only fails, or fails to fail, to reject it.

02 · The test statistic and the p-value

Surprise is measured by a test statistic — for a mean, \( z=\dfrac{\bar x-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt n} \), the number of standard errors the sample mean sits from the null value \( \mu_0 \). Testing \( \mu_0=50 \) with \( \bar x=52 \), \( \sigma=10 \), \( n=25 \) (so \( \text{SE}=2 \)) gives \( z=(52-50)/2=1 \). The p-value is the probability, if \( H_0 \) were true, of a result at least this extreme: here a two-tailed \( p\approx0.317 \), so a result this far out happens about a third of the time by chance alone. That is not surprising, so \( H_0 \) is not rejected.

03 · Significance, errors, and their trade-off

The decision threshold is the significance level \( \alpha \), commonly 0.05: reject \( H_0 \) when \( p<\alpha \). Two errors are possible. A Type I error rejects a true null (a false alarm), with probability \( \alpha \); a Type II error fails to reject a false null (a missed effect), with probability \( \beta \), and \( 1-\beta \) is the test's power. The two trade off: lowering \( \alpha \) to avoid false alarms raises \( \beta \) and misses real effects, and the only way to reduce both at once is more data. Choosing \( \alpha \) is therefore an engineering judgement about which error is costlier — a false alarm that halts a line, or a missed defect that ships.

04 · The hypothesis-testing working method

State \( H_0 \) and \( H_1 \) before seeing the data; choose \( \alpha \) by the cost of each error; compute the test statistic and its p-value; reject or retain \( H_0 \), and report the p-value, not just the verdict. Two disciplines guard against abuse. Fix the hypotheses and \( \alpha \) in advance — choosing them after seeing the data (p-hacking) manufactures false significance, the malpractice of Lesson 11. And separate statistical from practical significance: with a huge sample even a trivial, meaningless difference becomes "significant," so always report the effect size and its confidence interval alongside the p-value. A test answers "is the effect real?"; the interval answers "is it big enough to matter?" — and engineers need both. There is also a direct bridge between the two: a two-sided test at level \( \alpha \) rejects \( H_0 \) exactly when the null value falls outside the \( (1-\alpha) \) confidence interval, so the interval already contains the test's verdict plus the effect size the bare p-value hides — a good reason to report intervals by default.

Lesson 08 · Regression

01 · Relating one variable to another

Much of engineering is about relationships: how strength depends on carbon content, wear on load, yield on temperature. Regression fits a model to that relationship so it can be summarised, tested, and used to predict. The simplest and most important is simple linear regression, which fits a straight line \( \hat y=a+bx \) to paired data — \(x\) the predictor, \(y\) the response — capturing the trend in two numbers, a slope and an intercept.

02 · Least squares

The line is chosen by least squares: minimise the sum of squared vertical distances (residuals) from the data to the line. This gives the slope \( b=\dfrac{\sum(x-\bar x)(y-\bar y)}{\sum(x-\bar x)^2} \) and the intercept \( a=\bar y-b\bar x \). For the points \( (1,2),(2,4),(3,5),(4,4),(5,5) \) with \( \bar x=3,\ \bar y=4 \): the numerator \( \sum(x-\bar x)(y-\bar y)=6 \), the denominator \( \sum(x-\bar x)^2=10 \), so \( b=0.6 \) and \( a=4-0.6\times3=2.2 \). The fitted line is \( \hat y=2.2+0.6x \). The slope is the physical takeaway: on average \(y\) rises 0.6 per unit of \(x\).

03 · Goodness of fit and its limits

How well the line fits is measured by the coefficient of determination \( R^2 \), the fraction of the response's variation the model explains, between 0 and 1; for this data \( R^2=0.6 \), so 60% of the scatter in \(y\) is accounted for by \(x\). But \( R^2 \) is not the whole story, and two cautions are essential. First, correlation is not causation: a strong fit shows association, not that \(x\) causes \(y\) — a lurking third variable may drive both. Second, do not extrapolate: the line is evidence only across the range of the data, and predicting far outside it assumes a linearity nothing has tested. Always plot the residuals; a pattern in them means the straight line is the wrong model.

04 · The regression working method

To fit and use a regression: plot the data first to confirm a roughly linear trend; compute the slope and intercept by least squares; report \( R^2 \) and, crucially, inspect a residual plot for curvature, funnelling variance, or outliers that a single number hides. Use the line to interpolate within the data range, never to extrapolate beyond it, and never state a causal conclusion the design of the study cannot support. Regression generalises to many predictors (multiple regression) and underlies calibration curves, empirical models, and the design-of-experiments analysis of the next lesson — but every one of those inherits these same three disciplines: plot, check residuals, and resist reading causation into a correlation. A closely related number is the correlation coefficient \(r\), the square root of \( R^2 \) carrying the sign of the slope, which measures the strength and direction of a linear association on a \(-1\) to \(+1\) scale; \(r=0\) means no linear relationship, though a strong curved one can still hide behind it — another reason the scatter plot is not optional. In practice the slope answers "how much," \(R^2\) and \(r\) answer "how tightly," and the residual plot answers "is a line even the right shape" — three questions every fitted relationship must survive before it is trusted for prediction.

Lesson 09 · Design of experiments first contact

01 · Experimenting on purpose

Design of experiments (DOE) is the discipline of planning tests so that the data answer the question with the fewest runs and the clearest conclusions. Its target is the relationship between input factors (temperature, pressure, feed rate) and an output response (yield, strength). The naive approach — change one factor at a time (OFAT), holding the rest fixed — is intuitive but wasteful and, worse, blind: it cannot detect interactions, where the effect of one factor depends on the level of another. Factorial design fixes both problems at once.

02 · Factorial designs

A full factorial tests every combination of factor levels. With \(k\) factors each at two levels (low and high), the design has \( 2^k \) runs: three factors need \( 2^3=8 \) runs, four need \( 2^4=16 \). From these runs the analysis extracts two things OFAT cannot. The main effect of a factor is the average change in response as it moves from low to high — computed across all the other factors' settings, so it is more reliable than a single OFAT sweep. The interaction effect measures how one factor's influence shifts with another's level, which is often where the real engineering lives: a catalyst that helps at high temperature but hurts at low is an interaction no one-factor study would ever reveal.

03 · Efficiency and fractional designs

Factorial designs are efficient because every run contributes to estimating every effect — the data are reused, not spent one factor at a time. When \(k\) is large, \( 2^k \) grows fast (seven factors would need 128 runs), so a fractional factorial runs a carefully chosen subset — a half-fraction \( 2^{k-1} \) uses 8 runs for four factors instead of 16 — trading the ability to resolve high-order interactions (usually negligible) for a large saving in runs. Good DOE also uses randomisation (to protect against unknown drifts), replication (to estimate noise), and blocking (to remove known nuisance variation), the three safeguards that separate a designed experiment from a mere collection of trials.

04 · The DOE working method

To design an experiment: list the factors and the response; choose two levels (or more for curvature); pick a full or fractional factorial sized to the run budget; and randomise the run order. Analyse by computing main effects and interactions, testing each against the noise estimated from replication — the hypothesis-testing logic of Lesson 7 applied to effects. Report the significant effects and, above all, the interactions, because those are what OFAT and intuition miss. DOE is the bridge from passive statistics (describing data that happened) to active engineering (arranging data to be maximally informative), and it recurs across the quality, process, and reliability courses wherever a process must be optimised rather than merely observed. A final note on scope: two levels per factor reveal linear effects and interactions but cannot see curvature — a response that peaks in the middle looks flat if only the ends are tested. Detecting an optimum needs a centre point or a three-level (response-surface) design, the natural next step once a factorial has identified which few factors actually matter. This screen-then-optimise sequence — a cheap factorial to find the vital factors, a richer design to tune them — is the standard industrial recipe, and it is far more efficient than either exhaustive testing or one-factor guesswork.

Lesson 10 · Reliability statistics preview

01 · The statistics of failure

Reliability is the probability that a component or system performs its function for a stated time under stated conditions — statistics applied to failure. Its central function is the reliability function \( R(t) \), the probability of surviving to time \(t\), which falls from 1 at \(t=0\) toward 0. Its complement \( F(t)=1-R(t) \) is the cumulative failure probability. This lesson is a preview of the dedicated reliability courses, connecting the distributions already met to the language of survival, failure rate, and mean life.

02 · The exponential model and MTBF

The simplest and most-used life model is the exponential, which assumes a constant failure rate \( \lambda \): \( R(t)=e^{-\lambda t} \). It is memoryless — a survivor is statistically as good as new — which fits random, non-wearout failures. Its mean time between failures is \( \text{MTBF}=1/\lambda \). A component with \( \lambda=0.001 \) failures per hour has \( \text{MTBF}=1000 \) hours, and its reliability at that mean life is \( R(1000)=e^{-0.001\times1000}=e^{-1}\approx0.368 \) — a much-misunderstood fact: only 37% survive to the MTBF, because the MTBF is a mean, not a guarantee, and the exponential is heavily right-skewed.

03 · Wearout, the bathtub, and systems

Constant rate is not universal. The Weibull distribution generalises the exponential with a shape parameter that captures infant mortality (falling rate, early defects), random failures (constant rate), and wearout (rising rate, ageing) — the three regions of the classic bathtub curve of failure rate over life. For systems, reliability combines by structure exactly like the probability rules of Lesson 2. Components in series (all must work) multiply: two at \(0.9\) give \( 0.9\times0.9=0.81 \), and reliability only falls as parts are added. Components in parallel (redundant, one suffices) combine through their failure probabilities: two at \(0.9\) give \( 1-(0.1)(0.1)=0.99 \) — redundancy is how a system is made more reliable than any of its parts.

04 · The reliability working method

To reason about reliability: pick the life model from the failure physics — exponential for random failures, Weibull when the rate changes with age — and estimate its parameters from failure data (the project pattern of the computing course). Compute \( R(t) \), MTBF, or a survival probability as the question demands, and never quote MTBF as a lifetime: report \( R(t) \) at the actual mission time. Build system reliability from component reliability using series (multiply) and parallel (redundancy) rules, and use them to find the weak link — in series, the least reliable component dominates. This preview hands directly to the reliability-engineering stream, where Weibull analysis, redundancy design, and maintenance strategy are developed in full, all resting on the survival function and the probability rules established here. One more quantity ties the pieces together: the failure rate or hazard \( h(t) \), the instantaneous rate of failure among survivors, which is constant for the exponential, falling then rising for the bathtub, and monotonic for many Weibull shapes. It is the hazard, not the raw failure count, that tells maintenance when to intervene — a rising hazard justifies scheduled replacement, while a constant one means age-based replacement buys nothing and condition monitoring is the better strategy. Reading the hazard shape from failure data is thus the hinge between the statistics of this lesson and the maintenance decisions of the reliability courses.

Lesson 11 · Statistical malpractice and honest reporting

01 · How statistics goes wrong

Statistics is as easily used to mislead as to inform, usually without outright lying. This closing lesson catalogues the common failures — some honest mistakes, some motivated — so that an engineer can produce and demand honest analysis. The theme is that every technique in this course has an integrity condition attached, and violating it quietly produces numbers that are precise, confident, and wrong. Recognising the failure modes is as much a professional skill as computing the statistics themselves.

02 · P-hacking and multiple comparisons

The most corrosive abuse is p-hacking: trying many analyses and reporting only the one that reached significance. Its engine is the multiple-comparisons problem. A single test at \( \alpha=0.05 \) has a 5% false-alarm rate, but run many and false positives become nearly certain: with 20 independent tests, the chance of at least one spurious "significant" result is \( 1-0.95^{20}\approx0.64 \) — about a two-in-three chance of a false discovery even when nothing is real. The honest defences are to fix the hypothesis before the data, to correct the threshold for the number of tests, and to report all analyses run, not just the flattering one.

03 · Base rates, confounding, and Simpson's paradox

Ignoring the base rate is the classic probability error. A test that is 99% sensitive and 95% specific for a fault present in 1% of parts, applied to a positive result, gives \( P(\text{fault}\mid\text{positive})=\dfrac{0.01\times0.99}{0.01\times0.99+0.99\times0.05}\approx0.167 \) — only a 17% chance the flagged part is actually faulty, because the rare condition is swamped by false positives. Confounding — a lurking variable driving both an apparent cause and effect — turns correlation into false causation, and its dramatic form is Simpson's paradox, where a trend that holds in every subgroup reverses when the groups are pooled. All three are failures to condition on the right information, and all three are invisible to anyone who trusts a single summary number.

04 · The honest-reporting working method

Report to inform, not to persuade. State the hypothesis and analysis plan before collecting data; report the sample size, the effect size, and a confidence interval, not just a p-value; and disclose every analysis performed. Draw graphs honestly — a truncated or inverted axis, a cherry-picked time window, or a mismatched scale can make a trivial change look dramatic or hide a real one, and misleading graphs are the most common statistical deception precisely because they bypass the arithmetic. Distinguish statistical from practical significance, correlation from causation, and the sample from the population. This is the integrity floor of the whole discipline, and it is the same floor this platform is built on: every number verified, every claim sourced, nothing precise-but-false. An engineer who can compute a statistic and also refuse to misuse it is the one this course aims to produce. A short checklist captures the defences: was the hypothesis fixed before the data, or fished from them; is the significance corrected for how many things were tested; does the claimed cause survive the obvious confounders; is the effect large enough to matter, not merely detectable; and does every graph use an honest scale. A result that passes all five is worth acting on; one that fails any of them is, at best, a lead to investigate rather than a conclusion to ship. Carrying that checklist is the mark of a professional, and it is the note this statistics course deliberately ends on.

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Semiconductors, diodes, and power supplies

01 · The diode and rectification

A diode conducts current in one direction only — forward-biased, once forward voltage exceeds roughly \(0.6\)–\(0.7\ \text{V}\) for silicon, it presents low resistance; reverse-biased, it blocks, up to its rated breakdown voltage. A single diode gives half-wave rectification, passing only one half of the AC cycle. Four diodes in a bridge configuration give full-wave rectification, redirecting both halves of the cycle the same way — using the source more completely and, critically, doubling the ripple frequency relative to a half-wave circuit (twice the mains frequency instead of once), which matters directly for the next section.

02 · Filtering and ripple

A capacitor placed across the rectified output smooths the pulsing waveform: it charges to the peak, then discharges slowly through the load between peaks, and is refilled at the next one. The residual variation left riding on the DC output is ripple. Larger capacitance, relative to load current, means a longer discharge time constant between refills and therefore less droop — smaller ripple. Full-wave rectification helps further, since the capacitor is refilled twice as often as with half-wave, for the identical component values.

03 · Regulation and the datasheet numbers

A voltage regulator — linear or switching — takes the filtered-but-still-rippling DC and holds the output constant despite both input variation and load-current changes. Regulation specifically means how well output voltage stays constant as something else varies: line regulation is the response to input voltage changes, load regulation is the response to output current changes — genuinely different specifications from ripple, which describes the raw filtered waveform before regulation cleans it up further. A rectifier-plus-regulator chain runs: AC in, rectified (pulsing DC), filtered (DC plus ripple), regulated (clean, stable DC) — reading a power-supply datasheet means recognizing which stage each spec actually describes.

04 · The 24 VDC world

24 VDC is the near-universal control-panel and field-instrumentation standard because it threads a genuine engineering needle: high enough above typical wiring voltage-drop and noise margins to travel realistic field distances without the signal degrading, yet low enough to fall under most jurisdictions' extra-low-voltage safety threshold, commonly \(50\ \text{VDC}\) — meaning 24 VDC field wiring and terminals can be worked on with far lighter safety precautions than mains-voltage wiring. Nearly every PLC I/O card, sensor, and field transmitter in an industrial plant runs on or interfaces at 24 VDC for exactly this reason — a safety-driven engineering standard, not a historical accident.

Key chain — a power supply's stages

AC in → rectified (pulsing) → filtered (DC + ripple) → regulated (clean, stable DC). Each stage has its own, different spec.

Lesson 02 · Transistors and switching

01 · The transistor as a switch

Driven by a small base (or gate) current or voltage, a transistor can control a much larger current through its collector (or drain) — the basis of both amplification and switching. Used as a switch, the goal is to avoid the linear amplifying region entirely and drive the device hard into one of two extremes: cutoff, where base drive is insufficient and essentially no collector current flows — the switch is open; or saturation, where base drive is large enough that the transistor is fully on, collector current is set by the external circuit, and the voltage drop across the transistor itself is small — the switch is closed.

02 · Why lingering in between matters — heat

Power dissipated in the transistor is (approximately) collector current times the voltage across it. In cutoff, current is near zero — negligible power, regardless of voltage. In saturation, voltage is near zero — negligible power, regardless of current. In the linear region between them, both current and voltage can be substantial simultaneously, and their product — real dissipated power — can be large. A switching circuit is designed specifically to cross that middle region quickly, not to idle there, which is why switching transistor circuits stay cool while linear amplifier stages, deliberately biased into that middle region, need real heat-sinking.

03 · Driving relays and indicator loads

A transistor switching an inductive load — a relay coil, a solenoid — faces the same interruption hazard from Electrical Fundamentals: opening that current path abruptly induces a large voltage spike (\(V=L\,dI/dt\)) that can exceed the transistor's rated breakdown voltage and destroy it instantly. A flyback diode across the coil gives that stored energy a safe path to decay through, exactly as it does for any mechanical contact switching an inductive load — the transistor needs the identical protection a relay's own contacts would.

04 · Solid-state relays versus electromechanical ones

A solid-state relay (SSR) uses a transistor (or triac, for AC) as the switching element, optically isolated from the control side — no moving parts, extremely fast switching, no contact bounce or wear, essentially unlimited switching life for a given current rating. An electromechanical relay (EMR) uses a physical armature and contacts — genuinely isolated by an air gap, tolerant of large inrush and surge currents an SSR of similar rating often cannot survive, and visibly, audibly confirmable as open or closed. An SSR wins where switching is frequent, fast, or silent operation matters (many cycles per minute, PWM-style control); an EMR wins where isolation certainty, surge tolerance, or simple visual/audible confirmation of state matters more than switching speed.

Key rule — switching, not amplifying

A switching transistor sits at cutoff (off) or saturation (on) — the linear region between them is crossed quickly, since that is where real power dissipation (and heat) occurs.

Lesson 03 · Op-amps and signal conditioning

01 · Amplification, filtering, and instrumentation amplifiers

A basic op-amp amplifies the voltage difference between its two inputs by a large, controllable gain. Filtering — allowing some frequencies through while attenuating others — shapes a signal before or after amplification, removing noise components outside the frequency range the actual measurement occupies. An instrumentation amplifier is a specialized differential amplifier, built from op-amps, optimized specifically for small, noisy sensor signals: very high input impedance (so it does not load or disturb the sensor), precisely matched internal gain paths, and — critically — very high rejection of any signal that appears identically on both inputs at once.

02 · Common-mode rejection as noise defense

Real field wiring runs through electrically noisy environments — motor drives, switching power supplies, nearby power cabling — and that noise tends to couple onto both wires of a signal pair roughly equally, since they run side by side. A signal appearing identically on both inputs is common-mode; the actual measurement is the small differential voltage between the two wires. Common-mode rejection ratio (CMRR), typically well over 80–100 dB for a good instrumentation amplifier, measures how strongly the amplifier suppresses the common-mode component while passing the differential signal through at full gain — CMRR does not reject noise in general; it specifically rejects whatever portion of the noise is identical on both input lines.

03 · Why thermocouple microvolts survive a noisy plant at all

A thermocouple generates only tens of microvolts per degree of temperature difference — a signal easily smaller than the noise picked up by even a modest length of field wiring running near power cabling. What makes the measurement possible at all is that the noise, coupling onto both thermocouple leads roughly equally, arrives at the instrumentation amplifier as a large common-mode signal riding on top of the genuinely tiny differential signal — and a high-CMRR amplifier strips that common-mode part away, leaving the real microvolt-level signal largely intact. Twisted-pair wiring reinforces this by keeping both conductors physically close together, so they pick up nearly identical noise to begin with — a physical partner to the amplifier's own electronic rejection.

04 · Working method

(1) Identify whether a signal is inherently small and differential — a thermocouple, an RTD bridge, a strain gauge — which flags it as needing an instrumentation amplifier rather than a simple single-ended op-amp stage. (2) Route it as a twisted, and ideally shielded, pair to keep common-mode pickup on both conductors as equal as possible. (3) Check the amplifier's CMRR spec against the expected noise environment — a higher CMRR is genuinely needed near variable-frequency drives and switching supplies than in a quiet instrument room. (4) Remember that CMRR only helps with noise common to both lines; differential noise — picked up unequally on the two conductors — passes through at full gain along with the real signal, which is exactly why twisted-pair routing matters as much as the amplifier's own spec.

Key idea — what CMRR actually rejects

CMRR rejects only the noise component identical on both input lines (common-mode) — differential noise, picked up unequally, passes through untouched.

Lesson 04 · Digital logic and interfaces

01 · Logic levels, gates, and combinational thinking

Digital logic represents information as one of two levels — commonly \(0\ \text{V}\) (logic low) and a supply-referenced high (logic high, often \(24\ \text{V}\) in industrial I/O, or \(3.3/5\ \text{V}\) inside a processor). Basic gates (AND, OR, NOT, and combinations built from them) implement logical relationships between these levels — the foundation both of processor logic and of the simpler combinational thinking used in relay-replacement ladder logic.

02 · Discrete inputs and outputs, as the PLC sees them

A discrete input reads one of two states — a switch open or closed, a proximity sensor detecting or not — and reports it to the controller as a logic level, not as an analog value; there is no "half-detected" reading. A discrete output similarly drives a field device — a solenoid, an indicator lamp, a small relay coil — to one of two states. This is the fundamental distinction from the analog signals covered elsewhere in this course: discrete I/O carries yes/no information, analog I/O carries a measured quantity across a continuous range.

03 · Sinking versus sourcing — the wiring confusion resolved

The confusion is almost always about which side of the load a switching device controls, not about the load or supply themselves. A sourcing output supplies current — its output terminal sits at (or near) the positive supply voltage when active, with the load's other end returning to common (0 V). A sinking output instead provides a path to common — the load's other end connects to the fixed positive supply, and the output terminal itself pulls down to (near) 0 V when active. The load can be electrically identical in both cases; what differs is purely which terminal is switched and which is fixed. Mixing sourcing field devices with sinking-only PLC input cards (or the reverse) is a wiring mismatch that produces a device that appears to do nothing, or does the opposite of what is expected, even though every individual component tested fine in isolation.

04 · Why PLCs read logic levels on discrete inputs

A discrete input card is built to distinguish only two states reliably, with margin against noise on either side, rather than to measure a precise voltage — it is designed for certainty (definitely on, definitely off), not precision. This is exactly why discrete and analog inputs are physically different card types on a PLC: an analog input card, by contrast, is built to resolve a continuous range accurately, at the cost of the simple robustness a discrete input's two-state design provides. Working method: (1) identify whether a signal is genuinely a yes/no state (discrete) or a measured quantity (analog); (2) for discrete I/O specifically, confirm sinking versus sourcing on both the field device and the PLC card before wiring, since this single detail — not the device's function — is the most common discrete-I/O wiring error.

Key distinction — sinking vs. sourcing

Sourcing: output supplies positive voltage, load returns to common. Sinking: output pulls to common, load's other end sits at fixed positive supply. Same load, opposite wiring.

Lesson 05 · Sensors on a production line

01 · The 4–20 mA loop, derived as an engineering decision

A current loop, rather than a voltage signal, is used for long field runs because current is unaffected by the voltage drop along the wire's own resistance — the same current flows at both ends regardless of cable length or gauge, unlike a voltage signal, which sags along a long run. The specific range \(4\)–\(20\ \text{mA}\), rather than \(0\)–\(20\ \text{mA}\), is a deliberate choice: \(4\ \text{mA}\) is the live zero — the minimum current the loop ever carries when everything is working, even at the very bottom of the measured range.

02 · Why the live zero matters

Because a healthy loop never drops below \(4\ \text{mA}\), a reading of \(0\ \text{mA}\) is never a valid measurement — it can only mean a broken wire, a disconnected transmitter, or a dead power supply. This turns "signal absent" into an unambiguous, immediately recognizable fault condition rather than something that could be confused with a legitimate zero reading — a genuinely important safety and diagnostic property that a \(0\)–\(20\ \text{mA}\) (or a voltage) signal does not offer nearly as cleanly.

03 · Reading the loop — the linear transfer function

The loop current maps linearly onto the measured range: \[ I=4+16\times\frac{x-x_{min}}{x_{max}-x_{min}}, \] where \(x\) is the measured quantity and \(x_{min}\), \(x_{max}\) define the transmitter's calibrated span. Reading the loop backward — from a measured current to the actual process value — inverts this directly: a transmitter spanning \(0\)–\(5\ \text{m}\) of level reading \(12\ \text{mA}\) corresponds to \((12-4)/16=0.5\), or exactly mid-scale, \(2.5\ \text{m}\).

04 · A filling line's sensor set, walked end to end

A representative filling line uses several sensor families together: a level transmitter (often 4-20mA, as above) tracking tank level; a flow meter confirming actual delivered volume; temperature sensors protecting product quality or equipment; proximity or photoelectric sensors detecting container presence and position; pressure transmitters monitoring pump and line health. Each speaks its own native signal (4-20mA analog for continuous measurements, discrete on/off for presence detection, as in Lesson 4), and reading a control system's live data means recognizing which signal type each point actually is before interpreting the number on the screen — a "0" from a discrete proximity sensor means something entirely different from a "0 mA" fault on an analog loop, even though both might render as a red indicator on the same screen.

Key transfer function — 4-20 mA

\[ I = 4 + 16\left(\frac{x-x_{min}}{x_{max}-x_{min}}\right) \]

0 mA is always a fault — never a valid reading.

Lesson 06 · Temperature measurement in depth

01 · RTD classes and self-heating

A resistance temperature detector (RTD) measures temperature by its own resistance change — the most common, a platinum Pt100, reads \(100\ \Omega\) at \(0^\circ\)C with a well-defined, highly linear resistance-versus-temperature relationship, giving RTDs excellent accuracy and repeatability compared to a thermocouple. But reading that resistance requires passing an excitation current through the RTD, and that current dissipates power, \(P=I^2R\), heating the element slightly above the true process temperature — self-heating. Because power scales with the square of current, a small increase in excitation current produces a disproportionately larger heating error: doubling excitation current quadruples self-heating, the identical relationship behind a loose electrical connection's dangerous heating. Self-heating is managed by keeping excitation current low and, where the process allows it, by good thermal contact between the RTD and a moving process fluid that carries the excess heat away.

02 · Thermocouple types, cold junctions, and extension wire

A thermocouple generates a small voltage from the junction of two dissimilar metals, with the voltage depending on the temperature difference between the measuring junction and a reference — the cold junction. Because the thermocouple only ever reports a temperature difference, the instrument reading it must independently know the cold-junction temperature (measured separately, at the terminal block) and add it back in — cold-junction compensation — to recover the actual process temperature. Different thermocouple types (J, K, T, and others) use different metal pairs, each suited to a different temperature range and environment; extension wire — running from the cold junction back to the instrument — must be made of the same thermocouple alloy pair as the sensor itself, not ordinary copper wire, or a second, unaccounted-for junction is created partway along the run, corrupting the reading in a way that is easy to overlook and hard to diagnose from the symptom alone.

03 · Thermowells and response lag

A thermowell is a closed-end tube inserted into a process, protecting the sensor inside it from direct contact with the process fluid — allowing sensor replacement without draining or depressurizing the process, and protecting against corrosion or erosion. That protection comes at a real cost: the thermowell's own thermal mass and the air gap (or fill fluid) between it and the sensor both slow the sensor's response to a genuine temperature change — response lag. A fast process upset can move faster than a thermowell-protected sensor can follow, a real limitation to weigh against the thermowell's real safety and maintainability benefits.

04 · Choosing and installing a temperature point — the working method

(1) Match sensor type to the temperature range and required accuracy: RTDs for higher accuracy and stability at moderate temperatures, thermocouples for very high temperatures or where ruggedness matters more than ultimate precision. (2) Keep RTD excitation current low, and verify the specific installation's self-heating error against its actual accuracy requirement. (3) Use only matched extension wire for thermocouples, all the way to the cold-junction reference point. (4) Weigh a thermowell's protection and maintainability against its response-lag cost for the specific application — a slow-moving tank temperature can easily tolerate the lag; a fast-cycling process control loop may not.

Key relation — self-heating

\[ P = I^2R \Rightarrow \Delta T \propto I^2. \]

Same I²R relationship as a loose electrical connection — small current changes, large error changes.

Lesson 07 · Pressure and level measurement in depth

01 · Pressure: gauge, absolute, differential

Gauge pressure is measured relative to local atmospheric pressure — the everyday convention for most industrial readings. Absolute pressure is measured relative to a true vacuum, needed whenever atmospheric variation itself would corrupt the measurement (vacuum processes, some flow calculations). Differential pressure (DP) is the difference between two separate pressure points — neither necessarily atmospheric — and is the working principle behind both flow measurement (across a restriction) and, as this lesson develops, level measurement in a closed or pressurized tank.

02 · Hydrostatic level measurement

A column of liquid of height \(h\) and density \(\rho\) exerts a pressure \(P=\rho g h\) at its base — the same hydrostatic relationship from Fluid Mechanics. An open tank, vented to atmosphere, needs only a single pressure tap near the bottom: the gauge pressure read there is directly proportional to level, since the atmosphere above the liquid contributes the same constant (zero, in gauge terms) regardless of level.

03 · Closed tanks and the wet leg

A closed or pressurized tank complicates this directly: the vapor space above the liquid is no longer at atmospheric pressure, and that vapor pressure varies with process conditions, independent of level. A single bottom tap can no longer distinguish "vapor pressure changed" from "level changed." The fix is a genuine differential pressure measurement: the high side connects near the tank bottom (pressure \(=P_{vapor}+\rho g H_L\)), and the low side connects to the vapor space above the liquid — but doing so with a long impulse line means that line itself may fill with condensing process vapor, an uncontrolled and unpredictable column. The standard fix is a wet leg: the low-side impulse line is deliberately, permanently filled with a known reference fluid, so its contribution is fixed and calculable — \(P_{low}=P_{vapor}+\rho_{ref} g H_{wetleg}\) — rather than left to chance.

04 · Zero suppression, and specifying the transmitter

Subtracting low from high, the vapor pressure term cancels exactly, leaving \(DP=\rho g H_L-\rho_{ref}gH_{wetleg}\) — a constant negative offset from the wet leg riding on top of the genuinely wanted level signal. Left uncorrected, the transmitter would read a large constant error at every level. The transmitter's calibration is instead zero-suppressed: its internal zero point is shifted to account for exactly the known, fixed wet-leg contribution, so the displayed output once again tracks only \(H_L\). Specifying a DP level transmitter for a closed tank therefore means fixing four things together: the process's actual level span, the reference fluid and wet-leg height (fixed by the physical installation), the resulting suppression value, and confirming the transmitter's own range can accommodate that shift without saturating.

Key relation — wet-leg suppression

\[ DP = \rho g H_L - \rho_{ref}\,g\,H_{wetleg} \]

The second term is fixed by installation — it is suppressed out in calibration, not measured.

Lesson 08 · Vibration and speed sensors

01 · Why vibration is measured

Rotating machinery signals developing problems — bearing wear, imbalance, misalignment, looseness — well before those problems cause an outright failure, and each fault type tends to produce vibration at characteristic frequencies tied to shaft speed and component geometry. Condition-based monitoring tracks vibration levels over time specifically to catch this early signature, scheduling maintenance before a bearing actually seizes rather than running to failure or replacing on a fixed calendar regardless of actual condition.

02 · The accelerometer

A piezoelectric accelerometer generates a small electrical charge proportional to the acceleration applied to it, exploiting the same piezoelectric effect used elsewhere in sensing — a crystal inside the sensor deforms slightly under acceleration, generating a proportional charge that conditioning electronics convert to a usable voltage signal. Because vibration is fundamentally an oscillating acceleration, this makes the accelerometer the standard sensor for machinery vibration monitoring.

03 · Mounting method sets the usable range

An accelerometer does not measure the machine's vibration directly — it measures the vibration actually transmitted through its own mounting into the sensor. Every mounting method has its own mechanical resonance, above which the mounting itself distorts the reading rather than faithfully transmitting it. In descending order of usable frequency range: a stud mount (threaded directly into a drilled, tapped hole) gives the most rigid coupling and the highest usable frequency range; an adhesive pad mount is nearly as good without requiring drilling; a magnetic mount is fast and fully non-invasive but its usable range drops noticeably, since the magnetic coupling itself is less rigid; a handheld probe (pressed against the machine by hand) is the least rigid of all, usable only for low-frequency, quick spot checks. A high-frequency bearing defect frequency, read through a magnetic mount whose flat range has already rolled off, can be significantly under-read or missed entirely — not because the sensor is faulty, but because the mounting itself cannot faithfully transmit that frequency.

04 · Speed and proximity sensors

Vibration analysis is far more diagnostic when correlated with actual shaft speed, since most machinery fault frequencies scale directly with RPM. A tachometer or a proximity probe aimed at a shaft keyway or a once-per-revolution reflective mark provides a speed reference signal, letting vibration frequencies be expressed as orders of running speed (1×, 2×, 3× RPM, and so on) rather than raw Hz — a much more directly diagnostic way to distinguish imbalance (typically 1× RPM) from misalignment (typically 2× RPM) from other fault signatures.

Key ranking — mounting rigidity

Stud > adhesive > magnet > handheld probe, in both usable frequency range and measurement repeatability.

Lesson 09 · Analog-to-digital and sampling

01 · Analog-to-digital conversion

An ADC converts a continuous analog voltage into a discrete digital number, at a moment in time it is triggered to sample. Two independent specifications govern how faithfully that conversion represents the original signal: resolution — how many discrete levels the ADC can distinguish, set by its bit depth (a 12-bit ADC resolves \(2^{12}=4096\) levels across its input range) — and sampling rate — how often, in samples per second (Hz), the ADC takes a new reading. Resolution limits how finely a single reading is known; sampling rate limits how fast a changing signal can be tracked at all.

02 · The Nyquist criterion

The Nyquist criterion states that a signal must be sampled at a rate at least twice its highest frequency component to be reconstructed unambiguously — \(F_s>2f_{max}\). Half the sampling rate, \(F_s/2\), is the Nyquist frequency: the highest frequency that can be faithfully captured at that sampling rate. A signal containing energy above the Nyquist frequency is not simply lost — it reappears, corrupted, as described next.

03 · Aliasing — a false frequency, not a missing one

When a signal above the Nyquist frequency is sampled anyway, it does not vanish — it aliases, reappearing in the digitized data as a false signal at a lower frequency, indistinguishable from a genuine signal at that lower frequency. A signal at \(700\ \text{Hz}\), sampled at \(F_s=1000\ \text{Hz}\) (Nyquist frequency \(500\ \text{Hz}\)), aliases to \(|700-1000|=300\ \text{Hz}\) — the digitized data looks exactly as if a real \(300\ \text{Hz}\) signal had been sampled, and no processing performed after the fact on the digital samples alone can tell the two apart, because the samples themselves are identical either way.

04 · Anti-aliasing filters — fixing it before, not after

Because aliasing is unrecoverable after digitization — the information distinguishing "real 300 Hz" from "aliased 700 Hz" is simply gone from the samples — the only correct fix is prevention: an anti-aliasing filter, an analog low-pass filter placed before the ADC, removing any signal content above the Nyquist frequency before sampling ever occurs. Choosing a sampling rate for a new measurement therefore means identifying the highest genuine frequency of interest first (a bearing defect frequency, a fast pressure transient), setting \(F_s\) comfortably above twice that value, and fitting an anti-aliasing filter tuned to the resulting Nyquist frequency — sampling rate and filter are chosen together, not independently.

Key relation — Nyquist and aliasing

\[ F_s > 2f_{max}, \qquad f_{alias}=|f_{true}-F_s| \ \text{(when } f_{true}>F_s/2 \text{)} \]

Lesson 10 · Signal transmission and noise

01 · Ground is not one number everywhere

Ground symbols on a schematic suggest a single, shared \(0\ \text{V}\) reference — in practice, a real plant's grounding grid has resistance, and different equipment grounded at physically distant points can sit at slightly different actual potentials, especially when other equipment (large motors, welding, switching drives) injects current into that same grid. This ground potential difference, often just a volt or two, is normally harmless — until a cable's shield connects two such points directly together.

02 · How a ground loop forms

A cable shield grounded at both its field end and its control-room end forms a closed electrical path between those two ground points. If those two points sit at even a small potential difference, that difference drives a real current through the shield — a ground loop current — entirely independent of, and in addition to, the shield's intended job of blocking external electromagnetic interference. That circulating current can couple into the actual signal conductors, appearing as noise, drift, or an offset error that has nothing to do with the process being measured.

03 · The fix — single-point grounding

Grounding the shield at only one end — conventionally the source or control-room end, with the far end left floating (not connected to local ground) — leaves the loop open. No closed path exists, so no current can flow through the shield regardless of how different the two ground potentials are, while the shield still performs its intended job of intercepting external interference and carrying it away at the single grounded end. This is why a shielded cable correctly landed at one end, and correctly left open at the other, is not a wiring shortcut — it is the deliberate fix for a real, measurable failure mode.

04 · When isolation is worth the added cost

Sometimes both ends genuinely need a local ground connection for their own independent safety or EMI reasons, or the expected potential difference is too large to leave to single-point grounding alone. A signal isolator (or isolation amplifier) uses a transformer or optical coupling to pass the signal's information across a gap with no direct electrical (galvanic) connection between the two sides — each side can be grounded locally as needed, with no possible path for a loop current to form between them, at the cost of an added component in the signal path. Choosing between single-point grounding and full galvanic isolation is a judgment call based on the expected potential difference, the distance involved, and whether both ends have an independent reason to be locally grounded.

Key rule — shield grounding

Ground a cable shield at ONE end only. Grounding both ends turns any ground potential difference into a driving voltage for a loop current.

Lesson 11 · Sensor selection and installation project

01 · Define the measurement first

Before any sensor catalog is opened, the actual measurement needs must be pinned down: what physical quantity, over what range, at what accuracy the application genuinely requires. Over-specifying — demanding laboratory-grade accuracy for a rough trend indication — wastes money on unnecessary precision; under-specifying risks a control or safety decision built on a measurement that cannot actually resolve what it needs to. This single step, done carelessly, is the root cause of a large share of "wrong sensor" purchases — not a failure of the electronics that came later.

02 · Match the signal and wiring convention

With the measurement defined, the sensor's output must match the receiving system: an analog quantity typically means a 4-20 mA loop (Lesson 5) with its live-zero fault detection; a yes/no state means a discrete output, correctly matched for sourcing or sinking against the input card it will connect to (Lesson 4) — checked before purchase, not discovered during commissioning. A small, inherently noisy signal (a thermocouple, a strain-gauge bridge) additionally needs an instrumentation amplifier with adequate CMRR for the expected noise environment (Lesson 3).

03 · Weigh the installation constraints

The sensor also has to survive and perform in its actual physical installation: mounting method, chosen for the required accuracy and (for vibration) frequency range against cost and convenience (Lesson 8); protection against the process environment, weighed against the response-lag cost that protection can add (thermowells, Lesson 6); and the electrical noise environment, addressed through shielding, twisted-pair routing, correct single-point grounding, and — for genuinely difficult cases — galvanic isolation (Lesson 10). None of these are optional footnotes; each is a real, previously-demonstrated failure mode when skipped.

04 · Document the decision — the specification sheet

The output of this process is a documented specification: measured quantity and range, required accuracy, output signal type and wiring convention, mounting method, environmental protection, and any special wiring or grounding requirements — nothing left to be discovered later by whoever happens to install it. A worked example ties the whole course together: specifying a bearing-temperature point on rotating equipment means working through all four steps — range and accuracy from the equipment's known operating temperature (1); an RTD, not a thermocouple, chosen for its superior accuracy and stability at that moderate temperature, output as 4-20 mA (2, Lessons 5-6); a thermowell for maintainability, its response lag checked against how fast the process can actually change (3, Lesson 6); and a documented spec sheet recording all of it, including the low RTD excitation current chosen to keep self-heating error negligible (4) — the same reasoning this course has built lesson by lesson, now applied as one deliberate sequence rather than an afterthought.

Key discipline — the specification process

Define the measurement → match signal and wiring → weigh installation constraints → document the decision. Skipping a step does not remove the question, it only delays discovering the answer.

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Theory of metal machining

01 · The orthogonal cutting model

Orthogonal cutting simplifies real machining to two dimensions: a straight cutting edge perpendicular to the direction of motion, removing a layer of uncut thickness \(t_o\) and producing a chip of thickness \(t_c\). Chip formation is idealized as shear across a single shear plane, at angle \(\phi\) from the cutting direction — everything ahead of that plane is still workpiece; everything behind it is chip. The chip thickness ratio \(r=t_o/t_c\) is always less than 1 (the chip thickens as it forms, since it must move slower than the uncut layer approached the tool, by conservation of mass at steady state), and relates directly to the shear angle and the tool's rake angle \(\alpha\) (the angle of the tool's cutting face from the normal to the work surface): \[ \tan\phi = \frac{r\cos\alpha}{1-r\sin\alpha}. \] A higher rake angle, for the same chip thickness ratio, increases the shear angle — a thinner, more efficiently formed chip and lower cutting force, which is exactly why rake angle is a first lever a machinist or tool designer reaches for.

02 · Cutting forces and Merchant's circle

The tool applies a resultant force to the chip, conventionally decomposed into a cutting force \(F_c\) (in the direction of tool travel) and a thrust force \(F_t\) (perpendicular to it) — both directly measurable with a dynamometer. That same resultant, viewed from the shear plane's own geometry, decomposes instead into a shear force \(F_s\) along the shear plane (doing the actual work of separating the chip) and a normal force across it; viewed from the rake face, it decomposes into a friction force along the chip-tool interface and a normal force against the rake face. Merchant's circle is the construction that relates all of these to each other and to the two governing angles, \(\phi\) and \(\alpha\), plus the friction angle \(\beta\) (with \(\tan\beta=\mu\), the tool-chip friction coefficient) — once any one force and the geometry are known, the rest follow.

03 · Where cutting temperature and tool wear begin

Essentially all of the mechanical work done in cutting — shearing the chip plus overcoming friction on the rake face — converts to heat, concentrated in a small volume at the tool-chip interface and along the shear plane. Temperatures there commonly exceed several hundred degrees Celsius for ordinary steels, well into the range where a tool material's own hardness begins to fall off (previewed here, developed fully in Lesson 2). This is the direct mechanical link between cutting theory and tool wear: higher cutting speed means more heat generated per unit time in roughly the same small volume, which is the single biggest lever on how fast a tool wears out.

04 · Taylor's tool-life equation, read as economics

Tool life \(T\) (time to a defined wear failure) falls sharply as cutting speed \(V\) rises — captured empirically by the Taylor equation, \[ VT^n=C, \] where \(n\) and \(C\) are constants for a given tool-work material pair, found by cutting at several speeds and measuring life at each. Because \(n\) is typically small (often \(0.1\)–\(0.3\) for high-speed steel and carbide tools), a modest speed increase produces a disproportionately large tool-life reduction — the equation is less a physics law than a compact economic statement: pushing speed trades tool cost and changeover time against material removed per hour, and \(n\) tells you exactly how unfavorable that trade becomes.

Key relation — chip geometry to shear angle

\[ \tan\phi = \frac{r\cos\alpha}{1-r\sin\alpha}, \qquad r=\frac{t_o}{t_c} \]

Lesson 02 · Cutting tools and fluids

01 · Tool materials: the hardness-toughness trade

Every tool material sits somewhere on a trade-off between hot hardness (retaining cutting-edge hardness at the high temperatures Lesson 1 introduced) and toughness (resisting chipping and fracture under interrupted or shock loading). High-speed steel (HSS) is tough and inexpensive but loses hardness above roughly 600°C. Cemented carbides (tungsten carbide in a cobalt binder) hold hardness to much higher temperatures, enabling far higher cutting speeds, at some cost in toughness. Coated carbides — a thin, hard ceramic layer (titanium nitride, titanium carbonitride, aluminum oxide, among others) deposited on a tougher carbide substrate — combine a wear-resistant surface with a fracture-resistant core, and now dominate most general steel-turning work. Ceramics (aluminum oxide, silicon nitride based) push hot hardness further still, at further cost in toughness, reserved for high-speed finishing on hard, stable materials.

02 · Wear modes and what each one signals

Flank wear — a wear land on the tool's clearance face, measured as its width \(VB\) — is the everyday, expected wear mode, driven by abrasion and adhesion against the newly cut surface; it is what Taylor's equation (Lesson 1) actually tracks. Crater wear — a pocket worn into the rake face by the flowing chip, measured by depth \(KT\) — is driven primarily by diffusion and chemical wear at the high temperatures of the tool-chip interface, and signals a tool material or coating poorly matched to the temperature the cut is generating. Notch wear — a localized groove at the depth-of-cut line, where the tool meets the original work surface or a hardened scale — signals a work material or prior-pass condition attacking the tool at one specific point rather than uniformly.

03 · Insert geometry and grade, read from a catalog

A carbide insert's grade (an ISO-style code, roughly ranging from tougher/lower-hardness grades for interrupted or rough cuts to harder/more wear-resistant grades for continuous finishing cuts) is the manufacturer's own position on the hardness-toughness trade from 1, chosen to match the specific operation. Nose radius — the rounded tip connecting the two cutting edges — trades two things directly: a larger radius gives a smoother theoretical surface finish and a stronger tip, but increases the radial (thrust-like) force component and the tendency to chatter on a less rigid setup; a smaller radius cuts more freely but leaves a rougher theoretical finish and a weaker tip. Reading an insert catalog well means matching grade to the cut's continuity and grade-appropriate speed range, and nose radius to the finish requirement and the setup's rigidity — not simply picking the hardest grade or largest radius available.

04 · Cutting fluids

A cutting fluid serves two distinct functions that do not always point the same direction: cooling (removing heat from the cutting zone, favoring a fluid with good thermal properties, typically water-based) and lubrication (reducing friction at the tool-chip interface, favoring an oil-based fluid). Most industrial fluids are a formulated compromise — water-based emulsions and semi-synthetics for predominantly cooling-driven operations, straight or heavy-duty oils where friction reduction dominates, such as tapping and broaching. Fluid also serves a third, often underrated function: flushing chips clear of the cutting zone, especially in drilling and deep-hole operations where chip evacuation, not heat, is the limiting factor. Mist and fluid exposure carry real health considerations — inhalation of fine mist and skin contact with used fluid (which accumulates tramp oil, bacteria, and fines over service life) are managed through enclosure, extraction, and fluid maintenance, not treated as an unavoidable cost of the process.

Key distinction — wear modes

Flank wear (VB) — the expected, tracked wear. Crater wear (KT) — thermal/chemical, signals a material/coating mismatch. Notch wear — localized, signals a specific attack point.

Lesson 03 · Turning, milling, and drilling

01 · Turning arithmetic

For a workpiece of diameter \(D\) rotating at \(N\) rev/min, the surface — where cutting actually happens — moves at cutting speed \[ V=\pi D N. \] The tool advances axially at feed \(f\) (distance per revolution) to a depth of cut \(d\) (radial engagement). Material removal rate is \[ MRR = V f d \] (consistent units throughout), and machining time for a cut of length \(L\) is \[ T_m = \frac{L}{fN}. \] These four relations are the entire arithmetic core of turning — everything else is choosing \(V\), \(f\), and \(d\) sensibly for the material, tool, and machine at hand.

02 · Milling arithmetic

Milling's version of the same bookkeeping replaces single-point feed with feed per tooth \(f_z\), scaled by the number of teeth \(n_t\) and spindle speed \(N\) into a table feed rate \[ f_r = f_z\, n_t\, N. \] Material removal rate follows the same shape as turning, \(MRR = f_r\, w\, d\) (width of cut \(w\) replacing diameter's role), and machining time is again length of cut divided by feed rate. The practical difference from turning is mostly bookkeeping — more variables (number of teeth, feed per tooth) feeding the identical underlying logic of speed, feed, depth, and time.

03 · Drilling arithmetic

Drilling scales the turning relations to a rotating tool cutting into stationary stock: cutting speed is still \(V=\pi DN\) using the drill's own diameter, feed \(f\) is axial advance per revolution, and machining time for a hole of depth \(L\) is again \(T_m=L/(fN)\) (with an allowance commonly added for the drill point's own geometry reaching full diameter). Material removal rate follows the full-circle cross-section swept, \(MRR=\frac{\pi D^2}{4}fN\). Drilling's practical complication is less the arithmetic and more chip evacuation and heat — the cutting zone is buried inside the hole, which is exactly why cutting fluid delivery (Lesson 2) matters more here than almost anywhere else in machining.

04 · Workholding and one job worked end to end

None of the above arithmetic matters if the part is not held rigidly and accurately enough to survive the resulting forces: a three-jaw chuck for round stock in turning, vises and fixtures in milling, drill chucks or collets for the tool itself — each chosen for the required accuracy, the forces the operation generates, and how many parts must be loaded and unloaded per hour. Consider a shaft: 40 mm diameter, 200 mm length of cut, turned at \(V=120\) m/min, feed \(f=0.3\) mm/rev, depth of cut \(d=2\) mm per pass, two passes to size. Spindle speed \(N=V/(\pi D)=120{,}000/(\pi\times40)\approx955\) rev/min. Machining time per pass \(T_m=L/(fN)=200/(0.3\times955)\approx0.70\) min; two passes, roughly \(1.4\) min of cutting time — the number a planner turns into a cycle-time estimate and, ultimately, a quoted price.

Key relations — turning

\[ V=\pi DN, \qquad MRR=Vfd, \qquad T_m=\frac{L}{fN} \]

Lesson 04 · Machine tools and CNC basics

01 · Lathe anatomy

A lathe holds the workpiece and rotates it (the spindle, in the headstock), while the tool — held in a turret or tool post on the carriage — is fed along two axes: \(Z\), parallel to the spindle axis (controlling length of cut), and \(X\), radial (controlling diameter). A tailstock, at the opposite end from the headstock, supports the free end of a long or slender workpiece against the significant radial force cutting can generate. This two-axis logic (Z along the work, X radial) is the lathe's entire coordinate world — everything programmed or dialed in reduces to positions along these two directions.

02 · Machining center anatomy

A vertical machining center holds the tool in a rotating spindle and moves the workpiece (via the table) or the spindle itself along three linear axes: \(X\) and \(Y\) in the horizontal table plane, \(Z\) vertical, toward or away from the work. A tool changer, holding a magazine of pre-set tools, lets a single program call up a new tool automatically between operations — turning what would be a sequence of manual tool swaps into one continuous, unattended cycle.

03 · What G-code actually commands

G-code is a coordinate-and-motion instruction language: each block specifies a motion type and a target position. \(G00\) commands a rapid, non-cutting positioning move at maximum machine speed; \(G01\) commands a linear feed move at a programmed feed rate — the actual cutting motion; \(G02\)/\(G03\) command circular interpolation, clockwise and counterclockwise. A block such as G01 X50.0 Z-30.0 F0.3 commands a linear feed move to \(X=50.0\), \(Z=-30.0\), at feed \(0.3\) (units and axis interpretation depending on the controller's active mode) — every dimension in the print, translated into exactly this kind of coordinate instruction, is what a CAM system (or a programmer, for simple work) actually produces.

04 · Accuracy, repeatability, and backlash — machine health a maintenance engineer owns

Accuracy is how close the machine's actual position comes to the commanded position — affected by calibration, thermal growth, and wear, and correctable to a degree by offsets. Repeatability is how consistently the machine returns to the same commanded position across repeated moves — a tighter, more fundamental limit than accuracy, since a position that is merely wrong-but-consistent can be compensated for, while a position that is inconsistent cannot. Backlash — play in a leadscrew or drive train that shows up as a small position error specifically when an axis reverses direction — is a repeatability problem with a mechanical origin, distinguishable by that specific direction-reversal signature, and is exactly the kind of machine-health quantity a maintenance engineer measures and trends over a machine's life, not something a part programmer can compensate around reliably.

Key distinction — accuracy vs. repeatability

Accuracy: how close to commanded. Repeatability: how consistent, position to position. A repeatability problem cannot be reliably offset away; an accuracy problem often can.

Lesson 05 · Grinding and abrasive processes

01 · Abrasive mechanics

A grinding wheel's cutting edges are its abrasive grains — geometrically ill-defined, randomly oriented, and engaging the work at a strongly negative effective rake angle compared to a single-point tool. Each grain's individual depth of cut is tiny, often only a few micrometers, and a meaningful fraction of grains plow or rub against the surface rather than cleanly shearing a chip — fundamentally less efficient, per unit volume of material actually removed, than the orthogonal cutting of Lesson 1.

02 · Specific energy — why grinding burns parts when abused

Specific energy \(u=P_c/MRR\) (power consumed per unit volume removed per unit time) captures this inefficiency numerically: where ordinary turning of steel runs roughly \(1\)–\(3\ \text{J/mm}^3\), grinding the same material commonly runs one to two orders of magnitude higher — a direct consequence of the plowing and rubbing in 1. Nearly all of that energy still ends up as heat, concentrated in an extremely thin surface layer with almost none of the mass a turning chip carries away with it. Pushed too hard — too high a wheel speed relative to feed, too little coolant, a dull or loaded wheel — that heat concentration produces visible surface discoloration (burn) and, more seriously, metallurgical damage (untempered martensite, softening, or re-hardening) reaching well below the visible surface, exactly the reliability risk Lesson 6 develops further.

03 · Reading a wheel designation

A grinding wheel's marking encodes, in sequence, its abrasive type (aluminum oxide for steels; silicon carbide for cast iron, non-ferrous, and non-metallics; superabrasives — CBN, diamond — for hardened steel and carbide respectively), grit size (coarser numbers cut faster and cooler but leave a rougher finish; finer numbers the reverse), grade (a letter indicating bond hardness — how readily worn grains release to expose fresh cutting edges), and structure (grain spacing, denser or more open). Selecting a wheel is matching each of these to the work material, stock removal required, and finish target — the same kind of grade-to-application matching Lesson 2 developed for cutting tool inserts.

04 · Surface and cylindrical grinding, dressing, and balancing

Surface grinding (a reciprocating or rotary table beneath a horizontal wheel) produces flat surfaces; cylindrical grinding (workpiece rotating between centers or in a chuck, wheel traversing along its length) produces precision round features — both typically a finishing operation following rougher machining, given grinding's low material removal rate relative to its cost per unit volume removed. Dressing restores a wheel's cutting ability and geometric profile by fracturing away dulled grains and any embedded (loaded) chip material, using a diamond dresser or similar tool — a wheel left un-dressed rubs more than it cuts, driving exactly the specific-energy and burn risk of 2. Balancing corrects any mass eccentricity in the wheel itself, since an out-of-balance wheel running at typical grinding speeds — often tens of thousands of surface feet per minute — vibrates enough to degrade both finish and wheel life, and in the worst case risks the wheel itself.

Key comparison — specific energy

Turning: ~1–3 J/mm³. Grinding: roughly 10–50× higher for the same material — mostly plowing and rubbing, not clean shear.

Lesson 06 · Surface finish and integrity

01 · Ra and Rz defined

Ra (arithmetic average roughness) is the mean of the absolute deviation of the surface profile from its own mean line, measured over a defined sampling length — an averaging measure, which means a single unusually deep scratch or peak barely moves it, since it is diluted across the whole sampled length. Rz, by contrast, is built from peak-to-valley extremes (the specific averaging convention varies by standard, but the shared idea is the same) — far more sensitive to occasional deep features that Ra would average away. Both are measured with a profilometer, a stylus or optical instrument tracing the actual surface profile.

02 · Finish versus process capability

Each process has a characteristic achievable Ra range: rough turning or milling typically several micrometers Ra; fine turning or milling roughly \(0.8\)–\(3\ \mu\text{m}\); grinding roughly \(0.1\)–\(0.8\ \mu\text{m}\); lapping and honing well under \(0.1\ \mu\text{m}\) where required. Specifying a finish tighter than a process can reliably achieve forces an unnecessary second operation (typically grinding, following rougher machining) — reading a process-capability chart before specifying a finish on a drawing is what prevents that avoidable cost, exactly the same discipline Lesson 2's insert-grade selection and Lesson 5's grinding-wheel selection both apply to their own decisions.

03 · Residual stresses from machining and grinding

Cutting and grinding both leave a thin near-surface layer in a state of residual stress — compressive or tensile, depending on process parameters (aggressive grinding conditions, for instance, tend to leave tensile residual stress from the localized thermal expansion-and-cooling cycle of 2 in Lesson 5, while properly controlled conditions or processes like shot peening deliberately induce compressive stress instead). This matters directly for fatigue life (Strength of Materials): a near-surface tensile residual stress adds to any applied tensile stress, accelerating crack initiation at exactly the location fatigue cracks start; a compressive residual stress does the reverse, actively resisting crack initiation. Two parts with identical Ra and identical geometry can have meaningfully different fatigue lives purely from this residual-stress difference — a fact no surface-finish number alone reveals.

04 · Why a ground bearing journal's finish is a reliability parameter

A bearing journal's surface finish is not merely cosmetic: too rough a finish accelerates wear and can disrupt the lubricant film a rolling-element or journal bearing depends on to separate its surfaces; too aggressive a grind, chasing an ever-finer Ra without controlling heat and residual stress, risks exactly the tensile-residual-stress and metallurgical-damage problem of 3 and Lesson 5 — a journal that looks better on a profilometer printout while being mechanically worse underneath it. Specifying and verifying a critical journal therefore means controlling both the Ra number and the process conditions that produced it, not the Ra number alone.

Key distinction — Ra vs. Rz

Ra: average deviation — smooths over outliers. Rz: peak-to-valley — catches the occasional deep feature Ra hides.

Lesson 07 · Nontraditional machining

01 · Electrical discharge machining (EDM)

EDM removes material by repeated electrical sparks between a shaped tool electrode and a conductive workpiece, separated by a small gap filled with dielectric fluid that normally insulates the gap and breaks down momentarily at each spark. Each discharge melts and vaporizes a tiny crater of material, flushed away by the circulating dielectric; the tool electrode is fed down to maintain a constant gap as material erodes. Because removal is thermal and electrical rather than mechanical, EDM works on any electrically conductive material regardless of hardness — hardened tool steel and carbide, both difficult or impossible to machine conventionally once hardened, cut exactly as readily as soft steel, which is precisely why EDM is the standard way to finish a hardened die cavity after heat treatment.

02 · Wire EDM

Wire EDM replaces the shaped electrode with a continuously traveling thin wire (commonly brass or coated wire), acting like an electrically eroding band saw guided along a programmed 2D (or tapered) path through the workpiece. It excels at contour-cutting — extrusion dies, stamping punches, gears — profiles that would need an expensive custom-shaped electrode in sinker EDM, since the wire itself is the simple, constantly-renewed "tool."

03 · Electrochemical machining (ECM)

ECM works by controlled anodic dissolution — essentially electroplating run in reverse: the workpiece is the anode, the shaped tool is the cathode, and an electrolyte flows through the gap carrying dissolved workpiece material away as current passes. Like EDM, there is no mechanical contact and no tool wear in the conventional sense, and hardness is irrelevant — ECM is well suited to very hard, exotic, or difficult-to-machine alloys (nickel superalloys, for instance) where conventional cutting tools would wear unacceptably fast, with the added benefit of leaving no thermally affected layer, since the process is chemical/electrical rather than thermal.

04 · Laser and abrasive-waterjet cutting, and selecting among all four

Laser cutting uses a focused, intense beam to melt or vaporize material along a path, fast and precise on thinner sections but leaving a heat-affected zone the other nonconventional processes largely avoid. Abrasive-waterjet cutting uses a high-pressure water stream carrying abrasive particles to erode a path mechanically — cold-cutting, with no heat-affected zone at all, at the cost of a wider kerf and generally lower precision than laser or wire EDM. Selecting among EDM, wire EDM, ECM, laser, and waterjet is a genuine engineering decision, not a default: hardened or intricate die cavities favor sinker EDM; contour profiles in tool steel favor wire EDM; hard exotic alloys where thermal damage is unacceptable favor ECM; fast, thin-section production cutting favors laser; and thick sections or heat-sensitive materials where any heat-affected zone is unacceptable favor waterjet.

Key idea — why these processes exist

Each nonconventional process removes a specific constraint conventional cutting has (tool hardness limit, sharp-edge requirement, heat sensitivity) — matched by which constraint the job actually faces.

Lesson 08 · Welding processes I — arc processes

01 · The arc welding circuit

Every arc welding process shares the same basic circuit: a power source drives current through a cable to an electrode, across a small arc gap (an intensely hot, ionized-gas plasma), through the workpiece, and back to the power source via a ground return. The arc's heat melts both the electrode (in consumable-electrode processes) and the base metal locally, forming a shared molten pool that solidifies into the weld as the arc moves on. What distinguishes one arc process from another is almost entirely how the electrode is fed and consumed, and how the arc and molten metal are shielded from atmospheric oxygen and nitrogen — not this underlying circuit.

02 · Consumable-electrode processes — SMAW, GMAW, FCAW

SMAW (shielded metal arc welding, "stick" welding) uses a flux-coated consumable electrode rod, manually fed as it melts, with the flux coating decomposing to shield the arc and forming a slag layer that must be chipped off afterward — simple, portable equipment, well suited to field work and all positions, at a lower deposition rate than the wire-fed processes. GMAW (gas metal arc welding, "MIG") continuously feeds a bare wire electrode through a torch, shielded by an externally supplied gas (commonly argon, CO₂, or a mixture) — faster, cleaner (no slag), and more easily automated, but more sensitive to wind and drafts blowing the shielding gas away, making it primarily a shop process. FCAW (flux-cored arc welding) feeds a tubular wire filled with flux, either self-shielding (the flux alone providing shielding, tolerant of wind, suited to field and structural work) or gas-shielded (higher quality, shop-oriented) — a genuine hybrid combining SMAW's field robustness with GMAW's continuous-wire productivity.

03 · Non-consumable and submerged processes — GTAW, SAW

GTAW (gas tungsten arc welding, "TIG") uses a non-consumable tungsten electrode, with filler metal (if used) added separately by hand, shielded by inert gas — slow, requiring real operator skill, but producing the highest quality, cleanest welds of any common arc process, standard for thin sections, precision work, and demanding materials (stainless steel, aluminum, exotic alloys). SAW (submerged arc welding) feeds a continuous bare wire electrode beneath a blanket of granular flux that fully covers and shields the arc (the arc is genuinely not visible during welding) — very high deposition rates and deep penetration, but restricted to flat or near-flat positions by the flux blanket's dependence on gravity, standard for long, straight, heavy welds such as structural plate and large-diameter pipe seams.

04 · Heat input and typical defects

Heat input quantifies energy delivered per unit length of weld: \[ HI = \frac{\eta VI}{v}, \] where \(V\) is arc voltage, \(I\) is welding current, \(v\) is travel speed, and \(\eta\) is arc efficiency (process-dependent, commonly \(0.7\)–\(0.9\) for the processes above). Too little heat input risks lack of fusion (the weld metal fails to properly bond to the base metal) and porosity (trapped gas, often from inadequate shielding or moisture); too much heat input risks excessive grain growth and distortion, and in some materials, unwanted metallurgical changes in the heat-affected zone. Travel speed is often the most immediately controllable lever — slowing down at fixed voltage and current increases heat input roughly in inverse proportion, which is exactly why an inconsistent travel speed is one of the most common sources of inconsistent weld quality along a single joint.

Key relation — heat input

\[ HI = \frac{\eta VI}{v} \]

Slower travel at the same voltage/current means proportionally higher heat input.

Lesson 09 · Welding II, thermal cutting, and joint preparation

01 · Resistance welding — spot and seam

Resistance spot welding clamps two sheets between copper electrodes and passes a large current briefly through the stack; resistance is highest at the interface between the sheets (not through the sheets themselves, which conduct comparatively well), so \(I^2R\) heating is concentrated exactly there, melting a small "nugget" that fuses the sheets locally as it cools under the electrodes' clamping force — no filler metal, no external shielding, and a cycle time measured in a fraction of a second. Resistance seam welding replaces the point electrodes with rotating wheel electrodes, producing a continuous, overlapping series of nuggets — effectively a continuous leak-tight seam. Both are standard for high-volume sheet-metal assembly (automotive bodies are the classic example) precisely because of that speed and the complete absence of filler metal or shielding gas consumables.

02 · Friction, explosion, and oxyfuel welding, in outline

Friction welding joins two parts (commonly round, rotationally symmetric ones) by pressing them together while one rotates against the other, generating heat by friction alone at the interface, then forging them together under pressure as rotation stops — no filler metal, no arc, no melting in the conventional sense, well suited to joining dissimilar metals that would form brittle compounds if fusion-welded. Explosion welding uses a controlled explosive charge to slam one plate into another at extremely high velocity, creating a metallurgical bond through the resulting jetting and plastic deformation at the interface, without significant melting — a niche but genuinely important process for cladding large plates with a dissimilar corrosion-resistant metal. Oxyfuel welding burns a fuel gas (commonly acetylene) with oxygen to produce a flame hot enough to melt the base metal directly, with filler rod added by hand — largely superseded by arc processes for structural welding today, but still used for its portability and, differently sized to concentrate heat rather than melt broadly, for oxyfuel cutting (4).

03 · Brazing and soldering — properly distinguished from welding

Brazing and soldering join metals by melting only a filler metal (never the base metal), which is drawn into a small, controlled joint gap by capillary action and wets both surfaces on cooling — a fundamentally different mechanism from welding's fusion of the base metals themselves. The distinction between the two is purely one of filler melting temperature: brazing uses filler melting above \(450^\circ\text{C}\) (commonly a copper or silver alloy), soldering below it (commonly tin-based) — both rely on the same capillary-action principle and both require a joint gap sized correctly for that capillary action to actually work, since too wide a gap simply will not draw filler metal through it. Neither process melts the base metal, which is exactly why both can join dissimilar metals, and thin sections, that fusion welding would distort or damage.

04 · Thermal cutting, and joint prep as the quiet variable

Oxyfuel cutting uses a torch to preheat steel to ignition temperature, then a jet of pure oxygen to exothermically burn (not melt) a kerf through it — cheap and portable, but limited to materials that oxidize this way (essentially plain carbon steel) and producing a relatively wide kerf and heat-affected zone. Plasma cutting uses a constricted, superheated ionized-gas jet to melt and blow away material, cutting any electrically conductive material faster than oxyfuel with a narrower kerf, at higher equipment cost. Laser cutting (Lesson 7) gives the narrowest kerf and smallest heat-affected zone of the three, at the highest capital cost, typically reserved for thinner sections where precision matters most. Underlying every welding process covered in this lesson and the last, joint type (butt, lap, tee, corner, edge), edge preparation geometry (square-cut, beveled, root gap), and fit-up (how accurately the prepared edges actually align before welding begins) are the quiet variable behind weld quality that no amount of correct process selection or heat input control can compensate for — a joint with poor fit-up forces the welder to either bridge a gap the procedure never anticipated or leave a defect, regardless of how correctly every other parameter in Lesson 8 was set.

Key distinction — welding vs. brazing/soldering

Welding melts the base metal. Brazing and soldering melt only the filler, drawn in by capillary action — the base metal stays solid throughout.

Lesson 10 · Mechanical joining

01 · Preload — why a bolted joint holds

A bolted joint does not hold primarily by shear resistance in the bolt shank — it holds because the bolt, stretched slightly elastically when tightened, clamps the joined parts together with a preload force \(F\), and that clamping friction resists the parts sliding or separating under service load. Too little preload and the joint can separate or work loose under load (and, for a dynamically loaded joint, fatigue the bolt far sooner, since a poorly preloaded bolt sees the full cyclic load itself rather than the load being absorbed by a reduction in clamp force). Too much preload risks yielding the bolt or crushing whatever is clamped — a gasket, most consequentially, as developed in 3.

02 · The torque-tension relationship

Since preload itself is not directly measurable with a simple hand tool, torque is used as an indirect, practical proxy: \[ T = K D F, \] where \(T\) is applied torque, \(D\) is the bolt's nominal diameter, \(F\) is the resulting preload, and \(K\) is the nut factor — an empirical number (typically \(0.15\)–\(0.20\) for as-received steel fasteners) that lumps together thread friction and under-head friction. \(K\) is not a fundamental constant; it depends strongly on surface condition, plating, and especially lubrication — which is exactly why torque-tension is only as reliable as the assumption about \(K\) it was calculated from.

03 · Why gasketed flanges leak when torqued wrong

A gasket seals by being compressed to a specific stress the joint's design assumes — controlled indirectly through preload, and therefore through torque and the assumed \(K\). If a bolt is actually lubricated but torqued using a dry-\(K\) assumption, the same commanded torque produces significantly more preload than intended (a lower \(K\) means less torque is lost to friction, so more of it converts to clamping force) — over-compressing and potentially damaging the gasket, or over-stressing the flange. If instead a bolt is drier or more corroded than assumed, the same torque under-clamps the joint, leaving insufficient gasket compression to seal — the joint leaks not because it was under-torqued in some absolute sense, but because the torque delivered less preload than the seal actually needed. Both failure directions trace back to the same root cause: torque is not preload, and \(K\) is an assumption, not a guarantee.

04 · Riveting, adhesive bonding, and the maintenance view

Riveting joins by deforming a soft metal pin through aligned holes, clamping the parts by the resulting deformed head — permanent, vibration-resistant, and still standard in aircraft structures and some sheet-metal assembly. Adhesive bonding joins by a cured polymer layer distributing load over the entire bonded area rather than concentrating it at discrete points — excellent for joining dissimilar materials and distributing stress evenly, at the cost of surface preparation sensitivity and generally poorer performance under peel loading than shear. Bolted joints stand apart from both specifically because they are designed to be remade: a maintenance program built around scheduled disassembly, inspection, and reassembly depends entirely on that reversibility — which is exactly why a maintenance engineer's real interest in bolting is not just "is it tight," but "will it torque back to a known, correct preload the next time this joint is opened."

Key relation — torque-tension

\[ T = KDF \]

K is an assumption about friction — verify it, don't assume it, on anything gasketed or fatigue-critical.

Lesson 11 · Additive manufacturing first contact

01 · The AM process families, in one pass

Powder bed fusion (PBF) spreads a thin layer of metal or polymer powder, then selectively melts or sinters it with a laser or electron beam, layer after layer — good geometric freedom and reasonable accuracy, used for both prototypes and end-use metal parts. Directed energy deposition (DED) feeds powder or wire directly into a focused energy beam at a moving nozzle, building up (or repairing) material where directed — well suited to large features and repair/cladding work, at coarser resolution than PBF. Material extrusion (FDM/FFF) deposits a molten polymer filament through a moving nozzle, layer by layer — the cheapest, most widespread AM technology, standard for polymer prototypes, jigs, and fixtures. Binder jetting selectively deposits a liquid binder into a powder bed to build a green part, later sintered (for metals) or infiltrated/cured — fast, and free of the residual thermal stress that laser-based processes can leave, at the cost of a separate post-processing step to reach final properties.

02 · Where AM already earns its keep

Jigs and fixtures — shop-floor tooling with low volume and high geometric customization per application — are a genuinely strong AM fit today, since a fixture only needs to be functional, not certified to aerospace-grade properties. Obsolete spare parts — a component no longer manufactured, with no economical tooling remaining, needed in small quantity — is another strong fit, since AM's near-zero tooling cost matters most exactly when conventional manufacturing's tooling cost cannot be justified for a handful of parts. Prototypes remain AM's original and still most common use: rapid geometric iteration, checking fit and form, well before final material properties or production process matter.

03 · Honest limits

Surface finish as-built is typically rough compared to machined surfaces — commonly requiring secondary finishing (machining, bead blasting, polishing) for functional or aesthetic surfaces, a real added cost and step, not a footnote. Mechanical properties are frequently anisotropic — different in the build direction versus within a layer, exactly the effect the diagram above illustrates — and can carry porosity or residual stress depending on process control, both of which matter directly for fatigue-loaded parts (Strength of Materials). Qualification — the testing and certification burden to trust an AM part in a safety- or performance-critical application — is substantial and process-and-machine-specific; a process qualified on one machine, one material lot, and one parameter set does not automatically transfer to another, which is precisely why AM adoption in genuinely critical aerospace and medical applications has been careful and incremental rather than immediate, despite the technology's real capability.

04 · Choosing across the whole course — a capstone view

Every process this course has covered — machining, grinding, nontraditional processes, welding and joining, and now additive manufacturing — removes or adds material by a different mechanism, with a different cost structure, geometric freedom, and property outcome. Machining remains unmatched for tight tolerances and known, predictable mechanical properties at moderate to high volume; welding and mechanical joining remain how most large or assembled structures are actually built; AM earns its place specifically where geometric complexity, low volume, or tooling elimination matter more than achieving machining-grade surface finish and properties directly off the machine. Specifying a manufacturing process for a real part means weighing all of this together — exactly the kind of judgment this course has built lesson by lesson, not a single "best" process that wins in every case.

Key honesty — AM's real position

Strong fit: low-volume, complex-geometry, tooling-free parts (jigs, obsolete spares, prototypes). Real limits: surface finish, anisotropic properties, and a genuine qualification burden for critical applications.
