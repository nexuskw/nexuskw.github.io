MTH 207 · YEAR 2 · SEMESTER 1

Engineering Mathematics III — Differential Equations & Linear Algebra

The mathematics of dynamic systems: first- and second-order differential equations, Laplace transforms, and the matrix language of coupled systems — the toolset vibrations, controls, and heat transfer stand on.

Syllabus

01

First-order equations revisited: separable and linearFull lesson

Separable equations recapped from the calculus course, then the linear first-order equation solved with the integrating factor, derived rather than decreed. Solution families and the initial condition that picks one. The course's working vocabulary set in one lesson.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Solve y' + 2y = 6 with y(0) = 0: what value does y approach, and how fast? (3, with τ = 0.5)
  • What exactly does the integrating factor multiply away?
02

Modeling with first-order equationsFull lesson

Mixing tanks, thermal soak, and RC circuits: one balance statement each, and the same equation three times. Time constants read directly from coefficients. Steady state as the equation's destination, transient as its journey.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Write the ODE for a 5 m³ well-mixed tank flushed at 0.25 m³/min, and read off τ. (20 min)
  • Why do a cooling casting and a discharging capacitor share one mathematics?
03

Second-order equations: the homogeneous storyFull lesson

The characteristic equation turns a differential equation into algebra. Overdamped, critically damped, and underdamped responses classified by the discriminant. Why the damping ratio will become the vibration engineer's favorite number.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Classify y'' + 4y' + 3y = 0 by its roots. (−1 and −3: overdamped)
  • What does a complex-conjugate root pair mean physically?
04

Nonhomogeneous equations and forced responseFull lesson

Undetermined coefficients for constant, ramp, and sinusoidal forcing. The structure theorem: general solution = homogeneous + particular. Transient versus steady-state response — the vocabulary the controls course inherits whole.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does the transient die out while the forced response persists?
  • What particular-solution form do you try for a constant force input?
05

Free and forced oscillationsFull lesson

The mass–spring–damper equation solved end to end. Resonance: why amplitude grows when forcing approaches the natural frequency, and why machines care at one-times running speed. Beats in brief.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Compute ωn for m = 4 kg, k = 10 kN/m. (50 rad/s)
  • What limits the resonant amplitude in a real machine?
06

Laplace transforms IFull lesson

The transform defined and a working table built by direct integration. Transforms of derivatives: calculus becomes algebra, and initial conditions walk in automatically. Linearity as the property that makes the method industrial.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • What is the transform of e^(−3t)? (1/(s+3))
  • How do initial conditions enter a transformed equation without extra work?
07

Laplace transforms II: solving problemsFull lesson

Inverse transforms by partial fractions; step inputs and switched forcing handled cleanly. Initial-value problems solved entirely in the s-domain. The transfer-function idea planted for the controls course.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Invert 6/(s(s+3)). (2 − 2e^(−3t))
  • What does dividing by s do to a signal in the time domain?
08

Systems of equations: the matrix viewFull lesson

Coupled tanks and coupled masses written as x' = Ax. Why coupled dynamics needs matrices rather than more calculus. The eigenvalue preview: a system's tempo hides in A itself.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Write the two-tank mixing problem as a matrix equation.
  • What plays the characteristic equation's role for a system of equations?
09

Matrices and linear systemsFull lesson

Gaussian elimination, rank, and solvability; determinants at working strength. The numerical caution: ill-conditioned systems amplify data noise, and engineering data is never clean. Where matrix solving hides inside every simulation tool.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Solve a 3×3 system by elimination, tracking each pivot.
  • What does a near-zero determinant warn you about the answer?
10

Eigenvalues and eigenvectorsFull lesson

Eigenvalues computed for 2×2 and 3×3 matrices; eigenvalues as the system's tempo — decay rates and oscillation frequencies — and eigenvectors as its mode shapes. The modal language of the vibrations course is born here.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Find the eigenvalues of [[2,1],[1,2]]. (1 and 3)
  • Why do the signs of eigenvalues decide stability?
11

Numerical solution of differential equationsFull lesson

Euler's method and Runge–Kutta; step size traded against accuracy and stability, observed live on the cooling equation. When numerical solution is the only honest route — and how to distrust it productively. Closes the loop with the computing course.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Take two Euler steps of y' = −y, y(0) = 1, h = 0.5. (0.25)
  • Why can too large a step make a stable equation explode numerically?
  • MIT OCW 18.03 Differential Equations Watch lectures ▶ (ocw.mit.edu)

Career outlook — where this course pays

Differential equations are the native language of every engineer who predicts how a system behaves over time — the sector only changes the system:

  • Automotive & heavy equipment — ride and driveline engineers at manufacturers such as Toyota tune suspensions and mounts as mass–spring–damper systems, reading \( \zeta \) and \( \omega_n \) straight off this course to decide whether a chassis slams, settles, or rings.
  • Aerospace & defense — flight-control and structures engineers at houses such as Airbus live in second-order dynamics and Laplace transforms: every actuator loop and flutter margin is the forced-response and eigenvalue mathematics of Lessons 4–10.
  • Industrial technology & controls — automation engineers at suppliers such as Rockwell Automation size every servo, filter, and PID loop from time constants and pole locations — the state-space and eigenvalue view of Lessons 8–10 is their daily model.

Interview angle: the recurring probe is qualitative — "sketch the response of this system without solving it." The discriminant, \( \omega_n \), and \( \zeta \) drilled here are the expected instant answer, before any integration.
