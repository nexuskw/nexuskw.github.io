Modeling with first-order equations

Mixing tanks, thermal soak, and RC circuits: one balance statement each, and the same equation three times. Time constants read directly from coefficients. Steady state as the equation's destination, transient as its journey.

01 · One balance, three plants

First-order models arise the same way everywhere: write a balance for how a quantity accumulates. Rate of change = rate in − rate out. A mixing tank balances mass of solute; a cooling body balances heat; an RC circuit balances charge. Each yields \( \tau\,y' + y = y_{\text{in}} \), the same equation with different names on the symbols. Learning to read that common form means one piece of mathematics serves the thermal, fluid, and electrical courses at once.

02 · Newton cooling, end to end

A body at temperature \(T\) in surroundings at \(T_a\) loses heat at a rate proportional to the difference: \( T' = -k\,(T-T_a) \). This is linear; its solution is \( T(t)=T_a+(T_0-T_a)e^{-kt} \). For \(T_0=90^\circ\)C, \(T_a=20^\circ\)C, \(k=0.05\ \text{min}^{-1}\): \( T(10)=20+70e^{-0.5}\approx 62.5^\circ\)C. The body forgets its start exponentially and approaches the ambient value — the steady state \(T_a\), reached in principle only at infinity, in practice after a few time constants.

03 · The time constant, read from the coefficient

Rewrite any of these as \( \tau y' + y = y_{\text{in}} \). The coefficient \( \tau \) is the time constant — the time to fall to \(1/e\) (about 37%) of the way to steady state. For cooling, \( \tau=1/k \). For an RC circuit, \( \tau=RC \): with \(R=1000\ \Omega\) and \(C=100\,\mu\text{F}\), \( \tau=0.1\)s, and the circuit is essentially settled after \(5\tau=0.5\)s. For a stirred tank of volume \(V\) with throughput \(Q\), \( \tau=V/Q \): a 200-L tank at 5 L/min has \( \tau=40 \) min. The coefficient is the physics; you rarely need the full solution to size a response.

04 · The modelling method

The reusable procedure: (1) name the accumulating quantity and write its balance; (2) put the equation in \( \tau y' + y = y_{\text{in}} \) form and read \( \tau \) and the steady state directly; (3) solve only if a full time history is needed. Units audit the model — every term in a balance must carry the same units, and \( \tau \) must come out in time. A model whose \( \tau \) has the wrong units has a wrong term, caught before any numbers are run. This discipline — balance, normalise, read — is exactly how reliability, controls, and process courses set up their own first-order responses later.

05 · Step response and superposition

Because the equation is linear, its responses add. If the input \(y_{\text{in}}\) jumps from one level to another — a step — the output slides from its old steady state to the new one along the same exponential \(1-e^{-t/\tau}\), scaled by the size of the jump. Two steps at different times give the sum of two such slides, each starting when its step arrives. This superposition is why a single measured step response characterises the whole system: read \(\tau\) from how fast it moves and the gain from how far, and every future input can be built from steps. Reliability and controls engineers lean on exactly this — the step test is the cheapest honest experiment a first-order plant allows, and its arithmetic is the arithmetic of this lesson.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Cooling to a target time

Problem. A part at \(90^\circ\)C cools in \(20^\circ\)C air with \(k=0.05\ \text{min}^{-1}\). Find its temperature after 10 minutes.

Solution. \( T(t)=T_a+(T_0-T_a)e^{-kt}=20+70e^{-0.05\cdot10}=20+70e^{-0.5} \). Since \(e^{-0.5}\approx0.6065\): \( T\approx20+42.5=62.5^\circ\)C. Answer: \(\approx62.5^\circ\)C. Check: above ambient, below start — as it must be.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Half-way time

Problem. For the same part, when does it reach \(55^\circ\)C (half-way from 90 to 20)?

Solution. Need \(70e^{-0.05t}=35\Rightarrow e^{-0.05t}=0.5\Rightarrow t=\frac{\ln 2}{0.05}\approx13.86\) min. Answer: \(\approx13.9\) min. Check: this is \(\tau\ln2\) with \(\tau=1/k=20\) min — a half-life, independent of the start temperature.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Read a time constant

Problem. A 200-L well-mixed tank is fed and drained at 5 L/min. State its time constant and an approximate settle time.

Solution. \( \tau=V/Q=200/5=40 \) min. It is essentially settled after \(5\tau=200\) min. Answer: \( \tau=40 \) min; settle \(\approx200\) min.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

An RC circuit has \(R=1000\,\Omega\), \(C=100\,\mu\text{F}\). Its time constant is:

\( \tau=RC=1000\times100\times10^{-6}=0.1 \) s. The micro (\(10^{-6}\)) prefix is the trap; \(1000\times100=10^5\) then \(\times10^{-6}=0.1\).

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

In \( \tau y'+y=y_{in} \), the time constant \( \tau \) is the time to reach about:

After one \(\tau\) the response is \(1-1/e\approx0.63\) of the way to steady state. \(1/e\approx37\%\) is the fraction remaining, not reached.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

Newton cooling drives a body toward:

Steady state sets \(T'=0\Rightarrow T=T_a\). The transient \((T_0-T_a)e^{-kt}\) decays away, leaving \(T_a\).

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

Two tanks have the same volume but tank B has double the flow rate. Tank B's time constant is:

\( \tau=V/Q \); doubling \(Q\) halves \(\tau\). More throughput flushes the vessel faster.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

A first-order response is conventionally treated as essentially complete after about:

After \(5\tau\) the response is within \(e^{-5}\approx0.7\%\) of steady state — the standard settling rule. One \(\tau\) reaches only 63%.
