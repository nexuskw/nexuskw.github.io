Modeling with first-order equations

Mixing tanks, thermal soak, and RC circuits: one balance statement each, and the same equation three times. Time constants read directly from coefficients. Steady state as the equation's destination, transient as its journey.

01 · One balance, three plants

First-order models almost always arise the same way: write a balance for how a quantity accumulates. The statement is always rate of change = rate in − rate out, and everything downstream is bookkeeping. A mixing tank balances the mass of dissolved solute; a cooling body balances thermal energy; an RC circuit balances electric charge. Strikingly, all three collapse to \( \tau y'+y=y_{\text{in}} \) — the same equation with only the names on the symbols changed. That is why one piece of mathematics serves the thermal, fluid, and electrical courses at once, and why an engineer who reads this form fluently can size a response in any of those domains without re-deriving anything.

02 · Newton cooling, end to end

A body at temperature \(T\) sitting in surroundings at \(T_a\) loses heat at a rate proportional to the temperature difference: \( T'=-k(T-T_a) \). This is a linear first-order equation, and its solution (by the integrating factor of Lesson 1) is \( T(t)=T_a+(T_0-T_a)e^{-kt} \). For a part starting at \(T_0=90^\circ\)C in \(T_a=20^\circ\)C air with \(k=0.05\ \text{min}^{-1}\): \( T(10)=20+70e^{-0.5}\approx62.5^\circ\)C. The body forgets its starting temperature exponentially and approaches the ambient value; the excess \( (T_0-T_a) \) is what decays, never the absolute temperature, which is why cooling and heating problems share one formula with only a sign difference in the initial excess.

03 · The time constant, read from the coefficient

Rewrite any of these balances as \( \tau y'+y=y_{\text{in}} \). The coefficient \( \tau \) is the time constant — the time to cover \(1-1/e\), about 63%, of the distance to steady state. It is read straight off the physics: for cooling \( \tau=1/k \); for an RC circuit \( \tau=RC \), so \(R=1000\,\Omega\) with \(C=100\,\mu\text{F}\) gives \( \tau=0.1 \) s and a circuit essentially settled after \(5\tau=0.5\) s; for a stirred tank \( \tau=V/Q \), so a 200-L vessel at 5 L/min has \( \tau=40 \) min. The full time history is rarely needed to answer the question a plant actually asks — "how fast does it respond?" — because that answer is the coefficient itself.

04 · The modelling method

The reusable procedure has three steps. First, name the accumulating quantity and write its balance honestly, accounting for every stream in and out. Second, put the equation into \( \tau y'+y=y_{\text{in}} \) form and read \( \tau \) and the steady state directly, before solving. Third, integrate for a full time history only if one is genuinely required. Units audit the whole model: every term in a balance must carry the same units, and \( \tau \) must emerge with units of time. A time constant that comes out in the wrong units is proof of a wrong or missing term, and it is caught on inspection, before a single number is computed — the cheapest error check in modelling and the habit worth building first. A last discipline ties the modelling together: always state the assumptions the balance rests on — well mixed, constant properties, single dominant stream — because a first-order model is only as trustworthy as those assumptions, and naming them tells the next engineer exactly when the model may be reused and when it must be rebuilt. The same equation that describes a cooling casting will misdescribe a stratified tank, and the difference lives entirely in the assumptions, not the algebra.

05 · One reading, checked in seconds

Bring the three plants back to a single question and answer it without integrating. A 200-L tank stirred at 5 L/min has \( \tau=V/Q=40 \) min, so it is practically settled after \( 5\tau=200 \) min; the RC pair \( R=1000\,\Omega,\ C=100\,\mu\text{F} \) has \( \tau=RC=0.1 \) s and is done in half a second; the cooling part with \( k=0.05\ \text{min}^{-1} \) has \( \tau=1/k=20 \) min. Notice that the shape of the answer — an exponential approach governed by one time constant — is identical in every case, and the only work was reading a coefficient. This is the payoff of the balance-then-normalise method: response speed is available before, and independently of, any full solution, which is exactly the number a plant engineer is usually paid to know.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Cooling to a target time

Problem. A part at \(90^\circ\)C cools in \(20^\circ\)C air, \(k=0.05\ \text{min}^{-1}\). Temperature after 10 min?

Solution. \( T=20+70e^{-0.5}\), \(e^{-0.5}\approx0.6065\Rightarrow T\approx62.5^\circ\)C. Answer: \(\approx62.5^\circ\)C.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Half-way time

Problem. When does it reach \(55^\circ\)C (half of 90→20)?

Solution. \(70e^{-0.05t}=35\Rightarrow e^{-0.05t}=0.5\Rightarrow t=\frac{\ln2}{0.05}\approx13.9\) min. Answer: \(\approx13.9\) min (a half-life \(\tau\ln2\)).

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Read a time constant

Problem. A 200-L well-mixed tank is fed/drained at 5 L/min. Time constant and settle time?

Solution. \( \tau=200/5=40 \) min; settled after \(5\tau=200\) min. Answer: \( \tau=40 \) min.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

RC circuit, \(R=1000\,\Omega\), \(C=100\,\mu\text{F}\). Time constant:

\( \tau=RC=1000\times100\times10^{-6}=0.1 \) s.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

In \( \tau y'+y=y_{in} \), one \( \tau \) reaches about:

\(1-1/e\approx0.63\). \(1/e\approx37\%\) is what remains.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

Newton cooling drives a body toward:

Steady state \(T=T_a\); the transient decays.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

Tank B has double the flow of tank A (same volume). Its \( \tau \) is:

\( \tau=V/Q \); double \(Q\) halves \(\tau\).

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Doubling the cooling coefficient \(k\) changes the time constant \( \tau=1/k \) to:

\( \tau=1/k \); larger \(k\) means faster cooling and a smaller time constant.
