Second-order equations: the homogeneous story

The characteristic equation turns a differential equation into algebra. Overdamped, critically damped, and underdamped responses classified by the discriminant. Why the damping ratio will become the vibration engineer's favorite number.

01 · From calculus to algebra

The homogeneous second-order equation with constant coefficients, \( m x'' + c x' + k x = 0 \), models every mass–spring–damper — and by analogy every RLC circuit and every simple oscillator. Its solution is found by a guess that turns calculus into algebra: try \( x=e^{rt} \). Substituting gives \( (mr^2+cr+k)e^{rt}=0 \), and since the exponential never vanishes, the characteristic equation \( mr^2+cr+k=0 \) must hold. The differential problem is now a quadratic.

02 · Three regimes, set by the discriminant

The discriminant \( \Delta=c^2-4mk \) sorts the behaviour. If \( \Delta>0 \), two real negative roots: \( x=Ae^{r_1 t}+Be^{r_2 t} \), a pure decay with no oscillation — overdamped. If \( \Delta=0 \), a repeated root \(r\): \( x=(A+Bt)e^{rt} \), the fastest non-oscillating return — critically damped. If \( \Delta<0 \), complex roots \( r=-\sigma\pm i\omega_d \): \( x=e^{-\sigma t}(A\cos\omega_d t+B\sin\omega_d t) \), a decaying oscillation — underdamped. For \( x''+5x'+6x=0 \), \( \Delta=25-24=1>0 \), roots \(-2,-3\): overdamped. For \( x''+2x'+5x=0 \), \( \Delta=-16<0 \), roots \(-1\pm2i\): underdamped, oscillating at \( \omega_d=2 \) while decaying at rate 1.

03 · Natural frequency and damping ratio

Two derived numbers name the system independently of scale. The natural frequency \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) is how fast it would oscillate with no damping; with \(m=1,k=100\), \( \omega_n=10 \) rad/s. The damping ratio \( \zeta=\dfrac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}} \) measures damping against the critical amount \( c_c=2\sqrt{mk} \). Here \( c_c=20 \); a damper \(c=8\) gives \( \zeta=0.4 \) — underdamped, since \( \zeta<1 \). The three regimes restate cleanly as \( \zeta<1,\ =1,\ >1 \).

04 · The reading method

Given \( m x''+cx'+kx=0 \): compute \( \Delta=c^2-4mk \) first — its sign is the whole qualitative story before a single root is found. Then read \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) and \( \zeta=c/(2\sqrt{mk}) \) to quantify. This ordering — sign, then magnitudes — is how vibration and control engineers diagnose a system from its coefficients alone, and why \( \zeta \) and \( \omega_n \) are the two numbers every datasheet reports. The forced version in Lesson 4 adds a particular solution on top of exactly this homogeneous skeleton.

05 · Why the regimes matter physically

The three regimes are not mathematical curiosities — each is a design choice. An overdamped system returns to rest slowly and never overshoots: right for a door closer or a heavy instrument that must not bounce. A critically damped system returns as fast as possible with no overshoot: the target for most positioning systems and vehicle suspensions. An underdamped system overshoots and rings: unavoidable in lightly-damped structures, and the reason a rotating machine passing through its natural frequency shakes. The damping ratio \( \zeta \) is the single knob that moves between them, and reading it from \(c\), \(m\), and \(k\) tells you, before any test, whether a design will slam, settle, or ring. That diagnostic power — three coefficients to one verdict — is why this lesson underpins the vibrations and controls courses. One further reading is worth carrying forward: the roots always come in a conjugate pair when complex, so a single measured frequency and decay rate reconstruct both roots at once, and from them the effective \(c\), \(m\), and \(k\) of a black-box system. Engineers routinely run this backwards — strike a structure, measure the ring-down, and infer the stiffness and damping they never had a datasheet for. The homogeneous equation is therefore not only a solution method but a measurement principle.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • The quadratic formula — the characteristic equation is a quadratic.
  • Complex numbers — \(e^{i\omega t}=\cos\omega t+i\sin\omega t\) for the underdamped case.
  • Exponential solutions (Lessons 1–2) — the ansatz \(x=e^{rt}\).

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Classify and solve

Problem. Classify \( x''+5x'+6x=0 \) and write its general solution.

Solution. \( \Delta=5^2-4(1)(6)=1>0 \Rightarrow \) overdamped. Roots \( r=\frac{-5\pm1}{2}=-2,-3 \). Answer: \( x=Ae^{-2t}+Be^{-3t} \). Check: both roots negative, so \(x\to0\) with no oscillation.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Underdamped read-off

Problem. For \( x''+2x'+5x=0 \), give the decay rate and damped frequency.

Solution. \( \Delta=4-20=-16<0 \). Roots \( r=\frac{-2\pm\sqrt{-16}}{2}=-1\pm2i \). Decay rate \( \sigma=1 \), damped frequency \( \omega_d=2 \). Answer: \( x=e^{-t}(A\cos2t+B\sin2t) \), decay 1, \( \omega_d=2 \).

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Natural frequency and damping ratio

Problem. A system has \(m=1,\ k=100,\ c=8\). Find \( \omega_n \), critical damping \(c_c\), and \( \zeta \).

Solution. \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m}=\sqrt{100}=10 \) rad/s. \( c_c=2\sqrt{mk}=2\sqrt{100}=20 \). \( \zeta=c/c_c=8/20=0.4 \). Answer: \( \omega_n=10,\ c_c=20,\ \zeta=0.4 \) (underdamped).

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

The discriminant of \( m x''+cx'+kx=0 \) is \( c^2-4mk \). Overdamped means:

Positive discriminant gives two distinct real roots — pure decay, no oscillation. Negative is underdamped; zero is critical.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

A repeated characteristic root \(r\) gives the solution form:

A double root needs the extra independent solution \(t e^{rt}\), giving \((A+Bt)e^{rt}\). A single \(Ae^{rt}\) is only one constant, too few for second order.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

With \(m=1,k=100\), the critical damping coefficient \(c_c=2\sqrt{mk}\) equals:

\( c_c=2\sqrt{1\cdot100}=2\times10=20 \). Forgetting the factor 2 gives the common wrong answer 10.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A damping ratio \( \zeta=0.4 \) describes a system that is:

\( \zeta<1 \) is underdamped — decaying oscillation. \( \zeta=1 \) critical, \( \zeta>1 \) overdamped, \( \zeta=0 \) undamped.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Doubling the mass while holding stiffness fixed changes \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) by a factor of:

\( \sqrt{k/(2m)}=\omega_n/\sqrt2 \): more mass lowers the natural frequency, but by the square root, not linearly.
