Matrices and linear systems

01 · Elimination: the universal method

Cramer's rule and the inverse are elegant for \( 2\times2 \) systems but grow impossibly expensive with size. The method that scales is Gaussian elimination: use row operations to carve the system into an upper-triangular staircase, then read the unknowns off by back-substitution. Three operations are legal because none changes the solution set — swap two rows, scale a row by a nonzero constant, or add a multiple of one row to another. The strategy is to use the first equation to remove the first unknown from all the others, then the second to remove the second unknown from those below it, and so on, until the last equation involves a single unknown. Every computational linear-algebra library does exactly this.

02 · A 3×3 system, worked

Solve \( x+y+z=6,\ 2x+y-z=1,\ x-y+2z=5 \). Subtract \(2\times\)row 1 from row 2: \( -y-3z=-11 \). Subtract row 1 from row 3: \( -2y+z=-1 \). Now eliminate \(y\): subtract \(2\times\)the new row 2 from the new row 3, giving \( 7z=21 \), so \( z=3 \). Back-substitute into \( -y-3z=-11 \): \( -y-9=-11\Rightarrow y=2 \). Finally row 1: \( x+2+3=6\Rightarrow x=1 \). The solution is \( (x,y,z)=(1,2,3) \), and substituting back into all three originals confirms \( 6,1,5 \) exactly. The staircase turned a coupled system into three one-line reads.

03 · Pivots, rank, and when there is no unique answer

The nonzero leading entries the method produces are the pivots, and their count is the rank of the matrix — the number of genuinely independent equations. A \( 3\times3 \) system with three pivots has a unique solution. Fewer pivots means a row has collapsed to \( 0=0 \) (a redundant equation, giving infinitely many solutions) or to \( 0=c \) with \( c\neq0 \) (a contradiction, giving none). The matrix \( \begin{pmatrix}1&2\\2&4\end{pmatrix} \), with \( \det=0 \), has rank 1: elimination turns its second row into zeros, exposing that the two equations were one. Rank, read straight off the pivots, is how elimination reports the solvability that the determinant only summarised.

04 · The working method

The procedure is fixed and mechanical: (1) form the augmented matrix \( [A\,|\,\mathbf{b}] \); (2) sweep top-to-bottom, using each pivot to zero the entries below it, swapping rows if a pivot is zero; (3) once triangular, back-substitute bottom-to-top. Two habits guard accuracy — pick the largest available pivot at each step so you never divide by a tiny number (this is partial pivoting, the reason the method is numerically stable), and count pivots at the end to declare the rank before trusting a "unique solution." Elimination is not one trick among many; it is the single algorithm behind solving systems, inverting matrices, and computing determinants at any size.

05 · Elimination as the engine underneath

It is worth seeing how much rests on this one algorithm. The determinant of any square matrix is the product of the pivots elimination produces (with a sign for row swaps); a matrix inverse is found by running elimination on \( [A\,|\,I] \) until the left block becomes \(I\); and the rank that decides solvability is just the pivot count. So the three separate-looking operations of Lesson 8 — solve, invert, take a determinant — are one procedure seen from three angles. This is also why numerical libraries implement elimination once, carefully, and build everything else on it: get the sweep-and-back-substitute loop right, with partial pivoting for stability, and the whole of computational linear algebra follows.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

3×3 by elimination

Problem. Solve \( x+y+z=6,\ 2x+y-z=1,\ x-y+2z=5 \).

Solution. R2−2R1: \( -y-3z=-11 \). R3−R1: \( -2y+z=-1 \). Eliminate \(y\): (R3′)−2(R2′): \( 7z=21\Rightarrow z=3 \). Back-substitute: \( -y-9=-11\Rightarrow y=2 \); then \( x+2+3=6\Rightarrow x=1 \). So \( (x,y,z)=(1,2,3) \), which checks in all three equations (6, 1, 5).

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Rank of a matrix

Problem. Find the rank of \( \begin{pmatrix}1&2\\2&4\end{pmatrix} \) by elimination.

Solution. R2−2R1 gives \( (0,0) \). One nonzero (pivot) row remains, so the rank is \( 1 \). The rows were dependent — the second is twice the first — matching \( \det=0 \). A rank-deficient \( 2\times2 \) system has no unique solution.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Back-substitution

Problem. A system has been reduced to \( x+y+z=6,\ -y-3z=-11,\ 7z=21 \). Recover \( x,y,z \).

Solution. From the last row \( z=21/7=3 \). Substitute into the middle: \( -y-3(3)=-11\Rightarrow -y=-2\Rightarrow y=2 \). Substitute both into the first: \( x+2+3=6\Rightarrow x=1 \). Back-substitution reads the answer off the triangle from the bottom up.
