Nonhomogeneous equations and forced response

Undetermined coefficients for constant, ramp, and sinusoidal forcing. The structure theorem: general solution = homogeneous + particular. Transient versus steady-state response — the vocabulary the controls course inherits whole.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does the transient die out while the forced response persists?
  • What particular-solution form do you try for a constant force input?

01 · The structure theorem

A nonhomogeneous equation \( m x''+cx'+kx=f(t) \) has forcing on the right. Its general solution is always \( x=x_h+x_p \): the homogeneous solution \(x_h\) (Lesson 3, carrying the arbitrary constants) plus any single particular solution \(x_p\) that matches the forcing. This is not a convenience but a theorem — the difference of any two full solutions solves the homogeneous equation, so all solutions differ only by \(x_h\). Practically: solve the homogeneous problem for the transient, find one \(x_p\) for the steady behaviour, and add.

02 · Undetermined coefficients

When \(f(t)\) is a constant, polynomial, exponential, or sinusoid, guess \(x_p\) of the same shape with unknown coefficients and solve for them. Constant forcing \( x''+3x'+2x=10 \): try \(x_p=\)const, so \(x''=x'=0\) and \(2x_p=10\Rightarrow x_p=5\) — the steady state. Ramp forcing \( x''+3x'+2x=2t \): try \(x_p=at+b\); then \(x'_p=a,\ x''_p=0\), giving \(3a+2(at+b)=2t\). Match powers: \(2a=2\Rightarrow a=1\), and \(3a+2b=0\Rightarrow b=-1.5\). So \(x_p=t-1.5\).

03 · Transient versus steady state

For \( x''+3x'+2x=10 \) the homogeneous roots are \(-1,-2\) (both stable), so \( x=Ae^{-t}+Be^{-2t}+5 \). The exponentials are the transient — how the system gets there — and the \(5\) is the steady state — where it ends. After a few time constants only the \(5\) survives, whatever the initial conditions. This is why a steady-state calculation (Lesson 1's \(q/p\) idea, generalised) predicts the long-run answer without solving the full transient, and why controls engineers separate the two questions: where does it settle, and how does it get there.

04 · Sinusoidal forcing and the working method

Sinusoidal forcing is the important case: \(f(t)=F_0\cos\omega t\) gives a sinusoidal \(x_p\) of the same frequency but shifted phase and scaled amplitude. The amplitude depends on how close \(\omega\) is to \(\omega_n\) — the seed of resonance, developed in Lesson 5. The method is uniform: (1) solve homogeneous for \(x_h\); (2) guess \(x_p\) matching \(f\)'s form — and if that guess already solves the homogeneous equation, multiply by \(t\); (3) fix constants from initial conditions on the full \(x_h+x_p\), never on \(x_h\) alone. That last rule is the most common error in the whole topic, and worth stating every time.

05 · Why superposition holds

The whole method rests on linearity. Because the operator \(L[x]=mx''+cx'+kx\) satisfies \(L[x_1+x_2]=L[x_1]+L[x_2]\), a sum of forcings produces the sum of responses — so a complicated input can be broken into simple pieces (a constant, a ramp, each sinusoid), each solved by its own trial form, and the results added. This is the same superposition that lets Laplace transforms (Lessons 6–7) handle switched and combined inputs cleanly, and the same principle that makes the frequency response of Lesson 5 meaningful. Nonlinear equations forfeit all of it: there the responses to two inputs bear no simple relation to the response to their sum, and the tidy \(x_h+x_p\) split collapses. Recognising that this course lives inside the linear world — and where real systems leave it — is as important as any single technique in it, because it tells you exactly when these clean methods can be trusted and when a numerical solver becomes the only honest option.

Examples and Quiz

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Constant forcing

Problem. Solve \( x''+3x'+2x=10 \) for the general solution.

Solution. Homogeneous: roots of \(r^2+3r+2=0\) are \(-1,-2\), so \(x_h=Ae^{-t}+Be^{-2t}\). Particular: try constant, \(2x_p=10\Rightarrow x_p=5\). Answer: \( x=Ae^{-t}+Be^{-2t}+5 \). Check: steady state \(10/2=5\), matched.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Ramp forcing coefficients

Problem. Find a particular solution of \( x''+3x'+2x=2t \).

Solution. Try \(x_p=at+b\): \(x'_p=a,\ x''_p=0\), so \(3a+2(at+b)=2t\). Match \(t\): \(2a=2\Rightarrow a=1\). Match constant: \(3a+2b=0\Rightarrow b=-1.5\). Answer: \( x_p=t-1.5 \).

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

First-order sinusoidal amplitude

Problem. For \( x'+2x=\cos t \), the steady sinusoid has amplitude \( 1/\sqrt{2^2+1^2} \). Evaluate it.

Solution. \( \text{amp}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{4+1}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt5}\approx0.447 \). Answer: \(\approx0.447\). Check: the coefficient of \(x\) (2) and the forcing frequency (1) set the denominator \(\sqrt{a^2+\omega^2}\).

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

The general solution of a forced linear ODE is:

Homogeneous (transient, with constants) plus one particular (forced). Multiplying them or dropping either is wrong.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

For \( x''+3x'+2x=10 \), the steady-state value is:

Set derivatives to zero: \(2x=10\Rightarrow x=5\). The forcing 10 is divided by the stiffness coefficient 2.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

Initial conditions for a forced second-order ODE are applied to:

Constants live in \(x_h\), but the conditions constrain the actual solution \(x_h+x_p\). Applying them to \(x_h\) alone is the classic error.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

If the trial \(x_p\) already solves the homogeneous equation, you should:

The resonance rule: multiply by \(t\) (or \(t^2\) for a double root) to get an independent particular solution.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Under sinusoidal forcing \( F_0\cos\omega t \), the steady particular solution is:

The forced response oscillates at the forcing frequency \(\omega\) (not \(\omega_n\)), with an amplitude and phase set by how close \(\omega\) is to \(\omega_n\).
