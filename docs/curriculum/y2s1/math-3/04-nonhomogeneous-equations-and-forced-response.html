Nonhomogeneous equations and forced response

Undetermined coefficients for constant, ramp, and sinusoidal forcing. The structure theorem: general solution = homogeneous + particular. Transient versus steady-state response — the vocabulary the controls course inherits whole.

01 · The structure theorem

A nonhomogeneous equation \( m x''+cx'+kx=f(t) \) carries forcing on the right-hand side. Its general solution is always \( x=x_h+x_p \): the homogeneous solution \(x_h\) from Lesson 3, which carries the two arbitrary constants, plus any single particular solution \(x_p\) that matches the forcing. This is a theorem, not a convenience — the difference of any two full solutions satisfies the homogeneous equation, so every solution differs from any one particular solution only by some \(x_h\). Practically it splits the work cleanly: solve the homogeneous problem for the transient, find one \(x_p\) for the steady behaviour, and add them. The two halves answer two different questions — how the system settles, and where.

02 · Undetermined coefficients

When \(f(t)\) is a constant, polynomial, exponential, or sinusoid, guess an \(x_p\) of the same shape with unknown coefficients and solve for them. Constant forcing \( x''+3x'+2x=10 \): try a constant, so \(x''=x'=0\) and \(2x_p=10\Rightarrow x_p=5\) — the steady state. Ramp forcing \( x''+3x'+2x=2t \): try \(x_p=at+b\); then \(x'_p=a,\ x''_p=0\), giving \(3a+2(at+b)=2t\). Matching powers of \(t\): \(2a=2\Rightarrow a=1\); matching constants: \(3a+2b=0\Rightarrow b=-1.5\). So \(x_p=t-1.5\). The method is entirely mechanical once the trial shape is chosen correctly, which is its great virtue for hand computation.

03 · Transient versus steady state

For \( x''+3x'+2x=10 \) the homogeneous roots are \(-1\) and \(-2\), both stable, so the full solution is \( x=Ae^{-t}+Be^{-2t}+5 \). The exponentials are the transient — the record of how the system approaches its destination — and the \(5\) is the steady state, that destination itself. After a few time constants only the \(5\) survives, regardless of the initial conditions, which is precisely why a steady-state calculation predicts the long-run answer without ever solving the full transient. Controls engineers exploit this split constantly: the steady-state value fixes accuracy, while the transient fixes speed and overshoot, and the two are designed almost separately.

04 · Sinusoidal forcing and the working method

Sinusoidal forcing \(f(t)=F_0\cos\omega t\) produces a sinusoidal \(x_p\) of the same frequency but with shifted phase and scaled amplitude, and that amplitude depends on how close the forcing frequency \(\omega\) sits to the natural frequency \(\omega_n\) — the seed of resonance, developed in Lesson 5. The working method is uniform: (1) solve the homogeneous equation for \(x_h\); (2) guess an \(x_p\) matching the form of \(f\), and if that trial already solves the homogeneous equation, multiply it by \(t\) (or \(t^2\) for a double root); (3) fix the constants from the initial conditions applied to the full \(x_h+x_p\), never to \(x_h\) alone. That last rule is the single most common error in the whole topic, and it is worth restating on every problem until it is automatic. A final caution on stability closes the topic: everything here assumed the homogeneous roots have negative real parts, so the transient decays and a steady state exists. If a root has a positive real part the transient grows without bound and there is no steady state to settle to — the system is unstable, and no particular solution rescues it. Checking that the characteristic roots are stable before trusting a steady-state answer is therefore not a formality but the precondition that makes the whole transient-plus-steady framework meaningful, and it is exactly the check the controls course formalises later.

05 · The resonance the constant hid

The steady part of a forced response is a genuine physical prediction, not an algebra by-product. For constant forcing it is a fixed offset — the \( x_p=5 \) of 2 is where the system sits once the transient dies. For sinusoidal forcing it is an oscillation whose amplitude depends sharply on the driving frequency \( \omega \), and that dependence is the entire subject of Lesson 5: as \( \omega \) approaches \( \omega_n \) the steady amplitude peaks, limited only by damping. The structural point to carry forward is that the split \( x=x_h+x_p \) survives every kind of forcing — the transient \( x_h \) always decays on its own timescale set by \( \zeta,\omega_n \), while \( x_p \) is dictated entirely by \( f(t) \). Keep the two questions — how it settles, and where — permanently separate, and forced problems stop being hard.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Ramp forcing coefficients

Problem. Particular solution of \( x''+3x'+2x=2t \)?

Solution. \(x_p=at+b\): \(3a+2(at+b)=2t\); \(2a=2\Rightarrow a=1\); \(3a+2b=0\Rightarrow b=-1.5\). Answer: \( x_p=t-1.5 \).
