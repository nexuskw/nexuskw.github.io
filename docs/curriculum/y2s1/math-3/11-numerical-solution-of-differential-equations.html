Numerical solution of differential equations

01 · When no formula exists

Most differential equations that arise in practice have no closed-form solution — the tidy exponentials and sinusoids of the earlier lessons are the exception, not the rule. When a formula is unavailable, the answer is computed numerically: step forward in small increments of the independent variable, using the differential equation itself to predict each next value. The simplest scheme is Euler's method. Given \( y'=f(x,y) \) and a starting point, take \( y_{n+1}=y_n+h\,f(x_n,y_n) \), where \(h\) is the step size. Geometrically it follows the tangent line for one short step, then re-reads the slope and repeats. It is crude but it is the seed of every professional solver.

02 · Euler worked, with its error

Solve \( y'=y,\ y(0)=1 \) (exact answer \( e^x \)) with \( h=0.1 \). First step: \( y_1=1+0.1\cdot1=1.1 \). The exact value is \( e^{0.1}\approx1.10517 \), so Euler is low by about \( 0.00517 \). Second step: \( y_2=1.1+0.1\cdot1.1=1.21 \), against \( e^{0.2}\approx1.2214 \). The error accumulates because each step starts from an already-approximate value and uses the slope only at the step's beginning, so any curvature over the step is missed. Euler is a first-order method: halve \(h\) and the global error roughly halves — reliable improvement, but slow, since ten-fold accuracy costs ten-fold work.

03 · Better slopes: Heun and Runge–Kutta

The fix is to sample the slope more than once per step. Improved Euler (Heun's method) predicts with Euler, then averages the slope at the start and the predicted end: for the same problem and step, \( y_1=1+\tfrac{0.1}{2}(1+1.1)=1.105 \), already far closer to \(1.10517\). The workhorse of real computation is the fourth-order Runge–Kutta (RK4), which blends four slope samples per step; on this problem one RK4 step gives \(1.105171\), matching \( e^{0.1} \) to six figures. RK4 is fourth-order — halving \(h\) cuts the error roughly sixteen-fold — so it buys accuracy far more cheaply than Euler, which is why it is the default in engineering solvers.

04 · Step size, stability, and the method

Smaller steps mean less truncation error but more arithmetic and more accumulated round-off, so there is a practical sweet spot rather than "smaller is always better." Some problems are additionally stiff — they contain very fast and very slow modes together — and force explicit methods like Euler and RK4 to take punishingly tiny steps for stability, which is where implicit solvers take over. The working method is: (1) write \( y'=f(x,y) \) and the initial value; (2) choose a scheme by the accuracy needed — Euler to understand, RK4 to compute; (3) pick \(h\), then halve it and re-run to confirm the answer has stopped moving. That convergence check — does the solution stabilise as \(h\) shrinks — is the numerical analyst's substitute for an exact answer to compare against.

05 · Trading accuracy against cost

The choice among Euler, Heun, and RK4 is a deliberate trade. Euler costs one slope evaluation per step but needs tiny steps for accuracy; RK4 costs four evaluations per step yet reaches a target accuracy in so many fewer steps that it is cheaper overall, which is why it is the default. The order of a method is exactly this exchange rate: first-order Euler must do ~10× the work for 10× the accuracy, while fourth-order RK4 gets ~16× the accuracy from merely halving the step. The professional habit is to trust neither a single run nor the smallest possible step blindly, but to halve \(h\), re-run, and watch the answer converge — the same "does it stop moving?" test that stands in for an exact solution whenever, as is usual, no formula exists.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

One Euler step

Problem. For \( y'=y,\ y(0)=1 \) with \( h=0.1 \), take one Euler step and compare with the exact value.

Solution. \( y_1=y_0+h\,f(x_0,y_0)=1+0.1\cdot1=1.1 \). The exact value is \( e^{0.1}\approx1.10517 \), so Euler is low by \( \approx0.00517 \) — the tangent step misses the upward curvature.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Two Euler steps

Problem. Continue the previous problem for a second step to estimate \( y(0.2) \).

Solution. \( y_2=y_1+h\,f=1.1+0.1\cdot1.1=1.21 \). The exact value is \( e^{0.2}\approx1.2214 \); the error has grown to \( \approx0.0114 \) because each step builds on the previous approximation.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Improved Euler

Problem. Take one improved-Euler (Heun) step for \( y'=y,\ y(0)=1,\ h=0.1 \).

Solution. Predict with Euler: \( \tilde y=1+0.1\cdot1=1.1 \). Correct with the averaged slope: \( y_1=1+\tfrac{0.1}{2}\big(f(0,1)+f(0.1,1.1)\big)=1+0.05(1+1.1)=1.105 \). This is much closer to \( e^{0.1}\approx1.10517 \) than Euler's \(1.1\).
