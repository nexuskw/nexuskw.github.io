Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does a keyway multiply nominal stress, and by roughly how much order-wise?
  • What does notch sensitivity add to the geometric Kt story?

01 · Kt — the geometry tax on nominal stress

Any geometric discontinuity — a hole, fillet, keyway, or groove — locally disturbs smooth stress flow, concentrating stress well above the simple load-over-area nominal value right at the discontinuity: \[ \sigma_{actual}=K_t\,\sigma_{nominal}, \] where \(K_t\), the stress concentration factor, depends purely on geometry — the discontinuity's shape relative to the part's size — not on load magnitude or material. A small fillet radius relative to the part size gives a high \(K_t\); a generous fillet approaches \(K_t\approx1\).

02 · Why keyways matter so much

A keyway — a slot cut for a key transmitting torque between a shaft and a hub — combines a sharp internal corner geometry with sitting exactly in a highly-stressed region: a shaft's outer surface, already carrying peak bending or torsion stress. That combination — an inherently severe geometric discontinuity landing precisely where nominal stress is already highest — is why keyways are a classically dangerous fatigue location, with \(K_t\) often in the range of \(2\)–\(3\) for a sharp keyway, a reasonable order-of-magnitude starting point before consulting an actual chart for the specific geometry.

03 · Notch sensitivity — the metallurgy half of the story

Not every material "feels" the full geometric \(K_t\) equally. Notch sensitivity \(q\) (\(0\leq q\leq1\)) scales how much of the theoretical \(K_t\) actually translates into reduced fatigue strength: \[ K_f=1+q(K_t-1). \] \(q\approx0\) means a material is essentially notch-insensitive — often a ductile material with enough local ductility to blunt the stress peak through local yielding before a fatigue crack can start, giving \(K_f\approx1\) regardless of \(K_t\). \(q\approx1\) means the material takes the full geometric \(K_t\) at face value — typically harder, higher-strength, more brittle materials lacking that local ductility. The same geometric feature can be a minor concern in one material and a serious fatigue risk in another: geometry sets the ceiling, metallurgy decides how much of it the material actually experiences.

04 · Working method — the handshake with materials science

(1) Identify every geometric discontinuity — holes, fillets, keyways, grooves, threads — as a candidate stress-concentration site. (2) Look up or estimate \(K_t\) for each, from its specific geometry ratio. (3) For a fatigue assessment, find the material's notch sensitivity \(q\) and compute \(K_f=1+q(K_t-1)\) — the number that actually multiplies the nominal alternating stress in a fatigue-life estimate (Materials Science II's S-N curve, meeting this course's geometry). (4) For a static check on a ductile material, \(K_t\) is often reasonably ignored, since local yielding redistributes stress before the whole part fails — but never for a fatigue check, where localized, repeated stress well below yield can still eventually crack a part. This distinction — matters enormously for fatigue and for brittle materials, often legitimately ignored for static ductile loading — is easy to get backward and worth fixing firmly.

Key formulas — stress concentration

\[ \sigma_{actual}=K_t\,\sigma_{nominal}\ (\text{static, geometry only}),\qquad K_f=1+q(K_t-1)\ (\text{fatigue, geometry AND metallurgy}). \]

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Actual stress at a fillet

Problem. A stepped shaft has a fillet with \(r/d\approx0.1\), giving \(K_t\approx1.74\) (representative). Nominal bending stress there is 80 MPa. Find the actual peak stress at the fillet.

Solution. \( \sigma_{actual}=K_t\sigma_{nominal}=1.74\times80\approx139\ \text{MPa} \).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Notch sensitivity changes everything

Problem. The same shaft (Kt=1.74) is made first from a notch-insensitive material (q=0.3) and then from a notch-sensitive one (q=0.9), both under fatigue loading. Find Kf for each, and explain the practical difference.

Solution. \( K_f(q=0.3)=1+0.3\times0.74\approx1.22 \). \( K_f(q=0.9)=1+0.9\times0.74\approx1.67 \). For the identical geometry, the notch-sensitive material experiences a much larger effective fatigue concentration — it is far more vulnerable to fatigue failure at this same fillet than the notch-insensitive material, despite both parts sharing the same Kt.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Static vs. fatigue — does Kt apply?

Problem. A ductile steel bracket has a \(K_t=2.5\) hole under a static, one-time tensile load well below yield. Should the design stress be multiplied by 2.5? Would the answer change under cyclic (fatigue) loading?

Solution. For the static, ductile case, Kt is typically not applied at full value — local yielding right at the hole redistributes stress to surrounding material before the part as a whole fails. Under cyclic loading, the answer changes completely: fatigue cracks can initiate at the locally concentrated stress even below yield, so Kt — through Kf and the material's notch sensitivity — absolutely must be included in a fatigue assessment.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A rotating shaft that fails at a keyway, retaining-ring groove, or shoulder fillet — rather than at the section carrying the highest nominal stress — is showing 2 and 3 together: the fatigue crack started where Kt (geometry) and q (the specific alloy's notch sensitivity) combined to produce the highest effective stress, not necessarily where a simple bending-moment diagram alone would have predicted trouble.

A design review that specifies a generous fillet radius at every shaft shoulder, rather than the sharpest radius that still fits the adjacent bearing or gear, is applying 1's curve directly — the small extra machining cost of a larger radius is cheap insurance against a fatigue failure whose real root cause, traced back far enough, is almost always a geometry decision made once at the drawing stage.
