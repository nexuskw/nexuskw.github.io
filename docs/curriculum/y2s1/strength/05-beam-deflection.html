Beam deflection

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Stiffness and strength are different failures: give a case where deflection governs.
  • Why does superposition work for beam deflections?

01 · The elastic curve, from curvature

Beam curvature relates directly to bending moment, \( 1/\rho=M/(EI) \), and for the small deflections this course works with, curvature is well approximated by the second derivative of the deflection curve, giving the governing equation \[ EIv''=M(x). \] Integrating once gives slope, \( EIv'=\int M\,dx+C_1 \); integrating again gives deflection, \( EIv=\iint M\,dx\,dx+C_1x+C_2 \) — with the constants fixed by the actual boundary conditions at the supports.

02 · A worked cantilever, and reading the shape

For a cantilever fixed at \(x=0\) with a point load \(P\) at the free tip \(x=L\): \[ v(x)=\frac{P}{6EI}\left(3Lx^2-x^3\right), \] with maximum deflection at the tip, \[ \delta=v(L)=\frac{PL^3}{3EI}. \] The shape reflects both boundary conditions used at the wall — zero deflection and zero slope — curving away smoothly and accelerating toward the free end.

03 · Superposition — why it works

Because \(EIv''=M(x)\) is linear in \(v\), the deflection from several loads acting together is simply the sum of the deflections each would cause acting alone — superposition. This turns most practical beam-deflection problems from re-deriving a new differential-equation solution every time into a lookup-and-add exercise: break the actual loading into pieces matching a table of standard cases (cantilever tip load, cantilever uniform load, simply-supported centre load, and so on), find each piece's deflection, and add them.

04 · Stiffness as a distinct failure mode

A beam can be entirely safe against stress failure — well below yield — and still fail the actual application because it deflects too much: a machinery baseplate that flexes enough to misalign a coupling, a walkway that feels unacceptably springy. Stiffness, governed by \(EI\) and geometry, is a separate design criterion from strength, governed by section modulus and allowable stress (Lesson 4) — a design can be strength-limited or stiffness-limited, and which one actually governs must be checked explicitly, never assumed. Working method: (1) identify loading and supports, matching or superposing standard cases; (2) check both bending stress and deflection against whatever serviceability limit applies, often a fraction of span such as \(L/360\); (3) whichever check is more restrictive governs the real design.

Key result — cantilever tip deflection

\[ \delta = \frac{PL^3}{3EI} \]

Stiffness (this) and strength (Lesson 4) are separate checks — either can govern.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Cantilever tip deflection

Problem. A cantilever beam, \(L=2\ \text{m}\), \(EI=50\times10^6\ \text{N·m}^2\), carries a 5 kN load at its free tip. Find the tip deflection.

Solution. \( \delta=\dfrac{PL^3}{3EI}=\dfrac{5000\times2^3}{3\times50\times10^6}\approx2.67\times10^{-4}\ \text{m}\approx0.267\ \text{mm} \).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Superposing two loads

Problem. The same cantilever carries both the 5 kN tip load (0.267 mm deflection alone) and a uniformly distributed load that alone would give 0.15 mm tip deflection. Find the total tip deflection.

Solution. By superposition: \( 0.267+0.15=0.417\ \text{mm} \).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Stiffness vs. strength — which governs?

Problem. A beam is checked and found to be at 40% of its allowable bending stress (safe). Its measured deflection is L/200, while the serviceability limit for this application is L/360. Is the beam adequate? Which criterion governs?

Solution. Not adequate. Even though comfortably safe on strength (40% of allowable stress), it fails the stiffness limit — L/200 is a larger (worse) deflection ratio than the L/360 limit permits. Stiffness governs this design, not strength.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A machinery baseplate that passes every stress check with margin to spare can still cause real problems if it deflects enough to throw a pump-and-motor coupling out of alignment — the stiffness check from 4, not the strength check, is what a commissioning engineer runs before accepting a new skid, and a baseplate redesigned "thicker" after an alignment complaint is usually a stiffness fix wearing a strength-sounding explanation.

Walkways and platform grating spanning between support beams are sized against a deflection limit as routinely as a stress limit, because a walkway that is perfectly safe against collapse but visibly bounces underfoot fails its actual job — a serviceability complaint a strength calculation alone will never catch, which is exactly why both checks are run, not just one.
