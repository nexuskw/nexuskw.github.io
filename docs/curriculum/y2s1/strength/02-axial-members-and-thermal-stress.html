Axial members and thermal stress

Deformation of stepped and composite bars. Statically indeterminate axial problems. Thermal expansion against restraint — the piping stress problem in embryo.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Two bars in series under one load: which stretches more and why?
  • Why does restrained thermal expansion create stress without any external load?

01 · Bars in series: deformations add

Real members are rarely one uniform prism. A stepped bar — segments of different area or load — deforms by the sum of its parts: \( \delta=\sum \dfrac{P_iL_i}{A_iE_i} \). Each segment carries its own internal force (found by a free-body cut) over its own length and area. The method is bookkeeping: cut, find \(P_i\), compute each \( \delta_i \), add with sign. Tension lengthens (+), compression shortens (−); the signs must be respected or a stretch and a squeeze will wrongly cancel.

02 · Statically indeterminate axial problems

When a bar is held at both ends, statics alone cannot find the internal forces — there are more unknown reactions than equilibrium equations. The missing equation is compatibility: a geometric statement about how the deformations must fit, usually "the total length change is zero" or "the two segments share the same displacement." Pairing equilibrium with compatibility and Hooke's law closes the problem. This trio — equilibrium, compatibility, material law — is the general recipe for every indeterminate structure in the curriculum, first met here in its simplest axial form.

03 · Thermal strain and free expansion

Temperature changes strain a material even with no load: \( \varepsilon_T=\alpha\,\Delta T \), where \( \alpha \) is the coefficient of thermal expansion (steel \(\approx12\times10^{-6} /^\circ\text{C}\)). A 50 °C rise gives \( \varepsilon_T=600\times10^{-6} \) — a free bar simply grows by \( \alpha\,\Delta T\,L \) and feels no stress. Thermal strain is real deformation; it becomes a stress only when something stops it.

04 · Thermal stress: expansion against restraint

Restrain that bar between rigid walls so it cannot grow, and the thermal strain is converted entirely into mechanical strain of the opposite sign — a compressive stress \( \sigma=E\alpha\,\Delta T \). For steel over 50 °C: \( \sigma=200\times10^9\times12\times10^{-6}\times50=120\ \text{MPa} \), independent of the bar's length or area. That length-independence surprises students and explains real failures: a fully restrained pipe run heated 50 °C carries 120 MPa whether it is one metre or one hundred. Expansion joints, loops, and sliding supports exist precisely to give thermal strain somewhere to go before it becomes stress.

05 · The engineering lesson

The pattern to carry forward: deformation is additive over segments, indeterminate problems need compatibility, and temperature is just another source of strain that turns into stress only under restraint. The thermal case is the embryo of the entire piping- and pressure-stress world of later courses — where a plant's thermal cycling, not its pressure, is often the fatigue driver. An engineer who instinctively asks "is this free to expand, or restrained?" before worrying about the temperature itself has absorbed the core of this lesson. The arithmetic is easy; the modelling judgement — free versus restrained, determinate versus indeterminate — is the skill. It is worth stressing the counter-intuitive part once more: thermal stress is set by the temperature change, the material, and the degree of restraint alone, never by how long the member is. A designer who adds length hoping to relieve a restrained thermal load achieves nothing; only giving the expansion room — a loop, a joint, a sliding support — actually lowers the stress. That single insight prevents a whole class of piping and structural failures.

Examples and Quiz

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Thermal strain

Problem. A steel bar (\(\alpha=12\times10^{-6}/^\circ\)C) is heated 50 °C with no restraint. Find the thermal strain.

Solution. \( \varepsilon_T=\alpha\Delta T=12\times10^{-6}\times50=600\times10^{-6} \). Answer: \(600\ \mu\varepsilon\). Check: free bar, so this is pure growth — no stress.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Thermal stress under restraint

Problem. The same bar is now rigidly held between walls (E=200 GPa). Find the stress from the 50 °C rise.

Solution. Fully restrained: \( \sigma=E\alpha\Delta T=200\times10^9\times12\times10^{-6}\times50=120\times10^6\,\text{Pa} \). Answer: 120 MPa (compressive). Check: independent of length and area.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Series bar concept

Problem. Two aligned segments carry the same axial load but segment B has twice the area of A. Which stretches more, and by what ratio (equal lengths, same material)?

Solution. \( \delta=PL/AE \); with \(P,L,E\) equal, \(\delta\propto1/A\). Segment A (smaller area) stretches twice as much: ratio \( \delta_A/\delta_B=2 \). Answer: A stretches, ratio 2:1.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

For a bar heated but free to expand, the axial stress is:

No restraint means no induced stress — the bar simply grows. Stress \(E\alpha\Delta T\) appears only when expansion is prevented.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

A statically indeterminate axial bar needs, beyond equilibrium, an equation of:

Compatibility (a geometric fit condition) supplies the extra equation when reactions outnumber equilibrium equations.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

Fully-restrained thermal stress \(E\alpha\Delta T\) depends on the bar's length:

Neither length nor area appears in \(E\alpha\Delta T\); a long restrained pipe and a short one reach the same stress.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

Deformations of segments in a stepped bar are combined by:

Total \(\delta=\sum P_iL_i/A_iE_i\) with signs — a stretch and a squeeze partly cancel, so signs matter.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Thermal strain \(\varepsilon_T=\alpha\Delta T\) for steel over 50 °C (\(\alpha=12\times10^{-6}\)) is:

\(12\times10^{-6}\times50=600\times10^{-6}\). Forgetting to multiply by \(\Delta T\) leaves the bare \(\alpha\).
