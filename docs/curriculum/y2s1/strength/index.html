Strength of Materials

Stress where it lives: axial members, torsion, bending, combined loading, and failure theories — the course that turns statics diagrams into the numbers written on repair procedures and fitness-for-service notes.

12 lessons · 1 in the Core 60 · Primary texts:

  • MIT OCW 2.001 Mechanics & Materials I (ocw.mit.edu)
  • NPTEL: Strength of Materials (nptel.ac.in)
01

Stress and strain under axial loadCore 60 · guide queued

Normal stress and strain defined from first principles; the tensile curve reread as an engineer's contract — modulus, yield, ultimate, ductility. Allowable stress and factor of safety as institutional honesty. Saint-Venant's caution about where formulas apply.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A 16 mm bar carries 45 kN: compute the stress and the safety factor against 355 MPa yield.
  • Engineering versus true stress: which keeps rising after necking, and why?
  • Why do aluminum alloys need the 0.2% offset construction to define yield at all?

Taught from — CORE 60 · B1 · MIT OCW 2.001; Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 6; video: Jeff Hanson (Texas Tech) — mechanics of materials In Arabic — alternative

02

Axial members and thermal stress

Deformation of stepped and composite bars. Statically indeterminate axial problems. Thermal expansion against restraint — the piping stress problem in embryo.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Two bars in series under one load: which stretches more and why?
  • Why does restrained thermal expansion create stress without any external load?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001; video: Jeff Hanson (Texas Tech) — mechanics of materials In Arabic — alternative

03

Torsion of shafts

Shear stress and angle of twist in circular shafts derived. Power–torque–speed relations for drivelines. Sizing a pump shaft and reading a torsional failure surface's 45° signature.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does a torsional fatigue crack spiral at 45°?
  • Double a shaft's diameter: what happens to its torsional stress at the same torque?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001; video: Jeff Hanson (Texas Tech) — mechanics of materials In Arabic — alternative

04

Bending stress in beams

The flexure formula derived from plane sections. Section modulus as the designer's currency. Where beams actually fail — and why the extreme fiber earns its name.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does bending stress vanish at the neutral axis and peak at the surface?
  • What does section modulus let you compare across shapes?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001; video: Jeff Hanson (Texas Tech) — mechanics of materials In Arabic — alternative

05

Transverse shear in beams

Shear stress distribution and shear flow. Why webs carry shear and flanges carry moment in an I-beam. Built-up sections and their fastener spacing.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why do I-beam webs carry the shear while flanges carry the moment?
  • Where does shear stress peak in a rectangular section?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001; video: Jeff Hanson (Texas Tech) — mechanics of materials In Arabic — alternative

06

Beam deflection

Elastic curve by integration; superposition with standard cases. Stiffness as a serviceability limit distinct from strength. Machinery baseplate and walkway deflection checks.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Stiffness and strength are different failures: give a case where deflection governs.
  • Why does superposition work for beam deflections?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001; video: Jeff Hanson (Texas Tech) — mechanics of materials In Arabic — alternative

07

Combined loading

Axial, torsion, bending, and pressure superposed at a point. Critical-point hunting on real components. A shaft with a pulley worked completely — the pattern for machine design.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • At a shaft surface point under bending plus torsion: which stresses combine, and how?
  • Why is the critical point rarely obvious before you check candidates?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001; video: Jeff Hanson (Texas Tech) — mechanics of materials In Arabic — alternative

08

Stress transformation and Mohr's circle

Plane-stress transformation derived, then Mohr's circle as its graphical engine. Principal stresses and maximum shear located. Why fatigue cracks pick their planes.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What do Mohr's circle's center and radius each represent?
  • Why do principal planes carry no shear?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001; video: Jeff Hanson (Texas Tech) — mechanics of materials In Arabic — alternative

09

Pressure vessels: thin-walled theory

Hoop and axial stress derived from equilibrium. The 2:1 hoop-to-axial ratio and what it predicts about vessel failure orientation. First contact with the equipment class Asset Integrity will govern.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Derive the 2:1 hoop-to-axial ratio and say which way a vessel should split first.
  • When does thin-wall theory stop being honest?

10

Failure theories

Tresca and von Mises for ductile metals; maximum normal stress for brittle ones. Using them on combined-stress states from lesson 7. What 'the stress' means when there are six components.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does von Mises predict yielding better than max-normal-stress for ductile metals?
  • A pure-shear state: what does each theory predict for yield?

11

Buckling of columns

Euler buckling derived; effective length and slenderness. The crossover between yielding and buckling. Why long slender compression members fail sideways without warning.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does buckling depend on stiffness and length but not strength?
  • What does effective length encode about end conditions?

12

Stress concentration and fatigue preview

Kt at holes, fillets, and keyways; the geometry tax on nominal stress. Notch sensitivity in brief. The handshake with Materials II's fatigue lesson — geometry and metallurgy jointly set life.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does a keyway multiply nominal stress, and by roughly how much order-wise?
  • What does notch sensitivity add to the geometric Kt story?
