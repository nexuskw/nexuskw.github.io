Strength of Materials

Stress where it lives: axial members, torsion, bending, combined loading, and failure theories — the course that turns statics diagrams into the numbers written on repair procedures and fitness-for-service notes.

01

Stress and strain under axial loadCore 60 · in production

Normal stress and strain defined from first principles; the tensile curve reread as an engineer's contract — modulus, yield, ultimate, ductility. Allowable stress and factor of safety as institutional honesty. Saint-Venant's caution about where formulas apply.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A 16 mm bar carries 45 kN: compute the stress and the safety factor against 355 MPa yield.
  • Engineering versus true stress: which keeps rising after necking, and why?
  • Why do aluminum alloys need the 0.2% offset construction to define yield at all?

Taught from — CORE 60 · B1 · MIT OCW 2.001; Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 6; video: Jeff Hanson (Texas Tech) — mechanics of materials In Arabic — alternative

02

Axial members and thermal stress

Deformation of stepped and composite bars. Statically indeterminate axial problems. Thermal expansion against restraint — the piping stress problem in embryo.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Two bars in series under one load: which stretches more and why?
  • Why does restrained thermal expansion create stress without any external load?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001; video: Jeff Hanson (Texas Tech) — mechanics of materials In Arabic — alternative

Torsion of shafts

Shear stress and angle of twist in circular shafts derived. Power–torque–speed relations for drivelines. Sizing a pump shaft and reading a torsional failure surface's 45° signature.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does a torsional fatigue crack spiral at 45°?
  • Double a shaft's diameter: what happens to its torsional stress at the same torque?

Bending stress in beams

The flexure formula derived from plane sections. Section modulus as the designer's currency. Where beams actually fail — and why the extreme fiber earns its name.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does bending stress vanish at the neutral axis and peak at the surface?
  • What does section modulus let you compare across shapes?

  • Why do I-beam webs carry the shear while flanges carry the moment?
  • Where does shear stress peak in a rectangular section?

Combined loading

Axial, torsion, bending, and pressure superposed at a point. Critical-point hunting on real components. A shaft with a pulley worked completely — the pattern for machine design.

After this lesson you can answer:
