Pressure vessels: thin-walled theory

01 · Hoop and axial stress, derived from equilibrium

For a thin-walled cylinder (\(r/t\gtrsim10\)), cutting it longitudinally and balancing internal pressure pushing the two halves apart against the wall's tensile stress holding them together gives \[ \sigma_{hoop}=\frac{pr}{t}. \] Cutting it transversely instead, balancing pressure on an end cap against the wall's axial stress around the thin circumference, gives \[ \sigma_{axial}=\frac{pr}{2t}. \] The ratio \(\sigma_{hoop}/\sigma_{axial}=2\) always — the pressure, radius, and thickness all cancel out of that ratio, leaving a result that holds for any thin cylindrical vessel whatsoever.

02 · What the 2:1 ratio predicts

Since hoop stress is the larger of the two, a cylindrical vessel failing from pure overpressure is expected to crack longitudinally — along the axis — because a longitudinal crack is opened by the larger hoop stress, while a circumferential crack, driven only by the smaller axial stress, needs a higher pressure to propagate. This is a genuine, testable prediction that matches observed pipe and vessel failures, not just a bookkeeping result.

03 · Spherical vessels, briefly

A sphere has no preferred direction by symmetry — cutting it any way gives the identical equilibrium, \( \sigma=pr/2t \) — the same value as a cylinder's lower, axial stress, in every direction. This is exactly why spherical pressure vessels are the material-efficient choice for the highest-pressure applications: no direction is "wasted" carrying only half the stress the way a cylinder's axial direction is, so a sphere uses less material for the same pressure and volume than an equivalent cylinder.

04 · When thin-wall theory stops being honest

The \(r/t>10\) rule of thumb is the honesty boundary. Below it, stress varies significantly through the wall thickness — higher at the inner surface than the outer — and the simple average formulas above increasingly understate the true peak stress; thick-wall (Lamé) theory takes over from there. Working method: (1) check \(r/t\) before applying any formula; (2) if thin-wall applies, compute both stresses and remember the 2:1 relationship; (3) predict crack orientation from whichever stress is genuinely higher.

Key ratio — thin-walled cylinder

\[ \sigma_{hoop}=\frac{pr}{t},\qquad \sigma_{axial}=\frac{pr}{2t},\qquad \frac{\sigma_{hoop}}{\sigma_{axial}}=2\ \text{always}. \]

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Hoop and axial stress, computed

Problem. A cylindrical pressure vessel has inner radius \(r=500\ \text{mm}\), wall thickness \(t=10\ \text{mm}\), internal pressure \(p=2\ \text{MPa}\). Confirm thin-wall theory applies, then find the hoop and axial stress.

Solution. \( r/t=500/10=50>10 \), thin-wall applies. \( \sigma_{hoop}=pr/t=2\times500/10=100\ \text{MPa} \). \( \sigma_{axial}=pr/2t=50\ \text{MPa} \).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Predicting crack orientation

Problem. The vessel from the previous problem develops a crack from overpressure. Would you expect it to run longitudinally or circumferentially? Explain.

Solution. Longitudinally. Hoop stress (100 MPa) is double axial stress (50 MPa), and a longitudinal crack is opened by the larger hoop stress, making it the more likely failure orientation from pure overpressure.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Sphere vs. cylinder

Problem. A spherical vessel of the same radius, thickness, and pressure as the cylinder above is compared to it. Find the sphere’s stress, and explain why a sphere is more material-efficient for pressure containment.

Solution. Sphere: \( \sigma=pr/2t=50\ \text{MPa} \) in every direction — the same as the cylinder's axial stress, but well below the cylinder's peak (hoop) stress of 100 MPa. Because the sphere never reaches the cylinder's higher hoop stress, it could use a thinner wall for the same maximum allowable stress — no direction is stressed as hard as the cylinder's hoop direction.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A storage tank or pressure vessel inspection program that finds axial (vertical) cracking on a cylindrical shell is seeing exactly 2's predicted failure mode — hoop stress-driven, longitudinal cracking — and an inspector who understands why expects to find it running parallel to the vessel's axis, not around its circumference, which shapes where an ultrasonic or dye-penetrant scan actually concentrates its coverage.

A vessel's end caps (heads) are a routine trouble spot precisely because the transition from cylindrical shell to a curved or flat head is a geometric discontinuity where the clean thin-wall equilibrium of 1 no longer applies exactly — a detail that becomes very relevant once Year 4's asset-integrity coursework covers head design and nozzle reinforcement in full.
