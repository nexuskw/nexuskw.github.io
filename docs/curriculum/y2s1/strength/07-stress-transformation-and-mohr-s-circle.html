Stress transformation and Mohr's circle

Plane-stress transformation derived, then Mohr's circle as its graphical engine. Principal stresses and maximum shear located. Why fatigue cracks pick their planes.

MoM Lesson 50 — Mohr's Circle for Stress TransformationJEFF HANSON

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What do Mohr's circle's center and radius each represent?
  • Why do principal planes carry no shear?

01 · Plane-stress transformation, derived

Given a stress state \((\sigma_x,\sigma_y,\tau_{xy})\) on the standard faces, equilibrium of a wedge cut at angle \(\theta\) gives the stress on that rotated face directly: \[ \sigma_{\theta}=\frac{\sigma_x+\sigma_y}{2}+\frac{\sigma_x-\sigma_y}{2}\cos2\theta+\tau_{xy}\sin2\theta, \] with a parallel expression for \(\tau_\theta\). These follow from summing forces on a triangular element — genuinely derived, not merely asserted.

02 · Mohr’s circle as the graphical engine

The transformation equations are exactly the parametric equations of a circle in \((\sigma,\tau)\) space: centre at \(\left(\frac{\sigma_x+\sigma_y}{2},0\right)\), radius \[ R=\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_x-\sigma_y}{2}\right)^2+\tau_{xy}^2}. \] Every possible stress state at that point, for every possible face orientation, lies somewhere on this one circle. Reading it: plot \((\sigma_x,\tau_{xy})\) for the x-face and \((\sigma_y,-\tau_{xy})\) for the y-face — diametrically opposite points — and any other orientation is a rotation around the same circle, by \(2\theta\), not \(\theta\): the graphical angle doubles the physical one, a classic point of confusion worth fixing early.

03 · Principal stresses and maximum shear

Principal stresses — where shear is zero — occur where the circle crosses the \(\sigma\)-axis: \( \sigma_{1,2}=\text{centre}\pm R \). Maximum in-plane shear equals the radius \(R\) itself, occurring \(90^\circ\) around the circle — \(45^\circ\) physically — from the principal directions. This is exactly why fatigue cracks often pick planes offset \(45^\circ\) from the obvious loading direction: many failure mechanisms, especially in ductile materials, are shear-driven, so they follow the maximum-shear orientation, not the maximum-normal-stress one.

04 · Working method

(1) Identify \(\sigma_x\), \(\sigma_y\), \(\tau_{xy}\) at the point of interest, typically from a combined-loading analysis (Lesson 6). (2) Compute the centre and radius. (3) Principal stresses are centre \(\pm R\). (4) Maximum shear is \(R\), acting at \(45^\circ\) from the principal directions. This is the direct bridge from "here are the stresses on an arbitrary cut" to "here is the worst normal stress and the worst shear stress, and the planes they act on" — the necessary input before any failure theory (Lesson 9) can be applied.

Key results — Mohr’s circle

\[ \text{centre}=\frac{\sigma_x+\sigma_y}{2},\qquad R=\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_x-\sigma_y}{2}\right)^2+\tau_{xy}^2},\qquad \sigma_{1,2}=\text{centre}\pm R. \]

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Principal stresses from Mohr’s circle

Problem. A point has \(\sigma_x=100\ \text{MPa}\), \(\sigma_y=40\ \text{MPa}\), \(\tau_{xy}=30\ \text{MPa}\). Find the principal stresses.

Solution. Centre \(=(100+40)/2=70\ \text{MPa}\). \( R=\sqrt{30^2+30^2}=\sqrt{1800}\approx42.4\ \text{MPa} \). \( \sigma_1\approx112.4\ \text{MPa} \), \( \sigma_2\approx27.6\ \text{MPa} \).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Maximum in-plane shear

Problem. For the same stress state, find the maximum in-plane shear stress, and state at what physical angle from the principal planes it occurs.

Solution. \( \tau_{max}=R\approx42.4\ \text{MPa} \), occurring \(45^\circ\) physically (\(90^\circ\) graphically on the circle) from the principal-stress planes.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Pure shear on Mohr’s circle

Problem. A point is in pure shear: \(\sigma_x=0\), \(\sigma_y=0\), \(\tau_{xy}=50\ \text{MPa}\). Find the principal stresses, and explain what this reveals about pure shear physically.

Solution. Centre \(=0\), \(R=\sqrt{0^2+50^2}=50\ \text{MPa}\). \( \sigma_1=+50\ \text{MPa} \), \( \sigma_2=-50\ \text{MPa} \). This shows pure shear is equivalent to equal-and-opposite tension and compression at 45° to the shear planes — exactly why a piece of chalk twisted in torsion snaps along a 45° helical crack, following the principal tension direction rather than the shear plane.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A fractured shaft with a spiral, roughly 45°-pitched crack surface is showing 3's principal-stress geometry directly — a failure analyst reading that angle against the shaft's loading (mostly torsion, mostly bending, or a mix) can often say which load type dominated before running a single calculation, since pure torsion drives a crack toward 45° while pure bending tends toward a flatter, more transverse crack.

Strain-gauge rosettes bonded to a component surface measure strain in three fixed directions, and the standard reduction of that data runs Mohr's-circle logic in reverse — from three measured strains back to the principal stresses at that point — the exact skill this lesson builds, applied to real instrumentation rather than a textbook problem.
