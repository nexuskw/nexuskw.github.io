Buckling of columns

Euler buckling derived; effective length and slenderness. The crossover between yielding and buckling. Why long slender compression members fail sideways without warning.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does buckling depend on stiffness and length but not strength?
  • What does effective length encode about end conditions?

01 · Euler buckling, derived

A slender column under axial compression can fail by sideways deflection — buckling — at a load far below what would cause simple material yielding. Modeling the column with the same \(EIv''=M(x)\) logic as Lesson 5's beam, but now with \(M(x)=P\,v(x)\) — the moment caused by the deflection itself, growing as the deflection grows — leads to a solution where deflection stays exactly zero until the load reaches a critical value: \[ P_{cr}=\frac{\pi^2EI}{L_e^2}, \] the Euler buckling load, with \(L_e\) the effective length, not simply the physical length.

02 · Effective length — what end conditions encode

\( L_e=KL \), where \(K\) depends on end restraint: \(K=1\) for pinned-pinned (the baseline case), \(K=0.5\) for fixed-fixed (far stiffer against buckling, since \(P_{cr}\propto1/L_e^2\)), \(K=2\) for fixed-free (a cantilevered column, much weaker), \(K=0.7\) for fixed-pinned. Real end conditions rarely match these idealized cases exactly, so \(K\) is often an engineering judgment call, not a precisely derived number.

03 · Slenderness and the yield crossover

In terms of stress, \[ \sigma_{cr}=\frac{P_{cr}}{A}=\frac{\pi^2E}{(L_e/r)^2}, \] with \(r=\sqrt{I/A}\) the radius of gyration and \(L_e/r\) the slenderness ratio. Notice \(E\) — stiffness — appears, but the material's yield or ultimate strength does not: Euler buckling depends on stiffness and geometry alone. But the formula cannot be trusted forever — as slenderness falls, the predicted \(\sigma_{cr}\) would eventually exceed the material's actual yield strength, which is physically impossible. Below a critical slenderness ratio (where the Euler curve crosses the yield stress), the column simply yields in compression before it ever reaches the geometrically-predicted buckling load — short, stocky columns are a strength problem; long, slender ones are a stiffness problem, the same distinction from Lesson 5, now appearing in compression.

04 · Why buckling fails without warning

Unlike a ductile material's visible plastic elongation before tensile fracture, an idealized column near its buckling load shows almost no deflection right up to \(P_{cr}\), then diverges sharply — real imperfections (a slight initial crookedness, off-centre loading) soften this into a more gradual but still sudden-feeling transition. Working method: (1) compute the slenderness ratio \(L_e/r\); (2) compute the Euler critical stress; (3) compare against yield strength — whichever is lower actually governs; (4) always use effective length \(L_e=KL\), never raw physical length, since end conditions change the answer by a large factor.

Key result — Euler buckling

\[ P_{cr}=\frac{\pi^2EI}{L_e^2},\qquad \sigma_{cr}=\frac{\pi^2E}{(L_e/r)^2}. \]

Depends on stiffness (E) and geometry — never on the material's strength.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Slenderness and Euler stress

Problem. A pinned-pinned steel column (\(E=200\ \text{GPa}\)) has length \(L=3\ \text{m}\) and radius of gyration \(r=25\ \text{mm}\). Find the slenderness ratio and the Euler critical stress. If yield strength is 250 MPa, does buckling or yielding govern?

Solution. \(L_e=L=3\ \text{m}\) (K=1). Slenderness \(=3000/25=120\). \( \sigma_{cr}=\pi^2\times200{,}000/120^2\approx137\ \text{MPa} \). Since \(137<250\ \text{MPa}\), Euler buckling governs — the column buckles well before it could yield.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

The effect of end conditions

Problem. The same column is now fixed at both ends (K=0.5) instead of pinned, with the same physical length and cross-section. Find the new critical stress, and explain why it changed.

Solution. \( L_e=0.5\times3=1.5\ \text{m} \), slenderness \(=1500/25=60\). \( \sigma_{cr}=\pi^2\times200{,}000/60^2\approx548\ \text{MPa} \) — much higher than the pinned case (137 MPa). Fixed ends resist rotation, effectively halving the buckling length, and since \(\sigma_{cr}\propto1/L_e^2\), halving \(L_e\) quadruples \(\sigma_{cr}\).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

When the Euler formula cannot be trusted

Problem. A short column has slenderness ratio 30 (same steel, E=200 GPa, yield=250 MPa). What critical stress would the Euler formula predict, and why can this number not actually be trusted?

Solution. \( \sigma_{cr}=\pi^2\times200{,}000/30^2\approx2193\ \text{MPa} \) — far above the steel’s actual 250 MPa yield strength. This cannot be trusted because a real column cannot sustain stress beyond yield; at this low slenderness, the column simply yields in compression at 250 MPa long before the geometrically-predicted (but physically impossible) Euler stress could ever be reached.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A structural review of an existing pipe rack or equipment support column checks end-connection details as carefully as the column's own cross-section, because 2's K factor can swing the actual buckling capacity by a factor of four between a genuinely fixed base and a merely pinned one — a base plate that looks "fixed" on a drawing but is actually only lightly bolted can leave a column with far less buckling capacity than an as-designed calculation assumed.

Long, slender bracing members and jacking posts used temporarily during a shutdown or a lift are sized against Euler buckling specifically, not against material yield, since 3's crossover point makes clear that a long, thin member fails by suddenly bowing sideways at a load well below what the same cross-section could otherwise carry in pure compression — treating such a member as a simple yield-strength calculation is a real, avoidable safety error.
