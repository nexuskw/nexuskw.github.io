01 · The orthogonal cutting model

Orthogonal cutting simplifies real machining to two dimensions: a straight cutting edge perpendicular to the direction of motion, removing a layer of uncut thickness \(t_o\) and producing a chip of thickness \(t_c\). Chip formation is idealized as shear across a single shear plane, at angle \(\phi\) from the cutting direction — everything ahead of that plane is still workpiece; everything behind it is chip. The chip thickness ratio \(r=t_o/t_c\) is always less than 1 (the chip thickens as it forms, since it must move slower than the uncut layer approached the tool, by conservation of mass at steady state), and relates directly to the shear angle and the tool's rake angle \(\alpha\) (the angle of the tool's cutting face from the normal to the work surface): \[ \tan\phi = \frac{r\cos\alpha}{1-r\sin\alpha}. \] A higher rake angle, for the same chip thickness ratio, increases the shear angle — a thinner, more efficiently formed chip and lower cutting force, which is exactly why rake angle is a first lever a machinist or tool designer reaches for.

02 · Cutting forces and Merchant's circle

The tool applies a resultant force to the chip, conventionally decomposed into a cutting force \(F_c\) (in the direction of tool travel) and a thrust force \(F_t\) (perpendicular to it) — both directly measurable with a dynamometer. That same resultant, viewed from the shear plane's own geometry, decomposes instead into a shear force \(F_s\) along the shear plane (doing the actual work of separating the chip) and a normal force across it; viewed from the rake face, it decomposes into a friction force along the chip-tool interface and a normal force against the rake face. Merchant's circle is the construction that relates all of these to each other and to the two governing angles, \(\phi\) and \(\alpha\), plus the friction angle \(\beta\) (with \(\tan\beta=\mu\), the tool-chip friction coefficient) — once any one force and the geometry are known, the rest follow.

03 · Where cutting temperature and tool wear begin

Essentially all of the mechanical work done in cutting — shearing the chip plus overcoming friction on the rake face — converts to heat, concentrated in a small volume at the tool-chip interface and along the shear plane. Temperatures there commonly exceed several hundred degrees Celsius for ordinary steels, well into the range where a tool material's own hardness begins to fall off (previewed here, developed fully in Lesson 2). This is the direct mechanical link between cutting theory and tool wear: higher cutting speed means more heat generated per unit time in roughly the same small volume, which is the single biggest lever on how fast a tool wears out.

04 · Taylor's tool-life equation, read as economics

Tool life \(T\) (time to a defined wear failure) falls sharply as cutting speed \(V\) rises — captured empirically by the Taylor equation, \[ VT^n=C, \] where \(n\) and \(C\) are constants for a given tool-work material pair, found by cutting at several speeds and measuring life at each. Because \(n\) is typically small (often \(0.1\)–\(0.3\) for high-speed steel and carbide tools), a modest speed increase produces a disproportionately large tool-life reduction — the equation is less a physics law than a compact economic statement: pushing speed trades tool cost and changeover time against material removed per hour, and \(n\) tells you exactly how unfavorable that trade becomes.

Key relation — chip geometry to shear angle

\[ \tan\phi = \frac{r\cos\alpha}{1-r\sin\alpha}, \qquad r=\frac{t_o}{t_c} \]

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Shear angle from measured chip

Problem. A turning cut removes a layer of uncut thickness 0.25 mm with a tool of rake angle 10°. The resulting chip measures 0.50 mm thick. Find the chip thickness ratio and the shear angle.

Solution. \( r=t_o/t_c=0.25/0.50=0.5 \). \[ \tan\phi=\frac{0.5\cos10^\circ}{1-0.5\sin10^\circ}=\frac{0.492}{0.913}=0.540 \Rightarrow \phi=28.3^\circ. \]

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Cutting and thrust force from shear strength

Problem. For the cut in the previous problem (φ=28.3°, α=10°), the work material has shear strength 400 MPa, width of cut 5 mm, and the tool-chip friction coefficient is 0.5. Find the shear force, then the cutting and thrust forces.

Solution. Shear plane area \(A_s=t_o b/\sin\phi=0.25\times5/\sin28.3^\circ=2.63\ \text{mm}^2\). Shear force \(F_s=\tau_s A_s=400\times2.63\approx1053\ \text{N}\). Friction angle \(\beta=\tan^{-1}(0.5)=26.6^\circ\). Resolving through the shear-plane and rake-face geometry gives \(F_c\approx1426\ \text{N}\) and \(F_t\approx424\ \text{N}\) — the cutting force does most of the work; the thrust force is the smaller, radial component.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Taylor equation — the cost of pushing speed

Problem. A tool-work pair has Taylor constants \(n=0.25\), \(C=350\ \text{m/min}\). Find the tool life at 100 m/min and at 130 m/min, and state the percentage change in life for a 30% increase in speed.

Solution. \( T=(C/V)^{1/n} \). At \(V=100\): \(T=(3.5)^4=150\ \text{min}\). At \(V=130\): \(T=(2.69)^4\approx52.5\ \text{min}\). A 30% speed increase cuts tool life by about 65% — the small exponent \(n\) makes tool life extremely sensitive to speed.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A shop pushing spindle speed on a difficult job to hit a delivery date, without checking the Taylor relationship for that specific tool-work pairing, routinely discovers the true cost only when tools start failing mid-shift far more often than planned — the tool-life equation's small exponent means the trade was much steeper than a linear "10% faster, 10% more tools" intuition would suggest.

A programmer troubleshooting an unexpectedly rough or torn finish on a new job checks rake angle and chip thickness ratio before blaming the machine — per 1, a chip that is not forming and flowing the way the geometry predicts is often the first visible symptom of a wrong insert grade or a rake angle poorly matched to the material, well before it shows up as a dimensional or tool-life problem.
