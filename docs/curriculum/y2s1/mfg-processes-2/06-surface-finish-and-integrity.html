01 · Ra and Rz defined

Ra (arithmetic average roughness) is the mean of the absolute deviation of the surface profile from its own mean line, measured over a defined sampling length — an averaging measure, which means a single unusually deep scratch or peak barely moves it, since it is diluted across the whole sampled length. Rz, by contrast, is built from peak-to-valley extremes (the specific averaging convention varies by standard, but the shared idea is the same) — far more sensitive to occasional deep features that Ra would average away. Both are measured with a profilometer, a stylus or optical instrument tracing the actual surface profile.

02 · Finish versus process capability

Each process has a characteristic achievable Ra range: rough turning or milling typically several micrometers Ra; fine turning or milling roughly \(0.8\)–\(3\ \mu\text{m}\); grinding roughly \(0.1\)–\(0.8\ \mu\text{m}\); lapping and honing well under \(0.1\ \mu\text{m}\) where required. Specifying a finish tighter than a process can reliably achieve forces an unnecessary second operation (typically grinding, following rougher machining) — reading a process-capability chart before specifying a finish on a drawing is what prevents that avoidable cost, exactly the same discipline Lesson 2's insert-grade selection and Lesson 5's grinding-wheel selection both apply to their own decisions.

03 · Residual stresses from machining and grinding

Cutting and grinding both leave a thin near-surface layer in a state of residual stress — compressive or tensile, depending on process parameters (aggressive grinding conditions, for instance, tend to leave tensile residual stress from the localized thermal expansion-and-cooling cycle of 2 in Lesson 5, while properly controlled conditions or processes like shot peening deliberately induce compressive stress instead). This matters directly for fatigue life (Strength of Materials): a near-surface tensile residual stress adds to any applied tensile stress, accelerating crack initiation at exactly the location fatigue cracks start; a compressive residual stress does the reverse, actively resisting crack initiation. Two parts with identical Ra and identical geometry can have meaningfully different fatigue lives purely from this residual-stress difference — a fact no surface-finish number alone reveals.

04 · Why a ground bearing journal's finish is a reliability parameter

A bearing journal's surface finish is not merely cosmetic: too rough a finish accelerates wear and can disrupt the lubricant film a rolling-element or journal bearing depends on to separate its surfaces; too aggressive a grind, chasing an ever-finer Ra without controlling heat and residual stress, risks exactly the tensile-residual-stress and metallurgical-damage problem of 3 and Lesson 5 — a journal that looks better on a profilometer printout while being mechanically worse underneath it. Specifying and verifying a critical journal therefore means controlling both the Ra number and the process conditions that produced it, not the Ra number alone.

Key distinction — Ra vs. Rz

Ra: average deviation — smooths over outliers. Rz: peak-to-valley — catches the occasional deep feature Ra hides.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Ra of an idealized profile

Problem. A turned surface is idealized as a symmetric triangular profile with peak-to-valley height 8 μm. For this exact shape, Ra = h/4. Find Ra.

Solution. \( R_a = h/4 = 8/4 = 2.0\ \mu\text{m} \).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Choosing a process for a finish spec

Problem. A drawing calls for Ra 0.4 μm on a steel shaft. Fine turning achieves roughly 0.8–3 μm; grinding achieves roughly 0.1–0.8 μm. Which process should be specified, and what does specifying fine turning instead risk?

Solution. Grinding should be specified — 0.4 μm falls within grinding's achievable range but below fine turning's best achievable Ra. Specifying fine turning risks the part failing inspection regardless of how carefully it is turned, since the process is not capable of reliably reaching that finish at all.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Same Ra, different fatigue life

Problem. Two identical shafts are ground to the same Ra, but one was ground aggressively (leaving tensile residual stress) and the other under controlled conditions (leaving compressive residual stress). Which shaft is expected to have the better fatigue life, and why does the Ra number alone not reveal this?

Solution. The compressively-stressed shaft is expected to have the better fatigue life — compressive residual stress resists crack initiation, while tensile residual stress adds to applied stress and accelerates it. Ra alone does not reveal this because it is a geometric measure of the surface profile, entirely independent of the internal residual stress state left by the process that produced it.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A drawing that specifies Ra 0.4 μm on every dimension by default, rather than only where the function actually requires it, is quietly forcing grinding operations (and their cost and cycle time) onto surfaces that a controlled turning or milling operation could have satisfied — per 2, checking process capability against the real functional requirement before specifying a finish is a genuine cost lever, not a formality.

A failure investigation on a fatigue-cracked shaft that measured Ra and found it within spec, then stopped, is missing exactly what 3 describes: a within-spec Ra tells the investigator the geometry was fine, but says nothing about the residual stress state the finishing process actually left behind — a root-cause investigation on a fatigue failure that never checks residual stress is only half the story.
