01 · The arc welding circuit

Every arc welding process shares the same basic circuit: a power source drives current through a cable to an electrode, across a small arc gap (an intensely hot, ionized-gas plasma), through the workpiece, and back to the power source via a ground return. The arc's heat melts both the electrode (in consumable-electrode processes) and the base metal locally, forming a shared molten pool that solidifies into the weld as the arc moves on. What distinguishes one arc process from another is almost entirely how the electrode is fed and consumed, and how the arc and molten metal are shielded from atmospheric oxygen and nitrogen — not this underlying circuit.

02 · Consumable-electrode processes — SMAW, GMAW, FCAW

SMAW (shielded metal arc welding, "stick" welding) uses a flux-coated consumable electrode rod, manually fed as it melts, with the flux coating decomposing to shield the arc and forming a slag layer that must be chipped off afterward — simple, portable equipment, well suited to field work and all positions, at a lower deposition rate than the wire-fed processes. GMAW (gas metal arc welding, "MIG") continuously feeds a bare wire electrode through a torch, shielded by an externally supplied gas (commonly argon, CO₂, or a mixture) — faster, cleaner (no slag), and more easily automated, but more sensitive to wind and drafts blowing the shielding gas away, making it primarily a shop process. FCAW (flux-cored arc welding) feeds a tubular wire filled with flux, either self-shielding (the flux alone providing shielding, tolerant of wind, suited to field and structural work) or gas-shielded (higher quality, shop-oriented) — a genuine hybrid combining SMAW's field robustness with GMAW's continuous-wire productivity.

03 · Non-consumable and submerged processes — GTAW, SAW

GTAW (gas tungsten arc welding, "TIG") uses a non-consumable tungsten electrode, with filler metal (if used) added separately by hand, shielded by inert gas — slow, requiring real operator skill, but producing the highest quality, cleanest welds of any common arc process, standard for thin sections, precision work, and demanding materials (stainless steel, aluminum, exotic alloys). SAW (submerged arc welding) feeds a continuous bare wire electrode beneath a blanket of granular flux that fully covers and shields the arc (the arc is genuinely not visible during welding) — very high deposition rates and deep penetration, but restricted to flat or near-flat positions by the flux blanket's dependence on gravity, standard for long, straight, heavy welds such as structural plate and large-diameter pipe seams.

04 · Heat input and typical defects

Heat input quantifies energy delivered per unit length of weld: \[ HI = \frac{\eta VI}{v}, \] where \(V\) is arc voltage, \(I\) is welding current, \(v\) is travel speed, and \(\eta\) is arc efficiency (process-dependent, commonly \(0.7\)–\(0.9\) for the processes above). Too little heat input risks lack of fusion (the weld metal fails to properly bond to the base metal) and porosity (trapped gas, often from inadequate shielding or moisture); too much heat input risks excessive grain growth and distortion, and in some materials, unwanted metallurgical changes in the heat-affected zone. Travel speed is often the most immediately controllable lever — slowing down at fixed voltage and current increases heat input roughly in inverse proportion, which is exactly why an inconsistent travel speed is one of the most common sources of inconsistent weld quality along a single joint.

Key relation — heat input

\[ HI = \frac{\eta VI}{v} \]

Slower travel at the same voltage/current means proportionally higher heat input.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Computing heat input

Problem. A GMAW weld runs at 24 V, 180 A, travel speed 4 mm/s, arc efficiency 0.8. Find the heat input.

Solution. \[ HI=\frac{\eta VI}{v}=\frac{0.8\times24\times180}{4}=864\ \text{J/mm} \approx 0.86\ \text{kJ/mm}. \]

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Travel speed and heat input

Problem. For the weld in the previous problem, the welder slows travel speed to 2.5 mm/s while holding voltage and current constant. Find the new heat input and the percentage increase.

Solution. \[ HI=\frac{0.8\times24\times180}{2.5}\approx1382\ \text{J/mm}. \] This is a 60% increase (1382/864 ≈ 1.60) from slowing travel speed alone — heat input rises in inverse proportion to travel speed at fixed voltage and current.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Choosing a process for field structural work

Problem. A structural steel fabrication job must be welded outdoors, in a windy site, in multiple positions including overhead. Recommend a process from 2–3 and explain your reasoning against the alternatives.

Solution. Self-shielded FCAW (or SMAW) fits best. GMAW's externally supplied shielding gas is easily disrupted by wind, ruling it out outdoors. GTAW is far too slow for structural production work. SAW is restricted to flat or near-flat positions by its gravity-dependent flux blanket, ruling out overhead work entirely. Self-shielded FCAW combines wind tolerance with continuous-wire productivity across all positions, which SMAW alone cannot match in deposition rate.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A structural fabrication floor running FCAW for field erection welds and GMAW for shop sub-assembly welds — rather than one process everywhere — is applying 2's wind-sensitivity and productivity trade-offs as a matter of routine process planning, not an ad-hoc daily decision left to whichever welder is available.

A welding inspector reviewing a joint with visible lack-of-fusion indications checks the logged travel speed and amperage against the procedure's specified heat input range before assuming a training or technique problem — per 4, a genuinely correct technique executed at the wrong travel speed produces exactly this defect, and the heat input arithmetic is often the fastest way to confirm or rule out that specific cause.
