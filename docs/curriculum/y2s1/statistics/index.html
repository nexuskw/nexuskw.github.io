STS 206 · YEAR 2 · SEMESTER 1

Engineering Statistics

Data with judgment: probability, distributions, estimation, hypothesis tests, and regression — the quiet prerequisite under SPC, Weibull analysis, measurement uncertainty, and every reliability claim you will ever audit.

11 lessons11 of 11 at full depth11 interactive quizzes
Start lesson 01 Full curriculum

What you'll learn

✓Location, spread, and shape; histograms and box plots that don't lie.
✓Sample spaces, independence, and conditional probability.
✓Binomial and Poisson models with their assumptions audited.
✓The normal distribution and its z-arithmetic; lognormal for repair times; exponential for memoryless failure.
✓Why averages behave better than individuals.
✓Point estimates and confidence intervals for means and proportions.

Syllabus

01

Describing dataFull lesson

Location, spread, and shape; histograms and box plots that don't lie. Outliers treated as evidence, not embarrassment. Plant data pathologies previewed.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.05

Checkpoint questions
  • Mean versus median on skewed repair times: which tells the truer story?
  • What does a box plot show that a bar of averages hides?
02

Probability rulesFull lesson

Sample spaces, independence, and conditional probability. Bayes' theorem worked on an inspection-alarm example. The base-rate trap that fools test results everywhere.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.05

Checkpoint questions
  • Bayes in one line: why do false alarms dominate rare-event detection?
  • Independence: what does assuming it wrongly do to risk estimates?
03

Discrete distributionsFull lesson

Binomial and Poisson models with their assumptions audited. Defect counts and rare-event arrivals. When the Poisson approximation earns its keep.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.05

Checkpoint questions
  • When is Poisson the right model for failure counts?
  • What assumptions does a binomial defect model make?
04

Continuous distributionsFull lesson

The normal distribution and its z-arithmetic; lognormal for repair times; exponential for memoryless failure. Choosing a model from data shape and physics. The exponential's constant hazard flagged for Reliability I.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.05

Checkpoint questions
  • Why are repair times often lognormal?
  • What does the exponential's memoryless property claim — and when is it false?
05

Sampling distributions and the CLTFull lesson

Why averages behave better than individuals. Standard error and the central limit theorem demonstrated numerically. The statistical footing under control charts.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.05

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do averages of n samples scatter less by √n?
  • What does the CLT let you assume about sample means?
06

Estimation and confidence intervalsFull lesson

Point estimates and confidence intervals for means and proportions. Interpretation without the classic misreadings. Sample-size arithmetic before the test campaign, not after.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.05

Checkpoint questions
  • Interpret a 95% confidence interval without the classic misreading.
  • Why compute sample size before the test campaign?
07

Hypothesis testingFull lesson

Null logic, p-values, and both error types with plant-decision stakes. One- and two-sample t-tests. 'Statistically significant' versus 'operationally meaningful' — a distinction with budget consequences.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.05

Checkpoint questions
  • What do Type I and Type II errors cost in a plant decision?
  • Statistically significant but operationally meaningless: give an example.
08

RegressionFull lesson

Least squares fitting with residual diagnostics. Interpretation of slope, intercept, and R² without overclaiming. Degradation trends and calibration curves as the working examples.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.05

Checkpoint questions
  • What pattern in residuals invalidates a straight-line fit?
  • Why does R² alone never justify a model?
09

Design of experiments first contactFull lesson

One factor at a time versus factorial thinking. Main effects and interactions read from a 2² experiment. The efficiency argument that Lean Six Sigma will later monetize.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.05

Checkpoint questions
  • What is an interaction, and why does one-factor-at-a-time miss it?
  • Read main effects from a 2² experiment table.
10

Reliability statistics previewFull lesson

Censored data — the defining feature of failure records. Hazard and survival functions introduced gently. The bridge lesson into Weibull analysis in Reliability I.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.05

Checkpoint questions
  • What is censored data, and why do failure logs teem with it?
  • Hazard versus failure rate: what does each function describe?
11

Statistical malpractice and honest reportingFull lesson

P-hacking, survivorship bias, and cherry-picked baselines — recognized and named. Uncertainty statements as professional obligation. How to present data to management without lying by chart.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.05

Checkpoint questions
  • Name two chart crimes that flatter a KPI.
  • What must an honest uncertainty statement accompany?
