Hypothesis testing

01 · Testing a claim against data

A hypothesis test is a formal procedure for deciding whether data are consistent with a claim. It sets up two competing statements: the null hypothesis \( H_0 \), the default or "no effect" claim (a process is on target, a new method is no better), and the alternative \( H_1 \), what we would conclude if the null fails. The logic is that of proof by contradiction: assume \( H_0 \), compute how surprising the data would be under it, and reject \( H_0 \) only if the data are surprising enough. The test never proves the null — it only fails, or fails to fail, to reject it.

02 · The test statistic and the p-value

Surprise is measured by a test statistic — for a mean, \( z=\dfrac{\bar x-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt n} \), the number of standard errors the sample mean sits from the null value \( \mu_0 \). Testing \( \mu_0=50 \) with \( \bar x=52 \), \( \sigma=10 \), \( n=25 \) (so \( \text{SE}=2 \)) gives \( z=(52-50)/2=1 \). The p-value is the probability, if \( H_0 \) were true, of a result at least this extreme: here a two-tailed \( p\approx0.317 \), so a result this far out happens about a third of the time by chance alone. That is not surprising, so \( H_0 \) is not rejected.

03 · Significance, errors, and their trade-off

The decision threshold is the significance level \( \alpha \), commonly 0.05: reject \( H_0 \) when \( p<\alpha \). Two errors are possible. A Type I error rejects a true null (a false alarm), with probability \( \alpha \); a Type II error fails to reject a false null (a missed effect), with probability \( \beta \), and \( 1-\beta \) is the test's power. The two trade off: lowering \( \alpha \) to avoid false alarms raises \( \beta \) and misses real effects, and the only way to reduce both at once is more data. Choosing \( \alpha \) is therefore an engineering judgement about which error is costlier — a false alarm that halts a line, or a missed defect that ships.

04 · The hypothesis-testing working method

State \( H_0 \) and \( H_1 \) before seeing the data; choose \( \alpha \) by the cost of each error; compute the test statistic and its p-value; reject or retain \( H_0 \), and report the p-value, not just the verdict. Two disciplines guard against abuse. Fix the hypotheses and \( \alpha \) in advance — choosing them after seeing the data (p-hacking) manufactures false significance, the malpractice of Lesson 11. And separate statistical from practical significance: with a huge sample even a trivial, meaningless difference becomes "significant," so always report the effect size and its confidence interval alongside the p-value. A test answers "is the effect real?"; the interval answers "is it big enough to matter?" — and engineers need both. There is also a direct bridge between the two: a two-sided test at level \( \alpha \) rejects \( H_0 \) exactly when the null value falls outside the \( (1-\alpha) \) confidence interval, so the interval already contains the test's verdict plus the effect size the bare p-value hides — a good reason to report intervals by default.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Compute a z-test statistic

Problem. Test \( \mu_0=50 \) with \( \bar x=52 \), \( \sigma=10 \), \( n=25 \). Find the test statistic.

Solution. \( \text{SE}=10/\sqrt{25}=2 \); \( z=(52-50)/2=1 \). Answer: \(z=1\).

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Decision from the p-value

Problem. The two-tailed p-value for \(z=1\) is about 0.317. At \( \alpha=0.05 \), what is the decision?

Solution. \( p=0.317>0.05 \), so do not reject \( H_0 \) — the data are consistent with \( \mu=50 \). Answer: retain \(H_0\); the difference is not significant.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Identify the errors

Problem. In that test, what would a Type I and a Type II error mean?

Solution. Type I: rejecting \(H_0\) when the mean really is 50 (false alarm). Type II: retaining \(H_0\) when the mean has truly shifted (missed effect). Answer: false alarm vs missed real shift.
