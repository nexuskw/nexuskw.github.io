Fluid Mechanics

Pressure, flow, and loss: statics to Bernoulli to real pipe networks with pumps attached — taught so that a pump curve, a system curve, and an NPSH margin become native reading.

Syllabus

01

Fluid properties

Density, viscosity, vapor pressure, surface tension — each tied to a plant consequence. Newtonian behavior and where slurries and polymers depart from it. Vapor pressure flagged early: cavitation is coming.

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 2; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does vapor pressure, not boiling point, decide cavitation risk?
  • Newtonian or not: what does the label actually claim?
02

Pressure and fluid statics

Pressure fields, manometry, and forces on submerged surfaces. Gauge versus absolute pressure — the eternal instrumentation trap. Tank and dam wall calculations done cleanly.

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 3; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

Checkpoint questions
  • Gauge versus absolute: which does a transmitter read, and when does it bite?
  • Why does force on a submerged gate act below its centroid?
03

Flow description, Reynolds number, and continuity

Streamlines and flow regimes; the Reynolds number as the master dimensionless group, with laminar and turbulent flow distinguished physically and transition located in real pipe sizes. Control-volume mass balances, steady and unsteady, and velocity–area trades in ducts and nozzles. The one-line continuity equation that gating design already used in MFG 105.

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 4–5, 8; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

Checkpoint questions
  • Compute-style: what Reynolds number regime is water at 2 m/s in a 100 mm pipe?
  • Halve a duct's area at constant flow: what must velocity do?
04

Bernoulli, energy equation, pipe flow, and pump curvesCore 60 · in production

Bernoulli derived with its assumptions on the table, then upgraded to the energy equation with head loss and pump head. Major losses via friction factor and the Moody chart; minor losses by coefficient. System curve meets pump curve — the operating point found, the lesson this whole course exists to deliver.

Taught from — CORE 60 · B7 · Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 5, 8, 14; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

Checkpoint questions
  • Static head 12 m plus 6 m friction at design flow: what pump head is needed, and what happens if friction doubles?
  • Why does the pump-curve/system-curve intersection define a stable operating point?
  • In turbulent pipe flow, head loss scales with roughly which power of velocity — and what does that mean for 'just open the valve more'?
05

Momentum analysis

Control-volume momentum for forces on bends, nozzles, and vanes. Thrust blocks and pipe anchor loads. Why elbows in large lines need restraint calculations.

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 6; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does a pipe bend need an anchor even at steady flow?
  • What momentum change does a nozzle impose on its bracket?
06

Dimensional analysis and similarity

Buckingham Pi with engineering judgment. Model testing and scale-up logic. The affinity-law preview: why pump performance scales the way it does.

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 7; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

Checkpoint questions
  • What does Buckingham Pi promise about model tests?
  • Why do pump affinity laws follow from similarity?
07

Laminar and turbulent pipe flow in depth

Fully developed laminar flow derived to the Hagen–Poiseuille result. Turbulent velocity profiles and roughness effects. Entrance lengths and where gauges should not be installed.

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 8; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

Checkpoint questions
  • Where does Hagen–Poiseuille's fourth-power radius dependence come from?
  • Why do entrance lengths matter for meter placement?
08

Piping networks and valves

Series and parallel lines; three-reservoir style problems by iteration. Valve types and their loss and control characters. Reading a pump-and-piping isometric as a resistance network.

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 8; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

Checkpoint questions
  • Two parallel lines: how does flow split, physically?
  • What does a globe valve's high loss coefficient buy you?
09

Flow measurement

Orifice, venturi, rotameter, magnetic, ultrasonic, and Coriolis meters — principle, accuracy, and installation effects. Differential-pressure metering derived from Bernoulli. Straight-run requirements as physics, not superstition.

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 8; instrumentation sections; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does an orifice meter trade permanent loss for cheapness?
  • Which meter survives dirty slurry, and which demands clean fluid?
10

Turbomachinery fundamentals

Euler turbomachine equation derived from angular momentum. Velocity triangles for a centrifugal impeller. Specific speed as the shape parameter of the pump world.

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 14; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

Checkpoint questions
  • What does the Euler equation say a pump impeller does to angular momentum?
  • What does specific speed classify?
11

Compressible flow first contact

Mach number, choking, and converging-nozzle behavior. Relief and blowdown implications. Enough gas dynamics to respect what happens downstream of a PSV.

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, compressible flow chapter; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

Checkpoint questions
  • What chokes at a nozzle throat, and at what Mach number?
  • Why does a PSV's downstream pipe size matter?
  • Çengel & Cimbala, Fluid Mechanics: Fundamentals and Applications (McGraw-Hill)
