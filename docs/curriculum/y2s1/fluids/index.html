Fluid Mechanics

Pressure, flow, and loss: statics to Bernoulli to real pipe networks with pumps attached — taught so that a pump curve, a system curve, and an NPSH margin become native reading.

01

Fluid properties

Density, viscosity, vapor pressure, surface tension — each tied to a plant consequence. Newtonian behavior and where slurries and polymers depart from it. Vapor pressure flagged early: cavitation is coming.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does vapor pressure, not boiling point, decide cavitation risk?
  • Newtonian or not: what does the label actually claim?

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 2; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

02

Pressure and fluid statics

Pressure fields, manometry, and forces on submerged surfaces. Gauge versus absolute pressure — the eternal instrumentation trap. Tank and dam wall calculations done cleanly.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Gauge versus absolute: which does a transmitter read, and when does it bite?
  • Why does force on a submerged gate act below its centroid?

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 3; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

03

Flow description and Reynolds number

Streamlines, flow regimes, and the Reynolds number as the master dimensionless group. Laminar and turbulent flow distinguished physically. Where transition sits in real pipe sizes.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Compute-style: what Reynolds number regime is water at 2 m/s in a 100 mm pipe?
  • Why does transition matter for head-loss scaling?

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 4, 8; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

04

Mass conservation and continuity

Control-volume mass balances, steady and unsteady. Velocity–area trades in ducts and nozzles. The one-line equation that gating design already used in MFG 105.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Halve a duct's area at constant flow: what must velocity do?
  • When is the unsteady term in the mass balance unavoidable?

Taught from — Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 5; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative

05

Bernoulli, energy equation, pipe flow, and pump curvesCore 60 · in production

Bernoulli derived with its assumptions on the table, then upgraded to the energy equation with head loss and pump head. Major losses via friction factor and the Moody chart; minor losses by coefficient. System curve meets pump curve — the operating point found, the lesson this whole course exists to deliver.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Static head 12 m plus 6 m friction at design flow: what pump head is needed, and what happens if friction doubles?
  • Why does the pump-curve/system-curve intersection define a stable operating point?
  • In turbulent pipe flow, head loss scales with roughly which power of velocity — and what does that mean for 'just open the valve more'?

Taught from — CORE 60 · B7 · Çengel & Cimbala, Ch. 5, 8, 14; video: LearnChemE (CU Boulder) — fluids In Arabic — alternative
