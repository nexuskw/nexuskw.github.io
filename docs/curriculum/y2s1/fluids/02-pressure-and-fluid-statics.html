Pressure and fluid statics

01 · Pressure and the hydrostatic law

In a fluid at rest there is no shear, so the only stress is pressure, acting equally in all directions at a point (Pascal's principle). The one equation that governs a static fluid is the hydrostatic law: pressure increases with depth as \( \dfrac{dp}{dz}=-\rho g \), which for a constant-density fluid integrates to \( p=p_0+\rho g h \), where \(h\) is depth below the reference surface. Ten metres of water therefore adds \( \rho g h=1000\times9.81\times10=98{,}100 \) Pa \(=98.1\) kPa — close to one atmosphere for every ten metres, a number worth memorising.

02 · Gauge, absolute, and vacuum

Pressure is quoted against a reference. Absolute pressure is measured from a perfect vacuum; gauge pressure is measured from local atmospheric, \( p_{\text{abs}}=p_{\text{atm}}+p_{\text{gauge}} \). A depth reading of 98.1 kPa gauge is \( 101.325+98.1=199.4 \) kPa absolute. Confusing the two is a classic and consequential error — pump suction limits, boiling points, and gas-law calculations all demand absolute pressure, while structural loads on a wall care only about the gauge difference across it. State which you mean, every time.

03 · Manometers and pressure measurement

The hydrostatic law turns a column of liquid into a pressure gauge. In a manometer, a pressure difference is read as a height difference: \( \Delta p=\rho g\,\Delta h \). Because \(\rho\) sits in the formula, a dense fluid gives a short, readable column — mercury (\( \rho=13{,}600 \) kg/m³) reads 50 kPa as only \( \Delta h=50{,}000/(13{,}600\times9.81)\approx0.375 \) m, whereas water would need over five metres. The same law gives the force and centre of pressure on a submerged gate: pressure grows linearly with depth, so the resultant acts below the centroid, a fact that decides how dam gates and tank walls are hinged.

04 · The statics working method

The procedure is uniform. Choose a reference surface where the pressure is known (often the free surface at atmospheric). Move to the point of interest along a connected body of the same fluid, adding \( \rho g h \) for every descent and subtracting it for every rise. Switch \(\rho\) whenever the fluid changes (oil over water, gas over liquid). Keep gauge and absolute straight, and let units confirm the arithmetic — \( \rho g h \) must land in pascals. This "walk the column" method solves every manometer and submerged-surface problem without new equations, and it is the same energy-accounting habit that Bernoulli's equation extends to moving fluids in the next lessons. Two extensions of the static law are worth flagging now. Buoyancy — Archimedes' principle — is nothing more than the net of hydrostatic pressure over a submerged body: the upward force equals the weight of displaced fluid, \( F_B=\rho g V_{\text{disp}} \), which is why steel ships float and why a partly filled tank's contents shift the centre of buoyancy. And the force on a plane submerged surface is the pressure at its centroid times its area, applied at the centre of pressure below the centroid — the calculation behind every tank wall, lock gate, and dam face. Both follow from \( p=p_0+\rho g h \) with no new physics, only careful bookkeeping of where the pressure acts.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Pressure at depth

Problem. Find the gauge pressure 10 m below the surface of water (\( \rho=1000 \) kg/m³).

Solution. \( p=\rho g h=1000\times9.81\times10=98{,}100 \) Pa \(=98.1\) kPa. Answer: 98.1 kPa gauge.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Gauge to absolute

Problem. Convert that reading to absolute pressure (atmospheric \(=101.325\) kPa).

Solution. \( p_{abs}=101.325+98.1=199.4 \) kPa. Answer: \(\approx199.4\) kPa absolute.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Mercury manometer height

Problem. What mercury column (\( \rho=13{,}600 \) kg/m³) balances a 50-kPa pressure difference?

Solution. \( \Delta h=\Delta p/(\rho g)=50{,}000/(13{,}600\times9.81)\approx0.375 \) m. Answer: \(\approx0.375\) m (375 mm Hg).

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

In a static fluid, pressure varies with depth as:

Hydrostatic law; pressure rises linearly with depth. It is constant only along a horizontal plane.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Absolute pressure equals:

\( p_{abs}=p_{atm}+p_{gauge} \). Cavitation and gas-law work need absolute.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A denser manometer fluid gives, for the same \( \Delta p \):

\( \Delta h=\Delta p/(\rho g) \); larger \(\rho\) means smaller \(\Delta h\) — why mercury is used.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

Pascal's principle states that pressure at a point in a static fluid:

With no shear at rest, pressure is isotropic at a point.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

The resultant force on a vertical submerged wall acts:

Pressure grows with depth, so the pressure prism's centroid — the line of action — sits below the area centroid.
