01 · What a fluid is, and the properties that matter

A fluid is a substance that cannot resist a shear stress at rest — it deforms continuously under any tangential force, however small. That single fact separates fluid mechanics from solid mechanics and forces a different toolkit. Before any flow is analysed, four bulk properties must be pinned down. Density \( \rho \) (mass per unit volume, kg/m³) sets inertia and weight; specific weight \( \gamma=\rho g \) converts it to force per volume; specific gravity \( SG=\rho/\rho_{\text{water}} \) is the dimensionless ratio to water at 4°C; and viscosity \( \mu \) measures a fluid's resistance to shearing. Water has \( \rho\approx1000 \) kg/m³, so \( \gamma\approx9810 \) N/m³; an oil of \( \rho=850 \) has \( SG=0.85 \).

02 · Viscosity, defined by Newton's law

Viscosity is defined operationally. Place a fluid between two plates and slide the top one: the fluid develops a velocity gradient \( du/dy \), and the shear stress needed is \( \tau=\mu\,\dfrac{du}{dy} \) — Newton's law of viscosity. Fluids obeying it with constant \( \mu \) are Newtonian (water, air, light oils); those whose apparent viscosity changes with shear rate (paint, slurry, blood) are non-Newtonian. For \( \mu=0.1 \) Pa·s and a gradient of \( 50\ \text{s}^{-1} \), the shear stress is \( \tau=0.1\times50=5 \) Pa. Viscosity is the property that makes real flow lose energy; an idealised inviscid fluid (\( \mu=0 \)) would flow forever without friction, a useful fiction for some problems and a dangerous one for others.

03 · Kinematic viscosity and the role of temperature

Two viscosities appear in practice. The dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) (Pa·s) enters force balances; the kinematic viscosity \( \nu=\mu/\rho \) (m²/s) enters the Reynolds number and diffusion of momentum. For the oil above, \( \nu=0.1/850\approx1.18\times10^{-4} \) m²/s. Temperature dependence runs opposite ways for the two states of matter that concern us: liquids thin dramatically as they warm (their molecules escape each other's attraction), while gases thicken slightly as they warm (faster molecules transfer more momentum across streamlines). An engineer sizing a pump for cold-start oil and hot running oil is really sizing for two different fluids.

04 · The property-first working method

The discipline this lesson installs is to fix properties, with units, before touching a flow equation. Read \( \rho \) at the operating temperature, not a handbook default; compute \( \gamma=\rho g \) only when a weight or pressure is wanted; carry \( \mu \) and \( \nu \) separately and know which one a formula expects. Units are the audit: \( \tau=\mu\,du/dy \) must come out in pascals, and if it does not, a property is in the wrong units. Every later lesson — statics through compressible flow — takes these four numbers as given, so an error here propagates through the whole course. A further property, bulk modulus \( K=-V\,dp/dV \), measures resistance to compression and decides whether a fluid may be treated as incompressible: liquids have enormous \(K\) and are effectively incompressible below high pressures, while gases are compressible whenever speeds approach the speed of sound (Lesson 11). Get the fluid right first — its density, its viscosity, and whether it compresses — then analyse its motion.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Shear stress from Newton's law

Problem. A Newtonian oil has \( \mu=0.1 \) Pa·s. If the velocity gradient near a wall is \( 50\ \text{s}^{-1} \), find the wall shear stress.

Solution. \( \tau=\mu\,du/dy=0.1\times50=5 \) Pa. Answer: 5 Pa. Check: Pa·s × 1/s = Pa, units close.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Kinematic viscosity

Problem. For the same oil with \( \rho=850 \) kg/m³, find the kinematic viscosity.

Solution. \( \nu=\mu/\rho=0.1/850\approx1.18\times10^{-4} \) m²/s. Answer: \(1.18\times10^{-4}\) m²/s.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Specific weight and gravity

Problem. Water has \( \rho=1000 \) kg/m³. Give its specific weight, and the specific gravity of an \(850\)-kg/m³ oil.

Solution. \( \gamma=\rho g=1000\times9.81=9810 \) N/m³; \( SG_{oil}=850/1000=0.85 \). Answer: \( \gamma=9810 \) N/m³, \(SG=0.85\).
