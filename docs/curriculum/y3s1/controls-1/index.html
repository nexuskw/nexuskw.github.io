Control Systems I

Feedback made quantitative: modeling, transfer functions, stability, root locus, frequency response, and the PID controller — the theory under every temperature loop and speed governor in the plant.

11 lessons · 0 in the Core 60 · Primary texts:

  • Ogata-tradition control theory via MIT OCW 2.04A Systems and Controls (ocw.mit.edu)
  • NPTEL: Control Engineering
01

Dynamic modeling of physical systems

Mechanical, thermal, and fluid systems reduced to ODEs. Analogies across domains made explicit. The model as a decision about what to ignore.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Model a heated tank as first order: what are its gain and time constant physically?
  • What did your model deliberately ignore, and when does that bite?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.04A; NPTEL: Control Engineering

02

Laplace transfer functions

From ODE to transfer function; poles and zeros as the system's anatomy. Block-diagram algebra. The s-plane as a map of behavior.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What do poles and zeros each shape in a response?
  • Reduce a feedback loop's block diagram to one transfer function: what's the formula?

Taught from — NPTEL: Control Engineering

03

First- and second-order responses

Time constants, damping ratio, natural frequency, and the step-response vocabulary: rise time, overshoot, settling. Reading system character from a trend chart. The response shapes every operator recognizes without naming.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Estimate damping ratio from 25% overshoot — roughly what value?
  • Why does rise time trade against overshoot?

Taught from — NPTEL: Control Engineering

04

Closed-loop behavior and steady-state error

Feedback's gifts: disturbance rejection and desensitization — and its price: potential instability. Error constants and system type. Why integral action exists.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does feedback reject disturbances but risk instability?
  • Which system type removes steady-state error to a step?

Taught from — NPTEL: Control Engineering

05

Stability: Routh–Hurwitz

The stability question posed algebraically. Routh arrays and parameter ranges for stability. Gain limits computed before anything oscillates in the field.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What question does Routh–Hurwitz answer without solving roots?
  • Find the gain range for stability: what does the array's first column do?

Taught from — NPTEL: Control Engineering

06

Root locus

Pole migration with gain drawn by rule. Design by pole placement on the locus. Seeing why more gain destabilizes — graphically, permanently.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does increasing gain drive poles toward instability on a locus?
  • What do asymptotes of the root locus tell you?

Taught from — NPTEL: Control Engineering

07

Frequency response and Bode plots

Sinusoidal steady state; magnitude and phase plots constructed from asymptotes. Gain and phase margins as robustness currency. The frequency view that unifies control and vibration analysis.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Read gain and phase margin off a Bode plot: what does each protect?
  • Why does the frequency view unify control and vibration thinking?

Taught from — NPTEL: Control Engineering

08

PID control

P, I, and D actions each derived from a need; tuning by Ziegler–Nichols and by informed iteration. Integral windup and derivative noise handled practically. The controller that runs 90% of industry.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What does integral action remove and what does it risk?
  • Why does derivative action hate noisy sensors?

Taught from — NPTEL: Control Engineering

Practical loop hardware

Actuators, positioners, and valve characteristics inside the loop. Deadband, stiction, and hysteresis as control killers. Loop problems that masquerade as tuning problems.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What do stiction and deadband do to a well-tuned loop?
  • Why can a valve problem masquerade as a tuning problem?

Taught from — NPTEL: Control Engineering

Discrete-time and digital control basics

Sampling in the loop; the PLC/DCS as a discrete controller. Scan-rate effects on stability margins. Enough discrete theory to respect the digital reality.

  • What does sampling too slowly do to stability margins?

Case studies in machine control

Speed, tension, and temperature loops from real machinery contexts — an extruder barrel and a winder analyzed. Loop interaction previewed. Control theory attached to hardware nameplates.
