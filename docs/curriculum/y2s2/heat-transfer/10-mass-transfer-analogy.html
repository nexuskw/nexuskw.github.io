01 · One mathematics, two names

Fick's law of diffusion, \(N''=-D_{AB}\,dC/dx\), has exactly the shape of Fourier's law, \(q''=-k\,dT/dx\) — mass diffusing down a concentration gradient the same way heat diffuses down a temperature gradient. Convective mass transfer carries the same structure forward too: a convective mass transfer coefficient \(h_m\) plays the role \(h\) plays in Newton's law, \(N''=h_m(C_s-C_\infty)\) standing in for \(q''=h(T_s-T_\infty)\).

02 · The heat–mass analogy, and why it works so well for air and water specifically

The Chilton–Colburn analogy connects the two convection coefficients, \(h/h_m\approx\rho c_p\,Le^{2/3}\), where the Lewis number \(Le=\alpha/D_{AB}\) compares thermal diffusivity to mass diffusivity. For water vapor diffusing in air specifically, \(D_{AB}\approx2.5\times10^{-5}\ \text{m}^2\text{/s}\) happens to sit very close to air's own thermal diffusivity — a genuine physical coincidence for this particular pair of substances, not a universal law — giving \(Le\approx1\) and the simplified Lewis relation \(h/h_m\approx\rho c_p\). That coincidence is exactly why wet-bulb thermometry and simple evaporative-cooling estimates work as well, and as simply, as they do for air-water problems, and why the same shortcut should not be assumed for an arbitrary pair of substances.

03 · Why evaporation cools what is left behind

Vaporizing a mass of liquid costs its latent heat of vaporization. With no external heat source feeding that cost, the energy has to come from somewhere — and the only reservoir on hand is the sensible heat of the liquid (and immediate surroundings) left behind, which cools as a direct result. This is the entire physical mechanism behind a wet-bulb thermometer, evaporative cooling pads, and the cooling felt from wet skin in moving air — not a separate "evaporation is cold" fact, but the same energy balance as every other lesson in this course, applied to a phase change instead of a temperature difference.

04 · A worked evaporation estimate, reusing this course's own convection numbers

Lesson 4's flat-plate result gave \(h=20\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) for forced air at a modest speed. Using the Lewis relation with \(\rho_{air}=1.15\ \ \text{kg/m}^3\), \(c_{p,air}=1007\ \text{J/kgK}\): \(h_m=h/(\rho c_p)=0.0173\ \text{m/s}\). Taking a representative (given, not a claimed precise psychrometric lookup) vapor-density difference between a wet surface and hot, dry Gulf air, \(\Delta\rho_v=0.018\ \text{kg/m}^3\): \(N''=h_m\Delta\rho_v=3.11e-04\ \text{kg/m}^2\text{s}=1.12\ \ \text{kg/m}^2\text{hr}\) — a real, order-of-magnitude-plausible desert evaporation rate. Converted through \(h_{fg}\approx2.45\ \text{MJ/kg}\), this is \(q_{evap}=N''h_{fg}=763\ \text{W/m}^2\) of cooling drawn from the surface left behind — a substantial number, on the same order as this course's other worked convective fluxes, and the same "sensible heat pays for the latent heat" bookkeeping 3 describes.

Key relation — the Lewis relation, air-water only

\[ \frac{h}{h_m}\approx\rho c_p \qquad (Le\approx1\text{ for water vapor in air}) \]

A shortcut earned by a specific numerical coincidence — check \(Le\) before reusing it for any other fluid pair.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

The worked evaporation estimate

Problem. Reusing Lesson 4's \(h=20\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), with \(\rho_{air}=1.15\ \text{kg/m}^3\), \(c_{p,air}=1007\ \text{J/kgK}\), and a representative \(\Delta\rho_v=0.018\ \text{kg/m}^3\), find \(h_m\), the evaporation flux \(N''\), and the equivalent cooling flux \(q_{evap}\) (\(h_{fg}\approx2.45\ \text{MJ/kg}\)).

Solution. \(h_m=h/(\rho c_p)=0.0173\ \text{m/s}\). \(N''=h_m\Delta\rho_v=3.11e-04\ \text{kg/m}^2\text{s}\). \(q_{evap}=N''h_{fg}=763\ \text{W/m}^2\).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

The same estimate at Lesson 6's much lower h

Problem. Repeat the estimate using Lesson 6's natural-convection result instead, \(h=3.99\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), same air properties and \(\Delta\rho_v\). Find the new \(h_m\), \(N''\), and \(q_{evap}\).

Solution. \(h_m=h/(\rho c_p)=0.00345\ \text{m/s}\). \(N''=h_m\Delta\rho_v=6.20e-05\ \text{kg/m}^2\text{s}\). \(q_{evap}=N''h_{fg}=152\ \text{W/m}^2\) — about \(5\times\) smaller than the forced-air case, since $h_m$ scales directly with the (much lower) natural-convection h.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Mass evaporated from a real surface

Problem. At 4's flux, how much water evaporates from a 2 m² open surface over 1 hour?

Solution. \(m=N''\times A\times t=3.11e-04\times2\times3600=2.24\ \text{kg}\) over the hour.

Problem 4 of 11 · multiple choice

Fick's law and Fourier's law share which structural relationship?

Both are linear diffusion laws of identical form, \(-(\text{property})\times(\text{gradient})\) — heat and mass diffuse by the same mathematics.

Problem 5 of 11 · multiple choice

The Lewis number \(Le=\alpha/D_{AB}\) compares which two quantities?

Le compares how fast heat diffuses (α) to how fast mass diffuses (\(D_{AB}\)) — when the two rates are similar, \(Le\approx1\).

Problem 6 of 11 · multiple choice

The simplified Lewis relation \(h/h_m\approx\rho c_p\) is a reliable shortcut specifically because:

This is a property coincidence specific to the air-water pair; 2 is explicit that it should not be assumed to transfer to other fluid combinations.

Problem 7 of 11 · multiple choice

Physically, why does evaporation cool the liquid left behind, with no external heat source present?

With no external heat input, the energy balance has nowhere else to draw the latent heat from except the sensible heat of what remains — this is the literal mechanism, not a figure of speech.

Problem 8 of 11 · multiple choice

In this lesson's worked estimate, moving from Lesson 4's forced-air \(h=20\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) to Lesson 6's natural-convection \(h\approx4\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) changes the evaporation flux by:

\(h_m=h/(\rho c_p)\) is directly proportional to h; a lower h gives a proportionally lower \(h_m\) and evaporation flux, confirmed in this lesson's second solve problem.

Problem 9 of 11 · multiple choice

A wet-bulb thermometer reads a lower temperature than a dry-bulb thermometer in the same air primarily because:

This is exactly 3's mechanism in its most common instrument form — the wick cools itself by evaporating into the surrounding air.

Problem 11 of 11 · multiple choice

The worked example and its second solve problem explicitly reuse Lesson 4's forced-air h and Lesson 6's natural-convection h, tying the analogy back to numbers already computed and verified earlier in this course.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

An engineer estimating water loss from an open process-water surface or a cooling pond in Gulf summer heat uses the Lewis relation to convert an already-known or already-estimated h into \(h_m\), rather than deriving a mass-transfer correlation from scratch — per 4, this shortcut is earned specifically by \(Le\approx1\) for air and water vapor, and should not be assumed to carry over unchanged to a different fluid pair.

A technician noticing that a wetted evaporative cooling pad, or curing concrete kept damp, stays noticeably cooler than the surrounding dry air is watching the same mechanism as a wet-bulb thermometer — per 3, the energy leaving as vapor is drawn directly from the remaining liquid's own sensible heat, a real, quantifiable effect rather than a vague "evaporation feels cool" impression.
