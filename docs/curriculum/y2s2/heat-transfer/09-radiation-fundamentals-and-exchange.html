Lecture

01 · Blackbody laws, emissivity, and view factors

An ideal blackbody emits \(E_b=\sigma T^4\); a real gray surface emits \(E=\varepsilon\sigma T^4\), with emissivity \(\varepsilon\) (0 to 1) discounting it from the ideal. Between two surfaces, the view factor \(F_{12}\) is the fraction of radiation leaving surface 1 that lands directly on surface 2. Two bookkeeping rules make view factors tractable: reciprocity, \(A_1F_{12}=A_2F_{21}\), and the summation rule, \(\sum_j F_{1j}=1\) for a complete enclosure — every bit of radiation leaving a surface inside a closed enclosure has to land somewhere.

02 · The network method, shields, and combined losses

For two gray surfaces exchanging radiation, the net rate is one more resistance-network calculation: \(q_{12}=\sigma(T_1^4-T_2^4)/R_{tot}\), with \(R_{tot}=\dfrac{1-\varepsilon_1}{\varepsilon_1A_1}+\dfrac{1}{A_1F_{12}}+ \dfrac{1-\varepsilon_2}{\varepsilon_2A_2}\) — two surface resistances bracketing one space resistance, the exact structural echo of Lesson 1's conduction chain, now driven by \(T^4\) differences instead of \(T\). Inserting a radiation shield between two surfaces adds two more surface resistances in series (one per shield face); when every surface and shield face shares the same emissivity, \(N\) identical shields divide the unshielded heat rate by exactly \(N+1\) — verified directly from the resistances themselves, not merely quoted. Combining convection and radiation losses from one surface is instead a parallel combination: linearizing radiation into an equivalent coefficient \(h_{rad}=\varepsilon\sigma(T_s+T_{surr})(T_s^2+T_{surr}^2)\) lets total loss be written as one Newton's-law-style term, \(h_{combined}=h_{conv}+h_{rad}\).

03 · The energy-audit and thermography case

A bare, uninsulated hot pipe or vessel loses heat by convection and radiation simultaneously, and radiation is routinely the larger of the two at typical process temperatures — not a minor correction. An audit or a survey that estimates loss from convection alone, "because that's what comes to mind first," can understate the real opportunity by close to half, exactly the number 4 below computes directly rather than assumes.

04 · A worked bare pipe, oxidized and then polished

A bare steel pipe, \(D=10\ \text{cm}\), runs at \(T_s=150\)°C in \(T_\infty=35\)°C surroundings, oxidized (\(\varepsilon=0.8\)), with an estimated \(h_{conv}=10\ \ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) (Lesson 6's methods, at this modest \(\Delta T\)). \(h_{rad}=\varepsilon\sigma(T_s+T_\infty)(T_s^2+T_\infty^2)=9.09\ \ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — comparable to \(h_{conv}\) itself. \(h_{combined}=19.09\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), \(q''=h_{combined}\Delta T=2195\ \text{W/m}^2\), or \(q'=q''\pi D=689.7\ \text{W/m}\) of bare pipe — with radiation alone carrying 48% of the total. Polishing or foil-wrapping the same pipe to \(\varepsilon=0.1\) drops \(h_{rad}\) to 1.14, radiation's share to 10%, and the total loss to \(q'=402.3\ \text{W/m}\) — a 42% cut in total heat loss from changing surface finish alone, no insulation added at all.

Key relation — two-surface radiation network

\[ q_{12}=\frac{\sigma(T_1^4-T_2^4)}{\dfrac{1-\varepsilon_1}{\varepsilon_1A_1}+ \dfrac{1}{A_1F_{12}}+\dfrac{1-\varepsilon_2}{\varepsilon_2A_2}} \]

The same series-resistance idea as every other lesson in this course, one more time.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

The worked bare pipe, oxidized

Problem. A bare steel pipe, \(D=10\ \text{cm}\), runs at \(T_s=150\)°C in \(T_\infty=35\)°C surroundings, \(\varepsilon=0.8\), \(h_{conv}=10\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\). Find \(h_{rad}\), \(h_{combined}\), and the loss per metre of pipe.

Solution. \(h_{rad}=\varepsilon\sigma(T_s+T_\infty)(T_s^2+T_\infty^2)=9.09\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\). \(h_{combined}=19.09\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\). \(q''=2195\ \text{W/m}^2\), \(q'=q''\pi D=689.7\ \text{W/m}\).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

The same pipe, polished

Problem. The same pipe is polished or foil-wrapped to \(\varepsilon=0.1\), all else unchanged. Find the new loss per metre, and the percent reduction from the oxidized case.

Solution. \(h_{rad}=1.14\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), \(h_{combined}=11.14\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), \(q'=402.3\ \text{W/m}\), a \((1-402.3/689.7)\times100=42\%\) drop in total loss from surface finish alone.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Radiation shields, verified from the network

Problem. Two large parallel surfaces at \(T_1=400\ \text{K}\), \(T_2=300\ \text{K}\), all surfaces \(\varepsilon=0.5\), exchange heat with no shield. Insert 3 identical (\(\varepsilon=0.5\)) shields between them. Find the ratio of shielded to unshielded heat rate, computed from the resistances directly.

Solution. No shield: \(R_0=(1-0.5)/0.5+1+(1-0.5)/0.5=3\), \(q_0=\sigma(T_1^4-T_2^4)/R_0=330.75\ \text{W/m}^2\). With 3 shields, each contributing two identical surface resistances plus one more space resistance, the network totals \(R_3=4\times R_0=12\), so \(q_3=\sigma(T_1^4-T_2^4)/R_3=82.69\ \text{W/m}^2\), and \(q_3/q_0=0.25=1/(3+1)\) exactly, confirming the general \(N+1\) rule from the network itself rather than assuming it.

Problem 4 of 11 · multiple choice

Reciprocity for view factors, \(A_1F_{12}=A_2F_{21}\), expresses which physical fact?

Reciprocity is a geometric bookkeeping identity, independent of emissivity — it holds for any two surfaces' view factors regardless of material.

Problem 5 of 11 · multiple choice

The summation rule \(\sum_j F_{1j}=1\) applies to which situation?

Inside a closed enclosure, radiation leaving a surface has nowhere to go but onto the other enclosure surfaces (including possibly itself), summing to exactly 1.

Problem 6 of 11 · multiple choice

Combining convection and radiation losses from one surface, using \(h_{combined}=h_{conv}+h_{rad}\), is structurally which kind of combination?

Both modes act on the same surface and the same ΔT simultaneously, in parallel — adding their coefficients directly, unlike the series addition used for layers encountered one after another.

Problem 7 of 11 · multiple choice

N identical radiation shields (same emissivity as the two surfaces) inserted between them divide the unshielded heat rate by:

Each shield adds one more identical resistance unit to the series network; N identical shields give N+1 identical resistance units total, dividing q by N+1 — verified directly from the resistances in this lesson's third solve problem.

Problem 8 of 11 · multiple choice

In this lesson's bare-pipe example, radiation carries roughly what share of the total loss from the oxidized (\(\varepsilon=0.8\)) surface?

\(h_{rad}/h_{combined}=48\%\) — genuinely comparable to convection's share, exactly the field-relevant point 3 makes.

Problem 9 of 11 · multiple choice

Polishing or foil-wrapping the same bare pipe surface (lowering ε) primarily reduces loss through which mechanism?

\(h_{rad}\propto\varepsilon\) directly; lowering emissivity cuts radiative loss sharply while leaving convection essentially untouched, as this lesson's second solve problem shows numerically.

Problem 10 of 11 · multiple choice

An energy audit tallying only convective loss from a bare, uninsulated hot pipe risks:

This lesson's own worked numbers show radiation carrying nearly half the total loss — an audit ignoring it misses a comparable-sized opportunity, not a rounding error.

Problem 11 of 11 · multiple choice

Two identical surfaces exchange radiation. Doubling the absolute temperature of the hotter surface (surroundings fixed) changes the net radiative exchange by:

The fourth-power dependence in \(T_1^4-T_2^4\) makes radiative exchange far more sensitive to absolute temperature than any linear rate law — doubling \(T_1\) can raise \(T_1^4\) sixteen-fold on its own.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A facilities engineer surveying bare, uninsulated hot piping tallies both convection and radiation loss, not convection alone — per 4's own numbers, radiation can carry close to half of a bare hot pipe's total loss at typical process temperatures, and an audit that counts convection only understates the real energy-saving opportunity from insulating by nearly double.

A reliability engineer specifying a radiation shield between a hot process component and nearby instrumentation sizes the number of shields from the network method, not a guess — per 2, each identical shield divides the unshielded heat rate by exactly one more, a specific number worth checking against the actual protection required rather than assuming "a shield" is enough regardless of how many are fitted.
