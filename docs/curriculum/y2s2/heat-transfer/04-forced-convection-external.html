Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What do Nusselt, Reynolds, and Prandtl each encode?
  • Why does a correlation's validity range matter?

01 · Two boundary layers, and the laminar–turbulent split

A fluid moving past a solid surface develops a velocity boundary layer: zero velocity at the wall (no-slip), rising to the free-stream value across a thin layer of thickness \(\delta\). If the surface and fluid are at different temperatures, a parallel thermal boundary layer develops the same way, wall temperature to free-stream temperature across thickness \(\delta_t\). Close to a leading edge this layer is smooth and layered — laminar; far enough along, it becomes chaotic and well-mixed except for a thin viscous sublayer — turbulent. For a flat plate the transition sits near \(Re_{x,crit}\approx5\times10^5\), a Reynolds number, not a fixed distance — it depends on velocity and fluid as much as on position.

02 · The Nu–Re–Pr grammar

The Nusselt number \(Nu=hL/k\) is simply a dimensionless convection coefficient — how much better convection moves heat across length scale \(L\) than pure conduction through a stationary layer of the same fluid would. Forced-convection correlations fit \(Nu\) as a power law in the Reynolds number \(Re=VL/\nu\) (inertial versus viscous forces) and the Prandtl number \(Pr=\nu/\alpha\) (momentum versus thermal diffusivity — physically, how the velocity and thermal boundary layers compare in thickness): \(Nu=C\,Re^m Pr^n\). For laminar flow averaged over a flat plate, \(Nu_L=0.664\,Re_L^{0.5}Pr^{1/3}\) (valid for \(Pr\gtrsim0.6\)). Cylinders in crossflow and tube banks follow the same structural form with different constants and, for banks, a row-count correction — always fitted to data over a stated range, never derived from first principles the way the flat-plate boundary-layer result nearly is.

03 · Using a correlation without believing it past its range

Every correlation in 2 carries fine print: a Reynolds range, a Prandtl range, a specific geometry it was fitted to. Applying a flat-plate correlation to a cylinder in crossflow is not a rounding error — it is a category error, since the boundary-layer physics genuinely differs (a cylinder's boundary layer separates; a flat plate's does not). Applying a correlation validated for \(Pr\gtrsim0.6\) to a very low-\(Pr\) liquid metal is the same mistake in a quieter disguise: the fitted exponent on \(Pr\) was never tested down there, and there is no guarantee it still holds.

04 · A worked flat plate

Air at \(V=5\ \text{m/s}\) flows over a 200 mm-long flat plate (an electronics board or a heat-sink base), film properties \(\nu=1.6e-05\ \ \text{m}^2\text{/s}\), \(k=0.027\ \text{W/mK}\), \(Pr=0.71\). \(Re_L=VL/\nu=62,500\) — well under \(Re_{crit}\), laminar the whole length. \(Nu_L=0.664\,Re_L^{0.5}Pr^{1/3}=148.1\), giving \(h=Nu_L k/L=19.99\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — a modest number, typical of low-speed forced air, and the reason Lesson 2's fins matter so much on exactly this kind of surface.

Key relation — laminar flat plate

\[ Nu_L=\frac{hL}{k}=0.664\,Re_L^{0.5}Pr^{1/3} \]

Same grammar, different constants, for every other forced-convection correlation this course uses.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

The worked flat plate

Problem. Air at \(V=5\ \text{m/s}\) flows over a 200 mm flat plate, \(\nu=1.6e-05\ \text{m}^2\text{/s}\), \(k=0.027\ \text{W/mK}\), \(Pr=0.71\). Find \(Re_L\), confirm the flow stays laminar, then find \(Nu_L\) and \(h\).

Solution. \(Re_L=VL/\nu=62,500\), under \(Re_{crit}=5\times10^5\): laminar throughout. \(Nu_L=0.664\,Re_L^{0.5}Pr^{1/3}=148.1\). \(h=Nu_L k/L=19.99\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Doubling the approach velocity

Problem. The same plate now sees \(V=10\ \text{m/s}\), all else unchanged. Find the new \(Re_L\), \(Nu_L\), and \(h\), and the ratio to the original h.

Solution. \(Re_L=125,000\) (still laminar). \(Nu_L=0.664\,Re_L^{0.5}Pr^{1/3}=209.4\). \(h=28.27\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\). Ratio \(h_{new}/h_{old}=1.414\), matching \(\sqrt{2}=1.414\) exactly, since \(h\propto Re^{0.5}\propto V^{0.5}\) at fixed L.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Checking the correlation's own range

Problem. The same plate, same \(V=5\ \text{m/s}\) and fluid, is now 2 m long instead of 200 mm. Check whether the laminar flat-plate correlation used in 4 still applies over the full length.

Solution. \(Re_L=VL/\nu=625,000\), which exceeds \(Re_{crit}=5\times10^5\). The boundary layer transitions to turbulent before the trailing edge, so the purely laminar correlation no longer applies over the full 2 m — a mixed laminar-turbulent correlation would be required instead, exactly the range check 3 insists on.

Problem 4 of 11 · multiple choice

Physically, what does the Nusselt number \(Nu=hL/k\) represent?

Nu compares the actual convective h to the conduction that would occur across the same length scale L through the still fluid — a dimensionless convection coefficient.

Problem 5 of 11 · multiple choice

For a flat plate, the laminar-to-turbulent boundary-layer transition is associated with a Reynolds number near:

The classic flat-plate transition Reynolds number is roughly \(5\times10^5\), used directly in this lesson's own worked example.

Problem 6 of 11 · multiple choice

In \(Nu_L=0.664\,Re_L^{0.5}Pr^{1/3}\), doubling the free-stream velocity V (plate length and fluid fixed) multiplies h by approximately:

\(h\propto Re^{0.5}\propto V^{0.5}\); doubling V multiplies h by \(\sqrt{2}\approx1.41\), confirmed numerically in this lesson's second solve problem.

Problem 7 of 11 · multiple choice

Applying a flat-plate forced-convection correlation to a cylinder in crossflow is best described as:

Flow separation around a cylinder is physics a flat-plate correlation was never fitted to; 3 calls this a category error, not a rounding error.

Problem 8 of 11 · multiple choice

A correlation is validated for \(Pr\gtrsim0.6\). Applying it to a liquid metal (\(Pr\ll1\)) risks:

A correlation's exponents are empirical fits over a stated range; extrapolating far outside it — as with liquid metals at very low Pr — has no guarantee of holding.

Problem 9 of 11 · multiple choice

On a laminar flat plate, the boundary-layer thickness \(\delta(x)\) grows approximately as:

The classic laminar result \(\delta/x=5/\sqrt{Re_x}\) gives \(\delta\propto\sqrt{x}\), the shape plotted in this lesson's own diagram.

Problem 10 of 11 · multiple choice

In this lesson's worked example (\(Re_L=62,500\)), the 200 mm plate is:

\(Re_L=62,500\) is well under \(5\times10^5\); the entire plate length stays laminar, consistent with the smooth, unbroken growth curve in this lesson's diagram.

Problem 11 of 11 · multiple choice

The Prandtl number \(Pr=\nu/\alpha\) physically compares which two quantities?

Pr is the ratio of momentum diffusivity (ν) to thermal diffusivity (α); a high Pr fluid has a thermal boundary layer thinner than its velocity boundary layer, and vice versa.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

An engineer sizing air-cooled equipment checks a correlation's stated Reynolds and Prandtl range before trusting its predicted h — per 3, borrowing a flat-plate result for a cylinder, or a correlation's fitted Prandtl exponent for a fluid well outside the range it was tested on, is a category error a spec sheet's fine print would have caught before it ever reached a design calculation.

A technician noticing an air-cooled component running hotter than usual in summer checks whether actual airflow — not just ambient temperature — has quietly dropped, since a fouled filter or a degrading fan reduces \(V\) directly; per 4's own correlation, \(h\propto V^{0.5}\), so even a partial airflow loss produces a real, calculable derating in cooling capacity, not merely a vague seasonal excuse.
