Steady conduction and fins

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why do fins have diminishing returns with length?
  • Fin efficiency versus effectiveness: what does each measure?

01 · Why extend a surface at all

Newton's law of cooling, \(q=hA(T_s-T_\infty)\), leaves exactly three levers once a surface and its fluid are fixed: the convection coefficient \(h\), the temperature difference, and the area \(A\). When \(h\) is the bottleneck — the gas side of an air-cooled enclosure, an un-fanned motor housing, any application where \(h\) sits in the tens of W/m²K rather than the hundreds or thousands a liquid delivers — and the temperature difference is already set by the process, the only lever left is area. A fin is exactly that: manufactured extra area, bought in exchange for material, cost, and — crucially, per 3 — a fin's own internal conduction resistance that eventually limits how much that extra area is actually worth.

02 · The fin equation, derived and solved

An energy balance on a thin differential slice of a straight fin — conduction in one face, conduction out the other, the difference lost sideways by convection — gives, after dividing through by the slice's cross-sectional conduction term, the fin equation \(\dfrac{d^2\theta}{dx^2}-m^2\theta=0\), where \(\theta=T-T_\infty\) is the local temperature excess and \(m=\sqrt{hP/(kA_c)}\) bundles the perimeter \(P\) (convecting surface per unit length), cross-sectional area \(A_c\) (conducting area), conductivity \(k\), and \(h\) into one rate constant. With an adiabatic-tip idealization (the tip's own end-face loss neglected — reasonable once a fin is slender and not too short), the solution is \(\theta/\theta_b=\cosh(m(L-x))/\cosh(mL)\), and the total heat the fin carries away from its base works out to \(q_{fin}=\sqrt{hPkA_c}\,\theta_b\tanh(mL)\).

03 · Efficiency, effectiveness, and where fins stop paying

Fin efficiency \(\eta_{fin}=q_{fin}/q_{max}=\tanh(mL)/mL\) compares the real fin to a hypothetical one held entirely at base temperature — it falls as \(mL\) grows, because a longer or lower-conductivity fin loses more of its temperature excess before the tip. Fin effectiveness \(\varepsilon_{fin}=q_{fin}/(hA_c\theta_b)\) instead compares the fin to the same base area with no fin at all — a fin is worth adding once \(\varepsilon_{fin}\) climbs well past 1 (a common rule of thumb wants it past 2), and since \(\varepsilon_{fin}\) scales with \(\sqrt{k/h}\), fins pay off far more decisively on the low-\(h\) gas side of a system than on a high-\(h\) liquid or boiling side — the same \(k/h\) logic behind why air-cooled equipment bristles with fins and liquid-cooled equipment mostly does not. Length has diminishing returns for a separate reason: \(\tanh(mL)\) saturates toward 1 once \(mL\gtrsim 2.5\text{–}3\), so past that point almost no additional heat leaves per unit of added length — doubling this lesson's own 200 mm fin to 400 mm (4) buys only 9% more heat rate for twice the material, which is where a fin design stops paying for itself.

04 · A worked pin fin

An aluminum pin fin, \(k=200\ \text{W/mK}\), diameter 5 mm, length 200 mm, sits in \(h=15\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) air with a base excess \(\theta_b=60\)°C. \(A_c=\pi r^2=19.63\ \text{mm}^2\), \(P=\pi D=15.71\ \text{mm}\), \(m=\sqrt{hP/(kA_c)}=7.75\ \text{m}^{-1}\), \(mL=1.549\), \(\tanh(mL)=0.914\). Efficiency \(\eta_{fin}=\tanh(mL)/mL=59\%\); the fin delivers \(q_{fin}=\sqrt{hPkA_c}\,\theta_b\tanh(mL)=1.67\ \text{W}\), and against its own bare cross-section, \(\varepsilon_{fin}=q_{fin}/(hA_c\theta_b)=94\) — comfortably past the worth-adding threshold. Doubling the length to 400 mm drops efficiency to 32% and raises \(q_{fin}\) only to 1.82 W, the 9% gain 3 already flagged as the point of diminishing returns.

Key relation — adiabatic-tip fin

\[ \eta_{fin}=\frac{\tanh(mL)}{mL}, \qquad m=\sqrt{\frac{hP}{kA_c}} \]

Every fin-efficiency chart in every textbook is this same ratio, tabulated for other tip conditions and cross-sections.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

A worked pin fin

Problem. An aluminum pin fin (\(k=200\ \text{W/mK}\)), diameter 5 mm, length 200 mm, sits in \(h=15\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) air with base excess \(\theta_b=60\)°C. Find \(m\), \(mL\), the efficiency, and the heat rate \(q_{fin}\).

Solution. \(A_c=\pi(D/2)^2=19.63\ \text{mm}^2\), \(P=\pi D=15.71\ \text{mm}\). \(m=\sqrt{hP/(kA_c)}=7.75\ \text{m}^{-1}\), \(mL=1.549\), \(\tanh(mL)=0.914\). \(\eta_{fin}=\tanh(mL)/mL=59\%\). \(q_{fin}=\sqrt{hPkA_c}\,\theta_b\tanh(mL)=1.67\ \text{W}\).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Doubling the fin length

Problem. The same fin is remade at 400 mm instead of 200 mm, all else unchanged. Find the new efficiency and heat rate, and the percent gain over the original 200 mm fin.

Solution. \(mL=3.098\), \(\tanh(mL)=0.996\), \(\eta_{fin}=32\%\). \(q_{fin}=1.82\ \text{W}\), only \((1.82/1.67-1)\times100=9\%\) more than the 200 mm fin — twice the material for 9% more heat.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

The same fin, moved to a liquid

Problem. The original 200 mm fin (4) is instead installed where \(h=200\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) (a liquid rather than air). Find the new \(q_{fin}\) and effectiveness \(\varepsilon_{fin}\), and compare \(\varepsilon_{fin}\) to the original air-side value.

Solution. \(m=\sqrt{h_{liq}P/(kA_c)}=28.28\ \text{m}^{-1}\), \(mL=5.657\). \(q_{fin}=\sqrt{h_{liq}PkA_c}\,\theta_b\tanh(mL)=6.66\ \text{W}\) — larger in absolute terms than the air-side 1.67 W, because \(h\) itself is larger. But \(\varepsilon_{fin}=q_{fin}/(h_{liq}A_c\theta_b)=28\), down from 94 on the air side — a 3.3× drop in how much this fin is worth relative to the bare area it replaces.

Problem 4 of 11 · multiple choice

A fin is most worth adding when which quantity is the bottleneck in \(q=hA(T_s-T_\infty)\)?

When h is already large (liquid, boiling), a fin's own internal conduction resistance limits its payoff; low h is where extra area helps most.

Problem 5 of 11 · multiple choice

As the fin parameter \(mL\to 0\) (a very short or very conductive fin), what does the efficiency \(\eta_{fin}=\tanh(mL)/mL\) approach?

\(\lim_{x\to0}\tanh(x)/x=1\): a short, highly conductive fin stays almost entirely at base temperature, so it performs almost as well as the all-base-temperature ideal.

Problem 6 of 11 · multiple choice

This lesson's fin has \(m=7.75\ \text{m}^{-1}\). A different fin with a LARGER \(m\) (thinner, lower-k, or higher-h) decays in temperature excess along its length:

\(\theta/\theta_b=\cosh(m(L-x))/\cosh(mL)\): a larger m compresses the same decay into a shorter distance — thinner or lower-conductivity fins 'use up' their base excess faster.

Problem 7 of 11 · multiple choice

The adiabatic-tip idealization used in 2 assumes which loss is negligible?

The lateral surface is exactly where the fin is supposed to lose heat; the idealization only neglects the small end-face area at the very tip.

Problem 8 of 11 · multiple choice

Fin effectiveness \(\varepsilon_{fin}\) scales with which ratio, explaining why fins matter more on the gas side of a system than the liquid side?

\(\varepsilon_{fin}\propto\sqrt{k/h}\): high-conductivity metal fins pay off most where h is small, i.e. gas-side, low-h convection.

Problem 9 of 11 · multiple choice

In 4, doubling this lesson's fin from 200 mm to 400 mm changed the heat rate by roughly:

\(q_{fin}\) rose from 1.67 W to only 1.82 W — a 9% gain for double the material, exactly the saturation 3 describes.

Problem 10 of 11 · multiple choice

Moving the same pin fin from air (h=15 W/m²K) to a liquid (h=200 W/m²K) does what to its absolute heat rate \(q_{fin}\) and its effectiveness \(\varepsilon_{fin}\)?

Absolute heat rate tracks h directly and rises; but effectiveness compares the fin to its own bare area at that same higher h, and that comparison gets tougher as h rises — both verified in this lesson's third solve problem.

Problem 11 of 11 · multiple choice

A designer is choosing where to add fins across four surfaces on the same machine. Which surface benefits LEAST?

Boiling delivers a very high h; per 3's \(\sqrt{k/h}\) scaling, fins pay off least where h is already high — exactly the regime Lesson 7 covers next.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A design engineer specifying an air-cooled heat sink or a finned outdoor enclosure checks fin effectiveness, not just fin count, before adding material — per 3, effectiveness scales with \(\sqrt{k/h}\), which is exactly why finning pays off decisively on gas-cooled, low-\(h\) outdoor equipment and offers far less on a liquid-cooled jacket, where the same aluminum and the same geometry would buy proportionally less.

An engineer reviewing a finned air-cooled housing design recognizes, per 3's own doubling-length result, that past a certain length additional fin material buys almost nothing — a design that keeps extending fin length chasing a marginal cooling gain is spending mass and cost for a return close to noise, an easy, quantifiable objection to raise in a design review rather than a vague one.
