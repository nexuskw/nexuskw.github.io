01 · Three rate laws, one job

Heat moves by exactly three physical mechanisms, and this course is organized around learning each one's rate law well enough to design against it. Conduction, Fourier's law, \(q=-kA\frac{dT}{dx}\): a rate set by a material property \(k\) (thermal conductivity), the area it acts through, and the steepness of the temperature gradient it opposes. Convection, Newton's law of cooling, \(q=hA(T_s-T_\infty)\): a rate set by a convection coefficient \(h\) that is not a material property at all — it is a lumped stand-in for an entire boundary-layer flow field that Lessons 4–6 exist to unpack. Radiation, \(q=\varepsilon\sigma A(T_s^4-T_{surr}^4)\): the only mode that needs no material medium whatsoever, and the only one that is fundamentally nonlinear in temperature rather than linear in a temperature difference.

02 · The thermal resistance network

Despite their different physics, all three rate laws can be rearranged into the same shape — a driving temperature difference divided by a resistance: \(q=\Delta T/R\). For convection, \(R_{conv}=1/(hA)\), read directly off Newton's law. For conduction through a plane wall, \(R_{wall}=L/(kA)\). For conduction through a cylindrical shell (a pipe wall, a wrapped cable), \(R_{cyl}=\ln(r_2/r_1)/(2\pi k L)\) — a ratio of radii rather than a thickness, because the area available for conduction grows with radius, so a fixed radial increment does not carry a fixed resistance increment the way a fixed-thickness plane-wall increment does. Layers encountered in series carry the same heat flow through each one, so their resistances simply add: \(R_{tot}=\sum_i R_i\), exactly like resistors in a DC circuit, with temperature standing in for voltage and heat rate for current.

03 · Insulation economics, and the critical-radius surprise

Adding insulation to a plane wall always adds resistance and always reduces heat loss — area is fixed, so more thickness is pure gain. Wrapping insulation around a small-diameter cylinder — a wire, a small instrument line — does two things at once: it adds conduction resistance (wanted) but it also adds surface area for the outer convection film (unwanted, since \(R_{conv}=1/(hA)\) falls as area rises). The total resistance as a function of wrap radius \(r\) has a genuine extremum at the critical radius \(r_{cr}=k_{ins}/h\): for a bare radius smaller than \(r_{cr}\), adding insulation up to \(r_{cr}\) can increase total heat loss before it starts decreasing it. A windier site (higher \(h\)) shrinks \(r_{cr}\) and shrinks the zone this trap can occur in; a bare conductor already thicker than \(r_{cr}\) never sees it — insulation helps it immediately, as expected.

04 · A worked resistance chain, and a worked critical radius

A building envelope carries four layers in series, per square metre of wall: an outdoor film at \(h_{out}=25\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), a 20 mm brick layer at \(k=0.72\ \text{W/mK}\), a 50 mm insulation layer at \(k=0.04\ \text{W/mK}\), and an indoor film at \(h_{in}=8\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), between a 45 °C outdoor design day and a 24 °C indoor setpoint. The four resistances are \(R_1=0.040\), \(R_2=0.278\), \(R_3=1.250\), \(R_4=0.125\ \ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}\), summing to \(R_{tot}=1.693\ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}\) and a flux \(q''=\Delta T/R_{tot}=12.41\ \text{W/m}^2\) — the same 12.41 W/m² through every layer, stepping the temperature down 45 → 44.50 → 41.06 → 25.55 → 24.0 °C, the last figure closing the loop back to the indoor setpoint exactly. The insulation alone carries 74% of the total — the layer worth upgrading first. Separately: a cable insulated with \(k_{ins}=0.15\ \ \text{W/mK}\) material in \(h=15\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) still air has \(r_{cr}=k_{ins}/h=10\ \text{mm}\) — smaller than many real bare-cable radii, so a thin wrap on such a cable can genuinely raise its heat loss before enough insulation is added to lower it again.

Key relation — series thermal resistance

\[ R_{tot}=\sum_i R_i, \qquad q=\frac{\Delta T_{overall}}{R_{tot}} \]

Every later lesson's exchangers, fins, and radiation networks are this same sum, extended.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

The building-envelope resistance chain

Problem. An exterior wall carries, in series per m²: an outdoor film \(h_{out}=25\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), a 20 mm brick layer (\(k=0.72\ \text{W/mK}\)), a 50 mm insulation layer (\(k=0.04\ \text{W/mK}\)), and an indoor film \(h_{in}=8\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), between 45°C outdoors and 24°C indoors. Find each resistance, the total, and the heat flux.

Solution. \(R_1=1/25=0.040\), \(R_2=0.2/0.72=0.278\), \(R_3=0.05/0.04=1.250\), \(R_4=1/8=0.125\ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}\). \(R_{tot}=1.693\ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}\). \(q''=(45-24)/1.693=12.41\ \text{W/m}^2\).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Doubling the insulation thickness

Problem. For the same wall, the insulation is upgraded from 50 mm to 100 mm (same \(k=0.04\ \text{W/mK}\), all else unchanged). Find the new total resistance and heat flux, and the percent reduction in q''.

Solution. \(R_3'=0.1/0.04=2.500\ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}\), replacing \(R_3=1.250\). \(R_{tot}'=0.040+0.278+2.500+0.125=2.943\ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}\). \(q''=(45-24)/2.943=7.14\ \text{W/m}^2\), down from 12.41 — a 42% reduction from doubling one layer's thickness.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

A second critical-radius check

Problem. A different cable insulation has \(k_{ins}=0.20\ \text{W/mK}\), installed where the surrounding air convection coefficient is \(h=12\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\). Find the critical radius, and state what it means for a bare conductor of radius 12 mm.

Solution. \(r_{cr}=k_{ins}/h=0.2/12.0=16.7\ \text{mm}\). Since the bare radius (12 mm) already exceeds \(r_{cr}\) (16.7 mm), this conductor is past the extremum — any insulation added immediately reduces heat loss; there is no backfire zone left to cross.

Problem 4 of 11 · multiple choice

Which of the three rate laws in 1 is the only one that can transport heat with no material medium present at all?

Radiation is carried by electromagnetic waves and crosses a vacuum; conduction and convection both require a material medium.

Problem 5 of 11 · multiple choice

Fourier's law, Newton's law of cooling, and the Stefan–Boltzmann law all rearrange into which common structural form?

Every rate law in 2 becomes \(q=\Delta T/R\) once rearranged — the thermal-circuit analogy this whole course leans on.

Problem 6 of 11 · multiple choice

Why is a cylindrical shell's conduction resistance written as \(\ln(r_2/r_1)/(2\pi k L)\) rather than a plane-wall-style thickness over area?

Area grows as \(2\pi r L\), so the resistance contributed by each additional radial increment shrinks as r grows — hence the logarithmic form.

Problem 7 of 11 · multiple choice

Doubling the convection coefficient \(h\) at a surface, area held fixed, does what to \(R_{conv}=1/(hA)\)?

\(R_{conv}\) is inversely proportional to \(h\); doubling \(h\) halves the resistance.

Problem 8 of 11 · multiple choice

A windier installation site (higher external \(h\)) does what to the critical radius \(r_{cr}=k_{ins}/h\)?

\(r_{cr}\) is inversely proportional to \(h\); a higher \(h\) shrinks \(r_{cr}\) and with it the range of bare radii vulnerable to the effect.

Problem 9 of 11 · multiple choice

A bare conductor's radius is already larger than \(r_{cr}\). What happens when insulation is added?

The backfire zone only exists for bare radii below \(r_{cr}\); above it, \(R_{tot}\) is already increasing with added radius, so insulation helps immediately.

Problem 10 of 11 · multiple choice

In 4's worked wall, roughly what share of the total resistance does the insulation layer alone carry?

\(R_3/R_{tot}=1.250/1.693=74\%\) — by far the largest single share, which is why insulation is the first place a real audit looks.

Problem 11 of 11 · multiple choice

A new layer is added in series to an existing resistance network, with perfect contact and no new convection surface created. What happens to \(R_{tot}\) and \(q\)?

Series resistances simply add; one more strictly-positive term always raises \(R_{tot}\), and for a fixed overall \(\Delta T\) that always lowers \(q=\Delta T/R_{tot}\).

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A facilities engineer auditing a building envelope's heat gain checks which single layer dominates the resistance chain before recommending any upgrade — per 4's own numbers, a well-built wall's insulation layer alone can carry roughly three-quarters of the total resistance, so an audit that finds thin or missing insulation has found the highest-leverage fix, while re-specifying the indoor air film or the brick thickness would be a low-return distraction.

A technician insulating a small-diameter instrument-tracing line or control cable checks the critical radius before assuming that any added wrap helps — per 3, a modest layer on a thin enough conductor can raise, not lower, its heat loss until the wrap passes \(r_{cr}\), a genuinely counter-intuitive result worth a two-line check before insulating small-diameter lines as a matter of routine practice.
