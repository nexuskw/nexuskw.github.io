01 · The entrance region, and what "fully developed" really means

Flow entering a pipe with a uniform inlet profile takes a hydrodynamic entry length to settle into its fully developed velocity shape, and — separately — a thermal entry length to settle into a fully developed temperature profile. "Thermally fully developed" is a specific, easy-to-misread claim: it means the dimensionless temperature profile shape stops changing with distance, not that the fluid's actual bulk temperature stops changing — the bulk temperature keeps rising (or falling) the entire pipe length; only the shape of the profile riding on top of it has settled down.

02 · Constant flux versus constant wall temperature

For laminar, fully developed flow, the two common thermal boundary conditions give genuinely different Nusselt numbers even at identical Reynolds and Prandtl numbers: \(Nu=4.36\) for constant surface heat flux, \(Nu=3.66\) for constant surface temperature — different because the boundary condition itself reshapes the developed temperature profile. For turbulent flow, the Dittus–Boelter correlation \(Nu=0.023\,Re^{0.8}Pr^{n}\) (\(n=0.4\) heating the fluid, \(n=0.3\) cooling it) is the workhorse, with its own fine print: \(Re\gtrsim10{,}000\), \(0.6\lesssim Pr\lesssim160\), and \(L/D\gtrsim10\) — short pipes still carry an enhanced entrance-region \(h\) this fully developed correlation does not capture, so applying it uncorrected to a short pipe underpredicts the true average \(h\), not overpredicts it.

03 · Heat pickup along a real pipe run

An energy balance on the fluid, \(\dot m c_p\,dT_m/dx=q''P\), integrates very differently depending on which quantity is held constant. Constant heat flux gives a bulk temperature that rises linearly with \(x\) — a fixed \(q''\) delivers a fixed temperature rise per metre, the whole length. Constant wall temperature instead gives \(\ln\!\left(\dfrac{T_s-T_o}{T_s-T_i}\right)=-\dfrac{hPL}{\dot m c_p}\), an exponential approach toward \(T_s\) that never quite arrives. A steam-jacketed pipe is the constant-\(T_s\) case: steam condensing on the jacket's exterior releases a large latent heat at essentially one saturation temperature, holding the wall close to a fixed value regardless of how much heat the process fluid inside is drawing — unlike, say, a uniformly electrically heated pipe, which is closer to the constant-flux case instead.

04 · A worked steam-jacketed pipe

Water at \(\dot m=0.5\ \text{kg/s}\) (\(c_p=4180\ \text{J/kgK}\)) enters a \(D=50\ \text{mm}\), \(L=10\ \text{m}\) pipe at \(T_i=20\)°C; the jacket holds the wall at \(T_s=120\)°C, with \(h=1500\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\). \(P=\pi D=0.1571\ \text{m}\), so \(hPL/(\dot m c_p)=1.127\) and \((T_s-T_o)/(T_s-T_i)=e^{-1.127}=0.324\), giving \(T_o=T_s-0.324(T_s-T_i)=87.6\)°C — a substantial rise for a single 10 m run. As a check on the assumed \(h\): using representative water properties at the pipe's mean film temperature (\(\mu\approx5.5e-04\ \text{Pa·s}\), \(k\approx0.64\ \ \text{W/mK}\), \(Pr\approx3.5\) — given, representative values, not a claimed precise lookup), \(Re=4\dot m/(\pi D\mu)=23,150\) (turbulent), and Dittus–Boelter gives \(Nu=0.023\,Re^{0.8}Pr^{0.4}=117.8\), \(h=1507\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — within 1% of the 1500 W/m²K assumed, a reassuring cross-check rather than a coincidence.

Key relation — constant wall temperature

\[ \frac{T_s-T_o}{T_s-T_i}=\exp\!\left(-\frac{hPL}{\dot m c_p}\right) \]

An exponential approach, never a straight ramp — the signature of a constant-\(T_s\) boundary.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

The worked steam-jacketed pipe

Problem. Water, \(\dot m=0.5\ \text{kg/s}\), \(c_p=4180\ \text{J/kgK}\), enters a \(D=50\ \text{mm}\), \(L=10\ \text{m}\) pipe at \(T_i=20\)°C. The jacket holds \(T_s=120\)°C, \(h=1500\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\). Find the exit temperature.

Solution. \(P=\pi D=0.1571\ \text{m}\), \(hPL/(\dot m c_p)=1.127\). \((T_s-T_o)/(T_s-T_i)=e^{-1.127}=0.324\). \(T_o=120-0.324\times(120-20)=87.6\)°C.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Cross-checking h against Dittus–Boelter

Problem. For the same pipe and flow, representative water properties give \(\mu\approx5.5e-04\ \text{Pa·s}\), \(k\approx0.64\ \text{W/mK}\), \(Pr\approx3.5\). Find \(Re\), confirm turbulence, then find \(Nu\) and \(h\) from Dittus–Boelter, and compare to the assumed 1500 W/m²K.

Solution. \(Re=4\dot m/(\pi D\mu)=23,150\) — turbulent, and \(L/D=200\) clears the \(\gtrsim10\) entrance-length rule. \(Nu=0.023\,Re^{0.8}Pr^{0.4}=117.8\), \(h=Nu\,k/D=1507\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — within about 1% of the 1500 W/m²K assumed.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Doubling the pipe length

Problem. The same jacketed pipe is extended to \(L=20\ \text{m}\), all else unchanged. Find the new exit temperature, and the additional temperature rise bought by the second 10 m compared with the first.

Solution. \(hPL/(\dot m c_p)=2.255\), \((T_s-T_o)/(T_s-T_i)=e^{-2.255}=0.105\), \(T_o=120-0.105\times(120-20)=109.5\)°C. The first 10 m bought \(87.6-20=67.6\)°C of rise; the second 10 m bought only \(109.5-87.6=21.9\)°C more — the exponential approach paying off far less on the back half of the pipe.

Problem 5 of 11 · multiple choice

At identical Re and Pr, laminar fully developed pipe flow gives \(Nu=4.36\) for constant heat flux but \(Nu=3.66\) for constant wall temperature. Why do these differ?

Different boundary conditions produce genuinely different developed temperature profile shapes, and Nu is a property of that profile.

Problem 6 of 11 · multiple choice

Applying Dittus–Boelter, uncorrected, to a short pipe with \(L/D<10\) tends to:

The entrance region carries a higher true h than the fully developed value; a fully developed correlation applied to a short pipe misses that enhancement and underpredicts.

Latent heat release at a fixed saturation temperature is what pins the wall near a constant value — unlike a fixed-power electric heater, which is closer to constant flux.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

An engineer sizing a jacketed or coil heat-traced pipe run identifies which thermal boundary condition actually applies before reaching for a formula — per 3, a condensing-steam jacket behaves as constant wall temperature, giving an exponential approach that delivers most of its temperature rise in the first few metres, while an electrically traced line closer to constant flux delivers its rise linearly instead; treating one as the other misestimates exactly where along the run the temperature change actually happens.

A maintenance engineer troubleshooting a short jacketed spool that underperforms its rated duty checks the pipe's \(L/D\) against Dittus–Boelter's own \(L/D\gtrsim10\) fine print before assuming the correlation used to size it was ever valid — per 2, a short run actually carries a higher true average h from its entrance region than the fully developed correlation predicts, so the correlation itself, not the hardware, may be the source of an apparent underperformance.
