Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:

01 · Grashof and Rayleigh — natural convection's own dimensionless numbers

Natural convection has no imposed velocity to build a Reynolds number from — buoyancy itself drives the flow. The Grashof number, \(Gr=g\beta\Delta T L^3/\nu^2\), compares buoyancy force to viscous force, playing the role Reynolds number plays in forced convection. Multiplying by Prandtl number gives the Rayleigh number, \(Ra=Gr\cdot Pr\), which folds in a comparison of buoyancy to thermal diffusion as well — and it is \(Ra\), not \(Gr\) and \(Pr\) separately, that almost every natural-convection correlation is actually written in terms of.

02 · Correlations, and why orientation changes everything

A vertical plate or enclosure wall follows a Churchill–Chu-type correlation, \(Nu=0.68+\dfrac{0.670\,Ra^{1/4}}{[1+(0.492/Pr)^{9/16}]^{4/9}}\) for the laminar range (\(Ra\lesssim10^9\)). Orientation matters far more here than in forced convection: a horizontal plate with its hot face up lets buoyant plumes rise away freely, giving a meaningfully higher \(h\); the same plate hot face down traps warm, buoyant fluid directly underneath a cooler layer above it — a stable arrangement with almost none of the free plume motion, and a correspondingly much lower \(h\). The same \(\Delta T\), the same plate, a different number, purely from which way it faces.

03 · The Gulf-summer double penalty

A sealed, un-fanned electrical enclosure rejects heat by natural convection (and radiation, Lesson 9) alone. Two effects compound against it as ambient temperature climbs: \(h\) itself depends only weakly on \(\Delta T\) (roughly \(\Delta T^{1/4}\) in the laminar range, per 2's correlation), so it barely improves even as the surface runs hotter; meanwhile the available \(\Delta T\) between a fixed-rating surface and a hotter ambient shrinks directly. Both effects point the same unfavorable way at once — a genuine double penalty, not merely "it's hotter outside so everything runs a bit warmer."

04 · A worked cabinet wall

A sealed cabinet's vertical wall, height \(L=0.3\ \text{m}\), runs at \(T_s=70\)°C in \(T_\infty=45\)°C still Gulf-summer air (\(\Delta T=25\)°C). At the film temperature, \(Pr=0.71\), \(Gr=5.85e+07\), \(Ra=Gr\cdot Pr=4.16e+07\) — under \(10^9\), the laminar correlation applies. \(Nu=42.0\), giving \(h=Nu\,k/L=3.99\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — a strikingly low number next to Lesson 4's forced-air 20 W/m²K, and the reason natural convection sits at the very bottom of this lesson's own comparison chart. If ambient instead climbs to \(T_\infty=50\)°C (\(\Delta T=20\)°C) at the same surface temperature, \(h\) falls only slightly to 3.77 W/m²K, but the heat actually rejected, \(q''=h\Delta T\), falls from 99.7 to 75.4 W/m² — a 24% drop in cooling capacity from a mere 5°C rise in ambient, the double penalty of 3 made concrete.

Key relation — natural convection

\[ Ra=Gr\cdot Pr=\frac{g\beta\Delta T L^3}{\nu^2}\cdot Pr \]

Almost every correlation in this lesson is stated as \(Nu=f(Ra)\) — one number replaces the separate Reynolds and Prandtl numbers forced convection needed.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

The worked cabinet wall

Problem. A cabinet wall, \(L=0.3\ \text{m}\) tall, runs at \(T_s=70\)°C in \(T_\infty=45\)°C air (\(Pr=0.71\), film properties as in 4). Find \(Gr\), \(Ra\), \(Nu\), and \(h\).

Solution. \(Gr=g\beta\Delta TL^3/\nu^2=5.85e+07\). \(Ra=Gr\cdot Pr=4.16e+07\) (laminar range). \(Nu=42.0\). \(h=Nu\,k/L=3.99\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

A hotter ambient, same surface temperature

Problem. The same wall stays at \(T_s=70\)°C, but ambient rises to \(T_\infty=50\)°C. Find the new h and the new heat flux \(q''=h\Delta T\), and compare the heat flux to the original 45°C-ambient case.

Solution. \(\Delta T=20\)°C now. Recomputing, \(Ra=3.31e+07\), \(Nu=39.7\), \(h=3.77\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — only slightly lower than 3.99. But \(q''=h\Delta T=75.4\ \text{W/m}^2\), down from 99.7 W/m² — a 24% drop in rejected heat from a 5°C ambient rise alone.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

A shorter cabinet wall

Problem. A second, shorter cabinet has a wall only \(L=0.15\ \text{m}\) tall, same \(T_s=70\)°C and \(T_\infty=45\)°C as 4. Find its Ra, Nu, and h, and compare h to the taller 0.3 m wall.

Solution. \(Ra=5.20e+06\) (still laminar range), \(Nu=25.2\), \(h=4.80\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — higher than the taller wall's 3.99 W/m²K, since \(h=Nu\,k/L\) and \(Nu\) grows more slowly than \(L\) does; natural-convection h weakly falls as the characteristic length grows, the opposite of what 'bigger surface, better cooling' might suggest.

Problem 4 of 11 · multiple choice

The Grashof number \(Gr=g\beta\Delta TL^3/\nu^2\) plays which role in natural convection that Reynolds number plays in forced convection?

With no external velocity, buoyancy itself drives the flow, and Gr compares that buoyant driving force to viscous resistance — the natural-convection analog of Re.

Problem 5 of 11 · multiple choice

The Rayleigh number \(Ra=Gr\cdot Pr\) adds which comparison beyond Gr alone?

Multiplying by Pr (momentum vs. thermal diffusivity) folds thermal diffusion into the comparison, which is why almost every correlation is fitted to Ra directly.

Problem 6 of 11 · multiple choice

A horizontal hot plate facing UP versus the same plate facing DOWN, same ΔT:

Facing up allows buoyant plumes to rise freely; facing down traps stable stratified fluid beneath a cooler layer, suppressing motion and lowering h substantially.

Problem 7 of 11 · multiple choice

In the laminar range, natural-convection h depends on ΔT roughly as:

The Churchill–Chu form gives \(Nu\propto Ra^{1/4}\propto\Delta T^{1/4}\): h barely improves even as the surface runs much hotter.

Problem 8 of 11 · multiple choice

As Gulf-summer ambient temperature rises (fixed surface rating), a sealed enclosure's actual rejected heat flux \(q''=h\Delta T\):

Both effects compound: h barely improves and the available ΔT shrinks, so rejected heat falls — verified numerically in this lesson's second solve problem.

Problem 9 of 11 · multiple choice

This lesson's own computed \(h\approx4\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) compares to Lesson 4's forced-air result (\(h\approx20\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\)) as:

Natural convection in a gas sits near the bottom of the h ladder — a fraction of even modest forced-air values, as this lesson's comparison chart shows directly.

Problem 10 of 11 · multiple choice

A shorter wall (smaller characteristic length L), same ΔT and fluid, has natural-convection h that is:

Since \(h=Nu\,k/L\) and \(Nu\propto Ra^{1/4}\propto L^{3/4}\), h scales as roughly \(L^{-1/4}\) — a weak decrease with increasing L, confirmed in this lesson's third solve problem.

Problem 11 of 11 · multiple choice

Why does Lesson 2's fin effectiveness argument apply especially strongly to natural-convection surfaces?

Low h is precisely where \(\varepsilon_{fin}\propto\sqrt{k/h}\) is largest — exactly why finned, un-fanned enclosures are such a common design pattern.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

An engineer specifying a sealed, un-fanned electrical enclosure for outdoor Gulf installation checks natural-convection h at the design ambient temperature, not at a mild reference temperature, before setting the enclosure's maximum internal dissipation rating — per 3 and 4's own numbers, a modest ambient rise can cut real cooling capacity by roughly a quarter even though h itself barely changes, so a catalog rating benchmarked at a temperate ambient overstates what the same enclosure can actually reject on a Gulf summer day.

A technician orienting a horizontal hot surface — an outdoor junction-box lid, a horizontal duct run — checks which face is up before assuming a handbook horizontal-plate h applies as written: per 2, the same \(\Delta T\) gives a meaningfully higher h with the hot face up (free plumes) than with the hot face down (stable, trapped stratification), and mixing the two up is an easy, checkable mistake.
