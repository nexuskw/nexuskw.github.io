Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Counterflow exchanger: hot stream 150→90 °C, cold 30→70 °C. Compute the LMTD.
  • Fouling resistance doubles: what happens to U and to the outlet temperatures, qualitatively?
  • When is effectiveness-NTU the only honest method, and why does LMTD fail there?

01 · Architectures, and one overall coefficient

Shell-and-tube and plate exchangers both do the same job through different geometry — plates pack far more area into a given volume, shell-and-tube handles a wider range of pressures and fouling tendencies. Either way, the overall coefficient \(U\) is built exactly like Lesson 1's resistance chain: hot-side convection, tube-wall conduction, cold-side convection, in series — and in a real, aged exchanger, two more series terms as well: a fouling resistance on each side, \(R_{f,h}\) and \(R_{f,c}\), that only grow over an operating campaign.

02 · Two design methods, and what fouling does to each

The LMTD method uses \(q=UA\,\Delta T_{lm}\), with \(\Delta T_{lm}=(\Delta T_1-\Delta T_2)/\ln(\Delta T_1/\Delta T_2)\) built from the temperature gap at each end. The effectiveness–NTU method instead defines \(\varepsilon=q/q_{max}\), \(q_{max}=C_{min}(T_{h,in}-T_{c,in})\), and \(NTU=UA/C_{min}\), with closed-form \(\varepsilon(NTU,C_r)\) relations for each flow arrangement — for the special case \(C_r=0\) (one stream condensing or evaporating, so its capacity rate is effectively infinite), \(\varepsilon=1-e^{-NTU}\) regardless of flow arrangement. Fouling resistance added in series lowers \(U\) directly; for a fixed area and inlet conditions, a lower \(U\) means less heat exchanged — outlet temperatures drift back toward their inlet values on both sides, not just one.

03 · When effectiveness–NTU is the only honest method

LMTD needs the outlet temperatures to compute \(\Delta T_{lm}\) in the first place — fine for a sizing problem, where the duty and desired outlets are already specified and the question is how much area to buy. For a rating problem — a piece of hardware already exists, \(UA\) is known, and the question is what outlet temperatures it will actually deliver — the outlets are the unknown, and LMTD would need an iterative guess-and-check loop to use at all. Effectiveness–NTU solves directly from \(UA\), \(C_{min}\), \(C_{max}\), and the inlet temperatures, with no iteration — the only honest method once the outlets are what you are actually trying to find.

04 · A worked LMTD, an NTU check, and a fouling number

A counterflow exchanger runs hot 150→90°C against cold 30→70°C. \(\Delta T_1=T_{h,in}-T_{c,out}=80\)°C, \(\Delta T_2=T_{h,out}-T_{c,in}=60\)°C, \(\Delta T_{lm}=(80-60)/\ln(80/60)=69.52\)°C. Separately, a condenser (\(C_r\approx0\)) sized to \(NTU=2\) delivers \(\varepsilon=1-e^{-2}=86.5\%\) of its maximum possible duty. And on fouling: a clean \(U_{clean}=800\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) exchanger picking up just \(R_f=0.0005\ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}\) of combined fouling resistance drops to \(U_{fouled}=1/(1/U_{clean}+R_f)=571.4\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — a 29% loss in overall coefficient from a single, modest fouling term, exactly the kind of number a fouling-monitoring program exists to catch before it grows further.

Key relation — LMTD

\[ \Delta T_{lm}=\frac{\Delta T_1-\Delta T_2}{\ln(\Delta T_1/\Delta T_2)}, \qquad q=UA\,\Delta T_{lm} \]

Valid for sizing; switch to effectiveness–NTU the moment the outlets become the unknown.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

The worked LMTD

Problem. A counterflow exchanger runs hot 150→90°C against cold 30→70°C. Find \(\Delta T_1\), \(\Delta T_2\), and the LMTD.

Solution. \(\Delta T_1=T_{h,in}-T_{c,out}=80\)°C, \(\Delta T_2=T_{h,out}-T_{c,in}=60\)°C. \(\Delta T_{lm}=(80-60)/\ln(80/60)=69.52\)°C.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

An NTU rating check

Problem. A condenser (\(C_r\approx0\), one stream condensing) is sized to \(NTU=2\). Find its effectiveness, and the physical meaning of \(C_r\approx0\).

Solution. \(C_r=C_{min}/C_{max}\approx0\) because a condensing stream's effective heat capacity rate is enormous (it changes phase at essentially constant temperature). \(\varepsilon=1-e^{-NTU}=1-e^{-2}=86.5\%\) of the maximum possible duty is delivered.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

A fouling-derated U

Problem. A clean exchanger has \(U_{clean}=800\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\). In service, it accumulates a combined fouling resistance of \(R_f=0.0005\ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}\). Find the fouled overall coefficient, and the percent drop from clean.

Solution. \(U_{fouled}=1/(1/U_{clean}+R_f)=1/(1/800+0.0005)=571.4\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), a \((1-571.4/800)\times100=29\%\) drop from a single, modest fouling term.

Problem 5 of 11 · multiple choice

The LMTD method requires which piece of information that effectiveness–NTU does not strictly need to start from?

\(\Delta T_{lm}\) is built directly from the outlet temperatures; effectiveness–NTU instead solves for those same outlets from UA and the inlets alone.

Problem 6 of 11 · multiple choice

A heat exchanger already installed has a known UA. Its outlet temperatures at an off-design flow rate are unknown and need to be predicted. Which method is appropriate?

This is a rating problem (hardware fixed, outlets unknown) — the case 3 identifies as effectiveness–NTU's own, direct-solution territory.

Problem 7 of 11 · multiple choice

Adding fouling resistance in series, area and inlet conditions held fixed, does what to U and to the heat duty q?

Fouling resistance adds directly to the series network, lowering U; for fixed A and similar ΔT, that lowers q as well — verified numerically in this lesson's third solve problem.

Problem 8 of 11 · multiple choice

For a condensing or evaporating stream, \(C_r=C_{min}/C_{max}\approx0\) because:

Latent heat exchange at essentially one temperature makes the effective \(C\) of that stream enormous compared to the other, single-phase stream — hence \(C_r\approx0\).

Problem 9 of 11 · multiple choice

In this lesson's condenser example, \(NTU=2\) and \(C_r\approx0\) gives an effectiveness of roughly:

\(\varepsilon=1-e^{-NTU}=1-e^{-2}=86.5\%\), the direct closed form for \(C_r=0\), any flow arrangement.

Problem 10 of 11 · multiple choice

Why is a plate exchanger often chosen over shell-and-tube for a given duty?

Plates trade compactness (more area per volume) for typically lower pressure and fouling tolerance than shell-and-tube — a real design trade-off, not a strict improvement.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

An engineer commissioning a new exchanger uses LMTD to confirm the specified area delivers the contracted duty at the design terminal temperatures — a sizing question with the outlets already fixed by contract. The same engineer, months later, asked what outlet temperatures the same installed hardware delivers at an off-design flow rate, switches to effectiveness–NTU without a second thought — per 3, the outlets are now the unknown, and LMTD would need to be iterated blind.

A reliability engineer tracking a fouling-monitoring program treats a modest, single fouling resistance reading as a real, quantifiable loss in overall coefficient, not a rounding error — per 4's own number, well under a millimetre-scale fouling resistance cut this lesson's example exchanger's U by over a quarter, which is exactly why fouling factors get tracked as a trended number rather than checked once at commissioning and forgotten.
