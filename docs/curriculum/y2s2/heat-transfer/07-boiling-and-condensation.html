Boiling and condensation

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why is critical heat flux a safety limit, not a performance target?
  • Film versus dropwise condensation: which do real plants get?

01 · Four regimes, one curve

Plotting heat flux against excess temperature \(\Delta T_e=T_s-T_{sat}\) on log-log axes traces a shape every boiling fluid shares. Free convection boiling: too little excess temperature for bubbles yet, ordinary natural convection. Nucleate boiling: bubbles form, detach, and stir the liquid violently — \(q''\) climbs steeply for only a modest rise in \(\Delta T_e\), the most efficient and most desirable regime to operate in. The critical heat flux (CHF) is the peak of the curve. Past it, transition boiling is genuinely unstable: a partial, intermittent vapor blanket forms, and \(q''\) falls as \(\Delta T_e\) rises further. Film boiling follows: a stable vapor blanket fully covers the surface, and \(q''\) eventually climbs again at much higher \(\Delta T_e\) — but at surface temperatures that destroy most real equipment.

02 · Zuber's CHF, and Nusselt film condensation

Zuber's correlation predicts CHF from fluid properties alone, no need to trace the whole curve up to it: \(q''_{max}=C\,h_{fg}\rho_v\!\left[\dfrac{\sigma g(\rho_l-\rho_v)}{\rho_v^2} \right]^{1/4}\), \(C\approx0.131\) — density difference, surface tension, and latent heat set the limit; the heater material does not appear at all. On the condensing side, Nusselt film theory gives a mean coefficient of the same functional form for a draining condensate film, but with a different leading constant by geometry — \(0.943\) for a vertical plate, \(0.729\) for a horizontal tube — because the controlling drainage path genuinely differs: down a vertical surface's full length versus around a horizontal tube's circumference.

03 · Why CHF is a safety limit, and why real plants get film condensation

Many real systems are controlled by an imposed heat input — an electric heater, a fired process heater, a reactor fuel rod — not an imposed surface temperature. For such a system, an upset that pushes \(q''\) toward CHF risks trying to sit on the falling transition-boiling branch, which at a fixed \(q''\) has no stable point there at all — the system jumps almost immediately to the much higher surface temperature the film-boiling branch demands at that same \(q''\). This is literally what "burnout" means physically. Real designs therefore carry a margin below CHF, commonly operating in the 70% range of \(q''_{max}\) rather than approaching it. On the condensing side: dropwise condensation gives a coefficient often five to ten times higher than film condensation, but it requires special, often short-lived, surface treatments and is not self-sustaining on an untreated surface — which is why almost every real condenser tube runs in film condensation despite dropwise's better numbers on paper.

04 · A worked CHF, and a design check against it

For water at 1 atm (\(h_{fg}=2257\ \text{kJ/kg}\), \(\rho_l=958\), \(\rho_v=0.6\ \text{kg/m}^3\), \(\sigma=0.0589\ \text{N/m}\)), Zuber's correlation gives \(q''_{max}=1.11\ \text{MW/m}^2\) — the same order of magnitude every heat- transfer text quotes for water at atmospheric pressure. A conservative design running at 70% of this limit operates at \(q''=0.78\ \text{MW/m}^2\); a reboiler specified instead at 0.85 MW/m² is running at \(77\%\) of CHF — above the typical 70% design range, and worth a second look before it is approved.

Key relation — Zuber critical heat flux

\[ q''_{max}=C\,h_{fg}\rho_v\left[\frac{\sigma g(\rho_l-\rho_v)}{\rho_v^2} \right]^{1/4} \]

A limit computed from fluid properties alone — no heater material, no ΔT, enters at all.

The worked Zuber CHF

Problem. For water at 1 atm (\(h_{fg}=2257\ \text{kJ/kg}\), \(\rho_l=958\), \(\rho_v=0.6\ \text{kg/m}^3\), \(\sigma=0.0589\ \text{N/m}\), \(C=0.131\)), find the Zuber critical heat flux.

Solution. \(q''_{max}=C\,h_{fg}\rho_v[\sigma g(\rho_l-\rho_v)/\rho_v^2]^{1/4}=1.11\ \text{MW/m}^2\).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Checking a specified design against CHF

Problem. A reboiler for this same fluid is specified to operate at 0.85 MW/m². What fraction of the Zuber CHF is this, and is it within a typical 70% design-margin practice?

Solution. \(q''_{spec}/q''_{max}=0.85/1.11=77\%\) — above the typical 70% margin range, meaning this design sits closer to the CHF cliff edge than conservative practice recommends, and is worth flagging before approval.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Horizontal versus vertical condensation

Problem. For otherwise-identical fluid properties, characteristic dimension, and \(\Delta T\), compare the mean film-condensation coefficient for a horizontal tube (constant 0.729) against a vertical plate (constant 0.943), using the Nusselt constants alone.

Solution. \(h_{horiz}/h_{vert}=0.729/0.943=0.77\) — the horizontal-tube arrangement's coefficient is about 77% of the vertical arrangement's, purely from the geometry-driven constant, before any difference in characteristic length is even considered.

Problem 4 of 11 · multiple choice

Which regime of the boiling curve is the target for practical equipment design?

Nucleate boiling delivers the largest heat flux for the smallest excess temperature — the most efficient, most controllable regime, and where real boilers are meant to operate.

Problem 5 of 11 · multiple choice

Immediately past the critical heat flux peak, in transition boiling, what happens to \(q''\) as \(\Delta T_e\) continues to rise?

Transition boiling is the falling branch of the curve — an intermittent, unstable vapor blanket actually reduces q'' as ΔT_e rises further.

Problem 6 of 11 · multiple choice

Why is CHF treated as a safety limit rather than a performance target?

A fixed-q'' system has no stable point on the falling transition branch; per 3, it jumps almost immediately to film boiling's much higher surface temperature — the real mechanism behind 'burnout'.

Problem 7 of 11 · multiple choice

Zuber's CHF correlation is built from which properties?

Zuber's correlation is a fluid-properties-only result — no heater material, no ΔT_e, enters the formula at all.

Problem 8 of 11 · multiple choice

In practice, which condensation regime does a typical, untreated industrial condenser tube actually operate in?

Film condensation is the overwhelming practical default; dropwise's much higher h requires surface treatments most industrial tubes do not carry.

Problem 9 of 11 · multiple choice

The Nusselt film-condensation constant differs between a horizontal tube (0.729) and a vertical plate (0.943) primarily because:

Geometry sets how the condensate film drains, which is exactly what these leading constants encode — not an arbitrary labeling difference.

Problem 10 of 11 · multiple choice

A reboiler specified at 0.85 MW/m² against a fluid with CHF=1.11 MW/m² is running at roughly what fraction of CHF?

\(0.85/1.11=77\%\), above the conservative 70% range this lesson's 3 describes as typical practice.

Problem 11 of 11 · multiple choice

In film boiling, at very high \(\Delta T_e\), why does \(q''\) eventually rise again despite a complete, insulating vapor blanket?

At the very high surface temperatures film boiling reaches, radiative transfer across the vapor film becomes a real, additive contribution, per 1's curve shape.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A process engineer specifying an electrically or fire-heated reboiler sizes its operating heat flux with a real margin below the fluid's CHF, never at it — per 3, a controlled process that briefly pushes toward CHF during an upset does not fail gracefully, it can jump almost immediately to a much higher, damaging surface temperature, which is why a design margin belongs in the original sizing calculation rather than as an afterthought once trouble starts.

A maintenance engineer evaluating a fouled or scaled condenser tube recognizes that it is virtually certainly operating in film condensation, not the far more effective dropwise regime — per 3, expecting anywhere near dropwise-level performance from an untreated industrial tube surface sets an unrealistic benchmark for what cleaning alone can restore.
