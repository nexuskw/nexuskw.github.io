Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Mechanisms and the thermal circuit

01 · Three rate laws, one job

Heat moves by exactly three physical mechanisms, and this course is organized around learning each one's rate law well enough to design against it. Conduction, Fourier's law, \(q=-kA\frac{dT}{dx}\): a rate set by a material property \(k\) (thermal conductivity), the area it acts through, and the steepness of the temperature gradient it opposes. Convection, Newton's law of cooling, \(q=hA(T_s-T_\infty)\): a rate set by a convection coefficient \(h\) that is not a material property at all — it is a lumped stand-in for an entire boundary-layer flow field that Lessons 4–6 exist to unpack. Radiation, \(q=\varepsilon\sigma A(T_s^4-T_{surr}^4)\): the only mode that needs no material medium whatsoever, and the only one that is fundamentally nonlinear in temperature rather than linear in a temperature difference.

02 · The thermal resistance network

Despite their different physics, all three rate laws can be rearranged into the same shape — a driving temperature difference divided by a resistance: \(q=\Delta T/R\). For convection, \(R_{conv}=1/(hA)\), read directly off Newton's law. For conduction through a plane wall, \(R_{wall}=L/(kA)\). For conduction through a cylindrical shell (a pipe wall, a wrapped cable), \(R_{cyl}=\ln(r_2/r_1)/(2\pi k L)\) — a ratio of radii rather than a thickness, because the area available for conduction grows with radius, so a fixed radial increment does not carry a fixed resistance increment the way a fixed-thickness plane-wall increment does. Layers encountered in series carry the same heat flow through each one, so their resistances simply add: \(R_{tot}=\sum_i R_i\), exactly like resistors in a DC circuit, with temperature standing in for voltage and heat rate for current.

03 · Insulation economics, and the critical-radius surprise

Adding insulation to a plane wall always adds resistance and always reduces heat loss — area is fixed, so more thickness is pure gain. Wrapping insulation around a small-diameter cylinder — a wire, a small instrument line — does two things at once: it adds conduction resistance (wanted) but it also adds surface area for the outer convection film (unwanted, since \(R_{conv}=1/(hA)\) falls as area rises). The total resistance as a function of wrap radius \(r\) has a genuine extremum at the critical radius \(r_{cr}=k_{ins}/h\): for a bare radius smaller than \(r_{cr}\), adding insulation up to \(r_{cr}\) can increase total heat loss before it starts decreasing it. A windier site (higher \(h\)) shrinks \(r_{cr}\) and shrinks the zone this trap can occur in; a bare conductor already thicker than \(r_{cr}\) never sees it — insulation helps it immediately, as expected.

04 · A worked resistance chain, and a worked critical radius

A building envelope carries four layers in series, per square metre of wall: an outdoor film at \(h_{out}=25\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), a 20 mm brick layer at \(k=0.72\ \text{W/mK}\), a 50 mm insulation layer at \(k=0.04\ \text{W/mK}\), and an indoor film at \(h_{in}=8\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), between a 45 °C outdoor design day and a 24 °C indoor setpoint. The four resistances are \(R_1=0.040\), \(R_2=0.278\), \(R_3=1.250\), \(R_4=0.125\ \ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}\), summing to \(R_{tot}=1.693\ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}\) and a flux \(q''=\Delta T/R_{tot}=12.41\ \text{W/m}^2\) — the same 12.41 W/m² through every layer, stepping the temperature down 45 → 44.50 → 41.06 → 25.55 → 24.0 °C, the last figure closing the loop back to the indoor setpoint exactly. The insulation alone carries 74% of the total — the layer worth upgrading first. Separately: a cable insulated with \(k_{ins}=0.15\ \ \text{W/mK}\) material in \(h=15\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) still air has \(r_{cr}=k_{ins}/h=10\ \text{mm}\) — smaller than many real bare-cable radii, so a thin wrap on such a cable can genuinely raise its heat loss before enough insulation is added to lower it again.

Key relation — series thermal resistance

\[ R_{tot}=\sum_i R_i, \qquad q=\frac{\Delta T_{overall}}{R_{tot}} \]

Every later lesson's exchangers, fins, and radiation networks are this same sum, extended.

Lesson 02 · Steady conduction and fins

01 · Why extend a surface at all

Newton's law of cooling, \(q=hA(T_s-T_\infty)\), leaves exactly three levers once a surface and its fluid are fixed: the convection coefficient \(h\), the temperature difference, and the area \(A\). When \(h\) is the bottleneck — the gas side of an air-cooled enclosure, an un-fanned motor housing, any application where \(h\) sits in the tens of W/m²K rather than the hundreds or thousands a liquid delivers — and the temperature difference is already set by the process, the only lever left is area. A fin is exactly that: manufactured extra area, bought in exchange for material, cost, and — crucially, per 3 — a fin's own internal conduction resistance that eventually limits how much that extra area is actually worth.

02 · The fin equation, derived and solved

An energy balance on a thin differential slice of a straight fin — conduction in one face, conduction out the other, the difference lost sideways by convection — gives, after dividing through by the slice's cross-sectional conduction term, the fin equation \(\dfrac{d^2\theta}{dx^2}-m^2\theta=0\), where \(\theta=T-T_\infty\) is the local temperature excess and \(m=\sqrt{hP/(kA_c)}\) bundles the perimeter \(P\) (convecting surface per unit length), cross-sectional area \(A_c\) (conducting area), conductivity \(k\), and \(h\) into one rate constant. With an adiabatic-tip idealization (the tip's own end-face loss neglected — reasonable once a fin is slender and not too short), the solution is \(\theta/\theta_b=\cosh(m(L-x))/\cosh(mL)\), and the total heat the fin carries away from its base works out to \(q_{fin}=\sqrt{hPkA_c}\,\theta_b\tanh(mL)\).

03 · Efficiency, effectiveness, and where fins stop paying

Fin efficiency \(\eta_{fin}=q_{fin}/q_{max}=\tanh(mL)/mL\) compares the real fin to a hypothetical one held entirely at base temperature — it falls as \(mL\) grows, because a longer or lower-conductivity fin loses more of its temperature excess before the tip. Fin effectiveness \(\varepsilon_{fin}=q_{fin}/(hA_c\theta_b)\) instead compares the fin to the same base area with no fin at all — a fin is worth adding once \(\varepsilon_{fin}\) climbs well past 1 (a common rule of thumb wants it past 2), and since \(\varepsilon_{fin}\) scales with \(\sqrt{k/h}\), fins pay off far more decisively on the low-\(h\) gas side of a system than on a high-\(h\) liquid or boiling side — the same \(k/h\) logic behind why air-cooled equipment bristles with fins and liquid-cooled equipment mostly does not. Length has diminishing returns for a separate reason: \(\tanh(mL)\) saturates toward 1 once \(mL\gtrsim 2.5\text{–}3\), so past that point almost no additional heat leaves per unit of added length — doubling this lesson's own 200 mm fin to 400 mm (4) buys only 9% more heat rate for twice the material, which is where a fin design stops paying for itself.

04 · A worked pin fin

An aluminum pin fin, \(k=200\ \text{W/mK}\), diameter 5 mm, length 200 mm, sits in \(h=15\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) air with a base excess \(\theta_b=60\)°C. \(A_c=\pi r^2=19.63\ \text{mm}^2\), \(P=\pi D=15.71\ \text{mm}\), \(m=\sqrt{hP/(kA_c)}=7.75\ \text{m}^{-1}\), \(mL=1.549\), \(\tanh(mL)=0.914\). Efficiency \(\eta_{fin}=\tanh(mL)/mL=59\%\); the fin delivers \(q_{fin}=\sqrt{hPkA_c}\,\theta_b\tanh(mL)=1.67\ \text{W}\), and against its own bare cross-section, \(\varepsilon_{fin}=q_{fin}/(hA_c\theta_b)=94\) — comfortably past the worth-adding threshold. Doubling the length to 400 mm drops efficiency to 32% and raises \(q_{fin}\) only to 1.82 W, the 9% gain 3 already flagged as the point of diminishing returns.

Key relation — adiabatic-tip fin

\[ \eta_{fin}=\frac{\tanh(mL)}{mL}, \qquad m=\sqrt{\frac{hP}{kA_c}} \]

Every fin-efficiency chart in every textbook is this same ratio, tabulated for other tip conditions and cross-sections.

Lesson 03 · Transient conduction

01 · Lumped capacitance, and its entry ticket

If a body's own internal conduction is fast enough that it stays at one uniform temperature while it heats or cools, an energy balance on the whole body — stored energy lost to convection, \(\rho V c\,dT/dt=-hA(T-T_\infty)\) — integrates directly to \(\dfrac{T-T_\infty}{T_i-T_\infty}=e^{-t/\tau}\), a single exponential set by one time constant \(\tau=\rho V c/(hA)\). The entry ticket for this shortcut is the Biot number, \(Bi=hL_c/k\) with \(L_c=V/A\): it is a ratio of internal conduction resistance \((L_c/k)\) to external convection resistance \((1/h)\), and the common rule of thumb is \(Bi<0.1\) — internal resistance small enough next to the external bottleneck that "uniform temperature" is a good approximation, not merely a convenient one.

02 · When lumped fails: the semi-infinite solid and one-term charts

When \(Bi\) is not small — a thick section, a highly conductive-but-large body, or simply the first instants after a sudden surface change, before the interior has had time to feel it — a body is better modeled as a semi-infinite solid: an error-function temperature profile spreading in from the surface, whose surface heat flux falls as \(1/\sqrt{t}\), because the depth the disturbance has reached keeps growing while the local gradient keeps relaxing. For finite shapes (a plane wall, a long cylinder, a sphere) once enough time has passed that a single mode dominates (\(Fo=\alpha t/L_c^2\gtrsim0.2\)), a one-term solution \(\theta^*=C_1 e^{-\lambda_1^2 Fo}\) (times a position function), with \(\lambda_1\) and \(C_1\) tabulated against \(Bi\) and geometry, replaces the full infinite series — the same role Heisler charts play by hand.

03 · The same V/A ratio, a different exponent

Both \(\tau=\rho V c/(hA)\) here and Chvorinov's rule for casting solidification time (\(t\propto(V/A)^2\), covered in this curriculum's manufacturing-processes course) are governed by the same geometric ratio, \(V/A\) — a body's volume against the surface it must lose or gain heat through. The exponent differs because the limiting resistance differs: lumped capacitance's \(\tau\) is externally convection-limited, and \(V/A\) enters linearly through the energy balance; Chvorinov's rule describes a growing solid shell whose own conduction resistance is what's controlling, and that internally-limited problem is what pushes the exponent to two. Estimating a real time honestly means checking \(Bi\) first and picking the model that ticket actually admits — never assuming lumped capacitance applies just because it is the easier formula to reach for.

04 · A worked quench, checked before it is trusted

A 12 mm-diameter steel pin (\(k=45\ \text{W/mK}\), \(\rho=7850\ \text{kg/m}^3\), \(c=470\ \text{J/kgK}\)) is quenched in agitated oil, \(h=750\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\). For a long cylinder, \(L_c=V/A=r/2=3.0\ \ \text{mm}\), so \(Bi=hL_c/k=0.05\) — comfortably under 0.1, lumped capacitance is legitimate. \(\tau=\rho L_c c/h=14.76\ \text{s}\): physically, the time for the excess temperature to fall to \(1/e\approx37\%\) of its start. Reaching 5% of the initial excess takes \(t=\tau\ln(20)=44.2\ \text{s}\); reaching 10% takes \(t=\tau\ln(10)=34.0\ \ \text{s}\). Had the same pin instead been quenched in water (\(h=5000\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\)), \(Bi\) would climb to 0.33 — over the 0.1 line, meaning the lumped shortcut is no longer legitimate for that case, exactly the honest check 3 insists on before trusting any of these numbers.

Key relation — lumped capacitance

\[ \frac{T-T_\infty}{T_i-T_\infty}=e^{-t/\tau}, \qquad \tau=\frac{\rho V c}{hA}, \qquad Bi=\frac{hL_c}{k}<0.1 \]

Check \(Bi\) before trusting \(\tau\) — the formula does not warn you when it stops applying.

Lesson 04 · Forced convection: external

01 · Two boundary layers, and the laminar–turbulent split

A fluid moving past a solid surface develops a velocity boundary layer: zero velocity at the wall (no-slip), rising to the free-stream value across a thin layer of thickness \(\delta\). If the surface and fluid are at different temperatures, a parallel thermal boundary layer develops the same way, wall temperature to free-stream temperature across thickness \(\delta_t\). Close to a leading edge this layer is smooth and layered — laminar; far enough along, it becomes chaotic and well-mixed except for a thin viscous sublayer — turbulent. For a flat plate the transition sits near \(Re_{x,crit}\approx5\times10^5\), a Reynolds number, not a fixed distance — it depends on velocity and fluid as much as on position.

02 · The Nu–Re–Pr grammar

The Nusselt number \(Nu=hL/k\) is simply a dimensionless convection coefficient — how much better convection moves heat across length scale \(L\) than pure conduction through a stationary layer of the same fluid would. Forced-convection correlations fit \(Nu\) as a power law in the Reynolds number \(Re=VL/\nu\) (inertial versus viscous forces) and the Prandtl number \(Pr=\nu/\alpha\) (momentum versus thermal diffusivity — physically, how the velocity and thermal boundary layers compare in thickness): \(Nu=C\,Re^m Pr^n\). For laminar flow averaged over a flat plate, \(Nu_L=0.664\,Re_L^{0.5}Pr^{1/3}\) (valid for \(Pr\gtrsim0.6\)). Cylinders in crossflow and tube banks follow the same structural form with different constants and, for banks, a row-count correction — always fitted to data over a stated range, never derived from first principles the way the flat-plate boundary-layer result nearly is.

03 · Using a correlation without believing it past its range

Every correlation in 2 carries fine print: a Reynolds range, a Prandtl range, a specific geometry it was fitted to. Applying a flat-plate correlation to a cylinder in crossflow is not a rounding error — it is a category error, since the boundary-layer physics genuinely differs (a cylinder's boundary layer separates; a flat plate's does not). Applying a correlation validated for \(Pr\gtrsim0.6\) to a very low-\(Pr\) liquid metal is the same mistake in a quieter disguise: the fitted exponent on \(Pr\) was never tested down there, and there is no guarantee it still holds.

04 · A worked flat plate

Air at \(V=5\ \text{m/s}\) flows over a 200 mm-long flat plate (an electronics board or a heat-sink base), film properties \(\nu=1.6e-05\ \ \text{m}^2\text{/s}\), \(k=0.027\ \text{W/mK}\), \(Pr=0.71\). \(Re_L=VL/\nu=62,500\) — well under \(Re_{crit}\), laminar the whole length. \(Nu_L=0.664\,Re_L^{0.5}Pr^{1/3}=148.1\), giving \(h=Nu_L k/L=19.99\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — a modest number, typical of low-speed forced air, and the reason Lesson 2's fins matter so much on exactly this kind of surface.

Key relation — laminar flat plate

\[ Nu_L=\frac{hL}{k}=0.664\,Re_L^{0.5}Pr^{1/3} \]

Same grammar, different constants, for every other forced-convection correlation this course uses.

Lesson 05 · Forced convection: internal

01 · The entrance region, and what "fully developed" really means

Flow entering a pipe with a uniform inlet profile takes a hydrodynamic entry length to settle into its fully developed velocity shape, and — separately — a thermal entry length to settle into a fully developed temperature profile. "Thermally fully developed" is a specific, easy-to-misread claim: it means the dimensionless temperature profile shape stops changing with distance, not that the fluid's actual bulk temperature stops changing — the bulk temperature keeps rising (or falling) the entire pipe length; only the shape of the profile riding on top of it has settled down.

02 · Constant flux versus constant wall temperature

For laminar, fully developed flow, the two common thermal boundary conditions give genuinely different Nusselt numbers even at identical Reynolds and Prandtl numbers: \(Nu=4.36\) for constant surface heat flux, \(Nu=3.66\) for constant surface temperature — different because the boundary condition itself reshapes the developed temperature profile. For turbulent flow, the Dittus–Boelter correlation \(Nu=0.023\,Re^{0.8}Pr^{n}\) (\(n=0.4\) heating the fluid, \(n=0.3\) cooling it) is the workhorse, with its own fine print: \(Re\gtrsim10{,}000\), \(0.6\lesssim Pr\lesssim160\), and \(L/D\gtrsim10\) — short pipes still carry an enhanced entrance-region \(h\) this fully developed correlation does not capture, so applying it uncorrected to a short pipe underpredicts the true average \(h\), not overpredicts it.

03 · Heat pickup along a real pipe run

An energy balance on the fluid, \(\dot m c_p\,dT_m/dx=q''P\), integrates very differently depending on which quantity is held constant. Constant heat flux gives a bulk temperature that rises linearly with \(x\) — a fixed \(q''\) delivers a fixed temperature rise per metre, the whole length. Constant wall temperature instead gives \(\ln\!\left(\dfrac{T_s-T_o}{T_s-T_i}\right)=-\dfrac{hPL}{\dot m c_p}\), an exponential approach toward \(T_s\) that never quite arrives. A steam-jacketed pipe is the constant-\(T_s\) case: steam condensing on the jacket's exterior releases a large latent heat at essentially one saturation temperature, holding the wall close to a fixed value regardless of how much heat the process fluid inside is drawing — unlike, say, a uniformly electrically heated pipe, which is closer to the constant-flux case instead.

04 · A worked steam-jacketed pipe

Water at \(\dot m=0.5\ \text{kg/s}\) (\(c_p=4180\ \text{J/kgK}\)) enters a \(D=50\ \text{mm}\), \(L=10\ \text{m}\) pipe at \(T_i=20\)°C; the jacket holds the wall at \(T_s=120\)°C, with \(h=1500\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\). \(P=\pi D=0.1571\ \text{m}\), so \(hPL/(\dot m c_p)=1.127\) and \((T_s-T_o)/(T_s-T_i)=e^{-1.127}=0.324\), giving \(T_o=T_s-0.324(T_s-T_i)=87.6\)°C — a substantial rise for a single 10 m run. As a check on the assumed \(h\): using representative water properties at the pipe's mean film temperature (\(\mu\approx5.5e-04\ \text{Pa·s}\), \(k\approx0.64\ \ \text{W/mK}\), \(Pr\approx3.5\) — given, representative values, not a claimed precise lookup), \(Re=4\dot m/(\pi D\mu)=23,150\) (turbulent), and Dittus–Boelter gives \(Nu=0.023\,Re^{0.8}Pr^{0.4}=117.8\), \(h=1507\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — within 1% of the 1500 W/m²K assumed, a reassuring cross-check rather than a coincidence.

Key relation — constant wall temperature

\[ \frac{T_s-T_o}{T_s-T_i}=\exp\!\left(-\frac{hPL}{\dot m c_p}\right) \]

An exponential approach, never a straight ramp — the signature of a constant-\(T_s\) boundary.

Lesson 06 · Natural convection

01 · Grashof and Rayleigh — natural convection's own dimensionless numbers

Natural convection has no imposed velocity to build a Reynolds number from — buoyancy itself drives the flow. The Grashof number, \(Gr=g\beta\Delta T L^3/\nu^2\), compares buoyancy force to viscous force, playing the role Reynolds number plays in forced convection. Multiplying by Prandtl number gives the Rayleigh number, \(Ra=Gr\cdot Pr\), which folds in a comparison of buoyancy to thermal diffusion as well — and it is \(Ra\), not \(Gr\) and \(Pr\) separately, that almost every natural-convection correlation is actually written in terms of.

02 · Correlations, and why orientation changes everything

A vertical plate or enclosure wall follows a Churchill–Chu-type correlation, \(Nu=0.68+\dfrac{0.670\,Ra^{1/4}}{[1+(0.492/Pr)^{9/16}]^{4/9}}\) for the laminar range (\(Ra\lesssim10^9\)). Orientation matters far more here than in forced convection: a horizontal plate with its hot face up lets buoyant plumes rise away freely, giving a meaningfully higher \(h\); the same plate hot face down traps warm, buoyant fluid directly underneath a cooler layer above it — a stable arrangement with almost none of the free plume motion, and a correspondingly much lower \(h\). The same \(\Delta T\), the same plate, a different number, purely from which way it faces.

03 · The Gulf-summer double penalty

A sealed, un-fanned electrical enclosure rejects heat by natural convection (and radiation, Lesson 9) alone. Two effects compound against it as ambient temperature climbs: \(h\) itself depends only weakly on \(\Delta T\) (roughly \(\Delta T^{1/4}\) in the laminar range, per 2's correlation), so it barely improves even as the surface runs hotter; meanwhile the available \(\Delta T\) between a fixed-rating surface and a hotter ambient shrinks directly. Both effects point the same unfavorable way at once — a genuine double penalty, not merely "it's hotter outside so everything runs a bit warmer."

04 · A worked cabinet wall

A sealed cabinet's vertical wall, height \(L=0.3\ \text{m}\), runs at \(T_s=70\)°C in \(T_\infty=45\)°C still Gulf-summer air (\(\Delta T=25\)°C). At the film temperature, \(Pr=0.71\), \(Gr=5.85e+07\), \(Ra=Gr\cdot Pr=4.16e+07\) — under \(10^9\), the laminar correlation applies. \(Nu=42.0\), giving \(h=Nu\,k/L=3.99\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — a strikingly low number next to Lesson 4's forced-air 20 W/m²K, and the reason natural convection sits at the very bottom of this lesson's own comparison chart. If ambient instead climbs to \(T_\infty=50\)°C (\(\Delta T=20\)°C) at the same surface temperature, \(h\) falls only slightly to 3.77 W/m²K, but the heat actually rejected, \(q''=h\Delta T\), falls from 99.7 to 75.4 W/m² — a 24% drop in cooling capacity from a mere 5°C rise in ambient, the double penalty of 3 made concrete.

Key relation — natural convection

\[ Ra=Gr\cdot Pr=\frac{g\beta\Delta T L^3}{\nu^2}\cdot Pr \]

Almost every correlation in this lesson is stated as \(Nu=f(Ra)\) — one number replaces the separate Reynolds and Prandtl numbers forced convection needed.

Lesson 07 · Boiling and condensation

01 · Four regimes, one curve

Plotting heat flux against excess temperature \(\Delta T_e=T_s-T_{sat}\) on log-log axes traces a shape every boiling fluid shares. Free convection boiling: too little excess temperature for bubbles yet, ordinary natural convection. Nucleate boiling: bubbles form, detach, and stir the liquid violently — \(q''\) climbs steeply for only a modest rise in \(\Delta T_e\), the most efficient and most desirable regime to operate in. The critical heat flux (CHF) is the peak of the curve. Past it, transition boiling is genuinely unstable: a partial, intermittent vapor blanket forms, and \(q''\) falls as \(\Delta T_e\) rises further. Film boiling follows: a stable vapor blanket fully covers the surface, and \(q''\) eventually climbs again at much higher \(\Delta T_e\) — but at surface temperatures that destroy most real equipment.

02 · Zuber's CHF, and Nusselt film condensation

Zuber's correlation predicts CHF from fluid properties alone, no need to trace the whole curve up to it: \(q''_{max}=C\,h_{fg}\rho_v\!\left[\dfrac{\sigma g(\rho_l-\rho_v)}{\rho_v^2} \right]^{1/4}\), \(C\approx0.131\) — density difference, surface tension, and latent heat set the limit; the heater material does not appear at all. On the condensing side, Nusselt film theory gives a mean coefficient of the same functional form for a draining condensate film, but with a different leading constant by geometry — \(0.943\) for a vertical plate, \(0.729\) for a horizontal tube — because the controlling drainage path genuinely differs: down a vertical surface's full length versus around a horizontal tube's circumference.

03 · Why CHF is a safety limit, and why real plants get film condensation

Many real systems are controlled by an imposed heat input — an electric heater, a fired process heater, a reactor fuel rod — not an imposed surface temperature. For such a system, an upset that pushes \(q''\) toward CHF risks trying to sit on the falling transition-boiling branch, which at a fixed \(q''\) has no stable point there at all — the system jumps almost immediately to the much higher surface temperature the film-boiling branch demands at that same \(q''\). This is literally what "burnout" means physically. Real designs therefore carry a margin below CHF, commonly operating in the 70% range of \(q''_{max}\) rather than approaching it. On the condensing side: dropwise condensation gives a coefficient often five to ten times higher than film condensation, but it requires special, often short-lived, surface treatments and is not self-sustaining on an untreated surface — which is why almost every real condenser tube runs in film condensation despite dropwise's better numbers on paper.

04 · A worked CHF, and a design check against it

For water at 1 atm (\(h_{fg}=2257\ \text{kJ/kg}\), \(\rho_l=958\), \(\rho_v=0.6\ \text{kg/m}^3\), \(\sigma=0.0589\ \text{N/m}\)), Zuber's correlation gives \(q''_{max}=1.11\ \text{MW/m}^2\) — the same order of magnitude every heat- transfer text quotes for water at atmospheric pressure. A conservative design running at 70% of this limit operates at \(q''=0.78\ \text{MW/m}^2\); a reboiler specified instead at 0.85 MW/m² is running at \(77\%\) of CHF — above the typical 70% design range, and worth a second look before it is approved.

Key relation — Zuber critical heat flux

\[ q''_{max}=C\,h_{fg}\rho_v\left[\frac{\sigma g(\rho_l-\rho_v)}{\rho_v^2} \right]^{1/4} \]

A limit computed from fluid properties alone — no heater material, no ΔT, enters at all.

Lesson 08 · Heat exchangers

01 · Architectures, and one overall coefficient

Shell-and-tube and plate exchangers both do the same job through different geometry — plates pack far more area into a given volume, shell-and-tube handles a wider range of pressures and fouling tendencies. Either way, the overall coefficient \(U\) is built exactly like Lesson 1's resistance chain: hot-side convection, tube-wall conduction, cold-side convection, in series — and in a real, aged exchanger, two more series terms as well: a fouling resistance on each side, \(R_{f,h}\) and \(R_{f,c}\), that only grow over an operating campaign.

02 · Two design methods, and what fouling does to each

The LMTD method uses \(q=UA\,\Delta T_{lm}\), with \(\Delta T_{lm}=(\Delta T_1-\Delta T_2)/\ln(\Delta T_1/\Delta T_2)\) built from the temperature gap at each end. The effectiveness–NTU method instead defines \(\varepsilon=q/q_{max}\), \(q_{max}=C_{min}(T_{h,in}-T_{c,in})\), and \(NTU=UA/C_{min}\), with closed-form \(\varepsilon(NTU,C_r)\) relations for each flow arrangement — for the special case \(C_r=0\) (one stream condensing or evaporating, so its capacity rate is effectively infinite), \(\varepsilon=1-e^{-NTU}\) regardless of flow arrangement. Fouling resistance added in series lowers \(U\) directly; for a fixed area and inlet conditions, a lower \(U\) means less heat exchanged — outlet temperatures drift back toward their inlet values on both sides, not just one.

03 · When effectiveness–NTU is the only honest method

LMTD needs the outlet temperatures to compute \(\Delta T_{lm}\) in the first place — fine for a sizing problem, where the duty and desired outlets are already specified and the question is how much area to buy. For a rating problem — a piece of hardware already exists, \(UA\) is known, and the question is what outlet temperatures it will actually deliver — the outlets are the unknown, and LMTD would need an iterative guess-and-check loop to use at all. Effectiveness–NTU solves directly from \(UA\), \(C_{min}\), \(C_{max}\), and the inlet temperatures, with no iteration — the only honest method once the outlets are what you are actually trying to find.

04 · A worked LMTD, an NTU check, and a fouling number

A counterflow exchanger runs hot 150→90°C against cold 30→70°C. \(\Delta T_1=T_{h,in}-T_{c,out}=80\)°C, \(\Delta T_2=T_{h,out}-T_{c,in}=60\)°C, \(\Delta T_{lm}=(80-60)/\ln(80/60)=69.52\)°C. Separately, a condenser (\(C_r\approx0\)) sized to \(NTU=2\) delivers \(\varepsilon=1-e^{-2}=86.5\%\) of its maximum possible duty. And on fouling: a clean \(U_{clean}=800\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) exchanger picking up just \(R_f=0.0005\ \text{m}^2\text{K/W}\) of combined fouling resistance drops to \(U_{fouled}=1/(1/U_{clean}+R_f)=571.4\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — a 29% loss in overall coefficient from a single, modest fouling term, exactly the kind of number a fouling-monitoring program exists to catch before it grows further.

Key relation — LMTD

\[ \Delta T_{lm}=\frac{\Delta T_1-\Delta T_2}{\ln(\Delta T_1/\Delta T_2)}, \qquad q=UA\,\Delta T_{lm} \]

Valid for sizing; switch to effectiveness–NTU the moment the outlets become the unknown.

Lesson 09 · Radiation: fundamentals and exchange

01 · Blackbody laws, emissivity, and view factors

An ideal blackbody emits \(E_b=\sigma T^4\); a real gray surface emits \(E=\varepsilon\sigma T^4\), with emissivity \(\varepsilon\) (0 to 1) discounting it from the ideal. Between two surfaces, the view factor \(F_{12}\) is the fraction of radiation leaving surface 1 that lands directly on surface 2. Two bookkeeping rules make view factors tractable: reciprocity, \(A_1F_{12}=A_2F_{21}\), and the summation rule, \(\sum_j F_{1j}=1\) for a complete enclosure — every bit of radiation leaving a surface inside a closed enclosure has to land somewhere.

02 · The network method, shields, and combined losses

For two gray surfaces exchanging radiation, the net rate is one more resistance-network calculation: \(q_{12}=\sigma(T_1^4-T_2^4)/R_{tot}\), with \(R_{tot}=\dfrac{1-\varepsilon_1}{\varepsilon_1A_1}+\dfrac{1}{A_1F_{12}}+ \dfrac{1-\varepsilon_2}{\varepsilon_2A_2}\) — two surface resistances bracketing one space resistance, the exact structural echo of Lesson 1's conduction chain, now driven by \(T^4\) differences instead of \(T\). Inserting a radiation shield between two surfaces adds two more surface resistances in series (one per shield face); when every surface and shield face shares the same emissivity, \(N\) identical shields divide the unshielded heat rate by exactly \(N+1\) — verified directly from the resistances themselves, not merely quoted. Combining convection and radiation losses from one surface is instead a parallel combination: linearizing radiation into an equivalent coefficient \(h_{rad}=\varepsilon\sigma(T_s+T_{surr})(T_s^2+T_{surr}^2)\) lets total loss be written as one Newton's-law-style term, \(h_{combined}=h_{conv}+h_{rad}\).

03 · The energy-audit and thermography case

A bare, uninsulated hot pipe or vessel loses heat by convection and radiation simultaneously, and radiation is routinely the larger of the two at typical process temperatures — not a minor correction. An audit or a survey that estimates loss from convection alone, "because that's what comes to mind first," can understate the real opportunity by close to half, exactly the number 4 below computes directly rather than assumes.

04 · A worked bare pipe, oxidized and then polished

A bare steel pipe, \(D=10\ \text{cm}\), runs at \(T_s=150\)°C in \(T_\infty=35\)°C surroundings, oxidized (\(\varepsilon=0.8\)), with an estimated \(h_{conv}=10\ \ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) (Lesson 6's methods, at this modest \(\Delta T\)). \(h_{rad}=\varepsilon\sigma(T_s+T_\infty)(T_s^2+T_\infty^2)=9.09\ \ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) — comparable to \(h_{conv}\) itself. \(h_{combined}=19.09\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\), \(q''=h_{combined}\Delta T=2195\ \text{W/m}^2\), or \(q'=q''\pi D=689.7\ \text{W/m}\) of bare pipe — with radiation alone carrying 48% of the total. Polishing or foil-wrapping the same pipe to \(\varepsilon=0.1\) drops \(h_{rad}\) to 1.14, radiation's share to 10%, and the total loss to \(q'=402.3\ \text{W/m}\) — a 42% cut in total heat loss from changing surface finish alone, no insulation added at all.

Key relation — two-surface radiation network

\[ q_{12}=\frac{\sigma(T_1^4-T_2^4)}{\dfrac{1-\varepsilon_1}{\varepsilon_1A_1}+ \dfrac{1}{A_1F_{12}}+\dfrac{1-\varepsilon_2}{\varepsilon_2A_2}} \]

The same series-resistance idea as every other lesson in this course, one more time.

Lesson 10 · Mass transfer analogy

01 · One mathematics, two names

Fick's law of diffusion, \(N''=-D_{AB}\,dC/dx\), has exactly the shape of Fourier's law, \(q''=-k\,dT/dx\) — mass diffusing down a concentration gradient the same way heat diffuses down a temperature gradient. Convective mass transfer carries the same structure forward too: a convective mass transfer coefficient \(h_m\) plays the role \(h\) plays in Newton's law, \(N''=h_m(C_s-C_\infty)\) standing in for \(q''=h(T_s-T_\infty)\).

02 · The heat–mass analogy, and why it works so well for air and water specifically

The Chilton–Colburn analogy connects the two convection coefficients, \(h/h_m\approx\rho c_p\,Le^{2/3}\), where the Lewis number \(Le=\alpha/D_{AB}\) compares thermal diffusivity to mass diffusivity. For water vapor diffusing in air specifically, \(D_{AB}\approx2.5\times10^{-5}\ \text{m}^2\text{/s}\) happens to sit very close to air's own thermal diffusivity — a genuine physical coincidence for this particular pair of substances, not a universal law — giving \(Le\approx1\) and the simplified Lewis relation \(h/h_m\approx\rho c_p\). That coincidence is exactly why wet-bulb thermometry and simple evaporative-cooling estimates work as well, and as simply, as they do for air-water problems, and why the same shortcut should not be assumed for an arbitrary pair of substances.

03 · Why evaporation cools what is left behind

Vaporizing a mass of liquid costs its latent heat of vaporization. With no external heat source feeding that cost, the energy has to come from somewhere — and the only reservoir on hand is the sensible heat of the liquid (and immediate surroundings) left behind, which cools as a direct result. This is the entire physical mechanism behind a wet-bulb thermometer, evaporative cooling pads, and the cooling felt from wet skin in moving air — not a separate "evaporation is cold" fact, but the same energy balance as every other lesson in this course, applied to a phase change instead of a temperature difference.

04 · A worked evaporation estimate, reusing this course's own convection numbers

Lesson 4's flat-plate result gave \(h=20\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) for forced air at a modest speed. Using the Lewis relation with \(\rho_{air}=1.15\ \ \text{kg/m}^3\), \(c_{p,air}=1007\ \text{J/kgK}\): \(h_m=h/(\rho c_p)=0.0173\ \text{m/s}\). Taking a representative (given, not a claimed precise psychrometric lookup) vapor-density difference between a wet surface and hot, dry Gulf air, \(\Delta\rho_v=0.018\ \text{kg/m}^3\): \(N''=h_m\Delta\rho_v=3.11e-04\ \text{kg/m}^2\text{s}=1.12\ \ \text{kg/m}^2\text{hr}\) — a real, order-of-magnitude-plausible desert evaporation rate. Converted through \(h_{fg}\approx2.45\ \text{MJ/kg}\), this is \(q_{evap}=N''h_{fg}=763\ \text{W/m}^2\) of cooling drawn from the surface left behind — a substantial number, on the same order as this course's other worked convective fluxes, and the same "sensible heat pays for the latent heat" bookkeeping 3 describes.

Key relation — the Lewis relation, air-water only

\[ \frac{h}{h_m}\approx\rho c_p \qquad (Le\approx1\text{ for water vapor in air}) \]

A shortcut earned by a specific numerical coincidence — check \(Le\) before reusing it for any other fluid pair.

Lesson 11 · Thermal design mini-project

01 · Framing the brief: sizing, then margining

This course's capstone is a synthesis of Lesson 8: given a duty (a process stream's flow rate, properties, and required temperature change) and a coolant or heating stream, size a shell-and- tube exchanger to meet it, then carry a real fouling margin on top — because the duty and desired outlets are specified in advance, this is squarely a sizing problem, and the LMTD method from Lesson 8 is the right tool to reach for, not effectiveness–NTU.

02 · The full sizing chain

The process stream's duty follows directly from its own energy balance, \(Q=\dot m_h c_{p,h}(T_{h,in}-T_{h,out})\); the coolant's required flow or outlet temperature follows from the same energy balance applied to it, \(Q=\dot m_c c_{p,c}(T_{c,out}-T_{c,in})\). With all four terminal temperatures now known, \(\Delta T_{lm}\) builds exactly as in Lesson 8, and a clean-service area follows from \(A_{clean}=Q/(U_{clean}\Delta T_{lm})\), using a representative clean-\(U\) figure for the service (a given design input, not a fabricated precision). A fouling margin is then spent one of two mathematically equivalent ways: buy extra area, \(A_{design}=A_{clean}\times(1+\text{margin})\), or keep the area fixed and instead derate the design \(U\) by the same factor, \(U_{design}=U_{clean}/(1+\text{margin})\) — the same engineering decision, expressed two different ways on two different spec sheets.

03 · Critiquing the design against how it actually fails

Real fouling is not a one-time step to the design margin and then a flat line — it accumulates over an operating campaign, and the first symptom of it outrunning the design margin is a duty shortfall at unchanged flow: outlet temperatures drift back toward their inlet values, long before anything resembling a dramatic failure. At that point the practical options are a flow increase (which pushes back against the rest of the piping system's own curve, a cost this lesson's numbers do not include) or a cleaning outage. The fixed-percentage margin itself is a simplification worth stating plainly, not defending as exact — real fouling rates are time- and service-dependent, which is exactly why a trended duty calculation, not a one-time design check, is what actually catches the problem in the field. If any part of a real service could locally vaporize, Lesson 7's CHF caution becomes a second, independent failure mode to check — fouling margin does not protect against that at all; and an un-insulated unit runs Lesson 9's bare-surface loss on top of everything else.

04 · The worked capstone

Hot oil, \(\dot m_h=4\ \text{kg/s}\), \(c_{p,h}=2100\ \ \text{J/kgK}\), cools 95→60°C: \(Q=\dot m_h c_{p,h}\Delta T=294\ \text{kW}\). Cooling water, \(\dot m_c=3\ \text{kg/s}\), \(c_{p,c}=4180\ \text{J/kgK}\), enters at \(T_{c,in}=25\)°C and must leave at \(T_{c,out}=T_{c,in}+Q/(\dot m_c c_{p,c})=48.4\)°C to absorb the same duty. Counterflow: \(\Delta T_1=46.56\)°C, \(\Delta T_2=35\)°C, \(\Delta T_{lm}=40.50\)°C. At a representative clean \(U_{clean}=450\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) for this liquid-liquid service, \(A_{clean}=Q/(U_{clean}\Delta T_{lm})=16.13\ \text{m}^2\). Carrying a 20% fouling margin, \(A_{design}=19.36\ \text{m}^2\) — equivalently, the same margin as a derated \(U_{design}=U_{clean}/1.2=375 \ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}\) at the 19.36 m² area, exactly as 2 describes: two spec sheets, one design decision.

Key relation — sizing with a fouling margin

\[ A_{design}=A_{clean}(1+m)=\frac{Q}{U_{clean}\,\Delta T_{lm}}(1+m) =\frac{Q}{[U_{clean}/(1+m)]\,\Delta T_{lm}} \]

Extra area and a derated design U are the same margin, algebraically, every time.
