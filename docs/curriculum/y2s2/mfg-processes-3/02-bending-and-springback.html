Bending and springback

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Compute bend allowance for a 90° bend, R = 6 mm, t = 3 mm.
  • Springback scales with which material ratio — and why does high-strength steel spring back more than mild steel?
  • V-die bending force: what happens to required tonnage when sheet thickness doubles?

Objectives

  1. Compute bend allowance so a flat blank folds to the drawing's dimensions.
  2. Estimate V-bending force and pick a press with margin, units carried through.
  3. Explain what drives springback and name the two families of compensation.

Key equations — sourced (Groover, Ch. 20)

Bend allowance and V-die bending force
BA = 2π A360 (R + Kba t)(1)
F = Kbf · TS · w · t2D(2)

In (1), A is the bend angle, R the inside radius, and Kba locates the neutral axis (Groover: 0.33 for tight bends R < 2t, 0.5 for R ≥ 2t) — the neutral axis migrates inward as bending sharpens, which is the whole reason blanks are not sized by naive centerline arithmetic. In (2), TS is tensile strength, w bend width, t thickness, D the die opening, Kbf ≈ 1.33 for V-dies.

Springback: on unloading, the elastic part of the through-thickness strain distribution recovers; the bend opens and its radius grows. The drivers fall straight out of beam elasticity — springback grows with the material's yield-to-modulus ratio Y/E and with R/t (gentler bends are more elastic overall). That is why high-strength steels and aluminum (high Y/E) spring back hard, and why annealed mild steel is forgiving.

Condensed worked example — one brake bend

90° V-bend, mild steel TS = 450 MPa, t = 2 mm, w = 250 mm, R = 5 mm, die opening D = 25 mm:

R/t = 2.5 ≥ 2 → K_ba = 0.5
BA = 2π(90/360)(5 + 0.5×2) = (π/2)(6) = 9.4 mm of blank consumed by the bend
F = 1.33 × 450 × 250 × 2² / 25 = 23.9 kN (≈ 2.4 tonnes) — a small brake job; specify press tonnage with margin over this instantaneous peak.

Misconceptions

"Springback is a quality defect — good tooling eliminates it."
Springback is Hooke's law; it cannot be eliminated, only compensated. Family one: overbend past the target so recovery lands on it. Family two: bottoming/coining — plastically setting the bend zone at the stroke's end. Roll-forming lines compensate in the flower pattern's last passes. Fighting the physics instead of budgeting for it is the actual defect.
"Stronger sheet needs a tighter radius allowance, weaker sheet more."
Backwards on both ends. Minimum bend radius is a ductility limit (crack the outer fiber and no allowance saves you), while springback scales with Y/E. A high-strength, low-ductility sheet needs a larger minimum radius and more overbend — the two requirements stack, they don't trade.
