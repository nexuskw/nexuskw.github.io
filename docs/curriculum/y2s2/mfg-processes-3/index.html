MFG 254 · YEAR 2 · SEMESTER 2

Manufacturing Processes III — Sheet, Plastics & Additive

The discrete-products half of manufacturing: sheet-metal working with springback treated properly, plastics processing, packaging operations, and additive — Kirby's panel lines and KDD's filling halls as the anchors.

11 lessons1 of 11 at full depth2 Core-60 lessons
Start lesson 01 Full curriculum

What you'll learn

✓Shearing, blanking, and punching; clearance and its burr signature.
✓Bend allowance from neutral-axis geometry; bending force; and springback — why it happens, how much to expect, and the compensation strategies press brakes actually use.
✓Drawability and the limiting draw ratio; blankholder force and defect map — wrinkling versus tearing.
✓Progressive roll forming of profiles; line balancing and tooling.
✓Mechanical and hydraulic presses; die sets and progressive dies.
✓Screw extruder anatomy and melt behavior.

Syllabus

01

Sheet-metal cutting operations

Shearing, blanking, and punching; clearance and its burr signature. Cutting force estimates. Nesting and material yield as quiet profit levers.

Taught from — Groover, Ch. 20

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does punch-die clearance set burr height?
  • What does nesting efficiency do to material cost?
02

Bending and springbackStudy guide

Bend allowance from neutral-axis geometry; bending force; and springback — why it happens, how much to expect, and the compensation strategies press brakes actually use. Kirby's roll-formed and brake-formed building panels as the standing example.

Taught from — CORE 60 · A8 · Groover, Ch. 20; Kalpakjian & Schmid, sheet-forming chapter

Checkpoint questions
  • Compute bend allowance for a 90° bend, R = 6 mm, t = 3 mm.
  • Springback scales with which material ratio — and why does high-strength steel spring back more than mild steel?
  • V-die bending force: what happens to required tonnage when sheet thickness doubles?
03

Deep drawing and stretch forming

Drawability and the limiting draw ratio; blankholder force and defect map — wrinkling versus tearing. Redraws and ironing. Cans and cookware as engineered products.

Taught from — Groover, Ch. 20

Checkpoint questions
  • What limits the drawing ratio in one hit?
  • Wrinkling versus tearing: which control fixes each?
04

Roll forming and high-volume sheet lines

Progressive roll forming of profiles; line balancing and tooling. Coil handling, leveling, and slitting upstream. The continuous logic of Kirby-style panel production.

Taught from — Kalpakjian & Schmid, sheet-forming chapter

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does roll forming beat brake bending at volume?
  • What does the flower pattern encode?
05

Presses, dies, and tooling

Mechanical and hydraulic presses; die sets and progressive dies. Tonnage curves and where presses are overloaded. Die maintenance as a production-uptime discipline.

Taught from — Groover, Ch. 20; Kalpakjian & Schmid

Checkpoint questions
  • Mechanical versus hydraulic press: where does each win?
  • Why is die maintenance a production KPI?
06

Polymer melt processing: extrusion

Screw extruder anatomy and melt behavior. Die swell and downstream sizing. Cable insulation extrusion — Gulf Cable's polymer line — as the worked context.

Taught from — Groover, plastics chapters

Checkpoint questions
  • What does die swell reveal about polymer melts?
  • Why is cable insulation extruded over a moving conductor rather than moulded?
07

Injection molding

The molding cycle: fill, pack, cool, eject. Gate and runner logic; shrinkage and warpage. Defects — shorts, flash, sinks — mapped to their process causes.

Taught from — Groover, plastics chapters

Checkpoint questions
  • Map short shot, flash, and sink marks to their process causes.
  • Why does gate location decide warpage?
08

Blow molding, thermoforming, and film

Bottles, trays, and films — the packaging trio. Parison control and wall-thickness distribution. Food-grade material and hygiene constraints introduced.

Taught from — Groover, plastics chapters

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does parison control set bottle wall thickness?
  • Which food-contact constraints shape material choice?
09

Packaging and filling operationsCore 60 · in production

Filling, capping, labeling, date-coding, and case packing as a machine system; line speed, accumulation, and the V-curve logic that protects the filler. Hygienic design and changeover discipline. The KDD-style dairy line read as one machine.

Taught from — CORE 60 · A16 · Kalpakjian & Schmid; Groover, assembly/automation chapters

Checkpoint questions
  • Why is the filler the protected center of a packaging line, and what does the V-curve of speeds around it do?
  • Name two hygienic-design features that separate food-line equipment from generic machinery.
  • Why does changeover reduction (SMED) buy more capacity on packaging lines than almost anywhere else?
10

Additive manufacturing processes

PBF, DED, material extrusion, and binder jetting with their materials and defect signatures. Post-processing realities. Where AM fits a maintenance organization: fixtures and unobtainable spares.

Taught from — Groover, additive manufacturing chapter

Checkpoint questions
  • Rank PBF, FDM, binder jetting by surface finish and by strength honesty.
  • What post-processing does each AM family assume?
11

Design for manufacturing and assembly

DFM/DFA rules that move cost: part-count reduction, self-locating features, tolerance realism. Redesign exercises on real hardware. The conversation between design and the floor formalized.

Taught from — Groover, DFM sections; Kalpakjian & Schmid

Checkpoint questions
  • Give two DFM rules that remove cost without removing function.
  • Why does part-count reduction beat per-part cheapening?
  • Groover, Fundamentals of Modern Manufacturing (Wiley) — sheet metal & plastics chapters
  • Kalpakjian & Schmid, Manufacturing Engineering and Technology (Pearson)
