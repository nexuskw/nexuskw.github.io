PLC 255 · YEAR 2 · SEMESTER 2

PLC Fundamentals I

The plant's reflexes: PLC hardware, scan cycle, ladder logic, timers and counters, and disciplined I/O wiring — taught to read-and-troubleshoot level first, author level second.

01

Control before PLCs: relay logic

Contactors, control relays, and hardwired interlocks. Reading a motor-starter schematic fluently. The relay heritage that ladder logic still speaks.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Read a DOL starter schematic: trace the seal-in path.
  • What does a control relay isolate?

Taught from — NPTEL: Industrial Automation and Control alternative (enable captions); supplementary video: RealPars In Arabic — alternative

02

PLC architecture and the scan cycle

CPU, I/O, rack, and power supply; the read–execute–write scan and its timing consequences. Why outputs update when they do. Watchdogs and fault states.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Order the scan cycle's three phases and their consequence for fast pulses.
  • What does the watchdog timer catch?

Taught from — NPTEL: Industrial Automation and Control alternative (enable captions); supplementary video: RealPars In Arabic — alternative

03

Ladder logic essentialsCore 60 · guide queued

Contacts, coils, seal-in circuits, and interlocks — the core patterns of industrial logic, each drawn and traced. Start/stop of a motor with safety interlocks built the standard way. Reading unfamiliar ladder cold: the transition engineer's most bankable automation skill.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • In a seal-in rung, what keeps the motor contactor energized after Start is released — and what breaks it?
  • Why are stop functions wired through NC contacts: which failure does that choice survive?
  • Within one scan cycle, at what point do physical outputs actually change state?

Taught from — CORE 60 · G1 · NPTEL: Industrial Automation and Control alternative (enable captions); supplementary video: RealPars In Arabic — alternative

04

Timers and counters

On-delay, off-delay, and retentive timers; up/down counters. Debounce, staging, and timeout patterns. A star-delta starter sequence programmed and traced.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • On-delay versus off-delay: give one use each.
  • Why do retentive timers survive power cycles — and when is that dangerous?

Digital I/O wiring

Sinking/sourcing, wet and dry contacts, and field-device interfacing. Fusing and commons in the I/O rack. The wiring faults behind most 'PLC problems'.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Wire an NPN sensor to a sinking input: what goes where?
  • Why do I/O commons trip up mixed-vendor panels?

Analog I/O and scaling

4–20 mA and 0–10 V channels; raw counts to engineering units scaled correctly. Resolution and out-of-range handling. A transmitter read into a displayed value end to end.
