KDM 252 · YEAR 2 · SEMESTER 2

Kinematics & Dynamics of Machinery

Mechanisms in motion: linkages, cams, gear geometry and trains, flywheels, and machine balancing — how rotation becomes useful motion, and where the shaking forces that vibration analysts chase are born.

11 lessons0 of 11 at full depth
Start lesson 01 Full curriculum

What you'll learn

✓Links, joints, and kinematic chains; Grübler's mobility count.
✓Graphical and analytical position solutions.
✓Acceleration polygons and the Coriolis component in sliding contacts.
✓Follower motion laws — uniform, harmonic, cycloidal — and their acceleration signatures.
✓The involute and why conjugate action matters.
✓Simple, compound, and planetary trains; ratio arithmetic including the tabular planetary method.

Syllabus

01

Mechanisms and mobility

Links, joints, and kinematic chains; Grübler's mobility count. Four-bar and slider-crank as the industrial workhorses. Inversions and what each one becomes.

Taught from — NPTEL: Theory of Machines

Checkpoint questions
  • Count mobility for a four-bar: what does DOF = 1 buy?
  • Name two inversions of the slider-crank and their machines.
02

Position and velocity analysis

Graphical and analytical position solutions. Velocity by instant centers and by vector loops. Reading mechanism speed ratios without simulation.

Taught from — NPTEL: Theory of Machines

Checkpoint questions
  • Locate instant centers of a four-bar: what do they give you instantly?
  • Why does a mechanism's speed ratio vary through its cycle?
03

Acceleration analysis

Acceleration polygons and the Coriolis component in sliding contacts. Slider-crank acceleration solved completely. The source terms for inertia forces.

Taught from — NPTEL: Theory of Machines

Checkpoint questions
  • Where does Coriolis acceleration appear in a quick-return mechanism?
  • Why does acceleration, not velocity, size inertia forces?
04

Cams

Follower motion laws — uniform, harmonic, cycloidal — and their acceleration signatures. Profile synthesis and pressure angle. Why cam wear patterns encode their motion law. Filling-machine cams as the local example.

Taught from — NPTEL: Theory of Machines

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does cycloidal motion beat harmonic for high-speed cams?
  • What does pressure angle limit in a cam-follower?
05

Gear tooth geometry

The involute and why conjugate action matters. Module, pressure angle, and interference. Backlash as both necessity and defect.

Taught from — NPTEL: Theory of Machines

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does the involute tolerate center-distance error?
  • What does interference between teeth physically destroy?
06

Gear trains

Simple, compound, and planetary trains; ratio arithmetic including the tabular planetary method. Gearbox ratios decomposed. The gear-mesh frequencies that will reappear in vibration spectra.

Taught from — NPTEL: Theory of Machines

Checkpoint questions
  • Compute a compound train's ratio: what multiplies?
  • Why do planetary trains need the tabular method?
07

Static and inertia force analysis

Force transmission through mechanisms; free-body chains under motion. Equivalent offset inertia forces. Bearing loads born from acceleration, not just weight.

Taught from — NPTEL: Theory of Machines

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do accelerating links load bearings beyond gravity?
  • What is an equivalent offset inertia force?
08

Clutches and brakes

Friction torque of plate and cone clutches under uniform-pressure and uniform-wear assumptions — and which one to trust in service. Band and shoe brakes with self-energizing behavior located. The heat released in every engagement, and where it goes.

Taught from — NPTEL: Theory of Machines

Checkpoint questions
  • Uniform pressure versus uniform wear: which predicts less torque, and why is it the honest one for a worn clutch?
  • What makes a brake shoe self-energizing — and when does that become self-locking?
09

Flywheels and speed fluctuation

Turning-moment diagrams and coefficient of fluctuation. Flywheel sizing from energy variation. Presses and compressors as the plants' flywheel machines.

Taught from — NPTEL: Theory of Machines

Checkpoint questions
  • Read a turning-moment diagram: what does the shaded area mean?
  • What does the coefficient of fluctuation specify?
10

Balancing of rotating and reciprocating masses

Single- and multi-plane rotating balance done with vectors. Reciprocating unbalance and why it can't be fully balanced. The kinematic truth behind field balancing jobs.

Taught from — NPTEL: Theory of Machines

Checkpoint questions
  • Why can rotating unbalance be fully balanced but reciprocating not?
  • Two-plane balancing: why two?
11

Governors and gyroscopic effects

Centrifugal governors as proportional controllers made of iron. Gyroscopic couples on turbine and vehicle bearings. Legacy topics that still explain modern behavior.

Taught from — NPTEL: Theory of Machines

Checkpoint questions
  • What does a governor control that a flywheel cannot?
  • Where do gyroscopic couples load a turbine's bearings?
