01 · Compression work, derived

Compressing a gas from \(P_1\) to \(P_2\) costs specific work depending on the process path: isothermal compression (temperature held constant, requiring continuous cooling during compression) costs the least, \(w_{iso}=RT\ln(P_2/P_1)\); adiabatic (isentropic, no heat rejected during compression) costs the most, \(w_{adia}=\frac{k}{k-1}RT\left[(P_2/P_1)^{(k-1)/k}-1\right]\). For air (\(R=0.287\ \text{kJ/kg·K}\), \(k=1.4\)) compressing \(1\) to \(7\ \text{bar}\) at \(20^\circ\text{C}\): \(w_{iso}\approx163.6\), \(w_{adia}\approx218.9\ \text{kJ/kg}\) — real single-stage machines run much closer to the adiabatic case than the unreachable isothermal ideal, since genuinely holding temperature constant through a real compression process is impractical.

02 · Multistaging and intercooling

Multistaging splits the total pressure rise across two or more stages, cooling the gas back toward inlet temperature between them (intercooling) before the next stage compresses it further — since each stage then compresses cooler, lower-specific-volume gas, splitting the compression this way genuinely reduces total work, moving the real process closer to the isothermal ideal without the impracticality of continuous cooling during compression itself. For the same \(1\) to \(7\ \text{bar}\) rise, split into two equal-pressure-ratio stages with perfect intercooling between them, total specific work drops to about \(188.6\ \text{kJ/kg}\) — roughly \(14\%\) less than the single-stage adiabatic case, a real, worthwhile saving that is exactly why multistage compressors with intercoolers are the standard, not an exotic option, for any meaningfully large pressure ratio.

03 · Receivers, dryers, and dew point

A receiver (storage tank downstream of the compressor) smooths pulsating compressor output into steady supply pressure and provides buffer capacity against short demand spikes, reducing how often the compressor cycles. Compressed air carries the moisture that was in the intake air, now concentrated by compression — an air dryer (refrigerated, removing moisture by cooling and condensing it out, or desiccant, removing it by adsorption) controls the compressed air's dew point, preventing condensation forming downstream in pipework, instruments, and pneumatic equipment as the air cools after compression. Choosing dryer type and target dew point is itself an application decision: a refrigerated dryer reaching a moderate dew point suffices for general shop air; a desiccant dryer reaching a much lower dew point is needed wherever the compressed air will be exposed to near-freezing conditions or feeds moisture-sensitive instrumentation.

04 · Leak economics — the plant's most expensive utility per joule

A compressed air leak is a continuous loss, running every hour the system is pressurized, whether or not any equipment is actually using air — unlike most other utility losses, which at least scale with production. A modest leak losing \(8\ \text{L/s}\) of free air, evaluated even at the ideal adiabatic compression-work basis of 1 (\(\approx2.1\ \text{kW}\), real electrical input higher still once compressor and motor losses are included), costs on the order of \(18{,}000\ \text{kWh}\) over a year of continuous operation — from one leak, easily overlooked amid the hiss of an already-noisy compressor room. This is precisely why compressed air, despite being "just air," is routinely cited as a plant's most expensive utility per unit of useful energy delivered, and why a systematic leak survey belongs in the same category as Lesson 3's steam trap survey — a high-return, low-cost audit that a quiet system does not announce the need for on its own.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Isothermal vs. adiabatic work

Problem. Air at 20°C (293 K) is compressed from 1 to 7 bar. Using R=0.287 kJ/kg·K and k=1.4, find the isothermal and adiabatic specific work.

Solution. \(w_{iso}=RT\ln(P_2/P_1)=0.287\times293\times\ln(7)=163.6\ \text{kJ/kg}\). \(w_{adia}=\frac{1.4}{0.4}\times0.287\times293\times[7^{0.4/1.4}-1]\approx218.9\ \text{kJ/kg}\).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

The value of intercooling

Problem. The same 1-to-7-bar compression, split into two stages with perfect intercooling, requires about 188.6 kJ/kg total. Find the percentage work savings compared to the single-stage adiabatic case, and explain in words why splitting the compression reduces work at all.

Solution. Savings \(=(218.9-188.6)/218.9\approx13.8\%\). Splitting the compression and cooling between stages means the second stage compresses cooler, lower-specific-volume gas than it would have without intercooling — since compression work depends on the specific volume of the gas being compressed, starting the second stage cooler genuinely reduces the work that stage requires.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Annual cost of a small leak

Problem. A leak loses 8 L/s of free air (density 1.2 kg/m³). Using the adiabatic specific work of 218.9 kJ/kg as an ideal-basis estimate, find the power lost and the energy lost over a year of continuous operation (8760 hours).

Solution. Mass flow \(=8/1000\times1.2=0.0096\ \text{kg/s}\). Power \(=0.0096\times218.9\approx2.10\ \text{kW}\) (ideal-gas-work basis; real electrical input would be higher). Annual energy \(=2.10\times8760\approx18{,}400\ \text{kWh}\) — from a single modest leak.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A maintenance program running a quarterly ultrasonic leak survey across compressed air distribution — tagging and fixing leaks found, then tracking total estimated leak flow over time — is treating 4's arithmetic as a real, recurring budget line rather than a one-time cleanup; a distribution system that has never been surveyed typically loses a surprisingly large fraction of total compressor output to leaks that individually seem too small to matter.

A plant specifying a new multistage compressor with intercooling for a demanding pressure ratio, rather than defaulting to a cheaper single-stage unit, is applying 1 and 2's arithmetic as a capital decision — the intercooled machine costs more upfront but the ongoing energy savings, run continuously over the equipment's service life, are a real, computable number worth weighing against that upfront difference.
