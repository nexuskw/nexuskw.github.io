Lecture

01 · Reheat

Reheat interrupts turbine expansion partway, routes the steam back through the boiler for a second heating pass at constant (or near-constant) pressure, then continues expansion in a low-pressure turbine section. The primary reason plants pay for the extra piping and boiler surface is the same quality problem Lesson 1 flagged: pushing boiler pressure higher (itself an efficiency lever) drives turbine-exit quality down further into the wet region, risking blade erosion. Reheat lets the plant run a high boiler pressure for its efficiency benefit while still protecting the low-pressure turbine's final stages — a straight expansion from a high boiler pressure to condenser pressure without reheat can leave quality well below \(0.80\); reheating partway through commonly restores it above \(0.90\).

02 · Regeneration (feedwater heating)

Regeneration extracts a small fraction of steam partway through the turbine and uses it to preheat the feedwater on its way back to the boiler, rather than letting that steam do its full possible expansion work. This seems counterintuitive — deliberately extracting work-capable steam early — but it raises the average temperature at which the remaining heat is added in the boiler (since feedwater now enters hotter), which by the same average-temperature efficiency logic from Lesson 1 improves overall cycle efficiency, typically by several percentage points in a real plant with multiple feedwater heater stages — a genuine, well-established efficiency gain, not a minor refinement.

03 · Locating irreversibility on the h-s diagram

A real turbine does not expand isentropically — friction and other losses mean the actual exit state lands at a higher enthalpy than the ideal isentropic exit \(h_{4s}\), for the same exit pressure. Isentropic efficiency \(\eta_t=(h_3-h_4)/(h_3-h_{4s})\) quantifies exactly how much of the ideal work was actually captured — typically \(85\)–\(95\%\) for well-maintained modern turbines. The same idea applies to pumps (in reverse: actual work input exceeds the ideal isentropic work) and, differently, to heat exchangers and throttles, where irreversibility shows up as increased entropy generation rather than a simple efficiency ratio. Locating where on the cycle the largest such gaps occur is exactly how Lesson 10's exergy analysis later prioritizes where real improvement effort belongs.

04 · Heat rate — the operator's efficiency language

Plant operators rarely quote "thermal efficiency" directly — they quote heat rate: the heat input required per unit of net electrical output, commonly in kJ/kWh (or Btu/kWh). Since \(1\ \text{kWh}=3600\ \text{kJ}\) of useful output, heat rate and efficiency are directly related: \[ \text{heat rate}=\frac{3600}{\eta}\ \text{kJ/kWh}. \] A cycle at 27.9% efficiency (Lesson 1's base example) corresponds to a heat rate around \(12{,}900\ \text{kJ/kWh}\) — a real plant with reheat and multi-stage regeneration would push efficiency well above this base case, and heat rate correspondingly down, which is exactly why heat rate trending is the number a plant's operations team actually watches day to day, not a raw efficiency percentage.

Key relations — this lesson

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Turbine isentropic efficiency

Problem. A turbine has inlet enthalpy h3=3115.0 kJ/kg, isentropic exit enthalpy h4s=2297.0 kJ/kg, and actual exit enthalpy h4=2350.0 kJ/kg. Find the isentropic efficiency.

Solution. Ideal work \(=3115.0-2297.0=818.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\). Actual work \(=3115.0-2350.0=765.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\). \(\eta_t=765.0/818.0=93.5\%\).

Efficiency to heat rate

Problem. A cycle runs at 27.9% thermal efficiency. Find its heat rate in kJ/kWh.

Solution. \( \text{heat rate}=3600/0.279\approx12{,}903\ \text{kJ/kWh}. \)

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Why regeneration helps despite extracting steam early

Problem. Explain, without computing numbers, why extracting steam mid-expansion to preheat feedwater raises overall cycle efficiency, even though that extracted steam does not complete its full possible expansion in the turbine.

Solution. Preheating the feedwater means the boiler receives water already partway toward boiling temperature, so the remaining heat added in the boiler is added, on average, at a higher temperature than it would be feeding cold feedwater directly. Since cycle efficiency rises with the average temperature of heat addition (the same logic behind boiler pressure and superheat as efficiency levers), this temperature increase more than compensates for the smaller amount of work lost by not fully expanding the extracted steam.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A utilities team noticing a slow, sustained rise in heat rate over several weeks — rather than a sudden jump — checks turbine isentropic efficiency and feedwater heater performance before suspecting the boiler itself, per 3 and 2: a slowly fouling feedwater heater or a turbine losing internal clearance both degrade heat rate gradually, in a way a single daily reading would not obviously flag, but a trend clearly would.

A plant justifying the capital cost of adding a reheat stage to an existing cycle documents both benefits from 1 — the efficiency gain and the turbine-protection gain — in the business case, not efficiency alone, since the maintenance-cost and reliability argument is often what actually tips a marginal capital decision toward approval.
