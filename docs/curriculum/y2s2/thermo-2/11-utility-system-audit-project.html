01 · The audit as one connected balance

A real utility audit does not treat steam, compressed air, and chilled water as three separate exercises — it closes mass and energy balances across the whole utility deck simultaneously, since a boiler's fuel input, a compressor's electrical input, and a chiller's electrical input all ultimately appear on the same utility bill, competing for the same limited improvement budget. Every lesson in this course supplies one piece of that balance: Lessons 1-2 the steam generation side, Lesson 3 the steam distribution and condensate side, Lessons 4-6 alternative and auxiliary cycles, Lessons 7-8 the cooling and compressed air utilities, Lesson 9 the combustion input driving it all, Lesson 10 the common accounting framework tying every loss together on one footing.

02 · Closing the balances, ranking the losses

Closing a balance means every input (fuel, makeup water, purchased electricity) is accounted for against every output (useful process heat, useful cooling, useful compressed air) plus every identified loss (stack loss, blowdown, trap failures, leaks, condenser approach) — any unaccounted gap is exactly what a good audit hunts down before finalizing its numbers, since an unclosed balance means some real loss has not yet been found or correctly sized. Once closed, ranking every loss on a common basis (energy cost, or exergy destroyed, per Lesson 10) produces exactly the prioritized list 1's figure shows — the audit's actual deliverable, not an equipment condition report.

03 · Proposing fixes with payback

Every proposed fix pairs an estimated implementation cost against its estimated annual savings, yielding a simple payback period (cost divided by annual savings) — the practical, easily communicated metric that turns "we should fix this" into a fundable project a budget owner can actually compare against competing capital requests. A steam trap survey and repair program, a compressed air leak survey, and combustion tuning are routinely among the shortest-payback items on any utility audit — months, not years — precisely because they cost little to execute and address losses (Lessons 3, 8, 9) that are large, continuous, and easily overlooked without a deliberate survey.

04 · The Tier-2 utilities lesson, practiced end to end

This capstone project is deliberately not a single new calculation — it is every prior lesson's arithmetic, applied together against one representative factory utility deck: steam generation efficiency and heat rate (Lessons 1-2), condensate and trap losses (Lesson 3), cooling tower and condenser performance (Lesson 7), compressed air generation and leak cost (Lesson 8), combustion tuning (Lesson 9), and exergy-based prioritization (Lesson 10) — closed into one balance, ranked into one priority list, and costed into one set of payback estimates. This is precisely the working method a utilities engineer actually uses on a real audit, and precisely why this course builds toward it rather than treating it as a separate, disconnected final exercise.

Key discipline — the audit method

Close the balance → rank losses on a common basis → cost each fix with payback → fund the shortest paybacks first. Every number traces to a specific earlier lesson's formula.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Full audit walkthrough

Problem. A factory utility audit finds: (a) boiler running 25% excess air where 15% would suffice, (b) three failed-open steam traps, (c) a compressed air leak network estimated at 40 L/s total, (d) a cooling tower running a 10°C approach where 6°C is achievable. Rank these for investigation priority and state the reasoning, without needing exact numbers for each.

Solution. A defensible ranking, using this course's own lessons: excess air correction (Lesson 9) is typically cheap (a burner trim) and combustion is usually the single largest exergy destruction term in the plant (Lesson 10, 3) — likely highest priority. Failed steam traps (Lesson 3) and compressed air leaks (Lesson 8) are both continuous, easily-sized losses with short paybacks — next priority, roughly comparable, with the actual ranking between them depending on their computed kW figures. The cooling tower approach gap (Lesson 7) affects condenser pressure and therefore cycle efficiency, but correcting it (cleaning fill, or capital tower work) is typically a larger, longer-payback fix — reasonably placed last among these four, not because it does not matter, but because its fix is more expensive relative to its savings than the other three.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Simple payback

Problem. A steam trap survey and repair program costs $8,000 to execute and is estimated to save $32,000 per year in reduced steam loss. Find the simple payback period.

Solution. \( \text{Payback}=\text{cost}/\text{annual savings}=8{,}000/32{,}000=0.25\ \text{years}\approx3\ \text{months}. \)

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Why an unclosed balance is a red flag

Problem. An audit’s fuel input, converted to energy terms, exceeds the sum of all identified useful outputs and identified losses by a significant margin. Explain what this gap means and what the auditor should do before finalizing the report.

Solution. The gap means some real loss exists that has not yet been identified or correctly measured — energy cannot simply disappear from a correctly closed balance. The auditor should investigate further before finalizing the report, checking for unmeasured or underestimated losses (an unsurveyed steam trap population, an uncharacterized compressed air leak rate, an unmeasured stack loss) rather than reporting a ranked priority list built on an admittedly incomplete accounting.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A utilities manager presenting audit findings to plant leadership leads with the ranked, costed priority list from 2 and 3 — not with a raw equipment inspection report — because a payback-ranked list is what a budget decision actually requires; leadership approves projects by comparing paybacks against other capital requests, not by reading an equipment condition narrative.

A facility running its own internal utility audit annually, rather than only when a consultant is hired, treats this course's method as a standing, repeatable procedure — closing the balance, re-ranking losses, re-costing fixes — since a plant's loss profile genuinely changes year to year as equipment ages, operating patterns shift, and previously-funded fixes are implemented and drop off the priority list.
