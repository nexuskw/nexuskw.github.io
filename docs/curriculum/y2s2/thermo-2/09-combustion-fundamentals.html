01 · Stoichiometry — the air a fuel actually needs

Complete combustion of methane follows \(CH_4+2O_2\rightarrow CO_2+2H_2O\): exactly \(2\) mol of oxygen per mol of fuel, no more, no less, for complete combustion with nothing left over. Since air is only about \(21\%\) oxygen by mole, the air actually required is \(2/0.21\approx9.52\) mol air per mol fuel — converting to a mass basis (methane \(16\ \text{kg/kmol}\), air \(\approx28.97\ \text{kg/kmol}\)) gives a stoichiometric air-fuel ratio of \(\approx17.2\ \text{kg air/kg fuel}\). This ratio is fixed by chemistry alone, identical for every burner running this fuel — what varies burner to burner is how much air beyond this minimum is actually supplied.

02 · Why real combustion runs excess air

Supplying exactly the stoichiometric air requirement in practice, with real (imperfect) mixing of fuel and air, leaves pockets of incomplete combustion — unburned fuel, carbon monoxide, soot — so real burners deliberately supply excess air, commonly \(10\)–\(30\%\) above stoichiometric depending on fuel and burner design, ensuring every fuel molecule actually finds enough oxygen despite imperfect mixing. This is a genuine trade-off, not a free safety margin: excess air beyond what mixing quality actually requires carries away heat as sensible heat in the hotter-than-necessary flue gas, directly reducing combustion efficiency — too little excess air risks unburned fuel and CO; too much wastes heat up the stack. At \(20\%\) excess air on methane, dry flue gas oxygen content works out to roughly \(3.8\%\) by volume — a number directly measurable with a flue gas analyzer, and the practical proxy operators actually use for excess air rather than trying to infer it indirectly.

03 · Heating values and stack-loss efficiency

Higher heating value (HHV) includes the latent heat recovered by condensing the water vapor formed in combustion; lower heating value (LHV) excludes it, since most combustion equipment exhausts flue gas well above the water's condensation temperature and never actually recovers that latent heat. Combustion efficiency, estimated by the stack loss method, subtracts the sensible heat carried away in the hot flue gas (a function of flue gas temperature, excess air level, and fuel type) from \(100\%\) — a direct, on-site measurement requiring only stack temperature, ambient temperature, and flue gas oxygen (or CO2) content, no laboratory fuel analysis needed. This is exactly why stack loss, not a laboratory heating-value test, is the practical day-to-day combustion efficiency check a plant actually runs.

04 · Boiler tuning as applied chemistry

Boiler (or any combustion equipment) tuning is, mechanically, adjusting fuel and air flow to run at the excess air level that minimizes stack loss while staying safely clear of incomplete-combustion CO formation — reading flue gas O2 (or CO2) and CO together, not either alone, since O2 alone cannot distinguish "well-mixed, near-optimal excess air" from "poorly-mixed, needs more excess air despite already reading a similar O2 level." A boiler trimmed to run leaner (lower excess air) than its actual mixing quality supports will show rising CO before O2 drops to a genuinely dangerous level — which is exactly why a competent tuning procedure watches both together, adjusting toward the excess air level that keeps CO low while minimizing the O2 (and therefore stack loss) needed to get there.

Key relation — stoichiometric AFR

Fixed by the fuel's balanced combustion equation and air's 21% oxygen content — for methane, ≈17.2 kg air/kg fuel. Everything above this is excess air, a real efficiency trade-off.

Stoichiometric air-fuel ratio

Problem. Using CH4 + 2 O2 → CO2 + 2 H2O, air at 21% O2 by mole, methane molar mass 16 kg/kmol, and air molar mass 28.97 kg/kmol, find the stoichiometric air-fuel ratio by mass.

Solution. Air needed \(=2/0.21=9.52\ \text{mol air/mol fuel}\). By mass: \(AFR=9.52\times28.97/16\approx17.2\ \text{kg air/kg fuel}\).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Why zero excess air is not the efficient choice

Problem. Explain why running a burner at exactly the stoichiometric air-fuel ratio (zero excess air) is not the most efficient real-world choice, even though it minimizes flue gas heat loss in principle.

Solution. Real fuel-air mixing is imperfect, so at exactly stoichiometric air, some regions of the flame are locally fuel-rich and combust incompletely — producing unburned fuel, CO, and soot, all of which represent lost chemical energy (and CO poses a real safety risk). Some excess air is needed to ensure every fuel molecule actually finds enough oxygen despite imperfect mixing; the efficient choice is the minimum excess air that keeps CO acceptably low, not zero excess air.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Diagnosing a boiler tuning problem

Problem. A boiler’s flue gas O2 reading is trimmed progressively lower to save fuel, and CO begins rising well before O2 reaches a very low level. What does this indicate about the burner, and what should the operator do?

Solution. Rising CO at a still-moderate O2 level indicates the burner's actual fuel-air mixing quality cannot support running that lean — it needs more excess air than a well-mixed burner would, to avoid incomplete combustion. The operator should back off excess air trimming at the point CO begins rising, accepting a somewhat higher O2 (and stack loss) than theoretically minimal, rather than pushing O2 lower and risking incomplete combustion and CO formation.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A boiler operator running an annual (or more frequent) combustion tuning procedure — trimming excess air toward the minimum that keeps CO low, then re-verifying periodically as burner condition changes — treats 4's O2/CO logic as a routine maintenance procedure with a real, recurring fuel-cost payback, not a one-time commissioning step never revisited.

A plant engineer reviewing a stack-loss efficiency report checks both the flue gas temperature and the O2 reading together before drawing conclusions — per 3, a high stack loss reading could stem from either an unnecessarily hot flue gas (a heat-recovery opportunity) or excessive excess air (a tuning opportunity), and the right fix is different depending on which one is actually driving the number.
