01 · The Brayton cycle

The Brayton cycle — the gas-turbine counterpart to the Rankine cycle — compresses air, adds heat at constant pressure (combustion, in a real engine), expands the hot gas through a turbine, and (in the ideal closed-cycle idealization) rejects heat at constant pressure to return to the start; a real gas turbine runs this open, exhausting to atmosphere and drawing fresh air rather than literally cooling and recirculating the same gas. The turbine drives both the compressor and, for the remaining net output, a generator or propulsion load — meaning a gas turbine's compressor consumes a genuinely large fraction of its own turbine's gross output internally, unlike a steam cycle's comparatively tiny pump work.

02 · Ideal efficiency and pressure ratio

For the ideal air-standard cycle, efficiency depends on pressure ratio \(r_p\) alone: \[ \eta=1-\frac{1}{r_p^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}}, \] with \(\gamma\approx1.4\) for air. At \(r_p=8\), \(\eta\approx44.8\%\); at \(r_p=16\), \(\eta\approx54.7\%\) — a real, substantial gain, but with clearly diminishing returns per unit of additional pressure ratio, exactly as the figure shows. Real engines do not simply chase the highest achievable pressure ratio, because higher pressure ratio demands more compressor stages, tighter tolerances, and hotter turbine-inlet conditions to keep net work worthwhile — a genuine engineering trade-off, not a pure "higher is better" relationship.

03 · Real component efficiencies

Just as with a steam turbine (Lesson 2), real compressors and turbines depart from their ideal isentropic processes. Compressor isentropic efficiency \(\eta_c=(h_{2s}-h_1)/(h_2-h_1)\) compares ideal to actual work required (actual is always more); turbine isentropic efficiency \(\eta_t=(h_3-h_4)/(h_3-h_{4s})\) compares actual to ideal work extracted (actual is always less) — the same asymmetric pattern as before: real hardware always costs more work to compress and delivers less work expanding, never the reverse. Because the compressor consumes such a large share of gross turbine output, compressor efficiency losses are disproportionately damaging to net cycle output compared to an equivalent turbine efficiency loss — a smaller absolute inefficiency in the wrong component costs the cycle more net work.

04 · Intercooling and regeneration, in outline

Intercooling splits compression into stages with cooling between them, reducing the specific volume (and therefore the work) of the gas being compressed in each subsequent stage — lower total compressor work for the same overall pressure ratio, at the cost of the intercooler hardware and a lower turbine-inlet temperature benefit foregone. Regeneration (recuperation) uses the still-hot turbine exhaust to preheat compressed air before it enters the combustor, reducing the fuel needed to reach the same turbine-inlet temperature — a genuine efficiency gain, most valuable at lower pressure ratios where exhaust temperature remains high relative to compressor-exit temperature, and progressively less valuable as pressure ratio rises and that temperature gap closes. Both are the gas-turbine analogues of Lesson 2's reheat and regeneration for steam — the same underlying idea (raise average heat-addition temperature, or recover otherwise-wasted heat) applied to a different working fluid and cycle shape.

Key relation — ideal Brayton efficiency

\[ \eta=1-\frac{1}{r_p^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}} \]

Rising, but with diminishing returns — real hardware cost is why engines stop well short of the theoretical maximum.

Ideal Brayton efficiency

Problem. Find the ideal air-standard Brayton efficiency at a pressure ratio of 10, using γ=1.4.

Solution. \[ \eta=1-\frac{1}{10^{0.4/1.4}}=1-\frac{1}{10^{0.286}}=1-\frac{1}{1.931}\approx48.2\%. \]

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Component efficiencies and net work

Problem. A gas turbine has compressor inlet h1=300.0, ideal compressor exit h2s=560.0, actual compressor exit h2=590.0 kJ/kg; turbine inlet h3=1400.0, ideal turbine exit h4s=780.0, actual turbine exit h4=830.0 kJ/kg. Find both component isentropic efficiencies and the actual net specific work.

Solution. \(\eta_c=(560.0-300.0)/(590.0-300.0)=260/290\approx89.7\%\). \(\eta_t=(1400.0-830.0)/(1400.0-780.0)=570/620\approx91.9\%\). Actual net work \(=(h_3-h_4)-(h_2-h_1)=570-290=280\ \text{kJ/kg}\), compared to an ideal net work of \((h_3-h_{4s})-(h_{2s}-h_1)=620-260=360\ \text{kJ/kg}\) — real component losses cost about 22% of the ideal net work.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Why compressor efficiency matters disproportionately

Problem. Explain why a given percentage-point drop in compressor isentropic efficiency tends to hurt net cycle work more than the same percentage-point drop in turbine isentropic efficiency.

Solution. The compressor already consumes a large fraction of the turbine's gross work output internally, so any increase in compressor work (from lower compressor efficiency) subtracts directly from an already-thin net-work margin. A turbine efficiency drop reduces gross turbine output, but since gross turbine output is much larger than net work, the same percentage-point loss represents a smaller fraction of the net-work margin than an equivalent compressor efficiency loss does.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A power plant operator comparing a simple-cycle gas turbine against a combined-cycle plant (a gas turbine's exhaust heat recovered to run a steam Rankine bottoming cycle) is looking at exactly this lesson's exhaust-heat logic taken to its largest practical scale — regeneration recovers a modest amount of exhaust heat directly into the same cycle, while a combined cycle recovers far more of it into an entirely separate cycle, which is why combined-cycle plants routinely reach substantially higher overall efficiency than either cycle alone.

A maintenance team tracking compressor efficiency degradation from blade fouling or erosion — via a small, trended drop in compressor discharge temperature for a given pressure ratio — treats it as a genuine net-output problem worth a compressor wash or overhaul, not a minor efficiency footnote, precisely because 3's disproportionate-impact logic means a compressor efficiency loss costs more net work than the same percentage loss anywhere else in the machine.
