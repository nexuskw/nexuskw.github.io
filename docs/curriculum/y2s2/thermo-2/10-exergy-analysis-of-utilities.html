01 · Second-law accounting — exergy defined

Exergy is the maximum useful work theoretically extractable from a stream or a quantity of heat, relative to a specified reference (dead-state) environment — unlike energy, exergy is destroyed by irreversibility, never merely converted or conserved. Every irreversibility this course has covered — a turbine's isentropic efficiency shortfall (Lesson 2), a throttling valve (Lesson 3, Lesson 5), heat transfer across a finite temperature difference (any heat exchanger, boiler, or condenser) — destroys exergy, quantified by the Gouy-Stodola theorem: \[ \dot{X}_{destroyed}=T_0\dot{S}_{gen}, \] where \(T_0\) is the reference environment temperature and \(\dot{S}_{gen}\) is the rate of entropy generation. A first-law energy balance alone cannot see this loss at all — energy in still exactly equals energy out; exergy analysis is specifically what makes irreversibility visible and, critically, comparable across very different kinds of equipment.

02 · Applied to boilers, heat exchangers, and throttles

A heat exchanger (or boiler, or condenser) transferring heat \(\dot{Q}\) across a finite temperature difference between a hot stream at \(T_h\) and a cold stream at \(T_c\) destroys exergy at rate \[ \dot{X}_{destroyed}=T_0\dot{Q}\left(\frac{1}{T_c}-\frac{1}{T_h}\right). \] For \(\dot{Q}=500\ \text{kW}\), \(T_h=400\ \text{K}\), \(T_c=350\ \text{K}\), \(T_0=298\ \text{K}\): \(\dot{X}_{destroyed}\approx53.2\ \text{kW}\) — over \(10\%\) of the heat transferred, destroyed purely by the temperature gap it crossed. Narrowing that gap to \(T_c=380\ \text{K}\) (the same 500 kW, moved across a smaller \(20\ \text{K}\) difference instead of \(50\ \text{K}\)) drops destruction to \(19.6\ \text{kW}\) — a \(63\%\) reduction, at the real cost of needing more heat-exchanger surface area to move the same heat across a smaller driving temperature difference. A throttle (Lesson 3's flash steam, Lesson 5's expansion valve) destroys exergy differently — no heat crosses a temperature gap, but pressure drops with no work recovered from a pressure difference that, run through a turbine instead, could have produced real work; the exergy that pressure difference represented is simply gone.

03 · Exergy destruction as an audit's priority list

Ranking every major component's exergy destruction, largest to smallest, turns a utility audit from a scattered equipment inspection into a genuinely prioritized improvement list — the largest destruction terms are where real improvement money belongs first, regardless of how "important" or "central" a piece of equipment might otherwise seem. A boiler's combustion irreversibility (Lesson 9's excess air, and the fundamentally irreversible nature of combustion itself) is routinely the single largest exergy destruction term in an entire steam plant — often dwarfing every downstream component combined — which is precisely why combustion tuning (Lesson 9, 4) is disproportionately valuable compared to its modest engineering effort.

04 · Where the utility bill actually leaks

Every prior lesson in this course quietly builds toward this same accounting: reheat and regeneration (Lesson 2) reduce irreversibility in the turbine and feedwater train; a well-tuned burner (Lesson 9) reduces combustion's own large exergy destruction; a well-approached cooling tower (Lesson 7) reduces condenser exergy destruction; minimizing unnecessary throttling (Lesson 3, Lesson 8's leak economics) avoids destroying exergy for no purpose at all. Exergy analysis does not introduce new physics beyond what this course has already covered — it is the accounting framework that ranks all of it consistently, on the same footing, answering the audit's actual question directly: not "where does energy go" (the first law already answers that) but "where does the ability to do useful work actually disappear" — which is where the money is.

Key relation — exergy destruction (Gouy-Stodola)

\[ \dot{X}_{destroyed}=T_0\dot{S}_{gen} \]

For heat transfer across a finite ΔT: \( \dot{X}_{destroyed}=T_0\dot{Q}(1/T_c-1/T_h) \).

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Exergy destroyed in a heat exchanger

Problem. A heat exchanger transfers 500 kW from a hot stream at 400 K to a cold stream at 350 K, with reference temperature 298 K. Find the exergy destruction rate.

Solution. \[ \dot{X}_{destroyed}=T_0\dot{Q}\left(\frac{1}{T_c}-\frac{1}{T_h}\right)=298\times500\times\left(\frac{1}{350}-\frac{1}{400}\right)\approx53.2\ \text{kW}. \]

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

The payoff from a smaller driving temperature difference

Problem. The same 500 kW is instead transferred to a cold stream at 380 K (same hot stream 400 K, same T0=298 K). Find the new exergy destruction and the percentage reduction from the first problem.

Solution. \[ \dot{X}_{destroyed}=298\times500\times\left(\frac{1}{380}-\frac{1}{400}\right)\approx19.6\ \text{kW}. \] Reduction from 53.2 kW: \((53.2-19.6)/53.2\approx63\%\) — a large exergy saving from a smaller driving temperature difference, at the cost of needing more heat-exchanger surface area to move the same 500 kW across that smaller gap.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Why a first-law audit misses this entirely

Problem. Explain why a first-law (energy balance) audit of the heat exchanger in the first problem would report the device as "100% efficient" while an exergy audit reports a genuine loss.

Solution. The first law only accounts for energy quantity — all 500 kW leaving the hot stream arrives at the cold stream, with no energy created or destroyed, so a simple energy balance shows no loss at all. Exergy accounts for the quality (work potential) of that energy, not just its quantity — heat at 400 K has more work potential than the same heat at 350 K, and the 53.2 kW figure captures exactly the work potential lost by moving heat across that temperature gap, a loss the first law is structurally unable to see.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A utilities audit team ranking every major boiler, heat exchanger, and throttle by exergy destruction rate — rather than by equipment age, visible condition, or maintenance history alone — routinely finds that the biggest opportunity is not the equipment that looks the worst, but the one quietly destroying the most work potential in normal operation; per 3, this is exactly why exergy ranking, not visual inspection, belongs at the front of a serious audit.

An engineer justifying a heat exchanger upgrade (more surface area, a smaller approach temperature difference) to a skeptical budget review presents the exergy-destruction reduction in the same work-potential terms as this lesson's example — a 63% cut in destroyed exergy for a specific, computable extra heat-transfer-area cost — rather than a vaguer "improves efficiency" claim that does not connect to a specific number a reviewer can actually evaluate.
