01 · The vapor-compression cycle

The vapor-compression refrigeration cycle runs four devices in a loop: an evaporator where refrigerant boils at low pressure, absorbing heat from whatever is being cooled; a compressor raising the now-vapor refrigerant to high pressure (and correspondingly high saturation temperature); a condenser where the high-pressure vapor rejects heat and condenses to liquid; and an expansion valve (or capillary tube) throttling the high-pressure liquid back down to evaporator pressure — the same isenthalpic throttling process from Lesson 3's flash steam, here deliberately used to drop both pressure and temperature. Only the compressor consumes work; the expansion valve, with no moving parts, does the opposite of a turbine — dropping pressure without extracting any work at all.

02 · Coefficient of performance

Since a refrigeration cycle's purpose is heat removal, not work production, its performance metric is coefficient of performance (COP), not thermal efficiency: \[ COP=\frac{q_{evap}}{w_{comp}}=\frac{h_1-h_4}{h_2-h_1}, \] routinely greater than 1 (moving heat costs less work than the heat moved, unlike a heat engine's thermal efficiency, which is always less than 1). For representative state enthalpies \(h_1=400.0\), \(h_2=440.0\), \(h_3=250.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\) (with \(h_4=h_3\) from isenthalpic throttling): \(q_{evap}=400.0-250.0=150.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\); \(w_{comp}=440.0-400.0=40.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\); \(COP=150.0/40.0=3.75\) — a realistic figure for a well-operating system, meaning every unit of compressor work input moves nearly four units of heat out of the cooled space.

03 · Superheat and subcooling as health indicators

Superheat — the temperature rise above saturation at the evaporator exit (and compressor suction) — confirms the refrigerant has fully vaporized before reaching the compressor; a compressor ingesting liquid refrigerant (too little superheat, or none) risks serious mechanical damage, since liquid does not compress the way vapor does. Subcooling — the temperature drop below saturation at the condenser exit — ensures fully liquid refrigerant reaches the expansion valve, since any vapor bubbles there reduce the valve's effective cooling capacity and can cause erratic control. Technicians read both routinely as the two most direct field health checks on a running system: low superheat warns of liquid flood-back risk; low or absent subcooling warns of undercharge or a condenser problem — both diagnosable from simple pressure and temperature readings, without opening the system.

04 · Chillers and cold stores

A chiller applies exactly this cycle at building or process scale, cooling water (or another secondary fluid) that is then circulated to cool spaces or process equipment indirectly — decoupling the refrigerant circuit from the spaces being served, at the cost of an extra heat-exchange step compared to cooling air directly. A cold store or refrigerated warehouse runs the same cycle to hold product at a controlled low temperature continuously, where COP directly drives running cost — for a food-handling or cold-chain operation, refrigeration is routinely the largest single electrical load in the facility, making this cycle's efficiency a genuine, ongoing operating-cost concern, not just a design-time calculation.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

COP from state enthalpies

Problem. A refrigeration cycle has h1=400.0 kJ/kg (evaporator exit), h2=440.0 kJ/kg (compressor exit), h3=250.0 kJ/kg (condenser exit). Find q_evap, w_comp, and COP.

Solution. \(h_4=h_3=250.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\) (isenthalpic throttling). \(q_{evap}=h_1-h_4=400.0-250.0=150.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\). \(w_{comp}=h_2-h_1=440.0-400.0=40.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\). \(COP=150.0/40.0=3.75\).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Diagnosing low superheat

Problem. A technician measures unusually low superheat at a system’s compressor suction line. Explain the risk this indicates and why it matters mechanically, not just thermodynamically.

Solution. Low superheat means the refrigerant leaving the evaporator is close to, or has not fully left, its saturation state — some liquid refrigerant may still be present in the vapor reaching the compressor. Since liquid is essentially incompressible, a compressor ingesting liquid slugs can suffer serious mechanical damage (valve or piston damage in a reciprocating compressor) rather than simply losing some thermodynamic performance — this is a real equipment-protection issue, not only an efficiency concern.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Condenser rejected heat and overall balance

Problem. For the cycle in the first problem, find the heat rejected in the condenser and confirm the overall cycle energy balance.

Solution. \(q_{cond}=h_2-h_3=440.0-250.0=190.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\). Check: \(q_{evap}+w_{comp}=150.0+40.0=190.0=q_{cond}\), confirming that all the heat absorbed in the evaporator plus all the work put in by the compressor is rejected in the condenser — a direct consequence of the first law across the whole closed loop.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A cold-store technician checking superheat and subcooling readings as the first two numbers on every routine service visit — before opening any panel or connecting gauges to diagnose further — is following 3's logic directly: both readings are quick, non-invasive checks that immediately narrow down whether a system is undercharged, overcharged, or airflow-restricted, well before more time-consuming diagnostic steps are needed.

A facility manager reviewing electrical bills and finding refrigeration as the dominant load is applying 4's observation in practice — a COP improvement from better maintenance (clean condenser coils, correct charge, adequate airflow) is a genuine, recurring operating-cost lever in exactly the kind of facility where refrigeration dominates the load, not a marginal efficiency footnote.
