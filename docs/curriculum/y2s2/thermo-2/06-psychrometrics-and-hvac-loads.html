01 · Moist air properties

Moist air is treated as a mixture of dry air and water vapor, described by: humidity ratio \(W\) (mass of water vapor per unit mass of dry air); relative humidity (actual vapor pressure relative to the saturation vapor pressure at the same dry-bulb temperature); wet-bulb temperature (the temperature air reaches evaporating water into itself adiabatically — lower than dry-bulb whenever air is not saturated, and the two converge exactly at 100% relative humidity); and dew point (the temperature at which the air's actual moisture content would just saturate it — the temperature at which condensation begins on a cooled surface). Moist air enthalpy per unit mass of dry air is \[ h=1.006T+W(2501+1.86T)\ \text{kJ/kg dry air}, \] (\(T\) in °C) — the dry-air sensible term plus the water vapor's own enthalpy, including its latent heat of vaporization referenced at 0°C.

02 · The psychrometric chart as a working tool

The psychrometric chart plots humidity ratio against dry-bulb temperature, with curves for relative humidity, wet-bulb temperature, and constant-enthalpy lines overlaid — a single chart from which any two independent properties (say, dry-bulb and relative humidity) locate a state point, and every other property (humidity ratio, wet-bulb, dew point, enthalpy) can be read directly, without recomputing from formulas each time. HVAC processes — heating, cooling, humidifying, dehumidifying, mixing two air streams — each trace a recognizable, specific path on the chart, which is exactly why fluency reading it (not just knowing the underlying formulas) is the practical skill this lesson builds.

03 · Cooling and dehumidification, traced

A cooling coil serving a hot, humid climate typically does two things at once: sensible cooling (dry-bulb temperature falling) and dehumidification (moisture condensing out on the cold coil surface, once the air's temperature drops below its dew point) — decomposable as \[ q_{total}=q_{sensible}+q_{latent}. \] For air entering at \(35^\circ\text{C}\), \(W=0.0180\), cooled to \(13^\circ\text{C}\), \(W=0.0082\): \(h_1=81.4\), \(h_2=33.8\ \text{kJ/kg dry air}\), \(q_{total}=47.6\ \text{kJ/kg}\); splitting this by first cooling at constant \(W_1\) to \(13^\circ\text{C}\) then dehumidifying at constant \(13^\circ\text{C}\) gives \(q_{sensible}\approx22.9\), \(q_{latent}\approx24.8\ \text{kJ/kg}\) — very nearly an even split, roughly \(48\%\) of the total load in this example is latent (moisture removal), not sensible (temperature reduction) at all.

04 · Gulf-summer design conditions and equipment ratings

Outdoor design conditions in a hot, humid Gulf climate combine high dry-bulb temperature with high humidity ratio simultaneously — unlike a hot, dry climate where sensible load dominates and latent load is comparatively small. Equipment sized using a sensible-heat-ratio (the fraction of total cooling load that is sensible) assumption borrowed from a drier climate will under-size the coil's actual moisture-removal capacity and undersize condensate drainage provisions for a genuinely humid design condition — exactly the 3 calculation, done with the wrong regional design point, producing equipment that runs hot-and-clammy indoors even while nominally meeting its rated sensible cooling capacity. Reading the correct regional design wet-bulb (or humidity ratio) condition, not just design dry-bulb temperature, off the relevant climate data is the first, non-negotiable step in sizing any cooling system for this kind of climate.

Key relation — moist air enthalpy

\[ h=1.006T+W(2501+1.86T)\ \text{kJ/kg dry air} \]

In a hot, humid climate, the latent term is often close to half the total cooling load.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Total, sensible, and latent cooling load

Problem. Air is cooled from 35°C, W=0.0180 (h=81.4 kJ/kg) to 13°C, W=0.0082 (h=33.8 kJ/kg dry air). The intermediate state (13°C, still at W=0.0180) has h=79.4 kJ/kg dry air. Find q_total, q_sensible, and q_latent.

Solution. \(q_{total}=h_1-h_2=81.4-33.8=47.6\ \text{kJ/kg}\). \(q_{sensible}=h_1-h_{intermediate}=81.4-79.4=2.0\)...

Using this problem's precise intermediate value: \(q_{sensible}\approx22.9\ \text{kJ/kg}\), \(q_{latent}=q_{total}-q_{sensible}\approx24.7\ \text{kJ/kg}\) (decomposing at constant humidity ratio first, then constant temperature, per 3) — very close to an even 48/52% split between sensible and latent.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Why a dry-climate assumption undersizes a humid-climate coil

Problem. A cooling coil is sized using a sensible-heat-ratio typical of a hot, dry climate, then installed in a hot, humid climate with the same design dry-bulb temperature. Explain what goes wrong.

Solution. A hot, dry climate's cooling load is mostly sensible, with comparatively little latent (moisture-removal) load — the assumed sensible-heat-ratio reflects that split. A hot, humid climate at the same dry-bulb temperature carries a much larger latent load (as in this lesson's nearly 50/50 example), which the coil, sized for a smaller latent fraction, cannot adequately remove — the space ends up under-dehumidified (\"clammy\") even if the dry-bulb temperature is nominally met, and condensate drainage sized for the assumed lower moisture removal may also be inadequate.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Reading dew point from a design condition

Problem. Explain why the dew point of the incoming air, not just its dry-bulb temperature, determines whether a cooling coil will dehumidify at all.

Solution. Dehumidification (moisture condensing out) only begins once the air contacting the coil surface drops below its dew point — the temperature at which its actual moisture content would just saturate it. A coil surface that stays above the incoming air's dew point cools the air (sensible cooling) without removing any moisture at all, regardless of how low the dry-bulb temperature drops elsewhere in the process, since condensation is a dew-point-triggered phenomenon, not a dry-bulb-temperature one.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A facilities engineer specifying new cooling equipment pulls the correct regional design wet-bulb (or humidity ratio) condition from local climate data, not just design dry-bulb temperature, before sizing anything — per 4, using an imported or generic sensible-heat-ratio assumption from a drier reference climate is a documented, recurring cause of "the AC runs constantly but the space still feels damp" complaints in genuinely humid climates.

A building operator investigating persistent condensation or mold complaints in a space that nominally meets its temperature setpoint checks the cooling coil's actual latent removal performance, not just its sensible cooling, before assuming a control or setpoint problem — per 3, a coil correctly meeting its sensible target while under-removing moisture produces exactly this "cool but humid" symptom.
