01 · Evaporative cooling, read off the psychrometric chart

A cooling tower rejects heat mainly by evaporating a small fraction of the circulating water into the air stream passing through it — the same latent-heat mechanism from Lesson 6, run in reverse to cool water rather than to dehumidify air. Because evaporation drives this cooling, the theoretical coldest a tower could ever deliver water is the ambient wet-bulb temperature, not the (typically much higher) dry-bulb temperature — a tower cools water toward the wet-bulb limit, and no mechanical improvement to the tower itself can push water temperature below it, only closer to it.

02 · Range and approach

Range is the temperature drop across the tower itself: hot water in minus cold water out — driven by the plant's actual heat rejection load and circulating flow rate, largely independent of the tower's own design. Approach is how close the cold water leaving the tower gets to the ambient wet-bulb temperature: cold water temperature minus wet-bulb temperature — this one genuinely reflects tower design and condition (fill surface area, air flow, fill fouling), and it costs real capital (more fill volume, more fan power) to design for a smaller approach. A tower delivering \(30^\circ\text{C}\) water against a \(24^\circ\text{C}\) wet-bulb has a \(6^\circ\text{C}\) approach — a fouled or undersized tower drifts toward a larger approach for the same conditions, a directly measurable sign of degraded performance.

03 · Cycles of concentration, makeup, and blowdown

Evaporation leaves dissolved solids behind, concentrating them in the circulating water — left unchecked, this eventually causes scaling and corrosion. Blowdown deliberately discharges some concentrated water to control this, replaced by fresh makeup water. Cycles of concentration (COC) is the ratio of dissolved solids concentration in the blowdown to that in the makeup, and by mass balance (ignoring the small drift loss), \[ B=\frac{E}{COC-1}, \qquad M=E+B, \] where \(E\) is evaporation loss. For \(E=100\) (representative units) and \(COC=4\): \(B=33.3\), \(M=133.3\). Raising COC to 6 (better water treatment, tighter control) drops blowdown to 20.0 and makeup to 120.0 — a genuine \(10\%\) reduction in makeup water demand for the same evaporation, purely from running the system at a higher cycles of concentration.

04 · The condenser cooling loop as a managed system

A cooling tower rarely stands alone — it typically serves a condenser (Lesson 1's steam condenser, or a chiller's condenser, Lesson 5) in a closed loop, with the tower's approach directly setting condenser cooling water temperature, and condenser cooling water temperature directly setting condensing pressure and, through it, cycle efficiency. Fouling — scale, biological growth, or sediment on tower fill or condenser tubes — degrades both range and approach simultaneously, and is managed through the same water treatment (controlling COC, biocides, scale inhibitors) that blowdown and makeup rates already reflect. Reading a cooling tower's performance well means treating range, approach, and water chemistry as one connected system, not three separate numbers on three separate reports.

Key relations — cooling tower

Range = hot in − cold out. Approach = cold out − wet-bulb. \( B=E/(COC-1) \), \( M=E+B \).

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Range and approach

Problem. A tower receives water at 40°C and delivers it at 30°C, against an ambient wet-bulb temperature of 24°C. Find the range and the approach.

Solution. Range \(=40-30=10^\circ\text{C}\). Approach \(=30-24=6^\circ\text{C}\).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Blowdown and makeup from cycles of concentration

Problem. A tower evaporates 100 units of water and runs at 4 cycles of concentration. Find the blowdown and makeup water flow, in the same units.

Solution. \(B=E/(COC-1)=100/3\approx33.3\). \(M=E+B=100+33.3=133.3\).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

The water-saving effect of raising cycles of concentration

Problem. For the same tower (E=100), raise cycles of concentration from 4 to 6. Find the new blowdown and makeup, and the percentage reduction in makeup water demand.

Solution. At COC=6: \(B=100/5=20.0\), \(M=100+20.0=120.0\). Compared to the COC=4 case (M=133.3), this is a reduction of \((133.3-120.0)/133.3\approx10.0\%\) in makeup water — achieved purely through better water treatment allowing a higher COC, with no change in the actual evaporative cooling load.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A utilities team tracking cooling tower approach as a trended performance number — not just range or outlet temperature alone — catches fill fouling or air-flow degradation while it is still a cleaning or maintenance item, since a rising approach at the same ambient wet-bulb and load is a direct, hard-to-fake signature of the tower itself losing performance, distinct from a load or weather change that would show up in range instead.

A water treatment program justifying the cost of better chemical dosing or filtration by pointing to the resulting increase in achievable cycles of concentration — and the makeup water savings that follow directly from 3's arithmetic — is making a genuine, quantifiable resource-conservation case, not a vague water-stewardship argument, which matters directly in a water-scarce region where makeup water itself carries a real cost.
