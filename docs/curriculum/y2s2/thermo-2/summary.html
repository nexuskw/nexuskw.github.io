Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Rankine cycle

01 · The ideal Rankine cycle, state by state

The ideal Rankine cycle carries water and steam through four idealized processes: 1→2, isentropic compression of saturated liquid by the feed pump, from condenser pressure to boiler pressure; 2→3, constant-pressure heat addition in the boiler, turning compressed liquid into superheated steam; 3→4, isentropic expansion through the turbine, back down to condenser pressure; 4→1, constant-pressure heat rejection in the condenser, returning the working fluid to saturated liquid to close the loop. On temperature-entropy coordinates, this traces a shape that pump-compresses nearly vertically at the bottom, boils and superheats up and to the right, drops steeply back down through the turbine, and condenses horizontally along the bottom of the saturation dome.

02 · The steady-flow energy equation, applied to each device

Each of the four devices is a steady-flow control volume, and the steady-flow energy equation (SFEE) — with kinetic and potential energy changes normally negligible for these devices — reduces to \(q-w=h_{out}-h_{in}\) per unit mass. For the pump: \(w_p=h_2-h_1\) (work in, no heat). For the boiler: \(q_{in}=h_3-h_2\) (heat in, no work). For the turbine: \(w_t=h_3-h_4\) (work out, no heat). For the condenser: \(q_{out}=h_4-h_1\) (heat out, no work). This is the entire quantitative machinery of the cycle — every efficiency, every heat rate, every audit number in this course's later lessons reduces to applying this same equation, device by device, to the actual enthalpies involved.

03 · Efficiency and its three levers

Cycle thermal efficiency is net work out over heat in: \[ \eta=\frac{w_t-w_p}{q_{in}}. \] Three practical levers raise it, each visible on the T-s diagram as stretching the cycle's area: raising boiler pressure pushes state 2-3 further right and up, increasing the average temperature at which heat is added (a direct efficiency gain by the same average-temperature logic behind every heat engine); raising superheat temperature extends state 3 further right along the top, adding heat at a still-higher average temperature while also — critically — pushing state 4 further from the saturation dome's wet region; lowering condenser pressure (deeper vacuum) pulls states 1 and 4 down and left, lowering the average temperature of heat rejection, which by the same logic raises efficiency directly.

04 · A worked cycle — the arithmetic in practice

A cycle operates between \(10\ \text{kPa}\) (condenser) and \(3000\ \text{kPa}\) (boiler), with a superheated turbine inlet. Given state properties \(h_1=192.0\), \(h_2=195.0\), \(h_3=3115.0\), \(h_4=2297.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\) (representative values, of exactly the kind read from a steam table for a real cycle): pump work \(w_p=h_2-h_1=3.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\); boiler heat \(q_{in}=h_3-h_2=2920.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\); turbine work \(w_t=h_3-h_4=818.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\); net work \(w_{net}=815.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\); thermal efficiency \(\eta=w_{net}/q_{in}=27.9\%\) — a realistic figure for a basic cycle before the reheat and regeneration improvements of Lesson 2.

Key relation — SFEE across the cycle

\[ \eta=\frac{(h_3-h_4)-(h_2-h_1)}{h_3-h_2} \]

Every term is one device's own steady-flow energy balance.

Lesson 02 · Real steam cycles: reheat, regeneration, losses

01 · Reheat

Reheat interrupts turbine expansion partway, routes the steam back through the boiler for a second heating pass at constant (or near-constant) pressure, then continues expansion in a low-pressure turbine section. The primary reason plants pay for the extra piping and boiler surface is the same quality problem Lesson 1 flagged: pushing boiler pressure higher (itself an efficiency lever) drives turbine-exit quality down further into the wet region, risking blade erosion. Reheat lets the plant run a high boiler pressure for its efficiency benefit while still protecting the low-pressure turbine's final stages — a straight expansion from a high boiler pressure to condenser pressure without reheat can leave quality well below \(0.80\); reheating partway through commonly restores it above \(0.90\).

02 · Regeneration (feedwater heating)

Regeneration extracts a small fraction of steam partway through the turbine and uses it to preheat the feedwater on its way back to the boiler, rather than letting that steam do its full possible expansion work. This seems counterintuitive — deliberately extracting work-capable steam early — but it raises the average temperature at which the remaining heat is added in the boiler (since feedwater now enters hotter), which by the same average-temperature efficiency logic from Lesson 1 improves overall cycle efficiency, typically by several percentage points in a real plant with multiple feedwater heater stages — a genuine, well-established efficiency gain, not a minor refinement.

03 · Locating irreversibility on the h-s diagram

A real turbine does not expand isentropically — friction and other losses mean the actual exit state lands at a higher enthalpy than the ideal isentropic exit \(h_{4s}\), for the same exit pressure. Isentropic efficiency \(\eta_t=(h_3-h_4)/(h_3-h_{4s})\) quantifies exactly how much of the ideal work was actually captured — typically \(85\)–\(95\%\) for well-maintained modern turbines. The same idea applies to pumps (in reverse: actual work input exceeds the ideal isentropic work) and, differently, to heat exchangers and throttles, where irreversibility shows up as increased entropy generation rather than a simple efficiency ratio. Locating where on the cycle the largest such gaps occur is exactly how Lesson 10's exergy analysis later prioritizes where real improvement effort belongs.

04 · Heat rate — the operator's efficiency language

Plant operators rarely quote "thermal efficiency" directly — they quote heat rate: the heat input required per unit of net electrical output, commonly in kJ/kWh (or Btu/kWh). Since \(1\ \text{kWh}=3600\ \text{kJ}\) of useful output, heat rate and efficiency are directly related: \[ \text{heat rate}=\frac{3600}{\eta}\ \text{kJ/kWh}. \] A cycle at 27.9% efficiency (Lesson 1's base example) corresponds to a heat rate around \(12{,}900\ \text{kJ/kWh}\) — a real plant with reheat and multi-stage regeneration would push efficiency well above this base case, and heat rate correspondingly down, which is exactly why heat rate trending is the number a plant's operations team actually watches day to day, not a raw efficiency percentage.

Key relations — this lesson

\[ \eta_t=\frac{h_3-h_4}{h_3-h_{4s}}, \qquad \text{heat rate}=\frac{3600}{\eta}\ \text{kJ/kWh} \]

Lesson 03 · Industrial steam systems

01 · Boilers, headers, and PRVs as one system

An industrial steam system is not just a boiler — it is the boiler, distribution headers at multiple pressure levels, pressure-reducing valves (PRVs) stepping steam down between those levels, and the condensate return network bringing spent steam's liquid back to the boiler feed system. Treating this as one thermodynamic system, not a collection of independent equipment, is what makes energy accounting possible: steam quality, pressure, and flow at every header connects back, through mass and energy balances, to what the boiler must actually supply.

02 · Steam quality and flash steam

A PRV, and any throttling device generally, drops pressure with no work extracted and — for a well-insulated valve — negligible heat loss, meaning the process is isenthalpic: enthalpy in equals enthalpy out. When saturated (or near-saturated) condensate at higher pressure throttles to a lower pressure, the lower pressure's saturated-liquid enthalpy \(h_f\) is itself lower — the fluid arrives with more enthalpy than saturated liquid at the new pressure can hold, and the excess boils it into flash steam. For condensate at \(800\ \text{kPa}\) (\(h\approx721\ \text{kJ/kg}\)) throttled to roughly atmospheric pressure (\(h_f\approx419\), \(h_{fg}\approx2257\ \text{kJ/kg}\)): \[ x=\frac{h-h_f}{h_{fg}}=\frac{721-419}{2257}\approx0.134 — \] roughly \(13\%\) of the condensate mass flashes to steam, a genuinely large fraction that a poorly designed condensate return system simply vents to atmosphere as wasted energy, rather than recovering as low-pressure process steam.

03 · Steam traps and their failure modes, costed with the SFEE

A steam trap is a valve that passes condensate through while blocking live steam — the device that makes a condensate return network thermodynamically sensible at all. A trap failed open passes live steam continuously into the condensate system, an easily overlooked, continuous energy loss (often with no obvious symptom beyond a warm return line) exactly quantifiable with the SFEE: the lost rate is mass flow times the enthalpy difference between the escaping live steam and the condensate that should have returned instead. A representative failed trap venting \(0.03\ \text{kg/s}\) of live steam (\(h\approx2775\ \text{kJ/kg}\)) instead of returning condensate (\(h\approx420\ \text{kJ/kg}\)) wastes roughly \[ \dot{Q}_{loss}=\dot{m}(h_{steam}-h_{cond})\approx0.03\times(2775-420)\approx71\ \text{kW} — \] over a full year running continuously, over 2000 GJ, which is exactly why a systematic trap survey is one of the highest-return, lowest-cost audits a utilities engineer can run. A trap failed closed is the opposite failure — condensate backs up, water-logging the upstream equipment and reducing its effective heat-transfer surface, a process-quality problem rather than a pure energy-waste one.

04 · Why this is the utilities backbone lesson

Steam quality, flash steam behavior, and trap failure economics are not a specialty topic — they are the daily working vocabulary behind every steam-using facility's utility bill, from a food-processing plant's process steam and sterilization loads to a large process plant's steam-driven compressors and turbines. The same isenthalpic-throttling logic of 2 and the same SFEE-based loss accounting of 3 apply identically regardless of scale — a single failed trap and an entire condensate-recovery system redesign are both, fundamentally, the same handful of energy balances applied at different scope.

Key relation — flash steam quality

\[ x=\frac{h-h_f}{h_{fg}} \quad \text{(evaluated at the downstream pressure)} \]

Lesson 04 · Gas turbines and Brayton cycle

01 · The Brayton cycle

The Brayton cycle — the gas-turbine counterpart to the Rankine cycle — compresses air, adds heat at constant pressure (combustion, in a real engine), expands the hot gas through a turbine, and (in the ideal closed-cycle idealization) rejects heat at constant pressure to return to the start; a real gas turbine runs this open, exhausting to atmosphere and drawing fresh air rather than literally cooling and recirculating the same gas. The turbine drives both the compressor and, for the remaining net output, a generator or propulsion load — meaning a gas turbine's compressor consumes a genuinely large fraction of its own turbine's gross output internally, unlike a steam cycle's comparatively tiny pump work.

02 · Ideal efficiency and pressure ratio

For the ideal air-standard cycle, efficiency depends on pressure ratio \(r_p\) alone: \[ \eta=1-\frac{1}{r_p^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}}, \] with \(\gamma\approx1.4\) for air. At \(r_p=8\), \(\eta\approx44.8\%\); at \(r_p=16\), \(\eta\approx54.7\%\) — a real, substantial gain, but with clearly diminishing returns per unit of additional pressure ratio, exactly as the figure shows. Real engines do not simply chase the highest achievable pressure ratio, because higher pressure ratio demands more compressor stages, tighter tolerances, and hotter turbine-inlet conditions to keep net work worthwhile — a genuine engineering trade-off, not a pure "higher is better" relationship.

03 · Real component efficiencies

Just as with a steam turbine (Lesson 2), real compressors and turbines depart from their ideal isentropic processes. Compressor isentropic efficiency \(\eta_c=(h_{2s}-h_1)/(h_2-h_1)\) compares ideal to actual work required (actual is always more); turbine isentropic efficiency \(\eta_t=(h_3-h_4)/(h_3-h_{4s})\) compares actual to ideal work extracted (actual is always less) — the same asymmetric pattern as before: real hardware always costs more work to compress and delivers less work expanding, never the reverse. Because the compressor consumes such a large share of gross turbine output, compressor efficiency losses are disproportionately damaging to net cycle output compared to an equivalent turbine efficiency loss — a smaller absolute inefficiency in the wrong component costs the cycle more net work.

04 · Intercooling and regeneration, in outline

Intercooling splits compression into stages with cooling between them, reducing the specific volume (and therefore the work) of the gas being compressed in each subsequent stage — lower total compressor work for the same overall pressure ratio, at the cost of the intercooler hardware and a lower turbine-inlet temperature benefit foregone. Regeneration (recuperation) uses the still-hot turbine exhaust to preheat compressed air before it enters the combustor, reducing the fuel needed to reach the same turbine-inlet temperature — a genuine efficiency gain, most valuable at lower pressure ratios where exhaust temperature remains high relative to compressor-exit temperature, and progressively less valuable as pressure ratio rises and that temperature gap closes. Both are the gas-turbine analogues of Lesson 2's reheat and regeneration for steam — the same underlying idea (raise average heat-addition temperature, or recover otherwise-wasted heat) applied to a different working fluid and cycle shape.

Key relation — ideal Brayton efficiency

\[ \eta=1-\frac{1}{r_p^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}} \]

Rising, but with diminishing returns — real hardware cost is why engines stop well short of the theoretical maximum.

Lesson 05 · Vapor-compression refrigeration

01 · The vapor-compression cycle

The vapor-compression refrigeration cycle runs four devices in a loop: an evaporator where refrigerant boils at low pressure, absorbing heat from whatever is being cooled; a compressor raising the now-vapor refrigerant to high pressure (and correspondingly high saturation temperature); a condenser where the high-pressure vapor rejects heat and condenses to liquid; and an expansion valve (or capillary tube) throttling the high-pressure liquid back down to evaporator pressure — the same isenthalpic throttling process from Lesson 3's flash steam, here deliberately used to drop both pressure and temperature. Only the compressor consumes work; the expansion valve, with no moving parts, does the opposite of a turbine — dropping pressure without extracting any work at all.

02 · Coefficient of performance

Since a refrigeration cycle's purpose is heat removal, not work production, its performance metric is coefficient of performance (COP), not thermal efficiency: \[ COP=\frac{q_{evap}}{w_{comp}}=\frac{h_1-h_4}{h_2-h_1}, \] routinely greater than 1 (moving heat costs less work than the heat moved, unlike a heat engine's thermal efficiency, which is always less than 1). For representative state enthalpies \(h_1=400.0\), \(h_2=440.0\), \(h_3=250.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\) (with \(h_4=h_3\) from isenthalpic throttling): \(q_{evap}=400.0-250.0=150.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\); \(w_{comp}=440.0-400.0=40.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\); \(COP=150.0/40.0=3.75\) — a realistic figure for a well-operating system, meaning every unit of compressor work input moves nearly four units of heat out of the cooled space.

03 · Superheat and subcooling as health indicators

Superheat — the temperature rise above saturation at the evaporator exit (and compressor suction) — confirms the refrigerant has fully vaporized before reaching the compressor; a compressor ingesting liquid refrigerant (too little superheat, or none) risks serious mechanical damage, since liquid does not compress the way vapor does. Subcooling — the temperature drop below saturation at the condenser exit — ensures fully liquid refrigerant reaches the expansion valve, since any vapor bubbles there reduce the valve's effective cooling capacity and can cause erratic control. Technicians read both routinely as the two most direct field health checks on a running system: low superheat warns of liquid flood-back risk; low or absent subcooling warns of undercharge or a condenser problem — both diagnosable from simple pressure and temperature readings, without opening the system.

04 · Chillers and cold stores

A chiller applies exactly this cycle at building or process scale, cooling water (or another secondary fluid) that is then circulated to cool spaces or process equipment indirectly — decoupling the refrigerant circuit from the spaces being served, at the cost of an extra heat-exchange step compared to cooling air directly. A cold store or refrigerated warehouse runs the same cycle to hold product at a controlled low temperature continuously, where COP directly drives running cost — for a food-handling or cold-chain operation, refrigeration is routinely the largest single electrical load in the facility, making this cycle's efficiency a genuine, ongoing operating-cost concern, not just a design-time calculation.

Key relation — COP

\[ COP=\frac{q_{evap}}{w_{comp}}=\frac{h_1-h_4}{h_2-h_1} \]

Routinely greater than 1 — moving heat costs less work than the heat moved.

Lesson 06 · Psychrometrics and HVAC loads

01 · Moist air properties

Moist air is treated as a mixture of dry air and water vapor, described by: humidity ratio \(W\) (mass of water vapor per unit mass of dry air); relative humidity (actual vapor pressure relative to the saturation vapor pressure at the same dry-bulb temperature); wet-bulb temperature (the temperature air reaches evaporating water into itself adiabatically — lower than dry-bulb whenever air is not saturated, and the two converge exactly at 100% relative humidity); and dew point (the temperature at which the air's actual moisture content would just saturate it — the temperature at which condensation begins on a cooled surface). Moist air enthalpy per unit mass of dry air is \[ h=1.006T+W(2501+1.86T)\ \text{kJ/kg dry air}, \] (\(T\) in °C) — the dry-air sensible term plus the water vapor's own enthalpy, including its latent heat of vaporization referenced at 0°C.

02 · The psychrometric chart as a working tool

The psychrometric chart plots humidity ratio against dry-bulb temperature, with curves for relative humidity, wet-bulb temperature, and constant-enthalpy lines overlaid — a single chart from which any two independent properties (say, dry-bulb and relative humidity) locate a state point, and every other property (humidity ratio, wet-bulb, dew point, enthalpy) can be read directly, without recomputing from formulas each time. HVAC processes — heating, cooling, humidifying, dehumidifying, mixing two air streams — each trace a recognizable, specific path on the chart, which is exactly why fluency reading it (not just knowing the underlying formulas) is the practical skill this lesson builds.

03 · Cooling and dehumidification, traced

A cooling coil serving a hot, humid climate typically does two things at once: sensible cooling (dry-bulb temperature falling) and dehumidification (moisture condensing out on the cold coil surface, once the air's temperature drops below its dew point) — decomposable as \[ q_{total}=q_{sensible}+q_{latent}. \] For air entering at \(35^\circ\text{C}\), \(W=0.0180\), cooled to \(13^\circ\text{C}\), \(W=0.0082\): \(h_1=81.4\), \(h_2=33.8\ \text{kJ/kg dry air}\), \(q_{total}=47.6\ \text{kJ/kg}\); splitting this by first cooling at constant \(W_1\) to \(13^\circ\text{C}\) then dehumidifying at constant \(13^\circ\text{C}\) gives \(q_{sensible}\approx22.9\), \(q_{latent}\approx24.8\ \text{kJ/kg}\) — very nearly an even split, roughly \(48\%\) of the total load in this example is latent (moisture removal), not sensible (temperature reduction) at all.

04 · Gulf-summer design conditions and equipment ratings

Outdoor design conditions in a hot, humid Gulf climate combine high dry-bulb temperature with high humidity ratio simultaneously — unlike a hot, dry climate where sensible load dominates and latent load is comparatively small. Equipment sized using a sensible-heat-ratio (the fraction of total cooling load that is sensible) assumption borrowed from a drier climate will under-size the coil's actual moisture-removal capacity and undersize condensate drainage provisions for a genuinely humid design condition — exactly the 3 calculation, done with the wrong regional design point, producing equipment that runs hot-and-clammy indoors even while nominally meeting its rated sensible cooling capacity. Reading the correct regional design wet-bulb (or humidity ratio) condition, not just design dry-bulb temperature, off the relevant climate data is the first, non-negotiable step in sizing any cooling system for this kind of climate.

Key relation — moist air enthalpy

\[ h=1.006T+W(2501+1.86T)\ \text{kJ/kg dry air} \]

In a hot, humid climate, the latent term is often close to half the total cooling load.

Lesson 07 · Cooling towers and cooling water systems

01 · Evaporative cooling, read off the psychrometric chart

A cooling tower rejects heat mainly by evaporating a small fraction of the circulating water into the air stream passing through it — the same latent-heat mechanism from Lesson 6, run in reverse to cool water rather than to dehumidify air. Because evaporation drives this cooling, the theoretical coldest a tower could ever deliver water is the ambient wet-bulb temperature, not the (typically much higher) dry-bulb temperature — a tower cools water toward the wet-bulb limit, and no mechanical improvement to the tower itself can push water temperature below it, only closer to it.

02 · Range and approach

Range is the temperature drop across the tower itself: hot water in minus cold water out — driven by the plant's actual heat rejection load and circulating flow rate, largely independent of the tower's own design. Approach is how close the cold water leaving the tower gets to the ambient wet-bulb temperature: cold water temperature minus wet-bulb temperature — this one genuinely reflects tower design and condition (fill surface area, air flow, fill fouling), and it costs real capital (more fill volume, more fan power) to design for a smaller approach. A tower delivering \(30^\circ\text{C}\) water against a \(24^\circ\text{C}\) wet-bulb has a \(6^\circ\text{C}\) approach — a fouled or undersized tower drifts toward a larger approach for the same conditions, a directly measurable sign of degraded performance.

03 · Cycles of concentration, makeup, and blowdown

Evaporation leaves dissolved solids behind, concentrating them in the circulating water — left unchecked, this eventually causes scaling and corrosion. Blowdown deliberately discharges some concentrated water to control this, replaced by fresh makeup water. Cycles of concentration (COC) is the ratio of dissolved solids concentration in the blowdown to that in the makeup, and by mass balance (ignoring the small drift loss), \[ B=\frac{E}{COC-1}, \qquad M=E+B, \] where \(E\) is evaporation loss. For \(E=100\) (representative units) and \(COC=4\): \(B=33.3\), \(M=133.3\). Raising COC to 6 (better water treatment, tighter control) drops blowdown to 20.0 and makeup to 120.0 — a genuine \(10\%\) reduction in makeup water demand for the same evaporation, purely from running the system at a higher cycles of concentration.

04 · The condenser cooling loop as a managed system

A cooling tower rarely stands alone — it typically serves a condenser (Lesson 1's steam condenser, or a chiller's condenser, Lesson 5) in a closed loop, with the tower's approach directly setting condenser cooling water temperature, and condenser cooling water temperature directly setting condensing pressure and, through it, cycle efficiency. Fouling — scale, biological growth, or sediment on tower fill or condenser tubes — degrades both range and approach simultaneously, and is managed through the same water treatment (controlling COC, biocides, scale inhibitors) that blowdown and makeup rates already reflect. Reading a cooling tower's performance well means treating range, approach, and water chemistry as one connected system, not three separate numbers on three separate reports.

Key relations — cooling tower

Range = hot in − cold out. Approach = cold out − wet-bulb. \( B=E/(COC-1) \), \( M=E+B \).

Lesson 08 · Compressed air systems

01 · Compression work, derived

Compressing a gas from \(P_1\) to \(P_2\) costs specific work depending on the process path: isothermal compression (temperature held constant, requiring continuous cooling during compression) costs the least, \(w_{iso}=RT\ln(P_2/P_1)\); adiabatic (isentropic, no heat rejected during compression) costs the most, \(w_{adia}=\frac{k}{k-1}RT\left[(P_2/P_1)^{(k-1)/k}-1\right]\). For air (\(R=0.287\ \text{kJ/kg·K}\), \(k=1.4\)) compressing \(1\) to \(7\ \text{bar}\) at \(20^\circ\text{C}\): \(w_{iso}\approx163.6\), \(w_{adia}\approx218.9\ \text{kJ/kg}\) — real single-stage machines run much closer to the adiabatic case than the unreachable isothermal ideal, since genuinely holding temperature constant through a real compression process is impractical.

02 · Multistaging and intercooling

Multistaging splits the total pressure rise across two or more stages, cooling the gas back toward inlet temperature between them (intercooling) before the next stage compresses it further — since each stage then compresses cooler, lower-specific-volume gas, splitting the compression this way genuinely reduces total work, moving the real process closer to the isothermal ideal without the impracticality of continuous cooling during compression itself. For the same \(1\) to \(7\ \text{bar}\) rise, split into two equal-pressure-ratio stages with perfect intercooling between them, total specific work drops to about \(188.6\ \text{kJ/kg}\) — roughly \(14\%\) less than the single-stage adiabatic case, a real, worthwhile saving that is exactly why multistage compressors with intercoolers are the standard, not an exotic option, for any meaningfully large pressure ratio.

03 · Receivers, dryers, and dew point

A receiver (storage tank downstream of the compressor) smooths pulsating compressor output into steady supply pressure and provides buffer capacity against short demand spikes, reducing how often the compressor cycles. Compressed air carries the moisture that was in the intake air, now concentrated by compression — an air dryer (refrigerated, removing moisture by cooling and condensing it out, or desiccant, removing it by adsorption) controls the compressed air's dew point, preventing condensation forming downstream in pipework, instruments, and pneumatic equipment as the air cools after compression. Choosing dryer type and target dew point is itself an application decision: a refrigerated dryer reaching a moderate dew point suffices for general shop air; a desiccant dryer reaching a much lower dew point is needed wherever the compressed air will be exposed to near-freezing conditions or feeds moisture-sensitive instrumentation.

04 · Leak economics — the plant's most expensive utility per joule

A compressed air leak is a continuous loss, running every hour the system is pressurized, whether or not any equipment is actually using air — unlike most other utility losses, which at least scale with production. A modest leak losing \(8\ \text{L/s}\) of free air, evaluated even at the ideal adiabatic compression-work basis of 1 (\(\approx2.1\ \text{kW}\), real electrical input higher still once compressor and motor losses are included), costs on the order of \(18{,}000\ \text{kWh}\) over a year of continuous operation — from one leak, easily overlooked amid the hiss of an already-noisy compressor room. This is precisely why compressed air, despite being "just air," is routinely cited as a plant's most expensive utility per unit of useful energy delivered, and why a systematic leak survey belongs in the same category as Lesson 3's steam trap survey — a high-return, low-cost audit that a quiet system does not announce the need for on its own.

Key relations — compression work

\[ w_{iso}=RT\ln\frac{P_2}{P_1}, \qquad w_{adia}=\frac{k}{k-1}RT\left[\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right)^{\frac{k-1}{k}}-1\right] \]

Lesson 09 · Combustion fundamentals

01 · Stoichiometry — the air a fuel actually needs

Complete combustion of methane follows \(CH_4+2O_2\rightarrow CO_2+2H_2O\): exactly \(2\) mol of oxygen per mol of fuel, no more, no less, for complete combustion with nothing left over. Since air is only about \(21\%\) oxygen by mole, the air actually required is \(2/0.21\approx9.52\) mol air per mol fuel — converting to a mass basis (methane \(16\ \text{kg/kmol}\), air \(\approx28.97\ \text{kg/kmol}\)) gives a stoichiometric air-fuel ratio of \(\approx17.2\ \text{kg air/kg fuel}\). This ratio is fixed by chemistry alone, identical for every burner running this fuel — what varies burner to burner is how much air beyond this minimum is actually supplied.

02 · Why real combustion runs excess air

Supplying exactly the stoichiometric air requirement in practice, with real (imperfect) mixing of fuel and air, leaves pockets of incomplete combustion — unburned fuel, carbon monoxide, soot — so real burners deliberately supply excess air, commonly \(10\)–\(30\%\) above stoichiometric depending on fuel and burner design, ensuring every fuel molecule actually finds enough oxygen despite imperfect mixing. This is a genuine trade-off, not a free safety margin: excess air beyond what mixing quality actually requires carries away heat as sensible heat in the hotter-than-necessary flue gas, directly reducing combustion efficiency — too little excess air risks unburned fuel and CO; too much wastes heat up the stack. At \(20\%\) excess air on methane, dry flue gas oxygen content works out to roughly \(3.8\%\) by volume — a number directly measurable with a flue gas analyzer, and the practical proxy operators actually use for excess air rather than trying to infer it indirectly.

03 · Heating values and stack-loss efficiency

Higher heating value (HHV) includes the latent heat recovered by condensing the water vapor formed in combustion; lower heating value (LHV) excludes it, since most combustion equipment exhausts flue gas well above the water's condensation temperature and never actually recovers that latent heat. Combustion efficiency, estimated by the stack loss method, subtracts the sensible heat carried away in the hot flue gas (a function of flue gas temperature, excess air level, and fuel type) from \(100\%\) — a direct, on-site measurement requiring only stack temperature, ambient temperature, and flue gas oxygen (or CO2) content, no laboratory fuel analysis needed. This is exactly why stack loss, not a laboratory heating-value test, is the practical day-to-day combustion efficiency check a plant actually runs.

04 · Boiler tuning as applied chemistry

Boiler (or any combustion equipment) tuning is, mechanically, adjusting fuel and air flow to run at the excess air level that minimizes stack loss while staying safely clear of incomplete-combustion CO formation — reading flue gas O2 (or CO2) and CO together, not either alone, since O2 alone cannot distinguish "well-mixed, near-optimal excess air" from "poorly-mixed, needs more excess air despite already reading a similar O2 level." A boiler trimmed to run leaner (lower excess air) than its actual mixing quality supports will show rising CO before O2 drops to a genuinely dangerous level — which is exactly why a competent tuning procedure watches both together, adjusting toward the excess air level that keeps CO low while minimizing the O2 (and therefore stack loss) needed to get there.

Key relation — stoichiometric AFR

Fixed by the fuel's balanced combustion equation and air's 21% oxygen content — for methane, ≈17.2 kg air/kg fuel. Everything above this is excess air, a real efficiency trade-off.

Lesson 10 · Exergy analysis of utilities

01 · Second-law accounting — exergy defined

Exergy is the maximum useful work theoretically extractable from a stream or a quantity of heat, relative to a specified reference (dead-state) environment — unlike energy, exergy is destroyed by irreversibility, never merely converted or conserved. Every irreversibility this course has covered — a turbine's isentropic efficiency shortfall (Lesson 2), a throttling valve (Lesson 3, Lesson 5), heat transfer across a finite temperature difference (any heat exchanger, boiler, or condenser) — destroys exergy, quantified by the Gouy-Stodola theorem: \[ \dot{X}_{destroyed}=T_0\dot{S}_{gen}, \] where \(T_0\) is the reference environment temperature and \(\dot{S}_{gen}\) is the rate of entropy generation. A first-law energy balance alone cannot see this loss at all — energy in still exactly equals energy out; exergy analysis is specifically what makes irreversibility visible and, critically, comparable across very different kinds of equipment.

02 · Applied to boilers, heat exchangers, and throttles

A heat exchanger (or boiler, or condenser) transferring heat \(\dot{Q}\) across a finite temperature difference between a hot stream at \(T_h\) and a cold stream at \(T_c\) destroys exergy at rate \[ \dot{X}_{destroyed}=T_0\dot{Q}\left(\frac{1}{T_c}-\frac{1}{T_h}\right). \] For \(\dot{Q}=500\ \text{kW}\), \(T_h=400\ \text{K}\), \(T_c=350\ \text{K}\), \(T_0=298\ \text{K}\): \(\dot{X}_{destroyed}\approx53.2\ \text{kW}\) — over \(10\%\) of the heat transferred, destroyed purely by the temperature gap it crossed. Narrowing that gap to \(T_c=380\ \text{K}\) (the same 500 kW, moved across a smaller \(20\ \text{K}\) difference instead of \(50\ \text{K}\)) drops destruction to \(19.6\ \text{kW}\) — a \(63\%\) reduction, at the real cost of needing more heat-exchanger surface area to move the same heat across a smaller driving temperature difference. A throttle (Lesson 3's flash steam, Lesson 5's expansion valve) destroys exergy differently — no heat crosses a temperature gap, but pressure drops with no work recovered from a pressure difference that, run through a turbine instead, could have produced real work; the exergy that pressure difference represented is simply gone.

03 · Exergy destruction as an audit's priority list

Ranking every major component's exergy destruction, largest to smallest, turns a utility audit from a scattered equipment inspection into a genuinely prioritized improvement list — the largest destruction terms are where real improvement money belongs first, regardless of how "important" or "central" a piece of equipment might otherwise seem. A boiler's combustion irreversibility (Lesson 9's excess air, and the fundamentally irreversible nature of combustion itself) is routinely the single largest exergy destruction term in an entire steam plant — often dwarfing every downstream component combined — which is precisely why combustion tuning (Lesson 9, 4) is disproportionately valuable compared to its modest engineering effort.

04 · Where the utility bill actually leaks

Every prior lesson in this course quietly builds toward this same accounting: reheat and regeneration (Lesson 2) reduce irreversibility in the turbine and feedwater train; a well-tuned burner (Lesson 9) reduces combustion's own large exergy destruction; a well-approached cooling tower (Lesson 7) reduces condenser exergy destruction; minimizing unnecessary throttling (Lesson 3, Lesson 8's leak economics) avoids destroying exergy for no purpose at all. Exergy analysis does not introduce new physics beyond what this course has already covered — it is the accounting framework that ranks all of it consistently, on the same footing, answering the audit's actual question directly: not "where does energy go" (the first law already answers that) but "where does the ability to do useful work actually disappear" — which is where the money is.

Key relation — exergy destruction (Gouy-Stodola)

\[ \dot{X}_{destroyed}=T_0\dot{S}_{gen} \]

For heat transfer across a finite ΔT: \( \dot{X}_{destroyed}=T_0\dot{Q}(1/T_c-1/T_h) \).

Lesson 11 · Utility system audit project

01 · The audit as one connected balance

A real utility audit does not treat steam, compressed air, and chilled water as three separate exercises — it closes mass and energy balances across the whole utility deck simultaneously, since a boiler's fuel input, a compressor's electrical input, and a chiller's electrical input all ultimately appear on the same utility bill, competing for the same limited improvement budget. Every lesson in this course supplies one piece of that balance: Lessons 1-2 the steam generation side, Lesson 3 the steam distribution and condensate side, Lessons 4-6 alternative and auxiliary cycles, Lessons 7-8 the cooling and compressed air utilities, Lesson 9 the combustion input driving it all, Lesson 10 the common accounting framework tying every loss together on one footing.

02 · Closing the balances, ranking the losses

Closing a balance means every input (fuel, makeup water, purchased electricity) is accounted for against every output (useful process heat, useful cooling, useful compressed air) plus every identified loss (stack loss, blowdown, trap failures, leaks, condenser approach) — any unaccounted gap is exactly what a good audit hunts down before finalizing its numbers, since an unclosed balance means some real loss has not yet been found or correctly sized. Once closed, ranking every loss on a common basis (energy cost, or exergy destroyed, per Lesson 10) produces exactly the prioritized list 1's figure shows — the audit's actual deliverable, not an equipment condition report.

03 · Proposing fixes with payback

Every proposed fix pairs an estimated implementation cost against its estimated annual savings, yielding a simple payback period (cost divided by annual savings) — the practical, easily communicated metric that turns "we should fix this" into a fundable project a budget owner can actually compare against competing capital requests. A steam trap survey and repair program, a compressed air leak survey, and combustion tuning are routinely among the shortest-payback items on any utility audit — months, not years — precisely because they cost little to execute and address losses (Lessons 3, 8, 9) that are large, continuous, and easily overlooked without a deliberate survey.

04 · The Tier-2 utilities lesson, practiced end to end

This capstone project is deliberately not a single new calculation — it is every prior lesson's arithmetic, applied together against one representative factory utility deck: steam generation efficiency and heat rate (Lessons 1-2), condensate and trap losses (Lesson 3), cooling tower and condenser performance (Lesson 7), compressed air generation and leak cost (Lesson 8), combustion tuning (Lesson 9), and exergy-based prioritization (Lesson 10) — closed into one balance, ranked into one priority list, and costed into one set of payback estimates. This is precisely the working method a utilities engineer actually uses on a real audit, and precisely why this course builds toward it rather than treating it as a separate, disconnected final exercise.

Key discipline — the audit method

Close the balance → rank losses on a common basis → cost each fix with payback → fund the shortest paybacks first. Every number traces to a specific earlier lesson's formula.
