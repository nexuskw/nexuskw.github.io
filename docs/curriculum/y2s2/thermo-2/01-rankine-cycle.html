01 · The ideal Rankine cycle, state by state

The ideal Rankine cycle carries water and steam through four idealized processes: 1→2, isentropic compression of saturated liquid by the feed pump, from condenser pressure to boiler pressure; 2→3, constant-pressure heat addition in the boiler, turning compressed liquid into superheated steam; 3→4, isentropic expansion through the turbine, back down to condenser pressure; 4→1, constant-pressure heat rejection in the condenser, returning the working fluid to saturated liquid to close the loop. On temperature-entropy coordinates, this traces a shape that pump-compresses nearly vertically at the bottom, boils and superheats up and to the right, drops steeply back down through the turbine, and condenses horizontally along the bottom of the saturation dome.

02 · The steady-flow energy equation, applied to each device

Each of the four devices is a steady-flow control volume, and the steady-flow energy equation (SFEE) — with kinetic and potential energy changes normally negligible for these devices — reduces to \(q-w=h_{out}-h_{in}\) per unit mass. For the pump: \(w_p=h_2-h_1\) (work in, no heat). For the boiler: \(q_{in}=h_3-h_2\) (heat in, no work). For the turbine: \(w_t=h_3-h_4\) (work out, no heat). For the condenser: \(q_{out}=h_4-h_1\) (heat out, no work). This is the entire quantitative machinery of the cycle — every efficiency, every heat rate, every audit number in this course's later lessons reduces to applying this same equation, device by device, to the actual enthalpies involved.

03 · Efficiency and its three levers

Cycle thermal efficiency is net work out over heat in: \[ \eta=\frac{w_t-w_p}{q_{in}}. \] Three practical levers raise it, each visible on the T-s diagram as stretching the cycle's area: raising boiler pressure pushes state 2-3 further right and up, increasing the average temperature at which heat is added (a direct efficiency gain by the same average-temperature logic behind every heat engine); raising superheat temperature extends state 3 further right along the top, adding heat at a still-higher average temperature while also — critically — pushing state 4 further from the saturation dome's wet region; lowering condenser pressure (deeper vacuum) pulls states 1 and 4 down and left, lowering the average temperature of heat rejection, which by the same logic raises efficiency directly.

04 · A worked cycle — the arithmetic in practice

A cycle operates between \(10\ \text{kPa}\) (condenser) and \(3000\ \text{kPa}\) (boiler), with a superheated turbine inlet. Given state properties \(h_1=192.0\), \(h_2=195.0\), \(h_3=3115.0\), \(h_4=2297.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\) (representative values, of exactly the kind read from a steam table for a real cycle): pump work \(w_p=h_2-h_1=3.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\); boiler heat \(q_{in}=h_3-h_2=2920.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\); turbine work \(w_t=h_3-h_4=818.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\); net work \(w_{net}=815.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\); thermal efficiency \(\eta=w_{net}/q_{in}=27.9\%\) — a realistic figure for a basic cycle before the reheat and regeneration improvements of Lesson 2.

Key relation — SFEE across the cycle

\[ \eta=\frac{(h_3-h_4)-(h_2-h_1)}{h_3-h_2} \]

Every term is one device's own steady-flow energy balance.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Full cycle efficiency

Problem. A Rankine cycle has h1=192.0, h2=195.0, h3=3115.0, h4=2297.0 kJ/kg. Find the pump work, boiler heat input, turbine work, net work, and thermal efficiency.

Solution. \(w_p=h_2-h_1=3.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\). \(q_{in}=h_3-h_2=2920.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\). \(w_t=h_3-h_4=818.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\). \(w_{net}=w_t-w_p=815.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\). \(\eta=w_{net}/q_{in}=815.0/2920.0=27.9\%\).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

The condenser heat rejected

Problem. For the same cycle (h1=192.0, h4=2297.0 kJ/kg), find the heat rejected per kilogram in the condenser, and confirm it is consistent with the net work and heat input from the previous problem via an overall energy balance.

Solution. \(q_{out}=h_4-h_1=2297.0-192.0=2105.0\ \text{kJ/kg}\). Check: \(q_{in}-q_{out}=2920.0-2105.0=815.0\ \text{kJ/kg}=w_{net}\), confirming the overall cycle energy balance — everything not rejected as heat left as net work, exactly as the first law across the whole closed loop requires.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Superheat and turbine-exit quality

Problem. Two cycles share the same boiler and condenser pressures. Cycle A has less superheat, giving turbine exit quality x=0.82. Cycle B has more superheat, giving x=0.88. Explain, using 3, why Cycle B is preferred even if its efficiency gain over Cycle A is small.

Solution. Higher superheat pushes the turbine-exit state further from the saturation dome's wet region, raising exit quality from 0.82 to 0.88 — meaningfully less moisture in the last turbine stages. Even a small efficiency gain, this reduces blade erosion risk from moisture impact, a real reliability and maintenance benefit that is often the primary reason to run superheat higher, not the modest efficiency number alone.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A utilities engineer reading a steam plant's daily efficiency trend checks boiler pressure, superheat temperature, and condenser vacuum — in that order — before suspecting anything more exotic, precisely because 3's three levers are the standard, well-understood explanation for the vast majority of day-to-day efficiency drift; a slowly fouling condenser losing vacuum is a far more common cause of a declining efficiency trend than any single dramatic failure.

An operator asked to explain why a plant runs superheated steam rather than saturated steam straight from the boiler drum gives both halves of 3's answer, not just one — the efficiency gain from higher heat-addition temperature, and the turbine-blade protection from drier exhaust steam — since citing only the efficiency reason undersells why superheat is treated as non-negotiable in practice, not merely a nice-to-have.
