Reading plant drawings

GA drawings, fabrication drawings, and datasheets read side by side. Tracing a dimension from a pump GA to a foundation bolt plan. Practice on structural-steel and equipment drawings.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Trace a dimension from a pump GA to the foundation bolt plan: what drawings intervene?
  • What three things do you check first when handed an unfamiliar GA?

01 · The plant's drawing ecosystem

Beyond part and assembly drawings, a plant runs on: GA (general arrangement) drawings — equipment in space, with footprints, elevations, and maintenance clearances; P&IDs — process logic, every pipe, valve, and instrument as symbols (INS 352 goes deep); isometrics — pipe runs dimensioned for fabrication; foundation and structural drawings — what the equipment stands on; and electrical single-lines — power distribution's family tree. Each answers one question well; the reader's skill is knowing which sheet answers today's question.

02 · Navigating a GA

A GA is read in three passes. Pass one: orientation — north arrow, column grid, elevation datums. Pass two: the equipment item and its immediate space — footprint, anchor pattern, withdrawal clearances (tube bundle pull, rotor removal envelope, crane hook travel). Pass three: the cross-references — detail drawings, foundation sheets, vendor drawings called up by number. The grid reference (column line C-7) is the plant's postal address system; learn to speak it and rigging studies stop being arguments.

03 · The drawing package as a system

A pump replacement touches the GA (space and clearances), the foundation drawing (bolts and grout), the vendor GA (nozzle loads, base details), the P&ID (what it connects to), an isometric (how the pipe reaches it), and the single-line (its feeder). The engineer's product is often not a drawing but a package: the current revisions of all of these, cross-checked. Lesson 1's register discipline scales up here — a package with one stale sheet is a stale package.

Closing. The course ends where it began: drawings as the plant's contracts. You can now read the whole family — parts, assemblies, GAs — with revision discipline, datum eyes, and symbol fluency. DRW 102's exam is every job pack you'll ever assemble.

Glossary

  • GA drawing — general arrangement: equipment in plant space.
  • P&ID — piping & instrumentation diagram; process logic, not geometry.
  • Isometric (piping) — fabrication drawing of a pipe run.
  • Column grid — the lettered/numbered address system of a plant.
  • Withdrawal clearance — reserved space to pull bundles, rotors, filters.
  • Drawing package — the cross-checked set of current sheets for one job.

Examples and Quiz

Worked example 1 — will the new pump fit?

Problem. A replacement pump is 180 mm longer than the original. The GA shows the pump at grid E-4, discharge toward north, 1,100 mm clear to a cable tray column; the vendor GA gives the new overall length and the same anchor pattern. Find: the fit verdict and the checks that produce it.

Solution. (1) Anchor pattern identical → foundation unchanged (verify against the foundation sheet's bolt layout anyway — Lesson 5's accumulation ghosts live in old foundations). (2) Length grows 180 mm toward the coupling end; GA scaling of the 1,100 mm gap minus 180 leaves 920 mm — above the 800 mm maintenance aisle minimum noted on the GA. (3) Check withdrawal: rotor pulls toward the aisle, envelope still clear.

Answer: fits, with 920 mm aisle retained; foundation reused. Sanity check: every number came off a controlled sheet, not a tape measure — and the site verification tape measure comes anyway, because as-builts drift (Lesson 1).

Worked example 2 — assembling the job package

Problem. Scope: replace the E-4 pump. Find: the minimum drawing package and the currency check.

Solution. (1) Sheets: plant GA (E-4 area), foundation drawing, vendor GA + sectional drawing, P&ID for the loop, suction/discharge isometrics, electrical single-line for the feeder, plus the coupling and seal detail drawings. (2) Currency: pull revisions from the register the same day, list them on the package cover, and mark the package itself with a compile date. (3) One stale sheet — say an isometric missing a rerouted drain — invalidates the pressure-test plan; the register check is the package's foundation.

Answer: eight sheets, register-verified, cover-listed. Sanity check: the package answers space, support, process, pipe, and power — the five questions every equipment change asks.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the bundle with nowhere to go. You arrive at an exchanger cleaning job where the crane is rigged, the bundle is unbolted — and the pull path dead-ends into a pipe rack erected two projects ago. The GA's withdrawal envelope was never updated; the rack's drawing package didn't cross-check the reserved clearance. The bundle waits a week for a scaffold-and-skate solution. Your close-out attaches both drawings side by side and lands one standing rule for the site: no structure inside a marked withdrawal envelope without a GA revision — the drawing system defending physical space the way it defends dimensions.
