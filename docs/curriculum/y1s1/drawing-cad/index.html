Curriculum / DRW 102

DRW 102 · YEAR 1 · SEMESTER 1

Engineering Drawing & CAD

The plant's native written language: orthographic projection, sections, dimensions and tolerances, weld and surface symbols — read first, draw second — plus enough parametric CAD to model and detail real parts.

11 lessons11 of 11 at full depth
Start lesson 01 Full curriculum

What you'll learn

✓The drawing as a contract between design, fabrication, and maintenance.
✓First-angle and third-angle projection and how to tell them apart on sight.
✓Isometric drawing for communication with the shop floor.
✓Full, half, offset, and revolved sections.
✓Datum thinking: dimension from function, not convenience.
✓Limits and fits for shafts and holes; clearance, transition, and interference classes.

Syllabus

01

Why drawings rule the plantFull lesson

The drawing as a contract between design, fabrication, and maintenance. Title blocks, revisions, and drawing control. What 'as-built' means and why it is often untrue.

Taught from — NPTEL: Engineering Drawing

Checkpoint questions
  • Who is protected by drawing revision control, and what goes wrong without it?
  • Why is an 'as-built' stamp often untrue, and what does that mean for the maintenance engineer using it?
02

Projection theory and orthographic viewsFull lesson

First-angle and third-angle projection and how to tell them apart on sight. Choosing principal views. Reading a three-view drawing back into a 3-D object.

Taught from — NPTEL: Engineering Drawing

Checkpoint questions
  • Given the projection symbol, how do you tell first-angle from third-angle at a glance?
  • Why might two correct views still be ambiguous, and what resolves it?
03

Isometric and pictorial viewsFull lesson

Isometric drawing for communication with the shop floor. Sketching a part freehand in proportion. When a pictorial view misleads and a section is owed instead.

Taught from — NPTEL: Engineering Drawing

Checkpoint questions
  • When does an isometric view mislead about a feature, and which view type fixes it?
  • Why sketch in proportion even when CAD exists?
04

Sections and conventionsFull lesson

Full, half, offset, and revolved sections. Hatching conventions by material. The conventions for ribs, shafts, and fasteners that are deliberately not sectioned.

Taught from — NPTEL: Engineering Drawing

Checkpoint questions
  • Why are ribs and fasteners conventionally not hatched in section?
  • Choose a section type for an offset bolt circle on a flange — and defend it.
05

Dimensioning practiceFull lesson

Datum thinking: dimension from function, not convenience. Chain versus baseline dimensioning and tolerance stack-up. Over-dimensioning as an error, not a courtesy.

Taught from — NPTEL: Engineering Drawing

Checkpoint questions
  • Chain versus baseline dimensioning: which accumulates tolerance, and why?
  • What makes an over-dimensioned drawing an error rather than extra information?
06

Tolerances, fits, and limitsFull lesson

Limits and fits for shafts and holes; clearance, transition, and interference classes. Why a bearing seat tolerance is a maintenance issue, not a design nicety. Reading fit callouts on real drawings.

Taught from — NPTEL: Engineering Drawing

Checkpoint questions
  • An H7/g6 callout: clearance, transition, or interference — and what duty suits it?
  • Why is a bearing-seat fit a maintenance concern rather than a design nicety?
07

GD&T essentialsFull lesson

Feature control frames, datums, flatness, position, runout. What GD&T buys over coordinate tolerances. Runout callouts as the language of rotating-equipment repair standards.

Taught from — NPTEL: Engineering Drawing

Checkpoint questions
  • What does total runout control that circular runout does not?
  • Why does GD&T's datum system beat coordinate tolerancing for function?
08

Fasteners, welds, and surface symbolsFull lesson

Thread designation and fastener callouts. Weld symbols on structural drawings — the arrow side convention that everyone gets wrong once. Surface finish symbols and Ra values.

Taught from — NPTEL: Engineering Drawing

Checkpoint questions
  • On a weld symbol, what changes between arrow-side and other-side placement?
  • Decode M16×2: what do the numbers promise the fitter?
09

Parametric CAD: partsFull lesson

Sketch–constrain–feature workflow in any parametric CAD tool. Design intent: modeling so the part edits sanely. Fully defined sketches as a discipline.

Taught from — NPTEL: Engineering Drawing and Computer Graphics

Checkpoint questions
  • What is design intent in a parametric model, and how does a bad sketch betray it?
  • Why should sketches be fully defined before features are built?
10

Parametric CAD: assemblies and drawingsFull lesson

Mates and degrees of freedom in assemblies. Generating dimensioned drawings from models. Bills of materials and balloon callouts — the bridge to spare-parts thinking.

Taught from — NPTEL: Engineering Drawing and Computer Graphics

Checkpoint questions
  • What do assembly mates remove, and what should remain free?
  • Why do balloons and BOM line numbers matter to a storeroom?
11

Reading plant drawingsFull lesson

GA drawings, fabrication drawings, and datasheets read side by side. Tracing a dimension from a pump GA to a foundation bolt plan. Practice on structural-steel and equipment drawings.

Taught from — NPTEL: Engineering Drawing

Checkpoint questions
  • Trace a dimension from a pump GA to the foundation bolt plan: what drawings intervene?
  • What three things do you check first when handed an unfamiliar GA?
