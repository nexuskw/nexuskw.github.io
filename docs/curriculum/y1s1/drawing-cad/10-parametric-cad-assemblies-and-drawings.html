Parametric CAD: assemblies and drawings

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What do assembly mates remove, and what should remain free?
  • Why do balloons and BOM line numbers matter to a storeroom?

01 · Assemblies — constraints as kinematics

An assembly model positions parts with mates: coincident faces, concentric axes, distance offsets. Every mate removes degrees of freedom; the art is removing exactly the right ones. A shaft concentric to a bore and seated on a shoulder should still spin — leave that freedom alive and the model tells you how the machine moves; over-constrain it and the model fights itself with warnings that mean nothing and hide the one that does.

02 · The BOM is the assembly's ledger

The bill of materials — item numbers ballooned on the drawing, quantities, part numbers, materials — is what stores, procurement, and the fitter actually consume. Balloon 7 on the exploded view is line 7 in the table is the box on the shelf. Structure matters: sub-assemblies roll up (a bearing unit is one line here, four lines in its own BOM), and phantom items (bulk grease, thread-locker) appear where policy puts them. A drawing whose balloons and BOM disagree is a stores error factory.

03 · Drawings from models — still a craft

CAD auto-generates views, but choosing them is still Lesson 2's judgment: which section shows the fits, where the BOM table sits, which dimensions the fitter needs at the machine (coupling gap, seal compression, bolt torque table). Auto-ballooning follows assembly structure; verify it — a regenerated drawing can renumber balloons silently between revisions, and a fitter working from memory of "item 3" will draw the wrong part.

Closing. Mates encode kinematics, BOMs encode logistics, and the assembly drawing binds them for the person with the spanner. Model the motion, audit the balloons.

Glossary

  • Mate/constraint — a positional relation removing degrees of freedom.
  • Degrees of freedom — the six motions a free rigid body owns.
  • BOM — the ballooned parts ledger of an assembly.
  • Sub-assembly — a rolled-up unit with its own BOM.
  • Interference check — the assembly's clash audit before metal exists.

Examples and Quiz

Worked example 1 — count the freedoms

Problem. A pump shaft is mated into an assembly: concentric to the bearing bore, face-coincident at the shaft shoulder. Find: the remaining degrees of freedom, and state whether the assembly is correctly constrained for a rotating machine model.

Solution. (1) Free body: 6 DOF. (2) Concentric (axis-to-axis): removes 4 — two translations and two rotations transverse to the axis. (3) Face coincidence at the shoulder: removes axial translation (1 more). (4) Remaining: rotation about the axis — exactly the freedom a shaft must keep.

Answer: 1 DOF (spin) — correctly constrained. Sanity check: add a keyway parallel mate and DOF drops to 0: right for a static check, wrong for motion studies — constraint choice follows the question being asked.

Worked example 2 — the balloon audit

Problem. Rev F of a gearbox drawing ballooned the input seal as item 9; stores stocked shelf 9 accordingly. Rev G inserted a spacer as item 5, shifting the seal to item 10, and the drawing was reissued without a change note on numbering. Find: the failure mode and the two-line fix.

Solution. (1) Failure: fitters ordering "item 9" now receive the spacer's neighbor — wrong part at 2 a.m., the classic renumbering trap. (2) Fix line 1: BOMs reference immutable part numbers, balloons are per-revision pointers only — train and enforce. (3) Fix line 2: revision note must state "items renumbered" whenever structure changes, and stores cross-references by part number, never balloon.

Answer: renumbered balloons + part-number-blind ordering; enforce part-number ordering and renumbering notes. Sanity check: the failure needs no bad actor — two correct documents, one missing sentence.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the 2 a.m. wrong seal. You arrive the morning after a night crew rebuilt a filler gearbox with "item 9 per the wall drawing" — an uncontrolled Rev F print (Lesson 1's ghost returns) whose numbering Rev G had shifted. The machine ran eight hours before the too-narrow seal let go. Root cause is boring and structural: balloon-ordering culture plus an uncontrolled print. The fix package: register-only prints in the shop, part-number ordering policy, and a BOM audit of the five most-rebuilt assets. Nobody gets named; the system does.

Unit versatility

The theory above is in SI. Engineering practice also runs in US/Imperial and CGS — convert each quantity, then check. Get one wrong and the engine hands you a fresh variant of the same conversion.

Work every problem on paper first. Solved examples reveal their full solution; for the multiple-choice questions, select your answers and press Submit to see what was right, what was not, and why.

Problem 1 of 2 · multiple choice

Unit versatility. A quantity is \(2\ \mathrm{kW}\). In US units, this is closest to:

Using \(1\ \mathrm{kW} = 1.341\ \mathrm{hp}\): \(2 \times 1.341 = 2.682\ \mathrm{hp}\).
