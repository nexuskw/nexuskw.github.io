Projection theory and orthographic views

First-angle and third-angle projection and how to tell them apart on sight. Choosing principal views. Reading a three-view drawing back into a 3-D object.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Given the projection symbol, how do you tell first-angle from third-angle at a glance?
  • Why might two correct views still be ambiguous, and what resolves it?

01 · Projection — flattening three dimensions honestly

Orthographic projection maps a 3-D object onto 2-D planes with parallel rays, preserving true lengths and angles in each view. The object is imagined inside a glass box; each face of the box receives one view. Unfold the box and the views land in fixed positions relative to each other — that fixed arrangement is what lets a trained reader reconstruct the solid.

02 · First angle versus third angle

Two unfolding conventions rule the world. In first angle (ISO default, most of Europe and Asia), the object sits between observer and plane: the top view lands below the front view. In third angle (ASME, North America), the plane sits between observer and object: the top view lands above. The truncated-cone symbol in the title block declares which convention the sheet uses — read it first, every sheet, because a first-angle drawing read with third-angle eyes puts every feature on the wrong side of the part.

03 · Choosing and reading views

The front view carries the most shape information; additional views exist only to remove ambiguity. Hidden lines (dashed) show edges behind material; center lines (chain) declare axes and symmetry. A competent drawing shows the minimum views that fully define the part — and a competent reader cross-references features between views until the solid clicks into place mentally. That click is the skill; it comes only from reps.

Closing. Orthographic views are a lossless code for shape. Learn the two unfolding conventions, check the symbol before reading, and practice the mental refold until drawings become pictures.

Glossary

  • Orthographic projection — parallel-ray mapping preserving true size per view.
  • First/third angle — the two view-arrangement conventions; declared by symbol.
  • Hidden line — dashed; an edge behind material.
  • Center line — chain line marking axes and symmetry.
  • Auxiliary view — projection onto an inclined plane to show a slanted face true-size.

Examples and Quiz

Worked example 1 — which convention?

Problem. A drawing shows a stepped shaft: front view with the larger diameter on the left; the view placed below the front view shows a keyway slot. Find: if the sheet is first-angle, which face of the shaft carries the keyway?

Solution. (1) First angle: the view below the front is the top view (object between observer and plane — projections fall through). (2) A keyway visible in the top view lies on the shaft's upper surface as drawn in the front view. (3) Had the sheet been third angle, the same placement would be the bottom view and the keyway would sit underneath.

Answer: on top (first angle). Sanity check: one symbol, two opposite machining setups — this is why the convention symbol outranks every other mark on the sheet.

Worked example 2 — minimum views

Problem. Decide the minimum view set for (a) a plain cylindrical bushing, (b) an L-shaped bracket with two hole patterns on perpendicular faces.

Solution. (1) Bushing: one view plus a diameter symbol — the ⌀ mark encodes the revolution, so a single longitudinal view fully defines it. (2) Bracket: two views minimum (front + top or front + side) because perpendicular faces each carry features; a third view or an auxiliary is added only if hole positions remain ambiguous.

Answer: (a) one view; (b) two, sometimes three. Sanity check: every extra view is another place for revisions to miss — minimum views is quality practice, not laziness.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — mirror-image bracket. You arrive at a fabrication shop holding a freshly made support bracket that bolts up perfectly — mirrored. The drawing came from an overseas licensor in first angle; the shop, used to third-angle prints, read the side view on the wrong side. The truncated-cone symbol was on the sheet all along, small and ignored. The re-make is charged to "training," and the shop adds a one-line stamp to its job traveler: projection convention checked. Cheap lesson at one bracket; expensive at one heat exchanger.

Unit versatility

The theory above is in SI. Engineering practice also runs in US/Imperial and CGS — convert each quantity, then check. Get one wrong and the engine hands you a fresh variant of the same conversion.

Work every problem on paper first. Solved examples reveal their full solution; for the multiple-choice questions, select your answers and press Submit to see what was right, what was not, and why.

Unit versatility. A quantity is \(5\ \mathrm{N}\). In US units, this is closest to:
