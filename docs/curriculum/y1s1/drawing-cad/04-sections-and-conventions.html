Sections and conventions

Full, half, offset, and revolved sections. Hatching conventions by material. The conventions for ribs, shafts, and fasteners that are deliberately not sectioned.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why are ribs and fasteners conventionally not hatched in section?
  • Choose a section type for an offset bolt circle on a flange — and defend it.

01 · Cutting the part open on paper

Interior features drawn as forests of hidden lines become unreadable fast. A section view slices the part with an imaginary cutting plane and draws what the knife exposes: solid material carries hatching; voids stay clear. The cutting-plane line in the parent view — with arrows showing viewing direction and letters naming the section — is the key that connects slice to view.

02 · The section family

Full sections cut straight across. Half sections show half-in, half-out on symmetric parts. Offset sections bend the cutting plane to pass through features that don't share a line — the bends are never drawn in the section itself. Revolved and removed sections drop a cross-section onto or beside a long member; broken-out sections peel a small window. Choosing the right family is drawing craft: the goal is always the fewest, clearest slices.

03 · Conventions that lie honestly

Ribs, webs, shafts, bolts, and keys are conventionally not hatched even when the plane slices them — hatching them would suggest massive solidity and hide the part's real structure. Similarly, spokes rotate into the plane, and lugs align by convention. These are deliberate, standardized departures from literal truth that make drawings more truthful about function. Knowing them separates readers from decoders.

Closing. Sections replace hidden-line archaeology with visible truth; the conventions — unhatched ribs and fasteners above all — are shared grammar, not license.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • Orthographic views (Lesson 2) — sections are views with a knife.
  • Hidden-line reading — to appreciate what sectioning removes.

Glossary

  • Cutting plane — the imaginary knife; its line, arrows, and letters index the section.
  • Hatching — thin parallel lines marking cut material; pattern hints at material family.
  • Full/half/offset section — the main slice strategies.
  • Broken-out section — a local peel, bounded by a freehand break line.
  • Convention — a standardized deliberate simplification (unhatched ribs, bolts, shafts).

Examples and Quiz

Worked example 1 — choosing the slice

Problem. A pump casing has: a suction bore on the centerline, a gauge boss 40 mm off-center, and a drain boss lower still — none collinear. Find: the section strategy that shows all three bores in one view.

Solution. (1) A straight full section catches only the suction bore. (2) An offset section routes the cutting plane through all three features with two right-angle jogs. (3) The parent view shows the jogged cutting-plane line A–A; the section view draws the three bores as if they were coplanar, jogs invisible.

Answer: offset section A–A. Sanity check: three separate sections would say less with three times the ink — the offset section exists precisely for this case.

Worked example 2 — why isn't the rib hatched?

Problem. A sectioned bracket shows its central stiffening rib unhatched while the flanges carry hatching; a trainee flags it as a drafting error. Find: the ruling and the reason.

Solution. (1) Standard convention (ASME Y14.3 / ISO 128 practice): ribs and webs cut longitudinally are shown unhatched, to avoid the false impression of a solid block. (2) The convention preserves the reader's sense of the part as thin structure with a stiffener, which is the mechanical truth that matters for strength and casting alike.

Answer: not an error — convention. Sanity check: hatch the rib mentally and the bracket reads as a solid wedge — heavier, stiffer, and wrong.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the void that wasn't on the print. You arrive at a valve refurbishment where the machinist, boring out a seat, breaks into an unexpected cored passage. The drawing's single full section missed it; the casting's offset drain gallery ran exactly where nobody sliced. The repair becomes a weld build-up job, and your close-out note to document control requests one addition: an offset section through the gallery on the next revision. Sections are cheap; discovering geometry with a boring bar is not.
