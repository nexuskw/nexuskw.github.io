Fasteners, welds, and surface symbols

01 · Symbols — high-density engineering language

Drawings compress whole procedures into symbols. Three families dominate plant work: threads and fasteners, welds, and surface finish. Misread any of them and the error is built, not drawn. This lesson is deliberately a reading lesson: the symbols carry law.

02 · Threads and welds

M16×2 6g reads: ISO metric thread, 16 mm nominal, 2 mm pitch, external tolerance class 6g. Coarse pitch is default; fine pitch is specified. For welds, the ISO/AWS symbol hangs instructions on a reference line: the symbol below the line applies to the arrow side of the joint, above the line to the other side; numbers left of the symbol give throat or leg size, right of it length and pitch; a flag means field weld; a circle means weld-all-around. A fillet symbol with 6 below the line and 6 above commands both sides at 6 mm — miss the top symbol and half the strength stays undelivered.

03 · Surface finish

The checkmark symbol carries roughness (Ra in µm), sometimes process notes and lay direction. Ra 3.2 is honest machining; 1.6 careful turning; 0.8 grinding territory; 0.4 and below, lapping and money. Finish symbols on seal journals, gasket faces, and bearing seats are functional specifications: a seal lip on an Ra 3.2 journal wears out years early, a gasket on a too-smooth flange face slides instead of gripping. The number is a friction, sealing, and fatigue decision — never cosmetic.

Closing. Thread callouts, weld symbols, and finish marks are compressed contracts. Read them slowly once, and the shop reads your intent forever.

Glossary

  • Pitch — axial advance per thread turn (mm).
  • Arrow side / other side — the weld symbol's two shores of the reference line.
  • Throat / leg — a fillet weld's two size languages (a ≈ 0.7z).
  • Field weld flag — welded at site, not in the shop.
  • Ra — arithmetic average roughness, µm.

Examples and Quiz

Worked example 1 — decode a weld callout

Problem. A gusset drawing shows a fillet symbol below the line marked "z6", the same symbol above the line, a circle at the bend, and a flag. Find: the complete instruction.

Solution. (1) Fillet below line: 6 mm leg on the arrow side. (2) Fillet above: same weld on the other side — both sides welded. (3) Circle: all around the joint's perimeter. (4) Flag: executed at site, not in the shop.

Answer: 6 mm double-sided all-around fillet, field welded. Sanity check: leg 6 → throat ≈ 4.2 mm; if the design note requires a 4 mm throat, the callout closes it with margin.

Worked example 2 — the finish that seals

Problem. A shaft drawing marks the seal journal Ra 0.4 with lay circumferential, while the adjacent bearing seat reads Ra 0.8. The repair shop proposes "polishing everything to a mirror." Find: the ruling.

Solution. (1) Ra 0.4 circumferential on the seal track: the lip needs a smooth surface with micro-texture oriented so it pumps lubricant under the lip — mirror-smooth (Ra ≈ 0.1) can actually starve and cook the lip. (2) The bearing seat's 0.8 supports the fit's friction; over-polishing lowers grip of the interference. (3) Ruling: finish to the numbers, not beyond them.

Answer: hold Ra 0.4 (circumferential lay) and Ra 0.8 respectively; "better" finish is worse function. Sanity check: seal manufacturers specify Ra bands (typ. 0.2–0.8) with plunge-ground lay — bands, not ceilings, because both edges bite.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the missing top fillet. You arrive at a cracked conveyor-support gusset, eighteen months old. The drawing commanded double-sided fillets — symbol above and below the line; the fabricator welded the visible side only, and paint hid the omission until fatigue found it. The repair is one evening's welding; the systemic fix is a fabrication-QC checklist line — weld symbols verified both sides of reference line — and a toolbox talk where you draw the symbol grammar on the whiteboard. AWS A2.4 in ten minutes, cheaper than the next crack.
