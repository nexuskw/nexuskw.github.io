Measurement, units, and vectors

01 · Measurement is a contract

Physics begins where opinion ends: at measurement. A physical quantity is a number, a unit, and an uncertainty, and all three are part of the value. The SI system supplies seven base units, of which mechanics uses three — the metre, the kilogram, and the second — and builds everything else from them: newtons are \( \mathrm{kg\,m/s^2} \), joules are newton-metres, watts are joules per second. Because derived units are built by algebra, they can be CHECKED by algebra — both sides of any physical equation must carry identical units, and an equation that fails this test is wrong before any numbers enter. Significant figures carry the honesty further: a value written as 4.80 m promises more than 4.8 m does, and the professional habit is to promise no more than the instrument delivered.

02 · Vectors carry direction

Some quantities are complete as bare numbers — temperature, mass, energy. Mechanics' central quantities are not: force, velocity, acceleration, and displacement all point, and a number without its direction is half a fact. A vector is specified either by magnitude and angle or by components on chosen axes, and the two descriptions convert through the triangle: \( A_x = A\cos\theta \), \( A_y = A\sin\theta \), with \( A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2} \) returning the magnitude. Addition happens component by component — never magnitude by magnitude — which is the single most consequential sentence in this lesson: two 100 N forces can sum to anything from 0 to 200 N depending on their directions.

03 · Two products, two meanings

Vectors multiply in two distinct ways, each answering a physical question. The dot product, \( \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B} = AB\cos\theta = A_xB_x + A_yB_y + A_zB_z \), measures alignment and produces a scalar — work is force dotted with displacement, and a force at right angles to motion does none. The cross product, \( |\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B}| = AB\sin\theta \), measures turning capacity and produces a vector along the axis of turn — torque is position crossed with force, the right-hand rule assigning its sense. Choosing the wrong product is not a small error; it is answering a different question.

04 · The component working method

Every vector calculation in this course runs one procedure. Choose axes deliberately and draw them. Resolve every vector into components with a small sign table, committing each sine and cosine once, visibly. Do all arithmetic on components. Recompose at the end — magnitude from Pythagoras, direction from the arctangent WITH a quadrant check from the component signs, because the calculator's arctangent is wrong half the time on its own. Then run the two audits: units on both sides of every line, and magnitude inside the possible band (never more than the sum of the parts, never less than their difference). The method is mechanical on purpose — statics, dynamics, and every field theory after them will run it thousands of times, and a procedure executed identically is a procedure whose errors are findable. Speed comes later and automatically; correctness is the habit being installed here.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Resolving a pull

Problem. A cable pulls a skid with 100 N at 37° above the horizontal. Find: the horizontal and vertical components, and verify by recomposition.

Solution. (1) \( F_x = 100\cos 37° = 79.9\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) \( F_y = 100\sin 37° = 60.2\ \mathrm{N} \). (3) Recompose: \( \sqrt{79.9^2 + 60.2^2} = 100.0\ \mathrm{N} \) ✓ and \( \tan^{-1}(60.2/79.9) = 37.0° \) ✓.

Answer: \( (79.9,\ 60.2)\ \mathrm{N} \). Sanity check: below 45°, the horizontal component must be the larger of the two — it is; and the recomposition returning exactly the inputs certifies the arithmetic loop closed.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Work as a dot product

Problem. A force \( \mathbf{F} = (300, 150)\ \mathrm{N} \) acts on a trolley that displaces \( \mathbf{d} = (2, -1)\ \mathrm{m} \). Find: the work done.

Solution. (1) \( W = \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{d} = F_xd_x + F_yd_y = 300\times2 + 150\times(-1) \). (2) \( W = 600 - 150 = 450\ \mathrm{J} \).

Answer: 450 J. Sanity check: the negative contribution is physical — the vertical force component points up while the vertical displacement is down, so that pair FIGHTS the motion and subtracts. The dot product bookkeeps help and hindrance automatically; that is its job.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Torque as a cross product

Problem. A force of 250 N acts vertically (+y) at the end of a horizontal arm \( \mathbf{r} = (0.4, 0, 0)\ \mathrm{m} \) from a pivot. Find: the torque vector.

Solution. (1) \( \boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F} \); with \( \mathbf{r} \) along x and \( \mathbf{F} \) along y, the result points along z (right-hand rule). (2) Magnitude: \( rF\sin 90° = 0.4 \times 250 = 100\ \mathrm{N\,m} \). (3) \( \boldsymbol{\tau} = (0, 0, 100)\ \mathrm{N\,m} \) — counterclockwise seen from +z.

Answer: 100 N·m about +z. Sanity check: force perpendicular to arm is the maximum-torque geometry \( (\sin 90° = 1) \); any other angle would deliver less, and a force ALONG the arm would deliver zero — the cross product encodes the wrench-handle intuition exactly.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

Which of these quantities is a scalar?

Work is energy — a magnitude with no direction, born of a dot product, which always outputs scalars. Force and velocity point by definition, and torque points along its axis of turn (a cross-product output). The scalar/vector sorting matters operationally: scalars add like numbers; vectors must go through components.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

The unit vector in the direction of \( (3, 4) \) is:

The magnitude is \( \sqrt{3^2+4^2} = 5 \); dividing each component by it gives \( (0.6, 0.8) \), whose own magnitude is exactly 1 — the definition of a unit vector. Leaving the vector undivided keeps its length 5; dividing by the wrong number (4) or normalizing by eye produces the other traps. Unit vectors carry pure direction, ready to be scaled by any magnitude.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

Two vectors are perpendicular. Their dot product is:

\( \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B} = AB\cos 90° = 0 \): the dot product measures alignment, and perpendicular vectors share none. The product of magnitudes would require full alignment \( (\cos 0° = 1) \). Physically this is why a force at right angles to motion does no work — the centripetal force of Lesson 3's curves, working hard and doing zero joules.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A motor is labeled "10 kg". Its weight — the force the crane feels — is about:

\( W = mg = 10 \times 9.81 = 98\ \mathrm{N} \). Kilograms measure mass; newtons measure force, and the two differ by \( g \). The colloquial "kilogram-force" survives on old gauges but is not an SI unit and invites exactly this confusion — rigging calculations, spring selections, and load cells all live in newtons, and the conversion habit starts here.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

The magnitude of the vector \( (6, 8) \) is:

\( \sqrt{6^2 + 8^2} = \sqrt{100} = 10 \) — the 3-4-5 triangle scaled by two. Adding components (14) treats perpendicular parts as collinear — the cardinal vector sin; multiplying them (48) computes an area, not a length. The Pythagorean recomposition is half of the component method's closing audit, worth making reflexive now.

Unit versatility

The theory above is in SI. Engineering practice also runs in US/Imperial and CGS — convert each quantity, then check. Get one wrong and the engine hands you a fresh variant of the same conversion.
