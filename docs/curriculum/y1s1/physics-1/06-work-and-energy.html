Work and energy

Work as force through distance, with the sign conventions that matter. Kinetic energy and the work–energy theorem derived from F = ma. Power as the rate of doing work — the bridge to every kilowatt on a nameplate.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • How much work does it take to raise a 200 kg load 12 m against gravity? (≈23.5 kJ)
  • What computation does the work–energy theorem let you skip entirely?

01 · Work: force through distance, honestly counted

Work is energy in transit by force: \( W = Fd\cos\theta \) — only the component of force ALONG the motion counts, and the dot product of Lesson 1 was built for exactly this accounting. Force perpendicular to motion does nothing (the normal force on a rolling cart, the centripetal pull of a curve); force opposing motion does negative work, withdrawing energy (friction, always); and a force with no motion under it does no work at all, however hard it strains — the crane holding a load stationary spends money, not joules. The unit is the joule, one newton-metre, and keeping work's sign honest is keeping the energy books balanced.

02 · Kinetic energy and the theorem that shortcuts dynamics

A moving mass carries kinetic energy \( KE = \tfrac12 mv^2 \), and the work–energy theorem states the exchange rate: the NET work done on a body equals its change in kinetic energy, \( W_{net} = \Delta KE \). The theorem is a derived consequence of \( F = ma \), but as a tool it routinely beats its parent: it needs no timeline, no acceleration bookkeeping — just states, before and after. The \( v^2 \) matters more than any other exponent in safety engineering: doubling speed quadruples the energy to be absorbed, which is why impact severity, braking distances, and guard designs all scale on the square of speed.

03 · Power: the rate that sizes machines

Machines are bought by the rate they can move energy: power, \( P = W/t \), in watts — joules per second. For a force moving at speed, \( P = Fv \); for a hoist lifting steadily, \( P = mgv \); and the same energy delivered faster demands proportionally more machine. The kilowatt-hour on the invoice is a unit of ENERGY (a kilowatt sustained for an hour, 3.6 MJ), and keeping power and energy distinct — the rate versus the total — is the first literacy test of every utilities conversation.

04 · The energy-accounting working method

Solve energy problems as an audit, not a story. Choose the system and the interval — initial state, final state. List every force that acts, and classify: does it do positive work, negative work, or none (perpendicular or unmoving)? Write the theorem, \( W_{net} = \Delta KE \), with each work term signed, and solve for the unknown. Audit by units (joules throughout, watts only when time enters) and by sign sense: a body that sped up must have received positive net work; one that slowed must have paid energy out somewhere the ledger can point to. The method's power is its indifference to detail — it never asks HOW the velocity varied in between — and its discipline is completeness: a force forgotten is energy silently created or destroyed, which nature forbids and examiners notice. Run the classification aloud on every new problem — works, opposes, ignores — until the sorting is reflex; the next lesson adds stored energy to the same ledger, and the audit habit carries over unchanged.

Examples and Quiz

Work every problem on paper first. Solved examples reveal their full solution; for the multiple-choice questions, select your answers and press Submit to see what was right, what was not, and why.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Work at an angle

Problem. A tow rope pulls a skid 12 m across a floor with 250 N at 30° above horizontal. Find: the work done by the rope.

Solution. (1) Only the along-floor component works: \( W = Fd\cos\theta = 250 \times 12 \times \cos 30° \). (2) \( W = 250 \times 12 \times 0.866 = 2600\ \mathrm{J} \).

Answer: ≈2.6 kJ. Sanity check: the vertical component (125 N) does no work on the horizontal travel — it lightens the skid on the floor instead, quietly reducing friction's negative work: the same decomposition doing two different jobs in one pull.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Energy to speed up a vehicle

Problem. A 1500 kg utility vehicle accelerates from 15 m/s to 25 m/s. Find: the net work required, by the work–energy theorem.

Solution. (1) \( W_{net} = \Delta KE = \tfrac12 m(v^2 - v_0^2) \). (2) \( = 0.5 \times 1500 \times (625 - 225) = 750 \times 400 = 300{,}000\ \mathrm{J} = 300\ \mathrm{kJ} \).

Answer: 300 kJ. Sanity check: note the asymmetry the square creates — the same vehicle going 0→10 m/s needs only 75 kJ; the higher the speed, the costlier each further m/s. No timeline was needed: the theorem prices the change of state and ignores the journey, which is exactly its advantage over \( F = ma \) here.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Sizing the hoist motor

Problem. A hoist raises a 400 kg load at a steady 0.8 m/s. Find: the power delivered to the load, and the motor size with an overall efficiency of 75%.

Solution. (1) Steady lift: \( P = mgv = 400 \times 9.81 \times 0.8 = 3.14\ \mathrm{kW} \). (2) Motor input: \( 3.14/0.75 = 4.2\ \mathrm{kW} \) — a 4-to-5 kW frame.

Answer: ≈3.1 kW to the load; ≈4.2 kW at the motor. Sanity check: steady speed means zero net work on the load's kinetic energy — the motor's entire output goes into potential energy at rate \( mgv \) plus losses; double the speed and the same load demands double the motor, which is why hoist speed is a purchased quantity.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

Friction acting on a sliding crate does:

Friction on a slider points opposite the motion: \( \cos 180° = -1 \), so its work is negative — energy leaves the crate's motion budget (becoming heat, Lesson 7's accounting). The sign is the physics: negative work is how the ledger records energy withdrawn. Only static friction that PREVENTS sliding under a rolling or gripped contact can do zero or even positive work — a subtlety the belt problems of Lesson 5 already met.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Doubling a vehicle's speed multiplies its kinetic energy by:

\( KE \propto v^2 \): twice the speed, four times the energy — four times the braking work, the impact severity, the guard absorption. This square is the most consequential exponent in industrial safety, and it is why speed limits on forklifts and cranes are energy statements wearing traffic clothing. Eight belongs to \( v^3 \) (a POWER scaling, for fans and pumps — a different law for a different quantity).

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

One watt equals:

Power is the RATE of energy flow: \( \mathrm{W = J/s} \). A joule alone is energy with no rate; N/m is a stiffness. The distinction runs the plant's economics: motors are sized in kW (rate capability), the utility bills kWh (energy delivered) — and conflating the two is the classic first error of every energy meeting this course inoculates against.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A cart rolls at constant speed across a level floor. The work done by the normal force from the floor is:

The normal force points vertically while the motion runs horizontally: \( \cos 90° = 0 \), no work — however far the cart travels and however heavy it is. Weight-times-distance would apply to LIFTING through that distance, a different geometry. Perpendicular forces steer and support but never pay into or out of the kinetic-energy account.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

The kinetic energy of a 2 kg part moving at 3 m/s is:

\( KE = \tfrac12 mv^2 = 0.5 \times 2 \times 9 = 9\ \mathrm{J} \). Forgetting the half (18) and forgetting the square (6, from \( \tfrac12 \times 2 \times 3 \)) are the two standard slips, and both are unit-invisible — the audit that catches them is recomputing with the formula spoken aloud. Nine joules is also a dropped-from-half-a-metre energy: small numbers, but the same arithmetic prices crane loads.
