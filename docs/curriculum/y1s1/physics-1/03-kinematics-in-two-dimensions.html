Kinematics in two dimensions

Projectile motion as two independent one-dimensional motions running on a shared clock. Relative velocity and moving reference frames. Where 2-D kinematics lives in machinery: belt speeds, transfer trajectories, parts leaving conveyors.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A part leaves a horizontal conveyor at 2 m/s from 1.25 m height: how far out does it land? (≈1.0 m)
  • Why don't the horizontal and vertical motions of a projectile interfere?

01 · Two motions sharing one clock

The deepest fact of two-dimensional motion is independence: the horizontal and vertical motions of a projectile do not consult each other. Gravity acts only vertically, so the horizontal velocity is untouched — constant from launch to landing — while the vertical motion is ordinary free fall. The only thing the two share is the clock. Every projectile problem therefore splits into two one-dimensional problems solved with Lesson 2's toolkit, joined by the time variable: usually the vertical motion sets the time (how long until it lands?) and the horizontal motion spends it (how far did it get?).

02 · The projectile's arithmetic

Launched horizontally from height \( h \) with speed \( v_0 \): the fall time is \( t = \sqrt{2h/g} \) — identical to a dropped object, regardless of \( v_0 \) — and the reach is \( x = v_0 t \). Launched at angle \( \theta \): the initial velocity splits into \( v_0\cos\theta \) horizontal and \( v_0\sin\theta \) vertical, the apex arrives when the vertical velocity runs out, and the level-ground range is \( R = v_0^2 \sin 2\theta / g \), maximized at 45°. At the apex the vertical velocity is zero but the horizontal velocity is untouched — the projectile is still moving, a fact the classic "zero velocity at the top" error forgets.

03 · Relative motion: velocities add like vectors

Velocity is always measured relative to a frame, and frames stack by vector addition: the velocity of A relative to ground equals A relative to B plus B relative to ground. A worker walking at 1.0 m/s against a conveyor doing 1.5 m/s moves at 0.5 m/s over the floor; a part carried on a moving belt leaves the belt end with the belt's velocity as its inheritance. In one dimension this is signed addition; in two, components — nothing new, just Lesson 1's machinery applied to velocities.

04 · The two-column working method

Solve every 2-D motion with two columns and one clock. Write the horizontal column: constant velocity, \( x = v_x t \), nothing else. Write the vertical column: free fall with the full constant-acceleration kit. Identify which column the question interrogates and which column supplies the time, and pass \( t \) between them explicitly. Keep signs by one declared axis pair (right and up positive is conventional), and audit at the seams: the landing condition is a VERTICAL statement (height reaches zero), the reach is a HORIZONTAL one, and mixing the columns — using the launch speed where a component belongs — is the error the layout exists to prevent. The same two-column habit will return, upgraded, as normal-and-tangential components in the dynamics course; learning its discipline now on projectiles is the cheap rehearsal.

The applications are nearer than they look: discharge trajectories off conveyor heads set guard and bin positions, falling-object times set interlock reaction budgets, and every "how far can it reach" safety question — spray, jet, or dropped tool — is a projectile problem wearing overalls. The two columns answer all of them.

Examples and Quiz

Work every problem on paper first. Solved examples reveal their full solution; for the multiple-choice questions, select your answers and press Submit to see what was right, what was not, and why.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Off the end of the conveyor

Problem. A part leaves a horizontal conveyor at 2.0 m/s, 1.25 m above the floor. Find: the flight time and where it lands, measured from the conveyor end.

Solution. (1) Vertical column sets the clock: \( t = \sqrt{2h/g} = \sqrt{2 \times 1.25/9.81} = 0.505\ \mathrm{s} \). (2) Horizontal column spends it: \( x = v_x t = 2.0 \times 0.505 = 1.01\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: ≈0.5 s; ≈1.0 m out. Sanity check: the fall time is exactly a dropped part's — belt speed cannot buy hang time, only reach. Doubling the belt speed doubles the landing distance but changes the flight time not at all: the independence principle, priced in numbers a guard designer can use.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

The 45° maximum

Problem. A material-testing launcher fires at 20 m/s on level ground. Find: the maximum range and the angle that achieves it.

Solution. (1) \( R = v_0^2\sin 2\theta/g \), maximized where \( \sin 2\theta = 1 \), i.e. \( \theta = 45° \). (2) \( R_{max} = 20^2/9.81 = 40.8\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: ≈41 m at 45°. Sanity check: range scales as \( v_0^2 \) — a 10% speed increase buys 21% more reach — and the \( \sin 2\theta \) symmetry means 30° and 60° land in the same place, a fact worth knowing whenever a spray, jet, or thrown object's reach is being bounded for safety.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Walking against the belt

Problem. A maintenance worker walks at 1.0 m/s toward the tail of a conveyor that carries them forward at 1.5 m/s. Find: the worker's velocity over the floor, and their displacement after 8 s.

Solution. (1) Frames stack: \( v_{worker/ground} = v_{worker/belt} + v_{belt/ground} = -1.0 + 1.5 = +0.5\ \mathrm{m/s} \). (2) Displacement: \( 0.5 \times 8 = 4.0\ \mathrm{m} \) forward.

Answer: 0.5 m/s forward; 4 m in 8 s. Sanity check: walking exactly at belt speed would hold position (0 m/s), and walking slower than the belt must still drift forward — the signed addition delivers both limits correctly, which is the entire content of relative velocity in one dimension.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

During projectile flight (no air resistance), the horizontal velocity:

No horizontal force acts, so no horizontal acceleration exists: \( v_x \) at landing equals \( v_x \) at launch. Gravity spends itself entirely on the vertical column. The apex-stop intuition confuses the vertical component (which does reach zero there) with the whole velocity — the projectile is moving fastest horizontally precisely when it looks momentarily 'paused' at the top.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Two identical parts leave a table edge at the same instant — one dropped, one sliding off horizontally at 3 m/s. Which lands first?

Both fall the same height under the same vertical physics; the slider's horizontal speed buys distance, not time. This is the independence principle in its purest test form — and the classic demonstration that the vertical clock cannot be bribed horizontally. Mass cancels out of free fall entirely, a fact Newton's laws will formalize next lesson.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A part must fall 4.9 m from a chute to a bin. Its flight time is about:

\( t = \sqrt{2h/g} = \sqrt{2 \times 4.9/9.81} = 1.0\ \mathrm{s} \). The square root is the working surprise: quadrupling the height only doubles the time. Numbers like this size catch-guards and interlock timings — one second of flight is one second for a light curtain to react, and the calculation is exactly this one line.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

At the apex of its arc, a projectile launched at an angle has:

The vertical component has been spent \( (v_y = 0) \) while the horizontal component never changes — so the projectile moves horizontally at the top, at its MINIMUM (not zero, not maximum) speed. Gravity still acts: acceleration at the apex is the same \( g \) as everywhere else. Three classic errors, one question.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

A box rides a conveyor moving 2 m/s and slides off its end. At the instant it leaves, the box's horizontal velocity is:

Velocity belongs to the object, not the surface: leaving the belt removes the belt's FORCE, not the velocity already possessed. The box begins its projectile life at the belt's speed — which is why discharge trajectories, guard distances, and bin placements are computed from belt speed. Weight decides nothing here, and \( g \) is an acceleration, not a velocity.
