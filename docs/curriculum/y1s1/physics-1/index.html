PHY 107 · YEAR 1 · SEMESTER 1

Engineering Physics I — Mechanics

Classical mechanics built from scratch for engineers: units and vectors, motion, Newton's laws, energy, momentum, rotation, and first oscillations — the physical foundation under statics, dynamics, thermodynamics, and vibrations.

Syllabus

01

Measurement, units, and vectorsFull lesson

Physical quantities and SI base units; significant figures and estimation discipline. Vectors made operational: components, addition, dot and cross products in physical language. The toolkit every mechanics course after this one assumes.

Taught from — MIT OCW 8.01SC

Checkpoint questions
  • Decompose a 100 N force at 37° above horizontal into components — the operation statics repeats daily.
  • Why is torque a vector while temperature is not?
02

Kinematics in one dimensionFull lesson

Position, velocity, and acceleration defined as derivatives of motion, hand in hand with the calculus course. Motion graphs read fluently: slopes and areas carrying physical meaning. The constant-acceleration equations derived, not memorized.

Taught from — MIT OCW 8.01SC

Checkpoint questions
  • A hoist accelerates from rest at 0.5 m/s²: how far does it travel in 6 s? (9 m)
  • What does the area under a velocity–time graph mean physically?
03

Kinematics in two dimensionsFull lesson

Projectile motion as two independent one-dimensional motions running on a shared clock. Relative velocity and moving reference frames. Where 2-D kinematics lives in machinery: belt speeds, transfer trajectories, parts leaving conveyors.

Taught from — MIT OCW 8.01SC

Checkpoint questions
  • A part leaves a horizontal conveyor at 2 m/s from 1.25 m height: how far out does it land? (≈1.0 m)
  • Why don't the horizontal and vertical motions of a projectile interfere?
04

Newton's laws of motionFull lesson

The three laws stated precisely and read as engineering: equilibrium, F = ma, and action–reaction pairs that never act on the same body. Mass distinguished from weight for good. Free-body diagrams as the discipline statics will industrialize.

Taught from — MIT OCW 8.01SC

Checkpoint questions
  • What is the weight of a 50 kg motor, and why is 'kg' not a force? (≈490 N)
  • Why is a free-body diagram drawn for one body at a time?
05

Applying Newton's lawsFull lesson

Friction, static and kinetic, with its adaptive character; inclined planes; tension and pulleys. Multi-body problems solved with paired free-body diagrams. The physics behind holding brakes, belt slip, and why inclined conveyors have a maximum angle.

Taught from — MIT OCW 8.01SC

Checkpoint questions
  • A crate sits on a 20° incline with μs = 0.30: does it stay? (No — tan 20° = 0.36 > 0.30)
  • Why does static friction adjust to the load while kinetic friction does not?
06

Work and energyFull lesson

Work as force through distance, with the sign conventions that matter. Kinetic energy and the work–energy theorem derived from F = ma. Power as the rate of doing work — the bridge to every kilowatt on a nameplate.

Taught from — MIT OCW 8.01SC

Checkpoint questions
  • How much work does it take to raise a 200 kg load 12 m against gravity? (≈23.5 kJ)
  • What computation does the work–energy theorem let you skip entirely?
07

Potential energy and conservationFull lesson

Conservative forces and potential energy; mechanical energy conservation, and exactly where friction breaks it. Energy accounting as the engineer's first audit tool — losses always go somewhere, and the somewhere is billable.

Taught from — MIT OCW 8.01SC

Checkpoint questions
  • From what height must a 2 kg part fall to hit 7 m/s? (≈2.5 m)
  • When is mechanical energy not conserved, and where does the difference go?
08

Momentum, impulse, and collisionsFull lesson

The impulse–momentum theorem; conservation of momentum where external forces vanish; elastic and inelastic collisions distinguished by what happens to kinetic energy. Impact loading: why dropped loads and hammer blows produce forces far beyond their weights.

Taught from — MIT OCW 8.01SC

Checkpoint questions
  • Two 5 kg carts collide and stick, one moving at 4 m/s: final speed? (2 m/s)
  • Why does extending the stopping time reduce the impact force?
09

Rotation I: kinematics and torqueFull lesson

Angular position, velocity, and acceleration; the rolling connection v = ωr. Torque defined properly and computed about real axes; moment of inertia as rotational mass. RPM fluency — the native unit of the machine floor.

Taught from — MIT OCW 8.01SC

Checkpoint questions
  • Convert 1450 rpm to rad/s. (≈152)
  • Why does the same force produce different torques at different radii?
10

Rotation II: dynamics and angular momentumFull lesson

Newton's second law for rotation, τ = Iα, with rotational work and kinetic energy. Angular momentum and its conservation. Flywheels as kinetic-energy banks — the physics the machinery course will engineer into presses and compressors.

Taught from — MIT OCW 8.01SC

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does a flywheel's stored energy grow with the square of its speed?
  • A wheel spins down under constant friction torque: what does the linear speed-time trace look like?
11

Oscillations first contactFull lesson

Simple harmonic motion: the spring–mass system and the pendulum, with period formulas derived from F = ma. Natural frequency as a machine's fingerprint — the seed the vibrations course will grow. Waves in outline: propagation, frequency, wavelength.

Taught from — MIT OCW 8.01SC

Checkpoint questions
  • A 2 kg mass hangs on an 800 N/m spring: natural frequency? (≈3.2 Hz)
  • Why does a pendulum's period not care about its mass?
  • MIT OCW 8.01SC Classical Mechanics (ocw.mit.edu)

Career outlook — where this course pays

Classical mechanics is the literacy test of every hands-on engineering role — the sector merely changes the props:

  • FMCG & food processing — line and utilities engineers at dairy and beverage producers such as KDD live in this course daily: conveyor discharge trajectories, indexing accelerations against friction limits, filling-line collision and cushioning design, and the flywheel smoothing on packaging presses.
  • Automotive & heavy equipment — workshop, dyno, and fleet engineers at dealerships and equipment houses run on braking energy (\( v^2 \)), axle-load transfer, winch and hoist power sizing, and impact arithmetic — Lessons 5–8 with part numbers.
  • Global technology & OEM — drives and motion engineers at industrial houses such as Siemens size every motor start with \( \tau = I\alpha \), every servo move with kinematics, and every mount against resonance — this course's closing lessons are their catalog's opening assumptions.

Interview angle: the recurring probe is scaling sense — "double the speed: what happens to braking distance, stored energy, motor size?" The squares and square roots drilled in this course are the expected answers, delivered without a calculator.
