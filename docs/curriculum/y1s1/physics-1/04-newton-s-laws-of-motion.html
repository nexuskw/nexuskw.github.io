Newton's laws of motion

The three laws stated precisely and read as engineering: equilibrium, F = ma, and action–reaction pairs that never act on the same body. Mass distinguished from weight for good. Free-body diagrams as the discipline statics will industrialize.

01 · The three laws, stated for work

Newton's laws are the constitution of mechanics, and each earns a working translation. First law: a body's velocity is constant unless a net force acts — so anything at rest OR cruising steadily has balanced forces, and the interesting engineering question is always "balanced by what?" Second law: \( \mathbf{F}_{net} = m\mathbf{a} \) — the net force, the vector sum of everything acting, produces acceleration in its own direction, scaled inversely by the mass. Third law: forces come in pairs, equal and opposite, acting on DIFFERENT bodies — the floor pushes the crate up exactly as hard as the crate pushes the floor down, and the pair never meets on one free-body diagram. The third law is bookkeeping; the second is the engine; the first is the second at \( \mathbf{a} = 0 \), which is the entire statics course in four symbols.

02 · Mass versus weight, permanently

Mass measures inertia — the reluctance to accelerate — in kilograms, identical everywhere in the universe. Weight is the gravitational FORCE on that mass, \( W = mg \), in newtons, local to the gravity that produces it. The distinction stops being pedantry the moment anything accelerates vertically: in a lift accelerating upward, the floor must push harder than \( mg \) — the scale reads more — and in a dropping lift it reads less, while the mass never changes. Load cells, crane scales, and accelerating machinery all live on this distinction.

03 · Using the second law on systems

\( F = ma \) applies to any body you choose to isolate — a whole train of carts or any single cart within it. The professional trick is choosing wisely: the whole system hides internal forces and yields the acceleration cheaply; a single member exposes the internal force you actually want. Solve the system for \( a \), then isolate one piece and let its own second law reveal the coupling force. Connected bodies share the acceleration magnitude whenever their connection is inextensible — the rope constraint that makes multi-body problems solvable at all.

04 · The second-law working method

The procedure is Lesson 3's FBD checklist with one upgrade: the right side of the equation is no longer zero. Draw the free body. Choose axes — align one with the acceleration when its direction is known, and the algebra thanks you. Write \( \Sigma F_x = ma_x \) and \( \Sigma F_y = ma_y \) with the sign table. Solve symbolically, substitute, and audit: units (newtons throughout), limits (set \( a = 0 \) and the answer must collapse to the statics result), and magnitude sense (accelerations of industrial machinery are typically fractions of \( g \); an answer of \( 40\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) for a forklift is a flag, not a finding). When several bodies connect, write one second law per body plus the constraint, and count equations against unknowns before solving — exactly the bookkeeping the statics course taught, now with motion.

Examples and Quiz

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Force to accelerate a load

Problem. A tug moves a 1200 kg trailer from rest with a required acceleration of \( 1.5\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) on level ground (rolling resistance neglected). Find: the required drawbar force, and the speed after 4 s.

Solution. (1) \( F = ma = 1200 \times 1.5 = 1800\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) Kinematics inherits the a: \( v = at = 1.5 \times 4 = 6.0\ \mathrm{m/s} \).

Answer: 1.8 kN; 6 m/s. Sanity check: the force is about 15% of the trailer's 11.8 kN weight — a plausible drawbar figure; and the audit limit works: at \( a = 0 \) the required force collapses to zero, exactly as a frictionless statics problem demands.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

The scale in the lift

Problem. An 80 kg technician stands on a scale in a service lift accelerating upward at \( 2.0\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). Find: the scale reading (the normal force), and the reading in free fall.

Solution. (1) Second law, up positive: \( N - mg = ma \Rightarrow N = m(g + a) = 80 \times 11.81 = 945\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) Free fall \( (a = -g) \): \( N = m(g - g) = 0 \) — the scale reads nothing.

Answer: ≈945 N (as if 96 kg); zero in free fall. Sanity check: the reading exceeds the true 785 N weight by exactly \( ma = 160\ \mathrm{N} \) — the extra push the floor must supply to accelerate the technician. Load cells under moving loads misread for precisely this reason; the correction is this equation.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

The coupling force in a two-cart train

Problem. A 10 kg cart towing a 4 kg cart is pulled by 60 N on a smooth floor. Find: the acceleration of the train and the tension in the coupling.

Solution. (1) Whole system: \( a = F/m_{total} = 60/14 = 4.29\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). (2) Isolate the trailing cart — the coupling is its only horizontal force: \( T = m_2 a = 4 \times 4.29 = 17.1\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: \( 4.3\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \); ≈17 N in the coupling. Sanity check: the coupling carries \( 4/14 = 28.6\% \) of the applied force — exactly the trailing cart's share of the total mass. The system view found a cheaply; the member view exposed T: the two-step method in its standard form.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A pallet slides across a smooth floor at constant velocity. The net force on it is:

Constant velocity IS the first-law condition: net force zero — motion needs no force to continue, only to change. The push-to-keep-moving intuition (Aristotle's) survives in everyday friction-filled life but fails the moment friction leaves. Vertically, weight and the normal force cancel as part of that zero. This question separates physics from folk physics in one line.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

A crate weighs 981 N on Earth. On a moon base where \( g = 1.62\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), its mass is:

Mass is the invariant: \( m = W/g = 981/9.81 = 100\ \mathrm{kg} \) everywhere in the universe. Its moon WEIGHT would be \( 100 \times 1.62 = 162\ \mathrm{N} \) — a force, hence not an answer to a mass question (and kilograms, not newtons, is the mass unit). Inertia travels with the crate; the scale reading stays home with the gravity.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A net force of 500 N acts on a 250 kg trolley. Its acceleration is:

\( a = F/m = 500/250 = 2\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). Inverting the division (0.5) and multiplying instead (125,000) are the arithmetic traps; the physics trap is forgetting that F must be the NET force — if friction claimed 100 N of the 500, the answer would drop accordingly. Second-law arithmetic is trivial; assembling the net force is the actual work.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A tractor pulls a loaded sled. The sled pulls back on the tractor (third law). The pair can still accelerate because:

Third-law partners never meet on one free body: the sled's pull-back acts on the TRACTOR, whose own budget also includes ground traction — and the tractor accelerates if traction beats pull-back. The sled, on ITS ledger, feels the forward pull and accelerates too. The 'equal forces should cancel' paradox dissolves the moment each body keeps its own books — which is what FBDs are for.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

The same 30 N force is applied in turn to a 3 kg part and a 6 kg part. The accelerations compare as:

\( a = F/m \): 30/3 = 10 and 30/6 = 5 m/s² — acceleration inversely proportional to mass at fixed force. This inverse relationship IS inertia quantified, and it scales to every sizing decision where a drive must accelerate a load: double the tooling mass and the same motor delivers half the acceleration, before friction says a word.
