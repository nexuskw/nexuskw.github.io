Oscillations first contact

Simple harmonic motion: the spring–mass system and the pendulum, with period formulas derived from F = ma. Natural frequency as a machine's fingerprint — the seed the vibrations course will grow. Waves in outline: propagation, frequency, wavelength.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A 2 kg mass hangs on an 800 N/m spring: natural frequency? (≈3.2 Hz)
  • Why does a pendulum's period not care about its mass?

01 · The restoring recipe

Oscillation needs exactly two ingredients: a restoring force that pulls toward equilibrium in proportion to displacement, and inertia that overshoots it. The spring–mass system is the archetype — \( F = -kx \) feeding \( m\ddot{x} \) — and its motion is simple harmonic: \( x(t) = A\sin(\omega_n t) \), a sine forever, with amplitude \( A \) set by how the motion started and angular frequency set by the system itself: \( \omega_n = \sqrt{k/m} \). Stiffness accelerates the cycle; mass slows it; amplitude, remarkably, does not enter — small or large swings keep the same beat, which is what makes natural frequency a property of the MACHINE, not of the disturbance.

02 · The pendulum and the family of oscillators

The pendulum repeats the recipe with gravity as the spring: \( T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g} \) for small swings — mass absent, length and gravity only. Every oscillator in the family carries the same signature quantities: period \( T = 2\pi/\omega_n \), frequency \( f = 1/T \) in hertz, and the energy sloshing between kinetic (maximum at equilibrium, where speed peaks at \( v_{max} = A\omega_n \)) and potential (maximum at the extremes, where motion pauses). Reading \( x(t) = A\sin(\omega t) \) fluently — amplitude from the coefficient, frequency from the argument — is reading the native language of every vibration trace the later courses will put on screen.

03 · Why machines care: resonance previewed

Every machine element has natural frequencies, and rotating machinery supplies periodic forcing at running speed and its multiples. When forcing frequency approaches natural frequency, amplitude grows dramatically — resonance — and structures that seemed rigid begin to breathe. This lesson only introduces the actors; the vibrations course will quantify their meeting. But the design instinct starts now: know your \( \omega_n \), know your running speeds, and keep them apart.

04 · The oscillation working method

Identify the stiffness and the inertia — every oscillator has both, whatever it looks like — and form \( \omega_n = \sqrt{k/m} \) (or the pendulum's \( \sqrt{g/L} \)). Convert consciously among \( \omega_n \), \( f \), and \( T \): the \( 2\pi \) that separates rad/s from Hz is this chapter's border crossing, as rpm's was the last one's. Extract amplitude and frequency from any given \( x(t) \) by inspection, and derive the speed and acceleration peaks as \( A\omega \) and \( A\omega^2 \). Audit with the scaling instincts: quadrupling stiffness only doubles frequency (the square root taxes enthusiasm), adding mass always slows the beat, and amplitude never changes the clock. Where a real trace disagrees with these instincts — frequency shifting with load, beat patterns, growth over time — the disagreement is information, and the vibrations course will teach you to read it. Here, the assignment is fluency with the clean case that all the diagnostics measure against — the sine every analyzer decomposes real signals into, and the natural frequency every balancing report quotes. First courses end where careers begin; this one ends on the exact frequency.

Examples and Quiz

Work every problem on paper first. Solved examples reveal their full solution; for the multiple-choice questions, select your answers and press Submit to see what was right, what was not, and why.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

The isolator's beat

Problem. A 2.0 kg instrument sits on a mount of stiffness 800 N/m. Find: the natural frequency in rad/s and Hz, and the period.

Solution. (1) \( \omega_n = \sqrt{k/m} = \sqrt{800/2} = 20\ \mathrm{rad/s} \). (2) \( f = \omega_n/2\pi = 3.18\ \mathrm{Hz} \); \( T = 1/f = 0.314\ \mathrm{s} \).

Answer: 20 rad/s = 3.2 Hz; period ≈0.31 s. Sanity check: quadrupling the stiffness would only double the frequency (√), and doubling the mass would divide it by √2 — the square root damping every intuition of proportionality. A 3 Hz mount under a 24 Hz (1450 rpm) machine is comfortably clear of resonance: the design arithmetic in miniature.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

The inspection pendulum

Problem. A 0.70 m lanyard with a small tool swings as a pendulum. Find: its period, and what changes if the tool is twice as heavy.

Solution. (1) \( T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.70/9.81} = 1.68\ \mathrm{s} \). (2) Mass does not appear: the heavier tool swings with exactly the same period.

Answer: ≈1.7 s, at any mass. Sanity check: gravity provides both the restoring pull AND acts on the inertia, so mass cancels — the same cancellation as free fall. Only length and gravity remain: to slow a pendulum, lengthen it (fourfold length for double period, the square root again).

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Reading a vibration trace

Problem. A bearing housing's displacement follows \( x(t) = 0.030\sin(12t) \) (metres, seconds). Find: the amplitude, frequency in Hz, and peak velocity.

Solution. (1) Amplitude by inspection: \( A = 0.030\ \mathrm{m} = 30\ \mathrm{mm} \). (2) \( \omega = 12\ \mathrm{rad/s} \Rightarrow f = 12/2\pi = 1.9\ \mathrm{Hz} \). (3) \( v_{max} = A\omega = 0.030 \times 12 = 0.36\ \mathrm{m/s} \).

Answer: 30 mm at 1.9 Hz; peak speed 0.36 m/s. Sanity check: peak velocity occurs at mid-swing where the trace crosses zero — displacement and velocity peaks alternate, a quarter-cycle apart. Reading A and ω off a sine by inspection is the exact skill vibration analyzers automate; learning it manually first is what makes their output meaningful.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

For small oscillations, increasing a pendulum's swing amplitude:

Amplitude independence is SHM's defining courtesy: larger swings travel farther but proportionally faster, and the clock holds — which is why pendulums could keep time. (At LARGE angles the approximation frays and the period does grow, but that correction belongs to a later course.) The natural frequency is the system's property; the amplitude merely reports how hard it was disturbed.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

To raise a machine mount's natural frequency, you should:

\( \omega_n = \sqrt{k/m} \): frequency rises with stiffness and falls with mass. Softening and adding mass LOWER it — which is itself a design tool (isolation mounts deliberately sit far below forcing frequencies). Amplitude has no vote. The k-and-m tuning of natural frequencies against running speeds is the first design instinct of vibration engineering.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

In SHM, the speed of the mass is greatest:

At the extremes the motion pauses (all energy potential); at equilibrium the restoring force has finished accelerating it and the energy is all kinetic: \( v_{max} = A\omega_n \). The energy slosh of Lesson 7 sets this geography — and vibration instruments exploit it, velocity sensors reading peak values a quarter-cycle from displacement peaks.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

Quadrupling a spring's stiffness multiplies the oscillation frequency by:

\( \omega_n \propto \sqrt{k} \): four times the stiffness, twice the frequency — the square root taxing the investment, exactly as it taxed grain refinement and case depth in other courses. Linear thinking (×4) overpays; the lesson's scaling instincts exist to prevent precisely that procurement conversation.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

A machine's natural frequency is 24 Hz and its rotor runs at 1440 rpm (24 Hz). The expected behavior is:

Forcing at the natural frequency is the resonance condition: each cycle's push arrives in step with the motion, and amplitude climbs until damping or damage caps it. The numbers were chosen to collide — 1440 rpm IS 24 Hz. Real design keeps margins between running speeds and natural frequencies precisely to avoid this meeting; the vibrations course quantifies how much margin buys how much peace.
