Rotation I: kinematics and torque

Angular position, velocity, and acceleration; the rolling connection v = ωr. Torque defined properly and computed about real axes; moment of inertia as rotational mass. RPM fluency — the native unit of the machine floor.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Convert 1450 rpm to rad/s. (≈152)
  • Why does the same force produce different torques at different radii?

01 · The angular vocabulary

Rotation replays kinematics with angular characters. Angular position \( \theta \) is measured in radians — the natural unit, because it makes arc length \( s = \theta r \) with no conversion factor. Angular velocity \( \omega = d\theta/dt \) in rad/s, and angular acceleration \( \alpha = d\omega/dt \) in rad/s², chain exactly as their linear siblings did, and the constant-\( \alpha \) toolkit is Lesson 2's kit with letters swapped: \( \omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t \), \( \theta = \omega_0 t + \tfrac12\alpha t^2 \), \( \omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\theta \). The machine floor speaks rpm; physics speaks rad/s; the exchange rate \( \omega = 2\pi N/60 \) is crossed so often it should be reflex — 1450 rpm, the standard 4-pole motor speed, is 152 rad/s.

02 · The rolling connection

Rotation meets translation at the rim: a point at radius \( r \) moves at \( v = \omega r \) and, under angular acceleration, gains speed at \( a_t = \alpha r \). The relation runs every belt, chain, and traction drive: pulleys sharing a belt share rim speed, so their angular velocities trade inversely with radius — the speed-ratio law of every pulley pair and gear mesh. Radians earn their keep here: the formulas hold ONLY with angles in radians, and feeding degrees or rpm into \( v = \omega r \) is the classic rotational unit error.

03 · Torque: force's rotational agent

What force is to translation, torque is to rotation: \( \tau = Fr\sin\theta \), the force times its perpendicular lever arm, in newton-metres. The statics course built moments for equilibrium; dynamics now lets them cause angular acceleration. On a belt drive, the torque delivered is the TENSION DIFFERENCE times radius — \( (T_1 - T_2)r \) — because both belt runs pull, in opposition; on a bolted coupling, torque is what the wrench and the torque spec are negotiating. The moment of inertia \( I \), rotation's mass, waits one lesson ahead as torque's partner.

04 · The rotational working method

Run rotation problems with a currency-exchange discipline. Convert everything to SI angular units at the border — rpm to rad/s, revolutions to radians — work entirely inside, and convert back only for the final answer if the floor wants rpm. Choose the angular toolkit by the same known-versus-wanted matching as Lesson 2, and pass between the angular and linear worlds only through the radius: \( v = \omega r \), \( a_t = \alpha r \), \( s = \theta r \). Audit with the two standard checks — units (a radian is dimensionless, so \( \omega r \) lands in m/s cleanly) and magnitude sense (industrial machines mostly live between 0.5 and 300 rad/s; an answer of 15,000 rad/s is a units error announcing itself). The kinematics is deliberately familiar; fluency in the conversion discipline is what this lesson actually installs, because every machine after it — pumps, gearboxes, turbines — speaks both languages at once.

Examples and Quiz

Work every problem on paper first. Solved examples reveal their full solution; for the multiple-choice questions, select your answers and press Submit to see what was right, what was not, and why.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

From nameplate to belt speed

Problem. A motor runs at 1450 rpm carrying a 60 mm-radius pulley. Find: the angular velocity in rad/s and the belt speed.

Solution. (1) \( \omega = 2\pi N/60 = 2\pi \times 1450/60 = 152\ \mathrm{rad/s} \). (2) \( v = \omega r = 152 \times 0.060 = 9.1\ \mathrm{m/s} \).

Answer: ≈152 rad/s; ≈9.1 m/s of belt. Sanity check: the rim of a 12 cm pulley at motor speed doing ~9 m/s (33 km/h) is entirely typical of belt drives — and the number is why guards exist. The rpm-to-rad/s crossing (×0.1047) is the single most repeated conversion in machine work; owning it cold is the lesson's toll.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Torque through a belt

Problem. A belt drives a 100 mm-radius pulley with 350 N tension in the tight side and 150 N in the slack side. Find: the torque delivered to the shaft.

Solution. (1) Both runs pull the pulley rim in opposite rotational senses; the net turning agent is the difference. (2) \( \tau = (T_1 - T_2)\,r = (350 - 150) \times 0.100 = 20\ \mathrm{N\,m} \).

Answer: 20 N·m. Sanity check: using the tight side alone (35 N·m) forgets that the slack side fights back — the classic belt error; the 200 N difference is also exactly the net force the belt applies to the shaft bearings, which is why belt tension and bearing life are one conversation.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

The fan's spin-up

Problem. A fan accelerates uniformly from rest to 1200 rpm in 8.0 s. Find: the angular acceleration and the number of revolutions completed during the spin-up.

Solution. (1) \( \omega = 1200 \times 2\pi/60 = 125.7\ \mathrm{rad/s} \); \( \alpha = 125.7/8 = 15.7\ \mathrm{rad/s^2} \). (2) \( \theta = \tfrac12\alpha t^2 = 0.5 \times 15.7 \times 64 = 503\ \mathrm{rad} \). (3) Revolutions: \( 503/2\pi = 80 \).

Answer: ≈15.7 rad/s²; 80 revolutions. Sanity check: average speed during a uniform spin-up is half the final — 600 rpm = 10 rev/s — over 8 s: 80 revolutions ✓. The linear world's triangle-area logic transplants perfectly, which is the entire point of the shared toolkit.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A machine spindle at 3000 rpm is turning at about:

\( \omega = 2\pi \times 3000/60 = 314\ \mathrm{rad/s} \). Treating rpm as rad/s (3000) skips the conversion; 50 is rev/s mistaken for rad/s (missing the \( 2\pi \)); the huge option multiplies by 60 instead of dividing. The three distractors are precisely the three ways this conversion goes wrong in practice — one per forgotten factor.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Two points on the same rotating disc, one at 50 mm radius and one at 150 mm, have:

A rigid body shares one angular velocity — every point sweeps the same angle per second — while linear speed grows with radius: \( v = \omega r \), so triple the radius is triple the speed. This is why cutting speed depends on workpiece diameter at fixed spindle rpm, and why rim damage on large rotors is a higher-energy affair than hub damage: same \( \omega \), very different v.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

To maximize the torque from a 200 N pull on a 0.3 m wrench, the pull should be applied:

\( \tau = Fr\sin\theta \) peaks at \( \sin 90° = 1 \): \( 200 \times 0.3 = 60\ \mathrm{N\,m} \). Pulling along the handle gives zero torque however hard; 45° delivers only 71%; and shrinking r shrinks everything. Statics taught this as moments — rotation now spends the same geometry on causing angular acceleration rather than preventing it.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A shaft turns through 5 complete revolutions. In radians, that is about:

One revolution is \( 2\pi \) radians: \( 5 \times 2\pi = 31.4\ \mathrm{rad} \). Calling a revolution one radian (5) is the quiet error that breaks every later formula; 1800 converts to degrees, a unit the rotational toolkit never uses. Radians are the native currency because they make \( s = \theta r \) exact — everything else is exchange-rate arithmetic.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

A small motor pulley (60 mm) drives a large fan pulley (180 mm) by belt. The fan turns:

The belt imposes one rim speed on both pulleys: \( \omega_1 r_1 = \omega_2 r_2 \), so the triple-radius fan turns at a third the angular speed — and, by the coming lessons, with triple the torque, power flowing through unchanged (minus losses). Every belt-drive speed reduction and every gear ratio is this one equality, applied at the point of contact.
