Potential energy and conservation

Conservative forces and potential energy; mechanical energy conservation, and exactly where friction breaks it. Energy accounting as the engineer's first audit tool — losses always go somewhere, and the somewhere is billable.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • From what height must a 2 kg part fall to hit 7 m/s? (≈2.5 m)
  • When is mechanical energy not conserved, and where does the difference go?

01 · Stored work: potential energy

Some forces give back what work puts in. Lift a mass and gravity stores the effort: potential energy \( PE = mgh \), recoverable in full on the way down. Compress or stretch a spring and it banks \( PE = \tfrac12 kx^2 \). Forces with this repayment property — gravity, springs — are conservative: the work they do depends only on endpoints, not on the path, and that is what makes a stored-energy bookkeeping possible at all. The reference height for \( mgh \) is yours to choose; only DIFFERENCES in potential energy ever enter the physics, so the floor, the bench, or sea level serve equally, provided the choice is made once and kept.

02 · Conservation: the ledger balances

When only conservative forces do work, mechanical energy is conserved: \( KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2 \). Height converts to speed and back without loss — the falling pallet, the swinging hammer, the cam follower riding its profile. The equation answers speed-from-height questions in one line: \( v = \sqrt{2gh} \) for a drop from rest, with mass canceling entirely — a heavy tool and a light one arrive at the same speed, though not, as the momentum lesson will insist, with the same consequences.

03 · When friction joins: the ledger still balances

Friction does not break energy accounting; it opens a new account. With friction acting, \( KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2 + E_{lost} \), where the loss term — friction force times sliding distance — leaves the mechanical books and arrives as heat. The equation turns observed shortfalls into measurements: a crate that arrives slower than the frictionless prediction has told you exactly how much energy friction collected en route. Nothing disappears; it changes accounts, and the engineer's job is reading which account.

04 · The conservation working method

Choose two states — where the question starts and where it asks. Set the height reference and declare it. Write the full ledger for each state: kinetic, gravitational, spring if present. Ask the gatekeeper question — does anything non-conservative do work between the states? If no, equate the ledgers; if yes, add the loss term and route it honestly. Solve, then audit: mass should cancel in pure gravity problems (if it doesn't, a term is misplaced); speeds must come from energy DIFFERENCES, never absolute heights; and the frictionless answer should always bound the frictional one. The method's reach is its reward: trajectory shape, path curvature, and time all drop out of the accounting, which is why energy methods solve in one line what force methods solve in a page — whenever the question asks about states rather than histories.

The ledger habit is also a diagnostic instrument in the field: measure the arrival speed a machine element actually achieves, compare with the frictionless prediction, and the shortfall prices the losses without a single coefficient measured. Energy accounting reads machines the way the trend-reading of the mathematics course read signals — by what the books say is missing.

Examples and Quiz

Work every problem on paper first. Solved examples reveal their full solution; for the multiple-choice questions, select your answers and press Submit to see what was right, what was not, and why.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Speed at the bottom of a drop

Problem. A pallet slips from a rack and falls 2.5 m. Find: its impact speed, and show the mass does not matter.

Solution. (1) Conservation, drop from rest: \( mgh = \tfrac12 mv^2 \). (2) Mass cancels: \( v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.81 \times 2.5} = 7.0\ \mathrm{m/s} \).

Answer: ≈7 m/s (25 km/h), any mass. Sanity check: mass cancels in the SPEED — but not in the energy: a 50 kg pallet arrives with 1.2 kJ, a 500 kg one with 12 kJ, at the same 7 m/s. Same velocity, tenfold consequence — the distinction the momentum lesson formalizes next.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

The drop hammer's exchange

Problem. A 5 kg hammer head is raised 1.2 m and released to swing to its lowest point. Find: its speed and kinetic energy at the bottom, ignoring pivot losses.

Solution. (1) \( v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.81 \times 1.2} = 4.85\ \mathrm{m/s} \). (2) \( KE = \tfrac12 \times 5 \times 4.85^2 = 58.9\ \mathrm{J} \) — exactly the \( mgh = 5 \times 9.81 \times 1.2 \) invested.

Answer: ≈4.9 m/s carrying ≈59 J. Sanity check: the path — arc, ramp, or free drop — never entered the calculation; conservative forces pay by endpoints only. That path-independence is why energy methods dispatch swinging and sliding problems that force methods turn into geometry exercises.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

What friction collected on the ramp

Problem. A 20 kg crate leaves the top of a 3.0 m-high ramp from rest and arrives at the bottom at 5.0 m/s. Find: the energy lost to friction and the fraction of the invested energy it represents.

Solution. (1) Invested: \( mgh = 20 \times 9.81 \times 3.0 = 589\ \mathrm{J} \). (2) Delivered: \( \tfrac12 mv^2 = 0.5 \times 20 \times 25 = 250\ \mathrm{J} \). (3) Lost: \( 589 - 250 = 339\ \mathrm{J} \) — 58% of the investment, now heat in the ramp surface.

Answer: ≈339 J (58%) to friction. Sanity check: the frictionless arrival would have been \( \sqrt{2 \times 9.81 \times 3} = 7.7\ \mathrm{m/s} \); the observed 5.0 m/s < 7.7 respects the bound, and the shortfall MEASURED the friction without a single coefficient — the ledger used as an instrument.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

Choosing where \( h = 0 \) sits for gravitational potential energy:

PE is defined up to a constant: shift the reference and every state's PE shifts equally, leaving all DIFFERENCES — and therefore all speeds and forces — untouched. Any convenient level works, provided it is declared once and never moved mid-problem; moving it mid-ledger is the bookkeeping error that creates energy from nothing. Convention favors the lowest point purely for tidy positive numbers.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Mechanical energy \( (KE + PE) \) is conserved when:

Conservation is a property of WHICH forces do work, not of speed or path shape: gravity and springs repay in full; friction and drag divert energy to heat and break the mechanical ledger (though never the universal one). 'No forces' is unnecessarily strong — forces that do zero work, like normals, are harmless. The gatekeeper question of the working method is exactly this classification.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A spring of stiffness \( k = 800\ \mathrm{N/m} \) is compressed 50 mm. The stored energy is:

\( PE = \tfrac12 kx^2 = 0.5 \times 800 \times (0.05)^2 = 1.0\ \mathrm{J} \) — with x in METRES; feeding millimetres unconverted inflates the answer by \( 10^6 \). Choosing 40 forgets the square (\( \tfrac12 kx \) is a force-flavored hybrid, 20 N, not an energy); 20 forgets the half. Small spring energies still snap fingers: 1 J released in milliseconds is a hundred-watt event.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A crate slides to rest on a level floor. Its kinetic energy has:

Friction's negative work routes the energy to thermal accounts — the interface warms by exactly the kinetic energy spent. Nothing is destroyed (nature forbids it), no height changed (no PE), and energy does not flow backward up a drivetrain uninvited. The heat is usually too diffuse to notice — but brakes, clutches, and dragging belts make the same accounting vivid and occasionally expensive.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

An object dropped from height \( h \) passes the half-height point at what fraction of its final speed?

Speed goes as the square root of height fallen: at \( h/2 \), \( v = \sqrt{2g(h/2)} = \sqrt{gh} \), against a final \( \sqrt{2gh} \) — ratio \( 1/\sqrt{2} \). Half the DROP buys 71% of the speed (and half the energy). The intuition that speed accrues linearly with height is the error; the square root front-loads the gain, exactly as the braking-distance square did in reverse.
