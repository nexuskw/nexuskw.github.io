Kinematics in one dimension

01 · The three siblings: position, velocity, acceleration

Kinematics describes motion without asking why it happens, and its entire vocabulary is three quantities chained by calculus. Position \( x(t) \) says where; velocity \( v = dx/dt \) says how fast where is changing; acceleration \( a = dv/dt \) says how fast how-fast is changing. The chain runs both directions: differentiate to go from position toward acceleration, integrate to come back — and the mathematics course's slogan that "slopes and areas are inverses" becomes physical here. On a velocity–time graph, the slope at any instant is the acceleration and the area under the curve is the displacement; an engineer who can read those two facts off a trend owns most of kinematics.

02 · The constant-acceleration toolkit

When acceleration is constant — winch starts, braking runs, free fall — integration yields the standard kit: \( v = v_0 + at \), \( x = v_0t + \tfrac12 at^2 \), and the time-free combination \( v^2 = v_0^2 + 2ax \). Derive them once from the definitions and they stop being formulas to memorize and become consequences to trust. Each answers its own question — the first about speed at a time, the second about distance in a time, the third about speed after a distance — and choosing the one whose variables match the givens is the entire selection skill. Free fall is the same kit with \( a = g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) downward, air resistance ignored until a later course chooses to price it.

03 · Signs carry the physics

One axis, declared positive in one direction, governs every sign in the problem. Velocity opposite the axis is negative; acceleration opposite the VELOCITY is braking, but acceleration opposite the AXIS is just negative — the two statements differ, and conflating them is the classic kinematics error. A car moving in +x while slowing has negative acceleration; the same car reversing and speeding up ALSO has negative acceleration. The sign belongs to the axis, not to the feeling of speeding up or slowing down.

04 · The kinematics working method

Solve every problem the same way. Draw the axis and mark positive. List the knowns and the wanted symbolically — \( v_0, v, a, t, x \) — and strike what the problem doesn't mention. Pick the ONE equation containing exactly the knowns and the wanted; if none fits, the problem has two phases, so split it where the acceleration changes and chain the phases, the final state of one becoming the initial state of the next. Solve symbolically first, substitute numbers last, and audit twice: units through every line, and magnitude against experience — a forklift does not brake at \( 40\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), and an answer claiming so is a decimal slip announcing itself. The two-phase discipline matters most: most real motions — lift, travel, lower; accelerate, cruise, brake — are chains of simple segments, and the method scales to any chain length without changing shape.

Examples and Quiz

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

The hoist start

Problem. A hoist accelerates a load from rest at a constant \( 0.5\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) for 6 s. Find: the final speed and the distance risen.

Solution. (1) \( v = v_0 + at = 0 + 0.5 \times 6 = 3.0\ \mathrm{m/s} \). (2) \( x = v_0t + \tfrac12 at^2 = 0 + 0.5 \times 0.5 \times 36 = 9.0\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: 3 m/s after 9 m of travel. Sanity check: average velocity from rest to 3 m/s is 1.5 m/s; over 6 s that is 9 m — the area-under-the-graph reading (a triangle: ½ × 6 × 3) confirming the formula exactly.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Braking distance

Problem. A vehicle traveling at 20 m/s brakes at a constant \( 4\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). Find: the time to stop and the stopping distance.

Solution. (1) Time: \( 0 = 20 - 4t \Rightarrow t = 5\ \mathrm{s} \). (2) Distance (time-free check): \( 0 = 20^2 - 2 \times 4 \times x \Rightarrow x = 400/8 = 50\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: 5 s; 50 m. Sanity check: average speed while braking is 10 m/s for 5 s → 50 m ✓. Note the scaling buried here: stopping distance goes as \( v^2 \) — doubling speed quadruples the distance, the single most safety-relevant equation in kinematics.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

A two-phase travel

Problem. An automated cart accelerates from rest at \( 2\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) for 5 s, then holds its speed for 10 s. Find: the total distance covered.

Solution. (1) Phase 1: \( v = 2 \times 5 = 10\ \mathrm{m/s} \); \( x_1 = \tfrac12 \times 2 \times 25 = 25\ \mathrm{m} \). (2) Phase 2 (constant speed): \( x_2 = 10 \times 10 = 100\ \mathrm{m} \). (3) Total: \( 125\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: 125 m. Sanity check: the v–t graph is a triangle (area 25) followed by a rectangle (area 100) — the areas ARE the distances, and chaining phases at the speed handoff (10 m/s ends phase 1, begins phase 2) is the working method in miniature.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

On a velocity–time graph, the area under the curve represents:

Integrating velocity over time accumulates displacement — area under v–t is distance traveled (signed). Acceleration is the SLOPE of the same graph, one derivative up; average speed is total area over total time. Slope-versus-area on the v–t graph is the compact summary of all of kinematics, and this question is its passport check.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

A machine slide starts from rest and accelerates at \( 2\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) over 16 m. Its final speed is:

No time is given, so the time-free equation applies: \( v^2 = 0 + 2 \times 2 \times 16 = 64 \Rightarrow v = 8\ \mathrm{m/s} \). Multiplying a by distance (32) or halving things by instinct produces the distractors. Equation selection by matching knowns — here \( a, x \), no \( t \) — is the entire skill this item drills.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A wrench is dropped from a platform and falls for 2 s. Ignoring air resistance, it has fallen about:

\( x = \tfrac12 g t^2 = 0.5 \times 9.81 \times 4 = 19.6\ \mathrm{m} \) — roughly a six-story drop, which is why dropped-object prevention is a permit topic. Using \( gt \) (19.6 m/s) computes the SPEED, not the distance — the classic mix; 4.9 m is one second's fall. The square in \( t^2 \) means the second second does three times the damage of the first.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A cart moves in the +x direction while slowing down. Its acceleration is:

Slowing means the velocity (in +x) is being eaten away — the rate of change of velocity points against it: −x. "Deceleration" is not a separate quantity with its own rules; it is ordinary acceleration whose direction opposes the velocity. Attaching the sign to the axis rather than to the sensation of slowing is the discipline that keeps two-phase problems honest.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

The slope of a position–time graph at an instant gives:

\( v = dx/dt \): slope of position–time is instantaneous velocity — steep means fast, flat means stopped, downhill means moving backward. Acceleration lives one graph over (slope of v–t), and averages need whole intervals, not instants. Reading motion graphs at sight transfers directly to reading machine-axis traces and historian trends later.
