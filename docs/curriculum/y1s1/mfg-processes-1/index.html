MFG 105Year 1 · Semester 1

Manufacturing Processes I — Casting & Bulk Forming

Liquid metal to near-net shape: the plant taxonomy, casting physics taught at full lecture depth, continuous casting, and the bulk deformation family — rolling, forging, extrusion, drawing — with Kuwait Steel and Gulf Cable as the standing examples.

12 lessons · 7 in the Core 60 · Primary texts:

  • Groover, Fundamentals of Modern Manufacturing (Wiley) — casting & forming chapters
  • Kalpakjian & Schmid, Manufacturing Engineering and Technology (Pearson)
  • Flemings, Solidification Processing (McGraw-Hill, 1974)
  • ASM Handbook, Volume 15: Casting
01

The manufacturing plant: process taxonomy and material flowStudy guide

Every manufacturing operation sorted into a working taxonomy — casting, forming, machining, joining, finishing, assembly — and the material flow of a real plant traced end to end. The map the rest of the curriculum hangs on.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Which two process families shape most of the world's metal by tonnage — and where does machining actually sit?
  • Classify every step of rebar production, scrap to bundled bar, into the six-family taxonomy.
  • Primary versus secondary shaping: define both with one example of each from a cable plant.

Taught from — CORE 60 · A1 · Groover, Ch. 1; Kalpakjian & Schmid, General Introduction

02

Metal casting I — solidification, gating, and riser designFull lecture

The full lecture: pouring energy balance, Bernoulli-derived gating design, Chvorinov's rule derived from mold heat conduction, shrinkage regimes, and riser sizing — with worked examples carried through in units.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A casting has V/A = 2.0 cm and Cₘ = 2.5 min/cm²: how long until it is fully solid?
  • The free metal surface sits 250 mm above the sprue base: what is the base velocity by Torricelli?
  • Why must a riser out-modulus its casting, and what design margin did our worked example use?

Taught from — CORE 60 · A2 · Groover, Ch. 10; Flemings; Çengel & Ghajar, Ch. 4

03

Metal casting II — practice, quality, and defectsFull lecture

From theory to the foundry floor: patterns and allowances, molding sand behavior, fluidity, and the defect families — gas porosity via Sievert's law, shrinkage porosity, hot tears, sand defects — each tied to its physical cause and its inspection method.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Halving the hydrogen partial pressure over a melt cuts dissolved hydrogen by what factor — and why not one-half?
  • Smooth spherical pores near the surface versus a jagged void in a thick boss: name each family and its matching fix.
  • A 9,000 cm³ steel casting shrinks 3% on solidification; the riser feeds 14% of its own volume. Minimum riser volume?

Taught from — CORE 60 · A3 · Groover, Ch. 10–11; Campbell, Complete Casting Handbook; ASM Handbook Vol. 15

04

Continuous casting of steelStudy guide

The billet caster as a permanent-mold casting process running at steady state: shell growth, metallurgical length, breakout risk, and quality issues from rhomboidity to pinholes. Kuwait Steel's core process.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A 140 mm square billet cast with K = 26 mm/min^½: minutes until the core is solid?
  • At 2.4 m/min casting speed, roughly how far below the mold must the torch cut — and why no closer?
  • Why is casting speed not a free productivity knob? Name both penalties.

Taught from — CORE 60 · A4 · The Making, Shaping and Treating of Steel (AISE); Groover, Ch. 11

05

Casting processes survey: sand to die casting

Shell, investment, permanent mold, and die casting compared on tolerance, finish, section thickness, and economics. Expendable versus permanent molds as the organizing split. Choosing a casting route for a given part and volume.

Taught from — Groover, Ch. 11; Kalpakjian & Schmid, casting chapters

06

Fundamentals of metal forming

Flow stress and the flow curve; hot, warm, and cold working defined by homologous temperature. Strain-rate sensitivity in hot work. Friction and lubrication at the die interface — the tax every forming process pays.

Taught from — Groover, Ch. 18; Kalpakjian & Schmid, forming fundamentals

07

Rolling of metalsStudy guide

Flat and shape rolling from draft and bite condition through roll force and torque estimates. Hot rolling of billets into rebar as run at Kuwait Steel's mill. Rolled-product defects and their upstream causes.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • With μ = 0.25 and 350 mm radius rolls, what is the maximum draft in a single pass?
  • Compute the contact length for Δh = 6 mm on 300 mm radius rolls.
  • Rolling needs the friction that other forming processes fight — why?

Taught from — CORE 60 · A5 · Groover, Ch. 19; The Making, Shaping and Treating of Steel (AISE)

08

ForgingStudy guide

Open-die and impression-die forging; upsetting analysis with friction; flash, draft, and forging loads. Grain flow as forging's signature advantage. What a forged flange or valve body buys over a casting.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What true strain does upsetting from 80 mm to 50 mm impose?
  • For K = 600 MPa and n = 0.2 at ε = 0.47, what flow stress must the press overcome before friction?
  • What does grain flow buy in a forged crane hook — and in which loading direction?

Taught from — CORE 60 · A6 · Groover, Ch. 19; Kalpakjian & Schmid, forging chapter

09

Extrusion and wire drawingStudy guide

Direct and indirect extrusion; extrusion ratio and pressure estimates. Wire drawing through dies in tandem — reduction per pass, drawing stress, and why the wire must not yield in the drawn section. Gulf Cable's conductor line as the standing example.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What is the extrusion ratio when a 100 mm billet becomes a 25 mm rod?
  • Why is a 60% area reduction impossible in one wire-drawing pass even with a perfect, frictionless die?
  • A pass gives draw stress 60 MPa on wire whose exit flow stress is 160 MPa: safe or failing, and by what criterion?

Taught from — CORE 60 · A7 · Groover, Ch. 19; Kalpakjian & Schmid, extrusion & drawing chapters

10

Powder metallurgy

Powder production, compaction, and sintering. Density as the controlling quality variable. Where PM wins: small complex parts at volume, porous bearings, and hard-to-melt materials.

Taught from — Groover, powder metallurgy chapter; Kalpakjian & Schmid

11

Forming defects and workability

Edge cracking, center bursts (chevrons), laps, and surface defects mapped to their process causes. Workability limits and how processes are kept inside them. Defects as process diagnostics, not random events.

Taught from — Kalpakjian & Schmid, forming chapters; Groover, Ch. 19

12

Process selection and cost fundamentals

Choosing among casting, forming, and machining for a given geometry, alloy, tolerance, and volume. Fixed versus variable cost and the break-even volume between processes. Near-net-shape thinking as an economic argument.

Taught from — Groover, process selection discussions; Kalpakjian & Schmid
