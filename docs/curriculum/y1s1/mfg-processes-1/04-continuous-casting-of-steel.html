Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A 140 mm square billet cast with K = 26 mm/min^½: minutes until the core is solid?
  • At 2.4 m/min casting speed, roughly how far below the mold must the torch cut — and why no closer?
  • Why is casting speed not a free productivity knob? Name both penalties.

Objectives

  1. Trace the caster: ladle → tundish → water-cooled oscillating copper mold → spray zones → straightener → torch cutoff.
  2. Compute shell thickness at any point and the metallurgical length, and defend the torch position with them.
  3. Explain why casting speed trades against breakout risk, and name the main billet quality defects.

Key equations — sourced

Shell growth and metallurgical length (The Making, Shaping and Treating of Steel, AISE; same 1/√t physics as Lecture 1's Chvorinov derivation)
δ = K √t(1)
Lm = v · tfull = v (d2K)2(2)

δ is solid shell thickness after time t; K is the solidification constant (typically 25–30 mm/min½ for water-cooled casters); the strand is fully solid when δ reaches the half-thickness d/2, which at casting speed v happens a fixed distance Lm — the metallurgical length — below the mold.

Condensed worked example — representative billet-caster values, not published Kuwait Steel data

130 mm square billet, K = 27 mm/min½, v = 2.6 m/min, mold length 0.8 m:

t_full = (65/27)² = 5.8 min → L_m = 2.6 × 5.8 ≈ 15 m
shell at mold exit: t = 0.8/2.6 = 0.31 min → δ = 27√0.31 ≈ 15 mm
push v to 3.4 m/min → exit shell ≈ 13 mm, L_m ≈ 19.6 m — thinner skin, longer liquid core.

Misconceptions

"The strand is solid when it leaves the mold."
At mold exit it is a ~15 mm skin over a liquid core that persists for ~15 m of machine. Every roller and spray nozzle in between exists to contain and grow that skin. A skin rupture — a breakout — pours liquid steel over the machine; it is the melt shop's most feared routine event.
"Raising casting speed is free tonnage."
Equation (2): L_m grows with v while the mold-exit shell thins as 1/√v. Speed is bought with breakout risk, deeper liquid core, and quality penalties (centerline segregation, rhomboidity). The speed setpoint is a managed trade-off, not a throttle.

Quality connection

Lecture 2's defect families reappear at the caster with new names: pinholes (dissolved gas), internal and centerline cracks (thermal stress and bulging between rolls), rhomboidity (uneven mold cooling), and centerline segregation (the last liquid at the core is solute-rich — Lecture 1's dendritic argument at machine scale). Each traces to a specific subsystem: mold level control, oscillation, spray pattern, roll alignment — which is why caster maintenance quality is billet quality.

Kuwait floor

Kuwait Steel's route — EAF, ladle furnace, billet caster, rolling mill — makes this lesson the single most direct process-knowledge card you can play in their interviews. The four target roles all touch the caster: mold and oscillator health (reliability), strand containment (integrity), speed–quality trade-offs (production), and mold/segment change planning (planning).
