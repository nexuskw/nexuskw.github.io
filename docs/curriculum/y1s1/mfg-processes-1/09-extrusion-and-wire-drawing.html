Extrusion and wire drawing

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What is the extrusion ratio when a 100 mm billet becomes a 25 mm rod?
  • Why is a 60% area reduction impossible in one wire-drawing pass even with a perfect, frictionless die?
  • A pass gives draw stress 60 MPa on wire whose exit flow stress is 160 MPa: safe or failing, and by what criterion?

Objectives

  1. Compute extrusion ratio and estimate ram pressure with an empirical extrusion-strain model.
  2. Compute drawing stress for a wire pass and check it against the drawn wire's own yield strength.
  3. Explain why drawing reductions are limited per pass and how tandem drawing machines exploit that limit.

Key equations — sourced (Groover, Ch. 19)

Extrusion (Johnson's empirical extrusion strain) and round-wire drawing
rx = A0Af ,   εx = a + b · ln rx ,   p = Ȳf εx(1)
σd = Ȳf (1 + μα) φ · ln (A0Af)(2)

In (1), a ≈ 0.8 and b ≈ 1.2–1.5 fold ideal work, friction, and redundant shear into one empirical strain (Groover's treatment of Johnson's equation). In (2), μ is die friction, α the die half-angle (radians), and φ ≥ 1 a redundant-work factor that grows for short contact geometries. The absolute constraint: σd must stay well below the drawn wire's yield stress, or the wire yields and snaps at the exit instead of drawing at the die.

Condensed worked example — one copper drawing pass (Gulf Cable-type duty)

Annealed copper (K = 300 MPa, n = 0.50), wire 3.50 → 3.03 mm (25 % area reduction), μ = 0.07, α = 6° = 0.105 rad:

ln(A₀/A_f) = ln(1.333) = 0.288 → Ȳ_f = K εⁿ/(1+n) = 300 × 0.288^0.5 / 1.5 = 107 MPa
contact: L_c = (3.50−3.03)/(2 sin 6°) = 2.25 mm; D̄/L_c = 3.27/2.25 = 1.45 → φ = 0.88 + 0.12 × 1.45 = 1.05
σ_d = 107 × (1 + 0.07/0.105) × 1.05 × 0.288 ≈ 54 MPa
check: flow stress of drawn wire = 300 × 0.288^0.5 = 161 MPa ≫ 54 MPa ✓ safe pass
draw force = 54 MPa × 7.21 mm² ≈ 390 N per wire

Misconceptions

"Take the biggest reduction the die survives — fewer passes, faster line."
The die is not the limit; the wire is. σd rises with ln(A₀/A_f) and must stay below the wire's yield — the theoretical ceiling is 63 % area reduction even for a frictionless, ideal pass, and practice runs far lower. Hence tandem machines: many modest passes, each re-checked, often with anneals between blocks as strain hardening accumulates.
"Extrusion and drawing are the same process from the die's point of view."
The die geometry is similar; the stress state is opposite. Extrusion's compression lets it take enormous strains in one shot (rx of 40+ for aluminum) and even improves workability; drawing's tension caps every pass. That one sign change explains both process's products: massive sections from extrusion, kilometres of fine wire from drawing.

Kuwait floor

Gulf Cable's conductor route is this lesson end to end: copper (and aluminum) rod drawn through tandem die blocks toward conductor sizes, annealed to restore ductility, stranded into conductors, then insulated by polymer extrusion — equation (1)'s polymer cousin — over the moving conductor. Die wear, lubricant condition, and tension control are the line's daily maintenance-engineering diet, and wire breaks are its OEE story.

Study from — Groover, Ch. 19; Kalpakjian & Schmid, extrusion and drawing chapters. Video: SME's process video library covers extrusion and drawing; NPTEL (IIT Roorkee) is the captioned alternative — links on the course page.
