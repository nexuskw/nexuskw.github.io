Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What true strain does upsetting from 80 mm to 50 mm impose?
  • For K = 600 MPa and n = 0.2 at ε = 0.47, what flow stress must the press overcome before friction?
  • What does grain flow buy in a forged crane hook — and in which loading direction?

Objectives

  1. Distinguish open-die, impression-die, and flashless forging by tooling and by what each can promise.
  2. Compute an upsetting force including friction's barreling penalty, units carried through.
  3. Explain grain flow — what it genuinely buys and what it does not.

Key equations — sourced (Groover, Ch. 19)

Upsetting a cylinder: strain, flow stress, friction factor, force
ε = ln (h0h) ,   Yf = K εn(1)
Kf = 1 + 0.4 μ Dh ,   F = Kf Yf A(2)

Volume constancy sets the growing area A = V/h; the flow curve (K, n from the material's cold-work behavior) sets the strength at the current strain; Kf taxes the force for friction at the dies — the same friction that barrels the part.

Condensed worked example — cold upsetting a low-carbon steel slug

d₀ = 50 mm, h₀ = 70 mm, upset to h = 40 mm; annealed low-C steel K = 500 MPa, n = 0.25 (Groover's flow-curve table); μ = 0.25:

V = π/4 × 50² × 70 = 137,400 mm³ → A = V/h = 3,436 mm², D = √(4A/π) = 66.1 mm
ε = ln(70/40) = 0.56 → Y_f = 500 × 0.56^0.25 = 432 MPa
K_f = 1 + 0.4 × 0.25 × 66.1/40 = 1.17
F = 1.17 × 432×10⁶ × 3.436×10⁻³ ≈ 1.73 MN  (≈ 176 tonnes-force at the final instant)

Misconceptions

"Forged parts are simply stronger than cast parts."
Forging refines structure, closes porosity, and aligns inclusion stringers along the flow direction — so properties improve along the grain flow and fatigue resistance rises where flow follows the loading path. Across the flow, the advantage shrinks. A well-designed casting can beat a badly oriented forging; specify the loading direction before praising the process.
"Flash is just wasted metal."
In impression-die forging, flash is a pressure valve and a die-filling tool: the thin flash land chokes outward flow, building the internal pressure that forces metal into the last corners of the cavity. It is then trimmed — engineered waste, not accidental waste.

Kuwait floor

Kuwait imports most forgings, but a maintenance-to-manufacturing engineer meets them daily as flanges, valve internals, crane hooks, and shafts — and meets forging questions in HEISCO-type fabrication and repair scopes: verifying material certificates, reading grain flow in a failed hook's fracture, and knowing why a machined-from-bar replacement is not automatically equivalent to the forged original.
