Arrays and numerical work

NumPy arrays for vectorized calculation over whole datasets. Elementwise operations, aggregation, and broadcasting. A vibration record processed without a single explicit loop.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What does vectorization replace, and why is it faster and clearer?
  • Compute the RMS of an array in one line — conceptually, what happened?

01 · The array idea: operate on all of it

Lists process element by element through loops; numerical arrays (NumPy's ndarray) operate on whole datasets at once: kw = hp * 0.746 converts every horsepower value in one expression — vectorization. The gains are two: the code READS as the mathematics (one formula, visibly applied to everything), and it runs orders of magnitude faster, because the looping happens in optimized machinery below. For engineering data — a year of hourly readings is 8,760 numbers, a vibration waveform 65,536 — the array is the natural container, and the loop becomes the exception rather than the habit.

02 · The reductions: from many numbers to a few

Analysis usually ends in reductions: mean, std, min/max, sum — the descriptive statistics of a dataset in one call each. The standard deviation deserves engineering respect: it is the honest width of the scatter, the number that turns "about 4.25" into "4.25 ± 0.30", and the seed of every control chart the metrology course will draw. Alongside them, argmax answers WHERE — the index of the worst reading — which is often the more actionable question: not "how bad" but "which machine".

03 · Masks: asking questions of data

Comparison on an array yields an array of booleans — a mask — and the mask selects: readings[readings > 4.0] is every reading above the alarm line, in one expression: no loop, no if, no accumulator. Count them with sum of the mask, average them, or feed them onward. The idiom — condition, mask, selection — is the data-analysis sentence structure, and it compounds: masks combine with \( \texttt{\&} \) and \( \texttt{|} \) into compound questions ("above alarm AND after the overhaul") that still read as one line.

04 · The vectorized working method

Reach for the array whenever data is homogeneous and numeric; keep lists for mixed records. Write transformations as whole-array expressions and CHECK them on a three-value array first — vectorized code is compact enough that a wrong formula corrupts everything uniformly, so the small trace matters doubly. Prefer the built-in reductions to hand-rolled loops (they are tested code, and faster), and when a reduction's answer surprises, look at the mask counts before doubting the data: how many values, how many above threshold, how many NaN — the three questions that catch most data-quality issues at the door. Then the discipline that spans every lesson: units ride the variable names (vel_mm_s), conversions happen once, visibly, at the border, and any result consumed downstream gets an assertion pinning a known case. Arrays change the scale of what one line does; they change nothing about what makes the line trustworthy.

The payoff arrives immediately in the lessons ahead: plotting consumes arrays whole, curve fitting reduces them to parameters, and simulations generate them by the thousand. Vectorized thinking — state the operation once, apply it to everything, summarize with a reduction — is also precisely how plant historians and analysis platforms work internally, so fluency here reads directly onto the tools the later automation courses will operate.

Examples and Quiz

Work every problem on paper first. Solved examples reveal their full solution; for the multiple-choice questions, select your answers and press Submit to see what was right, what was not, and why.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Fleet conversion in one line

Problem. Motor ratings arrive in horsepower: [10, 25, 50]. Convert the whole set to kW (1 hp = 0.746 kW) vectorized, and state the result.

Solution. (1) hp = np.array([10, 25, 50]); kw = hp * 0.746. (2) Result: [7.46, 18.65, 37.3] — every element converted by the one expression.

Answer: [7.46, 18.65, 37.3] kW. Sanity check: the same operation on a plain LIST — [10,25,50] * 0.746 — is an error, and * 3 would have REPEATED the list: the array's element-wise arithmetic is a genuinely different contract, and knowing which container you hold decides what * means.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Mean and scatter of a survey

Problem. Four bearing-temperature readings: 4.1, 3.9, 4.3, 4.7 (°C above ambient). Find: the mean and the (population) standard deviation, and report them together.

Solution. (1) Mean: \( (4.1+3.9+4.3+4.7)/4 = 4.25 \). (2) Deviations: −0.15, −0.35, 0.05, 0.45; squares sum to 0.35; variance \( 0.35/4 = 0.0875 \). (3) \( \sigma = \sqrt{0.0875} = 0.30 \). Report: \( 4.25 \pm 0.30 \) °C.

Answer: mean 4.25, σ ≈ 0.30 — "4.25 ± 0.30 °C". Sanity check: σ has the data's own units and sits comfortably inside the observed spread (range 0.8) — a σ larger than the range would announce an arithmetic slip. The ± habit turns every reported mean into an honest claim, and the metrology course will build control charts on exactly this number.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

The alarm-line question

Problem. Vibration readings [3.2, 5.1, 2.8, 6.0] mm/s; the alert line is 4.0. Using a mask, find how many readings exceed it and their average.

Solution. (1) Mask: v > 4.0 → [False, True, False, True]. (2) Count: 2. Selection: v[v > 4.0] → [5.1, 6.0]. (3) Mean of offenders: \( (5.1+6.0)/2 = 5.55\ \mathrm{mm/s} \).

Answer: 2 readings above the line, averaging 5.55 mm/s. Sanity check: condition → mask → selection replaced a four-line loop-with-if, and reads as the question itself. Scale it to a year of route data and the same one-liner is a fleet exception report — the entire reason the mask idiom is worth owning.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

np.array([1, 2, 3]) * 2 evaluates to:

Arrays do element-wise arithmetic: every value doubles. The repetition answer is what a plain LIST does with * 2 — the container decides the contract, and this pair of behaviors side by side is the cleanest illustration of why. Knowing which type you hold (Lesson 2's discipline, scaled up) is what makes the one-character operator safe.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

argmax returns:

max answers 'how bad'; argmax answers 'WHERE' — the position, which maps to a machine, a timestamp, a route point. In maintenance data the location is usually the actionable half: tags[np.argmax(vibration)] names the machine to visit. The pairing of a value-question with its index-question runs through all of applied data work.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

The standard deviation of a set of readings tells you:

σ is scatter's width — the honest ± beside the mean. It bounds nothing absolutely (readings beyond σ are routine — most data holds ~1/3 of it there), says nothing about BIAS (a precisely wrong instrument scatters tightly around the wrong value), and instrument accuracy is a calibration question. Reporting mean ± σ claims exactly what the data supports, no more.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A vectorized formula applied to 30,000 readings should first be checked by:

Vectorization's power is uniform application — which means a wrong formula corrupts all 30,000 values uniformly and plausibly. The three-value trace with a known answer pins the formula; then scale merely repeats proven arithmetic. Re-running identical wrongness reproduces it identically; absence of errors proves syntax, not sense — the recurring theme every verification lesson has hammered.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

For a = np.array([10, 20, 30, 40]), the slice a[1:3] is:

Same convention as lists and range: start included, stop excluded — indices 1 and 2, values 20 and 30. The consistency across list, range, and array slicing is deliberate: one fencepost rule serves the whole language, and internalizing 'stop is excluded' once retires the off-by-one everywhere at once.
