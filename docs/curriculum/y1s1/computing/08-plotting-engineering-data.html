Plotting engineering data

Time series, scatter, and log-scale plots with honest axes and units. The plot as an argument, not a decoration. Reproducing a textbook figure from raw numbers.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Name three properties of an honest engineering plot.
  • When does a log axis reveal what a linear one hides?

01 · The plot is an argument

An engineering plot exists to support a decision, and its anatomy is the argument's grammar: axes labeled WITH UNITS (an unlabeled axis is a number with no meaning — the chart version of the unit sins every course here prosecutes), a title that states what is shown, and a legend when more than one series appears. The choice of plot type is the choice of claim: a LINE plot claims continuity — readings ordered in time, trends; a SCATTER plot claims only "these pairs were observed" — right for correlation questions and unordered data; a BAR chart compares discrete categories — machines, months, failure modes. Choosing a line where a scatter belongs invents continuity the data never promised; the plot type is the first honesty decision.

02 · Scales that reveal

Linear axes hide what logarithmic axes reveal. Exponential decay — the cooling curves and filter-loading trends of the mathematics course — plots as a curve on linear axes but as a STRAIGHT LINE on a semilog (log-y) plot, whose slope reads the time constant: plotting \( \ln(T - T_\infty) \) against t was exactly this move, done by hand. Data spanning decades of magnitude — vibration spectra, particle counts — needs log scales simply to be seen: on a linear axis, the small values flatten into the floor. The working question before every plot: does this data live on one scale, or several?

03 · Sampling honesty

Plotted data was SAMPLED, and sampling has physics. A signal changing faster than half the sampling rate does not disappear — it ALIASES, reappearing as a false slow signal: a 50 Hz vibration sampled at 60 Hz shows up as a phantom 10 Hz wobble (\( |50 - 60| \)). The instrumentation course will formalize Nyquist; the plotting lesson's version is the caution: before trusting a surprising slow oscillation in sampled data, ask what the sampling rate was. Equally, decimating data for plotting speed (every tenth point) is legitimate — STATED; silently, it is how spikes vanish from trend reviews.

04 · The plotting working method

Build every plot in the same order. State the question the plot must answer; choose the type by the claim (trend → line, correlation → scatter, comparison → bar); choose scales by the data's structure (exponential suspicion → semilog; decades of range → log); label axes with quantities AND units; then read the plot yourself before showing anyone — does it actually answer the question, and would a skeptic's eye find the axis tricks (truncated baselines, cherry-picked windows) that make weak cases look strong? The mathematics course called the plot the test of first-order behavior; this lesson generalizes: the plot is where data confesses its structure, provided the plotter asks honestly. A figure that survives your own skepticism is ready for the morning meeting — and a figure that decided a question honestly is worth saving beside the script and data that produced it, completing the audit trail this course keeps building.

Examples and Quiz

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Choosing the revealing scale

Problem. A casting's cooling data (temperature above ambient vs time) must be tested for first-order behavior and its time constant read graphically. Specify the plot, and what a straight line there proves.

Solution. (1) Plot \( \ln(T - T_\infty) \) against t — equivalently, the temperature EXCESS on a semilog (log-y) axis. (2) First-order decay \( e^{-t/\tau} \) becomes a straight line of slope \( -1/\tau \); curvature would refute the single-τ model. (3) Read τ as the negative reciprocal of the fitted slope.

Answer: semilog plot of the excess; straightness proves first-order; slope gives \( \tau \). Sanity check: this is the mathematics course's semilog method, now one plotting call instead of a hand table — same test, same honesty: the plot decides whether the model applies BEFORE the constant is quoted. Scale choice is model testing, not decoration.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Anatomy of a defensible trend plot

Problem. Specify the complete labeling for a plot of a week of bearing-vibration readings taken every 4 hours — and the plot type, with justification.

Solution. (1) Type: LINE plot — the readings are time-ordered, and the claim is a trend. (2) X-axis: "Time (h since Monday 00:00)" — quantity and unit; y-axis: "Vibration velocity (mm/s RMS)". (3) Title naming machine and bearing; if a second bearing shares the axes, a legend naming each series.

Answer: labeled line plot, units on both axes, titled and legended. Sanity check: strip any one element and a real failure mode appears: no units → is that mm/s or in/s?; no title → whose bearing?; scatter instead of line → the trend claim dissolves into dots. The labeling checklist is cheap; the meeting where a mislabeled chart decided wrongly is not.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

The phantom wobble

Problem. A 50 Hz vibration is logged by a system sampling at 60 Hz. Find: the frequency that appears in the data, and state the general caution.

Solution. (1) The sampling can only represent signals up to 30 Hz (half of 60). (2) The 50 Hz signal aliases to \( |50 - 60| = 10\ \mathrm{Hz} \) — a slow, entirely fictitious wobble. (3) Caution: any surprising slow oscillation in sampled data demands the question "what was the sampling rate?" before any diagnosis.

Answer: a phantom 10 Hz signal. Sanity check: the alias is not noise — it is a clean, convincing, WRONG signal, which is what makes it dangerous. The instrumentation course formalizes the Nyquist limit; the plotting lesson's duty is the reflex: sampling rate first, diagnosis second.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

Every axis of an engineering plot must carry:

An axis labeled 'Value' — or nothing — makes the entire figure unfalsifiable: 4.5 of WHAT? The quantity-plus-unit rule is the plotting form of the unit discipline every course here enforces, and reviewers read axes before they read curves. Ticks and trend lines are choices; labels are obligations.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Exponential decay data appears as a straight line on:

Taking the log of \( Ae^{-t/\tau} \) gives a line in t with slope \( -1/\tau \) — the semilog axis does the logarithm visually. Zooming a linear plot never straightens a curve; it magnifies it. The straightening test is diagnostic, not cosmetic: straight ⇒ first-order applies; curved ⇒ the model (not the plot) needs revisiting — the mathematics course's rule, automated.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

To show whether bearing temperature correlates with load across 200 unordered spot readings, the right plot is:

The question is pairwise association: temperature VERSUS load, each reading one dot — structure (or its absence) appears as a cloud's shape. A line through unordered readings draws meaningless connections; 200 bars is unreadable; pie charts answer share-of-whole questions only. Type follows question — the first choice of the working method.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

Plotting only every 10th point of a dense trend for speed is acceptable when:

Decimation is a legitimate rendering economy with a failure mode: a one-sample spike — the very event a review hunts — can fall between kept points and vanish. Stated ("plotted at 1/10 resolution; extrema verified separately"), the reader knows the contract; silent, the plot testifies to smoothness it never checked. Honesty here is disclosure, as everywhere.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

A bar chart comparing monthly downtime hours should generally have its y-axis:

Bar charts encode value as LENGTH, and lengths compare honestly only from a common zero — a truncated baseline turns a 5% difference into a visual 3:1 ratio, the oldest chart trick in the persuasion business. (Line plots may zoom, with axes labeled, since they encode position, not length.) The skeptic's-eye pass of the working method looks for exactly this.
