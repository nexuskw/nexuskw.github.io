Engineering Computing

Python as a plant engineer's calculator and data tool: scripts, arrays, plots, curve fits, and small simulations — enough computing to automate the analyses the rest of this curriculum assigns.

10 lessons · 0 in the Core 60 · Primary texts:

  • MIT OCW 6.0001 Watch lectures ▶ Introduction to Computer Science and Programming in Python (ocw.mit.edu)
01

Why engineers script

The spreadsheet's limits and the script's advantages: repeatability, audit trail, scale. Setting up Python and running a first calculation. Version thinking for engineering work.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Name two failure modes of spreadsheet engineering that scripts avoid.
  • What makes a calculation reproducible?

02

Variables, types, and expressions

Numbers, strings, booleans; assignment and naming that reads like engineering. Floating point and its surprises in unit conversions. Writing formulas as code without transcription errors.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does 0.1 + 0.2 ≠ 0.3 in floating point, and when does an engineer care?
  • Show a unit conversion written so the units audit themselves.

03

Control flow

Conditionals and loops for case logic and iteration. Guard clauses for invalid inputs. Translating a calculation procedure into control flow.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Turn 'if the reading exceeds limit, flag it' into control flow — what guards the invalid-input case?
  • When is a while loop honest and a for loop not?

Taught from — MIT OCW 6.0001 Watch lectures ▶

04

Functions

Functions as named, testable calculations. Arguments, returns, and docstrings that state units. Building a small library of personal engineering functions.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What belongs in a function's docstring for an engineering calculation?
  • Why do small, single-purpose functions beat one long script?

Taught from — MIT OCW 6.0001 Watch lectures ▶

05

Lists, dictionaries, and files

Collections for measurement sets and equipment records. Reading and writing CSV files — the lingua franca of plant data exports. Cleaning real, messy data defensively.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why is CSV the lingua franca of plant data, and what are its traps?
  • Dict versus list for an equipment register: which and why?

Taught from — MIT OCW 6.0001 Watch lectures ▶

06

Arrays and numerical work

NumPy arrays for vectorized calculation over whole datasets. Elementwise operations, aggregation, and broadcasting. A vibration record processed without a single explicit loop.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What does vectorization replace, and why is it faster and clearer?
  • Compute the RMS of an array in one line — conceptually, what happened?

07

Plotting engineering data

Time series, scatter, and log-scale plots with honest axes and units. The plot as an argument, not a decoration. Reproducing a textbook figure from raw numbers.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Name three properties of an honest engineering plot.
  • When does a log axis reveal what a linear one hides?

Taught from — MIT OCW 6.0001 Watch lectures ▶

08

Curve fitting and interpolation

Least-squares fits to calibration and performance data. Interpolation for table lookups — steam tables and pump curves as targets. Residuals as the fit's honesty check.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What do residuals tell you that R² alone does not?
  • Interpolation versus extrapolation: why is one routine and the other risky?

09

Small simulations

Time-stepping a first-order ODE: tank level, cooling, simple reliability decay. Step size and stability observed experimentally. Simulation as a cheap laboratory.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Time-step a cooling ODE: what goes wrong when the step is too large?
  • Why is simulation a cheap laboratory — and when is it a lying one?

10

Project: a maintenance data mini-analysis

End-to-end exercise: load a work-order export, clean it, compute simple KPIs, and plot the story. The habits of reproducible analysis. This project pattern returns in Reliability Engineering.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • From a work-order export, which cleaning steps must precede any KPI?
  • Why should analysis scripts read raw data and never edit it?
