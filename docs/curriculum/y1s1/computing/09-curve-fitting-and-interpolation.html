Curve fitting and interpolation

Least-squares fits to calibration and performance data. Interpolation for table lookups — steam tables and pump curves as targets. Residuals as the fit's honesty check.

01 · Two questions a table cannot answer alone

Engineering data arrives as points: rows of a steam table, samples on a pump test, readings from a calibration bench. The questions arrive between the points — the head at a flow the test never held, the transmitter output at a pressure between bench settings. Two distinct operations close that gap. Interpolation reads between the rows of data you trust, adding no opinion of its own. Fitting proposes a model — a line, a low-order polynomial — and asks the data to vote on its parameters. Keeping the two distinct is the first discipline of this lesson: interpolation honors every point; fitting deliberately smooths over their scatter, and each is wrong where the other is right.

02 · Interpolation: reading between the rows

Between two table rows \( (x_1, y_1) \) and \( (x_2, y_2) \), linear interpolation draws the chord: \( y = y_1 + \frac{x - x_1}{x_2 - x_1}\,(y_2 - y_1) \). The fraction is just "how far across the interval x sits", and the same fraction is applied to the y-interval. Property tables are published dense precisely so that this chord is an excellent stand-in for the true curve; np.interp(x, xs, ys) does the row hunting and the arithmetic in one call, provided the sample points xs increase monotonically. The method's honest boundary is the table's edge: extrapolation — reading beyond the last row — is a different act entirely, because past the data the curve owes you nothing. Worse, np.interp does not protest at the edge: it silently returns the endpoint value, so the guard clause of Lesson 3 — assert the query lies inside the table's range — is part of every lookup that matters.

03 · Least squares: the best line through scatter

Calibration data never lies on a line; it scatters around one. For each candidate line, every point carries a residual — measured minus predicted, \( r_i = y_i - (m x_i + b) \). Least squares chooses the \( m, b \) that minimize \( \sum r_i^2 \), and for a straight line the answer is closed-form: \( m = \frac{n\sum x y - \sum x \sum y}{n \sum x^2 - (\sum x)^2} \), with \( b = \bar{y} - m\bar{x} \). np.polyfit(x, y, 1) evaluates exactly this. The degree argument is a temptation: a degree-9 polynomial threads all ten of your points exactly — residuals zero, \( R^2 = 1 \) — and swings wildly everywhere between them. Adding parameters always improves the numbers and, past the physics, always degrades the model; the fit stops summarizing the data and starts memorizing its noise.

04 · The fitting working method

The routine that keeps fits honest: PLOT the data before fitting anything — the eye catches curvature, outliers, and regime changes that summary numbers hide. Fit the simplest model the physics justifies, then plot the RESIDUALS: random scatter of roughly constant width says the model has taken all the structure the data offers; any pattern — a bow, a trend, a fan — is physics the model missed, an instruction to think, not to raise the degree. Distrust \( R^2 \) as a verdict: it measures variance explained, and a badly bowed fit can still post 0.99; the residual plot, not the score, is the honesty check. Report parameters with their units and alongside the data's domain, because the fit is a summary of THAT range — using it outside is extrapolation wearing a calculator's confidence. And when the goal is only to read between trusted rows, skip the model entirely: interpolate. The habits are small, but they are the difference between a calibration you can defend and a curve that merely looks professional. Both return in force when Metrology fits gauge data and Reliability fits life data — this lesson is their rehearsal.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Reading a pump curve between test points

Problem. A pump test reports head 42 m at 30 m³/h and 38 m at 40 m³/h. By linear interpolation, what head does the curve predict at 34 m³/h?

Solution. (1) Fraction across the interval: \( (34-30)/(40-30) = 0.4 \). (2) Apply it to the head interval: \( h = 42 + 0.4\,(38 - 42) = 42 - 1.6 = 40.4 \) m.

Answer: 40.4 m. Sanity check: 34 m³/h sits 40% of the way across the flow interval, so the head must sit 40% of the way down the 4-m drop — between 42 and 38, nearer 42. Any interpolated value OUTSIDE its bracketing rows is an arithmetic slip: the chord never leaves the interval.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Calibrating a pressure transmitter

Problem. A bench calibration of a 4–20 mA pressure transmitter records output y (mA) at applied pressure x (bar): (0, 4.1), (2, 7.2), (4, 10.4), (6, 13.3), (8, 16.5). Fit the least-squares line and state the predicted output at 5.0 bar.

Solution. (1) The sums over the five points: \( \sum x = 20 \), \( \sum y = 51.5 \), \( \sum xy = 267.8 \), \( \sum x^2 = 120 \). (2) Slope: \( m = \frac{5(267.8) - 20(51.5)}{5(120) - 20^2} = \frac{309}{200} = 1.545 \) mA/bar; intercept: \( b = (51.5 - 1.545 \times 20)/5 = 4.12 \) mA — exactly what np.polyfit(x, y, 1) returns. (3) At 5.0 bar: \( y = 1.545(5.0) + 4.12 = 11.845 \approx 11.85 \) mA.

Answer: \( y = 1.545x + 4.12 \); predicted 11.85 mA at 5.0 bar. Sanity check: an ideal 4–20 mA device spanning 0–10 bar would give exactly 1.6 mA/bar from 4.0 mA — the fit (1.545 from 4.12) sits close, so the instrument is near nominal. The largest residual is 0.1 mA, scattered in sign — no structure, so the straight-line model is doing its job.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Reading residuals like an engineer

Problem. Flow-meter data at x = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 gives y = 1.0, 3.15, 5.6, 8.35, 11.4. The least-squares line is \( y = 2.6x + 0.7 \) with \( R^2 = 0.9954 \). Compute the five residuals and give the verdict on the model.

Solution. (1) Predictions at x = 0…4: 0.7, 3.3, 5.9, 8.5, 11.1. (2) Residuals (measured − predicted): +0.30, −0.15, −0.30, −0.15, +0.30. (3) The pattern is a symmetric U — positive at both ends, negative in the middle. That is CURVATURE the line cannot express: the data carries a quadratic term the model omitted.

Answer: residuals [+0.30, −0.15, −0.30, −0.15, +0.30] — reject the straight line; the data is curved. Sanity check: this is the lesson's central trap: \( R^2 = 0.9954 \) LOOKS like an endorsement, yet the residual plot convicts the model at a glance. Variance explained is not structure exhausted — the residual plot, not the score, is the honesty check.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A residual is:

\( r_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i \): what the data said minus what the model claims — each point's individual report on the fit. Everything diagnostic in this lesson (the U-pattern, the honesty check, the overfitting verdict) is read from these numbers, which is why they matter more than any single summary score.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Extrapolating a fitted calibration line far beyond the calibrated range is risky because:

The fit is a summary of the range that produced it. Beyond that range there is no data to vote, and real devices leave their linear region — sensors saturate, pumps run off-curve. The arithmetic extends happily; the EVIDENCE does not. That is why the working method reports a fit's domain right beside its coefficients.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A fit posts \( R^2 = 0.99 \). What does that establish?

\( R^2 \) reports variance explained — a bowed line through curved data can still score 0.99, as the solve problem showed at 0.9954. Model adequacy is read from the residual plot: random scatter passes, any structure fails. High \( R^2 \) with patterned residuals means "good summary, wrong model".

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A degree-9 polynomial fitted through 10 calibration points gives \( R^2 = 1 \). The model is:

Ten coefficients against ten points can thread every measurement exactly — including their noise — and oscillate wildly between them. Zero residuals here indict the model rather than endorse it. Parameters must be justified by physics, not by score-chasing: for a near-linear device, that means a line.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

np.interp(x, xs, ys) requires — and silently assumes — that:

The function hunts for the bracketing rows, so xs must be sorted ascending — and beyond either end it CLAMPS to the edge value without warning, which turns an out-of-range query into a plausible-looking wrong answer. Hence the working method's guard: assert the query lies inside the table before trusting the lookup.
