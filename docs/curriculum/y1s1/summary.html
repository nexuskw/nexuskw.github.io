MTH 101 — Engineering Mathematics I

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Functions, units, and engineering magnitudes

01 · Numbers that carry units

Engineering mathematics begins one step before calculus: every quantity you will ever differentiate or integrate carries a unit, and the units obey algebra just as the numbers do. When a pump curve says head is 42 m, that number is meaningless until you know it multiplies through \( P = \rho g Q H \) to give watts:

\[ [P] = \frac{\mathrm{kg}}{\mathrm{m}^3}\cdot\frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}^2}\cdot\frac{\mathrm{m}^3}{\mathrm{s}}\cdot \mathrm{m} = \frac{\mathrm{kg\,m^2}}{\mathrm{s}^3} = \mathrm{W} \tag{1} \]

Check the units first and half of all calculation errors die before they are born. This is the dimensional analysis discipline: both sides of any physical equation must carry identical dimensions, and arguments of \( \sin \), \( \ln \), \( e^x \) must be dimensionless.

02 · Functions as machine behavior

A function \( f(x) \) is a machine's behavior written down: give it a flow, it returns a pressure drop; give it a time, it returns a temperature. The pump curve \( H(Q) \), the motor torque curve \( T(\omega) \), the cooling curve \( T(t) \) — the whole plant is a library of functions. Domain matters physically: \( H(Q) \) exists only for \( Q \ge 0 \), and beyond runout flow the curve is not merely undefined but forbidden.

03 · Magnitudes and scales

An engineer estimates before calculating. Powers of ten are the estimating language: a 100 m³/h pump moves \(\sim\)28 L/s; a kilowatt is ten 100 W lamps; a bar is \(\sim\)10 m of water column. On the plant floor this means you can smell a wrong answer: if a small transfer pump computes to 300 kW, the arithmetic is wrong before you re-check it — the machine on the skid is the size of a suitcase, and suitcases don't dissipate 300 kW.

04 · The unit-check working method

Knowing that units must balance is not the same as having a method. Use this three-step protocol on every equation you meet, in this course and every one after it. Step one: write the equation symbolically and substitute units, not numbers — run the algebra on the units alone and confirm both sides match, exactly as equation (1) did for pump power. Step two: convert every input to base SI before any arithmetic: flows to m³/s, pressures to Pa, lengths to m. Most real-world calculation errors are unit errors, and most unit errors happen when kPa meets Pa or m³/h meets m³/s in the same line. Step three: after computing, translate the answer back into the working units of the trade — kW, bar, m³/h — and test it against an order-of-magnitude estimate before you write it down.

Two traps deserve names. The gauge trap: gauge and absolute pressure differ by one atmosphere, and equations built on absolute pressure quietly fail when fed gauge readings. The prefix trap: a dropped factor of 1000 from k, M, or m survives every later step and grows more embarrassing the longer it lives. Scientific notation is the antidote — write \( 2.5\times10^5 \) Pa and exponents stay visible and checkable.

Closing. Units are algebra, functions are machine behavior, and order-of-magnitude sense is the cheapest error detector you will ever own. Every lesson that follows leans on all three.

Lesson 02 · Limits and continuity

01 · Why limits exist in a plant

A limit answers one question: what value does a function approach as its input approaches a point? Formally, \( \lim_{x\to a} f(x) = L \) means we can force \( f(x) \) arbitrarily close to \( L \) by taking \( x \) close enough to \( a \). The definition sounds abstract; the plant meaning is concrete: what does the sensor settle toward, what does the ratio become, what happens at the operating edge?

02 · The ratio that defines everything

The most important limit in this course is a ratio of vanishing quantities,

\[ \lim_{\theta\to 0}\frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} = 1 \tag{1} \]

Try it numerically: at \( \theta = 0.1 \) rad the ratio is 0.9983; at 0.01, 0.99998. The two quantities shrink together but their ratio converges — that is the whole mental model for the derivative in the next lesson, where a vanishing rise over a vanishing run converges to a slope.

03 · Continuity — and what it means for signals

\( f \) is continuous at \( a \) when \( \lim_{x\to a} f(x) = f(a) \): no jump, no hole, no blow-up. Physical processes — temperature, level, pressure — are continuous; their measurements need not be. A thermocouple that jumps 40 °C between scans hasn't witnessed magic; it has witnessed electrical noise, a loose connection, or a step change in what is being touched. Knowing which physical variables can and cannot jump is a diagnostic tool: pressure can step (valve slam), metal temperature cannot.

One-sided limits close the lesson: a relay approaching its trip point from below and from above sees two different worlds, \( \lim_{x\to a^-} \) and \( \lim_{x\to a^+} \). Hysteresis is one-sided limits wearing work clothes.

04 · How to evaluate a limit — the decision tree

Faced with a limit, work a fixed sequence. First, substitute. If the function is continuous there, the limit is the value and you are done — most limits in practice end here. Second, if substitution returns 0/0, the form is telling you a common factor is hiding: factor polynomials, or multiply by the conjugate when square roots appear, cancel, and substitute again. Third, check the sides. When the expression involves absolute values, piecewise definitions, or a physical threshold, compute the left and right limits separately; the two-sided limit exists only when they agree. Fourth, verify numerically. Evaluate the expression at inputs marching toward the point — 0.1, 0.01, 0.001 away — and watch the trend. This is not cheating; it is how an engineer confirms an algebraic result the same way a technician confirms a calibration: with converging evidence.

Use continuity as a diagnostic tool the same disciplined way. Before blaming a sensor or believing a spike, ask which physical variables can genuinely jump (pressure, force, current) and which are integrals of finite flows and therefore cannot (metal temperature, tank level, accumulated volume). The mathematics sorts plant events from instrument events before the first work order is written.

Closing. Limits formalize approach; continuity formalizes no surprises. Tomorrow they combine into the derivative.

Lesson 03 · The derivative as a rate

01 · From average rate to instantaneous rate

Between Monday and Friday a bearing housing warmed from 62 °C to 74 °C: average rate 3 °C/day. But right now, this hour, how fast is it heating? Squeeze the interval and the average rate converges — Lesson 2's limit — to the instantaneous rate:

\[ \frac{dT}{dt} = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0}\frac{T(t+\Delta t)-T(t)}{\Delta t} \tag{1} \]

That is the derivative: the local slope of the trend, the speed of change at an instant. Units divide: °C per hour, m³ per second, mm/s of vibration per week. A derivative always has units, and always tells a maintenance story.

02 · The derivative as the trend's confession

Condition monitoring is derivative-reading as a profession. A flat vibration trend with \( dV/dt \approx 0 \) buys you time; the same amplitude with a steep positive slope buys you a work order. Two machines at identical 4 mm/s are in different worlds if one arrived there over three years and the other over three weeks. Value tells you where it is; the derivative tells you where it's going.

03 · Computing from a model

Given a fitted trend \( T(t) = 40 + 0.8t + 0.05t^2 \) (°C, hours), the power rule (proved next lesson) gives \( T'(t) = 0.8 + 0.1t \): the heating accelerates, because the rate itself grows. Set \( T'(t) \) against an alarm rate and you get a time-to-act, not just a number: solve \( 0.8+0.1t = 2.5 \) → \( t = 17\ \mathrm{h} \). The derivative converts a curve into a deadline.

04 · Computing derivatives from data — the working method

Real trends arrive as samples, not formulas, so the working derivative is a finite difference. From the two latest points you can only form the forward-looking secant \( (f_{now} - f_{prev})/\Delta t \); mid-record, prefer the central difference \( (f_{i+1} - f_{i-1})/(2\Delta t) \), which cancels the first layer of error and tracks the true tangent far better on smooth data. Three disciplines make the estimates trustworthy. State the units — a slope is always something per something, and writing mm/s per week forces the physical meaning into view. Respect noise: differentiation amplifies it, so difference over intervals long enough for the real change to dominate the scatter; a derivative computed from two noisy readings an hour apart is an opinion, not a measurement. Distinguish model from data: when a curve has been fitted, differentiate the model symbolically and use the data-side difference as its check — when the two disagree badly, the fit, the data, or the machine has changed, and every one of those is worth knowing.

Then read the result the way 2 taught: the value says where the machine is, the derivative says where it is going, and the difference between those two sentences is the difference between recording a number and making a decision.

Closing. The derivative is a limit of average rates, it carries units, and on the plant floor it is the difference between watching a number and reading its intent.

Lesson 04 · Differentiation rules and the chain rule

01 · The rules — machinery for slopes

Yesterday's limit definition is correct and slow. Today we industrialize it. For constants \(c\), functions \(u, v\):

\[ (cu)' = cu', \qquad (u+v)' = u' + v', \qquad (x^n)' = nx^{n-1} \tag{1} \] \[ (uv)' = u'v + uv', \qquad \left(\frac{u}{v}\right)' = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2} \tag{2} \]

Each is proved once from the limit and used forever. The power rule follows from expanding \( (x+\Delta x)^n \) and watching every term above first order vanish with \( \Delta x \) — the same "higher-order terms die" argument that engineering approximation lives on.

02 · The chain rule — rates through a chain of causes

Plant variables cause each other in chains: valve position sets flow, flow sets level, level sets pressure. If \( y = f(u) \) and \( u = g(x) \):

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx} \tag{3} \]

Rates multiply along the chain. This single rule powers every sensitivity argument you will make: how much does discharge pressure move per percent of speed? Differentiate through the chain of dependencies and multiply the local sensitivities.

03 · Relative rates — the engineer's favorite

Divide a derivative by its function and you get relative sensitivity. For a fan law \( P = kN^3 \), logarithmic differentiation gives \( \frac{dP}{P} = 3\frac{dN}{N} \): a 2% speed increase costs ≈6% more power ((1.02)³ = 1.061 exactly). This is why slowing a fan or pump slightly is the cheapest energy project in any plant — the cube works for you on the way down.

04 · Choosing the rule — structure before symbols

Differentiation errors rarely come from forgetting a rule; they come from misreading the structure of the expression. So classify before you differentiate. Is the expression a sum of powers? The power rule handles each term independently — after rewriting roots and reciprocals as powers, \( \sqrt{x} = x^{1/2} \), \( 1/x = x^{-1} \). Is it two varying quantities multiplied — force times velocity, pressure times flow? That is a genuine product: product rule. A ratio with the variable in the denominator? Quotient rule, with the subtraction order fixed. A function of a function — something raised to a power, an exponential of an expression? Chain rule, differentiating outer times inner, working from the outside in.

For the power laws that fill engineering — \( P = kN^3 \), \( \Delta P = kQ^2 \) — logarithmic differentiation earns its keep: take logs, differentiate, and relative rates appear directly, \( dP/P = n\,dN/N \). That one line is the professional's sensitivity language: percent out per percent in. Keep the honest distinction from 3 visible — the relative-rate figure is a first-order estimate, exact only in the limit; for large changes, compute the ratio exactly and let the linear estimate serve as its sanity check.

Finally, make numerical verification a reflex. Any symbolic derivative can be audited in ten seconds: evaluate the function at \( x \) and at \( x + 0.001 \), divide the difference by 0.001, and compare with the formula's prediction. Agreement to three figures means the algebra survived; disagreement means a dropped inner derivative or a sign error is hiding in the work. Professionals check their derivatives the way welders check their fit-up — before the error is buried under everything built on top of it.

Closing. Rules (1)–(2) mechanize differentiation; the chain rule (3) walks rates through cause-and-effect; relative rates turn calculus into percentage talk that managers act on.

Lesson 05 · Exponentials, logarithms, and growth/decay

01 · The function that is its own derivative

One function grows at a rate equal to its own size: \( \frac{d}{dt}e^{t} = e^{t} \). Scale time by a constant and \( \frac{d}{dt}e^{kt} = k e^{kt} \): growth or decay proportional to the current amount. That single property makes \( e^{kt} \) the native language of charging capacitors, cooling castings, decaying vibration transients, draining accumulators, and compounding costs.

02 · Decay with an offset — the plant's favorite curve

A hot object doesn't decay to zero; it decays to ambient. The working form is

\[ T(t) = T_\infty + (T_0 - T_\infty)\,e^{-t/\tau} \tag{1} \]

where \( \tau \) is the time constant: after one \( \tau \), 63% of the gap to ambient is closed; after 3\( \tau \), 95%; after 5\( \tau \), it's over. Ask "how many time constants?" and any first-order transient becomes readable at a glance.

03 · Logarithms — solving for time

The logarithm inverts the exponential; it is how you extract when:

\[ t = \tau \,\ln\!\frac{T_0 - T_\infty}{T(t) - T_\infty} \tag{2} \]

Logs also compress scale (decibels, pH, Richter) and straighten exponentials: plot \( \ln(T - T_\infty) \) against \( t \) and first-order data falls on a line whose slope is \( -1/\tau \). Fitting a straight line to a log plot is how you measure a time constant from plant data.

04 · The transient protocol

Every first-order transient yields to the same four questions, asked in order. What is the destination? Identify \( T_\infty \) — ambient, setpoint, asymptote — because everything is measured as a gap from it. What is the gap? Compute the current excess over the destination; the transient's whole story is this gap shrinking. What is the tempo? Get \( \tau \) — from a datasheet, from a fit, or from data via the semilog method of 3: plot \( \ln(\text{gap}) \) against time, and the slope is \( -1/\tau \). When? Turn any target into time with \( t = \tau\,\ln(\text{gap}_0/\text{gap}_{target}) \), or count in the coarse currency — one \( \tau \) closes 63% of the gap, three close 95%, five end the story.

The same machinery runs upward for growth. Compounding at rate \( r \) per period doubles in \( \ln 2/\ln(1+r) \) periods — the honest version of the rule of 70 — and any quantity that grows in proportion to itself, from an escalating tariff to a fouling deposit, is the exponential wearing different clothes. When data arrives, straighten it first: if the semilog plot is not a line, the process is not first-order, and no amount of \( \tau \)-talk will make it one. The plot is the test; the constants come after.

Know the honest failure modes too. A curve on the semilog plot usually means two time constants stacked — a fast surface effect riding on a slow bulk one — and a flat early section means deadtime: transport delay before the response begins. Both are information, not nuisance. Report what the data shows, fit the region the model genuinely describes, and say so; a stated approximation is engineering, an unstated one is luck.

Closing. \( e^{kt} \) encodes self-proportional change; \( \tau \) makes transients countable; logarithms solve for time and straighten curves so data confesses its constants.

Lesson 06 · Extrema, curve behavior, and optimization

01 · Where curves turn

A smooth function can only reverse direction where its derivative is zero. Those points — where \( f'(x) = 0 \) — are the critical points, and among them live the maxima and minima that engineering actually cares about: least material, least energy, most throughput. The second derivative classifies them: \( f'' > 0 \) cups upward (minimum), \( f'' < 0 \) caps downward (maximum).

02 · The optimization recipe

Every optimization in this course follows one recipe:

\[ \text{model} \to \text{constraint} \to \text{single-variable objective} \to f'(x)=0 \to \text{check ends} \tag{1} \]

Write the quantity to optimize; use the constraint to eliminate variables; differentiate; solve; then — the step everyone forgets — check the boundary. In a plant, optima frequently live at the constraint (valve fully open, maximum allowed speed) rather than at an interior zero of the derivative. The mathematics is honest about this only if you ask.

03 · A worked shape decision

Minimum-surface tanks, minimum-loss pipe runs, maximum-volume boxes from fixed sheet — these classic problems all reduce to recipe (1). In the Worked Examples tab we size an open-top square tank for 4 m³ with least plate area — the kind of trade a fabrication shop quotes weekly — and find the optimum has height equal to half the base side, a proportion worth memorizing.

04 · From words to optimum — the translation discipline

The calculus in recipe (1) is the easy half; the profession lives in the translation that precedes it. Practice the sequence deliberately. Name the objective — the single quantity to minimize or maximize — and write its formula with every variable it depends on. Name the constraint — the fixed volume, the required duty, the forbidden region — as an equation, and use it to eliminate variables until the objective depends on one. Differentiate, set to zero, solve — then classify with the second derivative, and walk the boundary: evaluate the objective at every end of the allowed interval, because constrained optima are the plant's normal case, not its exception. Finally, sanity-check the geometry of the answer: optima usually land on proportions — height equals half the base, cost terms equal at the minimum — and a result that looks arbitrary is a result worth re-deriving.

Two honest cautions. A mathematical optimum is only as good as the model's costs: a plate that is cheapest by area may not be cheapest by welding length, and writing the model down makes that assumption visible and challengeable. And when the objective is nearly flat near its optimum — as it often is — precision fetishism wastes effort: report the flat region, not a false fourth digit.

Real decision variables are also rarely continuous. Batches come in integers, plate comes in stock sizes, speeds come in motor steps. The calculus still earns its keep: find the continuous optimum, then evaluate the objective at the feasible values on either side of it and take the better — the curve's flatness near the optimum usually makes the rounding cost negligible, and computing it proves that instead of assuming it.

Closing. Derivative zero finds candidates; the second derivative sorts them; the boundary check keeps you honest. Optimization is calculus with a purchase order attached.

Lesson 07 · The integral as accumulation

01 · Accumulation is the other half of calculus

The derivative asks: how fast? The integral asks the inverse question: how much, in total? A flow meter reads m³/h instant by instant; the invoice wants m³. Adding up a changing rate over time is integration:

\[ V = \int_{0}^{T} Q(t)\,dt \tag{1} \]

Geometrically this is the area under the \( Q(t) \) curve — approximated by stacking thin rectangles \( Q \cdot \Delta t \) and letting \( \Delta t \to 0 \). Every totalizer in the plant is performing equation (1) in silicon, all day long.

02 · The Fundamental Theorem — rates and totals are inverses

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus says accumulation and differentiation undo each other: if \( F(t) = \int_a^t f(x)\,dx \), then \( F' = f \). Consequently

\[ \int_a^b f(t)\,dt = F(b) - F(a) \quad\text{where } F' = f \tag{2} \]

Find any antiderivative, evaluate at the ends, subtract. The theorem is why energy (kWh) is the antiderivative of power (kW), why distance is the antiderivative of speed, and why a totalizer can be checked against two readings of a cumulative register.

03 · Reading areas like an engineer

Area under a pump's power trend = energy consumed. Area under a flow curve = volume transferred. Area between inflow and outflow curves = inventory change. When the curve is data rather than formula, the integral is done numerically — trapezoids on historian samples — which is Lesson 9's business. The concept is identical: total = area.

04 · Setting up an integral — the working method

Every accumulation problem is set up the same way, and the setup is where the marks are won or lost. Identify the rate — the quantity per time (or per length, per angle) being accumulated — and write its units down. Fix the interval: the start and end of the accumulation, in the rate's own time variable. Integrate, and check the units of the result: a rate in m³/h integrated over hours must land in m³, and if it doesn't, the setup — not the arithmetic — is wrong. Then exploit the Fundamental Theorem in both directions. Given a formula, find an antiderivative and evaluate at the ends. Given a cumulative register — a totalizer, an energy meter, an odometer — the theorem says the accumulation over any window is simply the difference of two readings: engineering's most underrated free lunch.

Sketch first, always. A rough graph of the rate shows whether the answer should be near the peak-times-width rectangle or well under it, whether any region runs negative, and where the accumulation concentrates. The sketch is the estimate, the integral is the precision, and disagreement between them is the error alarm that catches transposed limits and dropped signs before they reach a report.

One caution completes the method: an integral answers exactly the question its limits ask, no more. A total over a shift says nothing about which hour did the accumulating, and a net accumulation of zero can hide furious movement in both directions. Keep the question and the interval welded together when you present the number, and totals stop being ambushed by follow-up questions.

Closing. Integrals accumulate rates into totals; the Fundamental Theorem makes them computable from antiderivatives; and the plant's totalizers, energy meters, and inventory balances are integrals with tag numbers.

Lesson 08 · Integration techniques

01 · Substitution — the chain rule reversed

Most integrals you meet are chain-rule results wearing a disguise. Substitution unmasks them: set \( u = \) the inner function, trade \( dx \) for \( du \), integrate, return.

\[ \int x\,(x^2+1)^3 dx \;\xrightarrow{\,u = x^2+1,\ du = 2x\,dx\,}\; \tfrac12\int u^3 du = \frac{(x^2+1)^4}{8} + C \tag{1} \]

The skill is seeing the derivative of the inside sitting beside it. Flow, decay, and geometry integrals in plant work overwhelmingly fall to substitution.

02 · Integration by parts — the product rule reversed

When the integrand is a genuine product — a polynomial times an exponential, say — parts applies:

\[ \int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du \tag{2} \]

Choose \( u \) to simplify when differentiated, \( dv \) to stay tame when integrated. The workhorse case \( \int t\,e^{-t}dt \) models energy in decaying transients and first-moment calculations, and the Worked Examples tab runs it end to end.

03 · Knowing when to stop

Real plant data rarely hands you a clean antiderivative. The professional sequence is: try substitution; try parts; recognize a table form; otherwise integrate numerically — trapezoids or Simpson on sampled data — and say so. A numerically honest answer beats a heroic symbolic detour every time. Software does symbolic work well; your job is to set the integral up correctly and sanity-check its output.

04 · Choosing the technique — a decision procedure

Integration is pattern recognition with a fallback ladder, and the ladder has exactly four rungs. Rung one: is the derivative of an inner function sitting beside it? If the integrand contains \( g(x) \) inside another function and \( g'(x) \) — up to a constant — as a factor, substitution finishes it in one line. Train the eye on the constant: in \( \int x\,e^{x^2}dx \), the \( x \) outside is \( \tfrac12 \) of the needed \( 2x \), and the half simply rides along. Rung two: a genuine product of unrelated factors — polynomial times exponential, polynomial times sine — goes to parts, choosing \( u \) as the factor that simplifies when differentiated. Rung three: recognize a table form and write the answer. Rung four: integrate numerically — trapezoids on the samples you have — and say so plainly.

Two habits turn technique into reliability. Verify every antiderivative by differentiating it — ten seconds that catch dropped halves and sign slips before they propagate. And when limits are involved in a substitution, transform them with the variable so the evaluation happens in \( u \)-space and nothing needs converting back. The techniques are few; the discipline around them is what makes the answers dependable.

Know also what the ladder is for. In professional work, symbolic software integrates faster than any human — but it cannot decide whether the integral was set up correctly, whether the limits mean what the question meant, or whether the answer's magnitude is plausible. The techniques you practice here build exactly that judgment: someone who has integrated by hand recognizes a wrong machine answer on sight, the way a machinist who has cut threads manually reads a bad CNC part at a glance. Learn the moves to own the judgment, then delegate the labor freely.

Closing. Substitution reverses the chain rule, parts reverses the product rule, and numerical integration is the licensed fallback — not a defeat.

Lesson 09 · Applications: areas, volumes, work, and averages

01 · Average value — what the meter should have read

The average of a continuously varying quantity is its integral spread evenly across the interval:

\[ \bar f = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(t)\,dt \tag{1} \]

Average demand, average flow, average temperature — every KPI that begins with the word "average" is equation (1), whether computed honestly or not. When someone averages three spot readings taken at convenient times, you now know precisely what they did wrong.

02 · RMS — the average that heats

Electrical heating goes as the square of current, so the meaningful "average" for AC is the root of the mean of the square:

\[ f_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T f^2(t)\,dt}, \qquad \text{for } f = A\sin\omega t:\ f_{rms} = \frac{A}{\sqrt 2} \tag{2} \]

The sine result follows because \( \sin^2 \) averages to exactly ½ over a cycle. This is why 230 V mains has a 325 V peak, and why vibration severity standards quote RMS velocity: RMS tracks energy, and energy does the damage.

03 · Work by integration

When force varies along the path, work is accumulated force-times-distance: \( W = \int F\,dx \). Pumping a tank empty is the signature case: each layer of liquid at depth \( y \) has weight \( \rho g A\,dy \) and must rise a different height. Summing layers is an integral, executed in the Worked Examples tab for a real tank. The same layer-summing pattern computes hydrostatic forces on gates and centroids of plates — one method, many billables.

04 · Choosing the right average — a field guide

Three different averages answer three different questions, and choosing the wrong one is a quiet, expensive error. The plain mean — equation (1) — answers "how much per unit time on the invoice": billing, throughput, duty totals. The RMS — equation (2) — answers "what steady level would heat, stress, or wear the same": electrical heating, vibration severity, fatigue-relevant loading, because damage physics runs on squares. The peak answers "will it survive the worst instant": trips, relief sizing, insulation. A signal has all three numbers at once, and the crest factor — peak over RMS — is the single figure that says how far apart they live: near 1.4 for a clean sine, climbing whenever impacts and spikes enter, which is why analysts watch it as an early bearing-damage flag.

On sampled data the integrals become sums without changing meaning: mean the samples for equation (1), mean the squares and take the root for equation (2), weighting by interval when the sampling is uneven. For work-by-layers problems, the recipe is mechanical: draw the layer, write its weight, write its travel distance as a function of position, integrate over the geometry — then check against the centroid shortcut, which agrees every time the geometry is uniform and catches the setup when it isn't.

The same layer-summing pattern prices hydrostatic force on a gate, the metal in a tapered column, and the moment of a distributed load — one method, many invoices. What changes between problems is only what each layer carries: weight, pressure, or load per metre. Master the pattern once, in the tank problem, and every later course that says "integrate over the geometry" is asking for a rerun.

Closing. Integrals define honest averages (1), energy-true averages (2), and path-dependent work — three tools that turn "roughly" into "defensibly."

Lesson 10 · First-order differential equations

01 · Equations that contain their own rates

A differential equation relates a quantity to its own rate of change. The plant's archetype: a hot mass losing heat in proportion to its excess over ambient,

\[ \frac{dT}{dt} = -\frac{T - T_\infty}{\tau} \tag{1} \]

Read it aloud: the hotter it is above ambient, the faster it cools. Most first-order plant dynamics — thermal soak, tank draining, filter loading, RC circuits, instrument response — are equation (1) with different nouns.

02 · Solving by separation

Separable equations split variables to opposite sides and integrate:

\[ \int\frac{dT}{T-T_\infty} = -\int\frac{dt}{\tau} \;\Rightarrow\; \ln|T-T_\infty| = -\frac{t}{\tau}+C \;\Rightarrow\; T = T_\infty + (T_0-T_\infty)e^{-t/\tau} \tag{2} \]

Lesson 5's cooling curve wasn't handed down; it is derived here, in three lines, from a physical statement. The constant of integration is fixed by the initial condition \( T(0) = T_0 \) — every first-order ODE needs one known point to pin its trajectory.

03 · Reading dynamics without solving

Much of an ODE's story is available without solution. Set the rate to zero and you find equilibria (here \( T = T_\infty \)). The sign of the rate tells direction of travel; its size sets speed. The coefficient \( 1/\tau \) is the speed of the whole approach — measure it from data and you know the system's tempo. Engineers read ODEs more often than they solve them; the solving builds the reading instinct.

04 · From balance statement to equation — the modeling method

First-order models are not found; they are written, and the writing follows one template. State the balance: rate of change of the stored quantity equals rate in minus rate out. Express each rate in the state variable: outflow proportional to level, heat loss proportional to excess temperature, contaminant leaving at concentration times flow. Divide through until the equation reads \( \dot{y} = -(y - y_\infty)/\tau \), and read \( \tau \) directly from the coefficients — \( V/Q \) for a flushed tank, thermal mass over loss coefficient for a cooling body. The physics enters in the rates; the mathematics is the same three lines every time.

Respect the sign conventions and the model's edges. A negative rate coefficient decays toward equilibrium; a positive one runs away, and recognizing which you have written is a stability judgment made before any solving. Step inputs — a heater switching on, a setpoint moving — reuse the same solution with the roles renamed: the response climbs \( 1 - e^{-t/\tau} \) toward its new destination. And when the data refuses the model — curved semilog plots, delayed starts — the honest conclusion is a second time constant or a deadtime, not a forced fit. Write what the balance supports; report what the data shows.

This template is the most reused page in the course. The same three lines will reappear as the RC circuit in the electrical course, the thermal soak in heat transfer, the flushed volume in fluid mechanics, and the instrument lag in controls — identical mathematics under different nouns. Learn to see the balance statement through the surface vocabulary and every one of those courses starts a lesson ahead.

Closing. First-order ODEs encode self-referential change; separation solves the classics; initial conditions select the one real trajectory among the many possible.

Lesson 11 · Taylor series and approximation

01 · Every smooth function is locally a polynomial

Zoom into any smooth curve and it straightens; zoom with more care and it bends like a parabola. Taylor's theorem makes this precise: near \( x = a \),

\[ f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + \frac{f''(a)}{2}(x-a)^2 + \cdots \tag{1} \]

The first two terms are the tangent line — linearization — and the third begins to track curvature. Engineering lives on truncations of (1): every "small-angle," "small-signal," and "about the operating point" argument you will ever meet is a Taylor series with the tail cut off.

02 · The classic small approximations

\[ \sin\theta \approx \theta - \frac{\theta^3}{6}, \qquad \cos\theta \approx 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2}, \qquad e^x \approx 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2} \tag{2} \]

At \( \theta = 10° = 0.1745 \) rad, \( \sin\theta = 0.17365 \): the one-term approximation \( \theta \) errs by 0.5%, and adding the cubic term shrinks the error to 0.0004%. This is why pendulum periods, beam deflections, and control models replace \( \sin\theta \) with \( \theta \) below ~15° and nobody blushes.

03 · Knowing the size of the tail

An approximation without an error bound is a wish. The first neglected term estimates the error: truncating after \( \theta \) leaves \( \sim\theta^3/6 \). Professional habit: state the operating range, evaluate the first dropped term at the range's edge, and write both down. That habit is what separates "we linearized about 50% flow" from hand-waving — and it is exactly how instrument nonlinearity specs and control-model validity limits are written.

04 · Building a linearization you can defend

Linearization is a contract with three clauses, written in this order. Choose the operating point deliberately — the flow, load, or angle the system actually lives at — because the model is exact there and degrades with distance. Compute the two numbers: the function's value and its derivative at that point; the tangent model is just \( f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) \), a straight line wearing the local slope. Price the error before anyone asks: evaluate the first dropped term, \( \tfrac12 f''(a)(x-a)^2 \), at the edge of the range you intend to use, and write both the range and the error into the work. A linearization delivered with its validity range and worst-case error is engineering; delivered bare, it is a hope with a slope.

The quadratic error growth is the practical lever: halve the excursion and the error quarters, which is why tight-range instruments tolerate linear scaling that wide-range ones cannot. Run the contract in reverse to audit existing systems — find where a legacy scaling block or control model was linearized, evaluate the dropped term at today's operating range, and you have a defensible number for how much the old approximation now costs. The habit generalizes: every model is an approximation; professionals differ only in whether the error is priced.

Carry that sentence out of the course. Instrument linearity specifications, control-model validity ranges, and datasheet accuracy classes are all Taylor-series contracts written by someone else — read them by asking where the expansion point sits and how far the promised range strays from it. The engineer who can price an approximation can also audit one.

Closing. Taylor series turn smooth functions into local polynomials; linearization is its two-term truncation; the first dropped term prices the honesty of the approximation. This lesson closes the course by naming the approximation culture the whole curriculum will use.

PHY 107 — Engineering Physics I — Mechanics

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Measurement, units, and vectors

01 · Measurement is a contract

Physics begins where opinion ends: at measurement. A physical quantity is a number, a unit, and an uncertainty, and all three are part of the value. The SI system supplies seven base units, of which mechanics uses three — the metre, the kilogram, and the second — and builds everything else from them: newtons are \( \mathrm{kg\,m/s^2} \), joules are newton-metres, watts are joules per second. Because derived units are built by algebra, they can be CHECKED by algebra — both sides of any physical equation must carry identical units, and an equation that fails this test is wrong before any numbers enter. Significant figures carry the honesty further: a value written as 4.80 m promises more than 4.8 m does, and the professional habit is to promise no more than the instrument delivered.

02 · Vectors carry direction

Some quantities are complete as bare numbers — temperature, mass, energy. Mechanics' central quantities are not: force, velocity, acceleration, and displacement all point, and a number without its direction is half a fact. A vector is specified either by magnitude and angle or by components on chosen axes, and the two descriptions convert through the triangle: \( A_x = A\cos\theta \), \( A_y = A\sin\theta \), with \( A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2} \) returning the magnitude. Addition happens component by component — never magnitude by magnitude — which is the single most consequential sentence in this lesson: two 100 N forces can sum to anything from 0 to 200 N depending on their directions.

03 · Two products, two meanings

Vectors multiply in two distinct ways, each answering a physical question. The dot product, \( \mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B} = AB\cos\theta = A_xB_x + A_yB_y + A_zB_z \), measures alignment and produces a scalar — work is force dotted with displacement, and a force at right angles to motion does none. The cross product, \( |\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B}| = AB\sin\theta \), measures turning capacity and produces a vector along the axis of turn — torque is position crossed with force, the right-hand rule assigning its sense. Choosing the wrong product is not a small error; it is answering a different question.

04 · The component working method

Every vector calculation in this course runs one procedure. Choose axes deliberately and draw them. Resolve every vector into components with a small sign table, committing each sine and cosine once, visibly. Do all arithmetic on components. Recompose at the end — magnitude from Pythagoras, direction from the arctangent WITH a quadrant check from the component signs, because the calculator's arctangent is wrong half the time on its own. Then run the two audits: units on both sides of every line, and magnitude inside the possible band (never more than the sum of the parts, never less than their difference). The method is mechanical on purpose — statics, dynamics, and every field theory after them will run it thousands of times, and a procedure executed identically is a procedure whose errors are findable. Speed comes later and automatically; correctness is the habit being installed here.

Lesson 02 · Kinematics in one dimension

01 · The three siblings: position, velocity, acceleration

Kinematics describes motion without asking why it happens, and its entire vocabulary is three quantities chained by calculus. Position \( x(t) \) says where; velocity \( v = dx/dt \) says how fast where is changing; acceleration \( a = dv/dt \) says how fast how-fast is changing. The chain runs both directions: differentiate to go from position toward acceleration, integrate to come back — and the mathematics course's slogan that "slopes and areas are inverses" becomes physical here. On a velocity–time graph, the slope at any instant is the acceleration and the area under the curve is the displacement; an engineer who can read those two facts off a trend owns most of kinematics.

02 · The constant-acceleration toolkit

When acceleration is constant — winch starts, braking runs, free fall — integration yields the standard kit: \( v = v_0 + at \), \( x = v_0t + \tfrac12 at^2 \), and the time-free combination \( v^2 = v_0^2 + 2ax \). Derive them once from the definitions and they stop being formulas to memorize and become consequences to trust. Each answers its own question — the first about speed at a time, the second about distance in a time, the third about speed after a distance — and choosing the one whose variables match the givens is the entire selection skill. Free fall is the same kit with \( a = g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) downward, air resistance ignored until a later course chooses to price it.

03 · Signs carry the physics

One axis, declared positive in one direction, governs every sign in the problem. Velocity opposite the axis is negative; acceleration opposite the VELOCITY is braking, but acceleration opposite the AXIS is just negative — the two statements differ, and conflating them is the classic kinematics error. A car moving in +x while slowing has negative acceleration; the same car reversing and speeding up ALSO has negative acceleration. The sign belongs to the axis, not to the feeling of speeding up or slowing down.

04 · The kinematics working method

Solve every problem the same way. Draw the axis and mark positive. List the knowns and the wanted symbolically — \( v_0, v, a, t, x \) — and strike what the problem doesn't mention. Pick the ONE equation containing exactly the knowns and the wanted; if none fits, the problem has two phases, so split it where the acceleration changes and chain the phases, the final state of one becoming the initial state of the next. Solve symbolically first, substitute numbers last, and audit twice: units through every line, and magnitude against experience — a forklift does not brake at \( 40\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), and an answer claiming so is a decimal slip announcing itself. The two-phase discipline matters most: most real motions — lift, travel, lower; accelerate, cruise, brake — are chains of simple segments, and the method scales to any chain length without changing shape.

Lesson 03 · Kinematics in two dimensions

01 · Two motions sharing one clock

The deepest fact of two-dimensional motion is independence: the horizontal and vertical motions of a projectile do not consult each other. Gravity acts only vertically, so the horizontal velocity is untouched — constant from launch to landing — while the vertical motion is ordinary free fall. The only thing the two share is the clock. Every projectile problem therefore splits into two one-dimensional problems solved with Lesson 2's toolkit, joined by the time variable: usually the vertical motion sets the time (how long until it lands?) and the horizontal motion spends it (how far did it get?).

02 · The projectile's arithmetic

Launched horizontally from height \( h \) with speed \( v_0 \): the fall time is \( t = \sqrt{2h/g} \) — identical to a dropped object, regardless of \( v_0 \) — and the reach is \( x = v_0 t \). Launched at angle \( \theta \): the initial velocity splits into \( v_0\cos\theta \) horizontal and \( v_0\sin\theta \) vertical, the apex arrives when the vertical velocity runs out, and the level-ground range is \( R = v_0^2 \sin 2\theta / g \), maximized at 45°. At the apex the vertical velocity is zero but the horizontal velocity is untouched — the projectile is still moving, a fact the classic "zero velocity at the top" error forgets.

03 · Relative motion: velocities add like vectors

Velocity is always measured relative to a frame, and frames stack by vector addition: the velocity of A relative to ground equals A relative to B plus B relative to ground. A worker walking at 1.0 m/s against a conveyor doing 1.5 m/s moves at 0.5 m/s over the floor; a part carried on a moving belt leaves the belt end with the belt's velocity as its inheritance. In one dimension this is signed addition; in two, components — nothing new, just Lesson 1's machinery applied to velocities.

04 · The two-column working method

Solve every 2-D motion with two columns and one clock. Write the horizontal column: constant velocity, \( x = v_x t \), nothing else. Write the vertical column: free fall with the full constant-acceleration kit. Identify which column the question interrogates and which column supplies the time, and pass \( t \) between them explicitly. Keep signs by one declared axis pair (right and up positive is conventional), and audit at the seams: the landing condition is a VERTICAL statement (height reaches zero), the reach is a HORIZONTAL one, and mixing the columns — using the launch speed where a component belongs — is the error the layout exists to prevent. The same two-column habit will return, upgraded, as normal-and-tangential components in the dynamics course; learning its discipline now on projectiles is the cheap rehearsal.

The applications are nearer than they look: discharge trajectories off conveyor heads set guard and bin positions, falling-object times set interlock reaction budgets, and every "how far can it reach" safety question — spray, jet, or dropped tool — is a projectile problem wearing overalls. The two columns answer all of them.

Lesson 04 · Newton's laws of motion

01 · The three laws, stated for work

Newton's laws are the constitution of mechanics, and each earns a working translation. First law: a body's velocity is constant unless a net force acts — so anything at rest OR cruising steadily has balanced forces, and the interesting engineering question is always "balanced by what?" Second law: \( \mathbf{F}_{net} = m\mathbf{a} \) — the net force, the vector sum of everything acting, produces acceleration in its own direction, scaled inversely by the mass. Third law: forces come in pairs, equal and opposite, acting on DIFFERENT bodies — the floor pushes the crate up exactly as hard as the crate pushes the floor down, and the pair never meets on one free-body diagram. The third law is bookkeeping; the second is the engine; the first is the second at \( \mathbf{a} = 0 \), which is the entire statics course in four symbols.

02 · Mass versus weight, permanently

Mass measures inertia — the reluctance to accelerate — in kilograms, identical everywhere in the universe. Weight is the gravitational FORCE on that mass, \( W = mg \), in newtons, local to the gravity that produces it. The distinction stops being pedantry the moment anything accelerates vertically: in a lift accelerating upward, the floor must push harder than \( mg \) — the scale reads more — and in a dropping lift it reads less, while the mass never changes. Load cells, crane scales, and accelerating machinery all live on this distinction.

03 · Using the second law on systems

\( F = ma \) applies to any body you choose to isolate — a whole train of carts or any single cart within it. The professional trick is choosing wisely: the whole system hides internal forces and yields the acceleration cheaply; a single member exposes the internal force you actually want. Solve the system for \( a \), then isolate one piece and let its own second law reveal the coupling force. Connected bodies share the acceleration magnitude whenever their connection is inextensible — the rope constraint that makes multi-body problems solvable at all.

04 · The second-law working method

The procedure is Lesson 3's FBD checklist with one upgrade: the right side of the equation is no longer zero. Draw the free body. Choose axes — align one with the acceleration when its direction is known, and the algebra thanks you. Write \( \Sigma F_x = ma_x \) and \( \Sigma F_y = ma_y \) with the sign table. Solve symbolically, substitute, and audit: units (newtons throughout), limits (set \( a = 0 \) and the answer must collapse to the statics result), and magnitude sense (accelerations of industrial machinery are typically fractions of \( g \); an answer of \( 40\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \) for a forklift is a flag, not a finding). When several bodies connect, write one second law per body plus the constraint, and count equations against unknowns before solving — exactly the bookkeeping the statics course taught, now with motion.

Lesson 05 · Applying Newton's laws

01 · Friction joins the diagram

Newton's laws meet real surfaces through friction, and the rules carry over from statics with one upgrade: motion. Static friction adapts — it supplies exactly what equilibrium demands, up to the ceiling \( \mu_s N \) — while kinetic friction, once sliding begins, holds the steady lower value \( \mu_k N \). The normal force is never assumed: on an incline it is \( mg\cos\theta \), under a downward push it grows, under an angled pull it shrinks, and every friction calculation begins by solving for \( N \) from the perpendicular equation. The incline is the standard stage: gravity resolves into \( mg\sin\theta \) along the slope and \( mg\cos\theta \) into it, and the contest between \( mg\sin\theta \) and \( \mu_s mg\cos\theta \) — that is, between \( \tan\theta \) and \( \mu_s \) — decides whether anything moves at all.

02 · Ropes, pulleys, and shared fates

An ideal rope is inextensible and massless: it transmits one tension throughout its length, and it forces connected bodies to share one acceleration magnitude. Those two facts turn multi-body problems into systems of simple equations — one second law per body, plus the constraint. An ideal pulley redirects the rope without changing its tension; the hanging-mass-drags-sliding-mass arrangement is the archetype, and its logic reappears in every counterweight, hoist, and tensioning system built since.

03 · Friction as the transmitter of motion

Friction does not only resist motion — it CAUSES most of it. A box accelerates with its conveyor belt only because static friction drags it along; the maximum acceleration friction can transmit is \( a_{max} = \mu_s g \), and a belt that accelerates harder leaves its cargo sliding behind. The same ceiling governs tires on tarmac and shoes on floors: traction is static friction employed, and the μ-times-g budget is the hard limit on every start and every stop that relies on it.

04 · The applying-Newton working method

The procedure composes everything so far. Draw the free body; solve the perpendicular axis first to capture \( N \); decide the friction regime honestly — compute the force equilibrium would require, compare with \( \mu_s N \), and only then choose static (adaptive, at the required value) or kinetic (fixed, at \( \mu_k N \), opposing the sliding). Align one axis with the acceleration, write the second law per axis, add the rope constraint when bodies connect, and count equations against unknowns before solving. Audit with limits: set \( \mu = 0 \) and the answer must collapse to the frictionless case; set \( a = 0 \) and it must collapse to statics. The regime decision is where the marks — and the real machines — are won or lost: assuming sliding when the part actually grips, or grip when it actually slides, produces answers that are precisely and confidently wrong. When in doubt, solve both regimes and let the comparison with the ceiling name the truth — two short calculations beat one plausible guess every time.

Lesson 06 · Work and energy

01 · Work: force through distance, honestly counted

Work is energy in transit by force: \( W = Fd\cos\theta \) — only the component of force ALONG the motion counts, and the dot product of Lesson 1 was built for exactly this accounting. Force perpendicular to motion does nothing (the normal force on a rolling cart, the centripetal pull of a curve); force opposing motion does negative work, withdrawing energy (friction, always); and a force with no motion under it does no work at all, however hard it strains — the crane holding a load stationary spends money, not joules. The unit is the joule, one newton-metre, and keeping work's sign honest is keeping the energy books balanced.

02 · Kinetic energy and the theorem that shortcuts dynamics

A moving mass carries kinetic energy \( KE = \tfrac12 mv^2 \), and the work–energy theorem states the exchange rate: the NET work done on a body equals its change in kinetic energy, \( W_{net} = \Delta KE \). The theorem is a derived consequence of \( F = ma \), but as a tool it routinely beats its parent: it needs no timeline, no acceleration bookkeeping — just states, before and after. The \( v^2 \) matters more than any other exponent in safety engineering: doubling speed quadruples the energy to be absorbed, which is why impact severity, braking distances, and guard designs all scale on the square of speed.

03 · Power: the rate that sizes machines

Machines are bought by the rate they can move energy: power, \( P = W/t \), in watts — joules per second. For a force moving at speed, \( P = Fv \); for a hoist lifting steadily, \( P = mgv \); and the same energy delivered faster demands proportionally more machine. The kilowatt-hour on the invoice is a unit of ENERGY (a kilowatt sustained for an hour, 3.6 MJ), and keeping power and energy distinct — the rate versus the total — is the first literacy test of every utilities conversation.

04 · The energy-accounting working method

Solve energy problems as an audit, not a story. Choose the system and the interval — initial state, final state. List every force that acts, and classify: does it do positive work, negative work, or none (perpendicular or unmoving)? Write the theorem, \( W_{net} = \Delta KE \), with each work term signed, and solve for the unknown. Audit by units (joules throughout, watts only when time enters) and by sign sense: a body that sped up must have received positive net work; one that slowed must have paid energy out somewhere the ledger can point to. The method's power is its indifference to detail — it never asks HOW the velocity varied in between — and its discipline is completeness: a force forgotten is energy silently created or destroyed, which nature forbids and examiners notice. Run the classification aloud on every new problem — works, opposes, ignores — until the sorting is reflex; the next lesson adds stored energy to the same ledger, and the audit habit carries over unchanged.

Lesson 07 · Potential energy and conservation

01 · Stored work: potential energy

Some forces give back what work puts in. Lift a mass and gravity stores the effort: potential energy \( PE = mgh \), recoverable in full on the way down. Compress or stretch a spring and it banks \( PE = \tfrac12 kx^2 \). Forces with this repayment property — gravity, springs — are conservative: the work they do depends only on endpoints, not on the path, and that is what makes a stored-energy bookkeeping possible at all. The reference height for \( mgh \) is yours to choose; only DIFFERENCES in potential energy ever enter the physics, so the floor, the bench, or sea level serve equally, provided the choice is made once and kept.

02 · Conservation: the ledger balances

When only conservative forces do work, mechanical energy is conserved: \( KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2 \). Height converts to speed and back without loss — the falling pallet, the swinging hammer, the cam follower riding its profile. The equation answers speed-from-height questions in one line: \( v = \sqrt{2gh} \) for a drop from rest, with mass canceling entirely — a heavy tool and a light one arrive at the same speed, though not, as the momentum lesson will insist, with the same consequences.

03 · When friction joins: the ledger still balances

Friction does not break energy accounting; it opens a new account. With friction acting, \( KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2 + E_{lost} \), where the loss term — friction force times sliding distance — leaves the mechanical books and arrives as heat. The equation turns observed shortfalls into measurements: a crate that arrives slower than the frictionless prediction has told you exactly how much energy friction collected en route. Nothing disappears; it changes accounts, and the engineer's job is reading which account.

04 · The conservation working method

Choose two states — where the question starts and where it asks. Set the height reference and declare it. Write the full ledger for each state: kinetic, gravitational, spring if present. Ask the gatekeeper question — does anything non-conservative do work between the states? If no, equate the ledgers; if yes, add the loss term and route it honestly. Solve, then audit: mass should cancel in pure gravity problems (if it doesn't, a term is misplaced); speeds must come from energy DIFFERENCES, never absolute heights; and the frictionless answer should always bound the frictional one. The method's reach is its reward: trajectory shape, path curvature, and time all drop out of the accounting, which is why energy methods solve in one line what force methods solve in a page — whenever the question asks about states rather than histories.

The ledger habit is also a diagnostic instrument in the field: measure the arrival speed a machine element actually achieves, compare with the frictionless prediction, and the shortfall prices the losses without a single coefficient measured. Energy accounting reads machines the way the trend-reading of the mathematics course read signals — by what the books say is missing.

Lesson 08 · Momentum, impulse, and collisions

01 · Momentum: motion's currency

Momentum, \( \mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v} \), is the quantity of motion — a vector, in kilogram-metres per second. Newton's second law in its original and most general form says force is the RATE of change of momentum: \( \mathbf{F} = d\mathbf{p}/dt \). Integrated over a contact, this becomes the impulse–momentum theorem: \( \mathbf{F}\Delta t = \Delta\mathbf{p} \) — the product of force and its duration equals the momentum change it produces. The product is the point: the same momentum change can be bought with a large force briefly or a small force at leisure, and nearly all impact engineering — packaging, bumpers, crash padding, soft-landing programs — is the deliberate purchase of more \( \Delta t \) to shrink \( F \).

02 · Conservation: the system keeps its total

When no net EXTERNAL force acts on a system, its total momentum cannot change — the internal pushes and pulls cancel in third-law pairs. Collisions, couplings, and separations all obey it: \( m_1v_1 + m_2v_2 \) before equals the same sum after, signs carried by direction. The law holds regardless of how violent, brief, or lossy the interaction is — which is what makes it the collision analyst's first equation, written before anything is known about the forces themselves.

03 · Collisions: what momentum keeps, energy may lose

Momentum is conserved in every collision; kinetic energy is not. A perfectly INELASTIC collision — the bodies couple and move together — conserves momentum while converting the maximum possible kinetic energy to deformation and heat. An ELASTIC collision conserves both, an idealization that hard steel balls and billiards approach. Real impacts live between the limits. The two conservation questions are therefore always asked separately, and the energy shortfall in a sticking collision is not an error — it is the crumple, the dent, and the noise, priced exactly.

04 · The impulse–momentum working method

Define the system so the interesting forces become internal — both carts, hammer plus pile — and check that external forces are absent, negligible, or perpendicular during the brief event. Declare the positive direction and carry every velocity's sign. Write momentum conservation across the event; solve; then, if energies matter, audit the kinetic-energy books separately and route any loss honestly to deformation and heat. For contact-force questions, use the theorem in its \( F = \Delta p/\Delta t \) form and respect its sensitivity: halving the stopping time doubles the average force, which is why hard surfaces break what padded ones spare. The method's discipline is the system boundary — drawn well, the messy contact forces never need computing at all; drawn badly, nothing conserves and everything confuses.

Momentum and energy together complete the collision toolkit: momentum answers the velocities, energy names the damage, and the pair of audits — run separately, never merged — resolves every impact question this course will pose. The dynamics course will add rotation to both ledgers; the bookkeeping discipline installed here is the part that does not change.

Lesson 09 · Rotation I: kinematics and torque

01 · The angular vocabulary

Rotation replays kinematics with angular characters. Angular position \( \theta \) is measured in radians — the natural unit, because it makes arc length \( s = \theta r \) with no conversion factor. Angular velocity \( \omega = d\theta/dt \) in rad/s, and angular acceleration \( \alpha = d\omega/dt \) in rad/s², chain exactly as their linear siblings did, and the constant-\( \alpha \) toolkit is Lesson 2's kit with letters swapped: \( \omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t \), \( \theta = \omega_0 t + \tfrac12\alpha t^2 \), \( \omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\theta \). The machine floor speaks rpm; physics speaks rad/s; the exchange rate \( \omega = 2\pi N/60 \) is crossed so often it should be reflex — 1450 rpm, the standard 4-pole motor speed, is 152 rad/s.

02 · The rolling connection

Rotation meets translation at the rim: a point at radius \( r \) moves at \( v = \omega r \) and, under angular acceleration, gains speed at \( a_t = \alpha r \). The relation runs every belt, chain, and traction drive: pulleys sharing a belt share rim speed, so their angular velocities trade inversely with radius — the speed-ratio law of every pulley pair and gear mesh. Radians earn their keep here: the formulas hold ONLY with angles in radians, and feeding degrees or rpm into \( v = \omega r \) is the classic rotational unit error.

03 · Torque: force's rotational agent

What force is to translation, torque is to rotation: \( \tau = Fr\sin\theta \), the force times its perpendicular lever arm, in newton-metres. The statics course built moments for equilibrium; dynamics now lets them cause angular acceleration. On a belt drive, the torque delivered is the TENSION DIFFERENCE times radius — \( (T_1 - T_2)r \) — because both belt runs pull, in opposition; on a bolted coupling, torque is what the wrench and the torque spec are negotiating. The moment of inertia \( I \), rotation's mass, waits one lesson ahead as torque's partner.

04 · The rotational working method

Run rotation problems with a currency-exchange discipline. Convert everything to SI angular units at the border — rpm to rad/s, revolutions to radians — work entirely inside, and convert back only for the final answer if the floor wants rpm. Choose the angular toolkit by the same known-versus-wanted matching as Lesson 2, and pass between the angular and linear worlds only through the radius: \( v = \omega r \), \( a_t = \alpha r \), \( s = \theta r \). Audit with the two standard checks — units (a radian is dimensionless, so \( \omega r \) lands in m/s cleanly) and magnitude sense (industrial machines mostly live between 0.5 and 300 rad/s; an answer of 15,000 rad/s is a units error announcing itself). The kinematics is deliberately familiar; fluency in the conversion discipline is what this lesson actually installs, because every machine after it — pumps, gearboxes, turbines — speaks both languages at once.

Lesson 10 · Rotation II: dynamics and angular momentum

01 · Moment of inertia: rotation's mass

Newton's second law rotates into \( \tau = I\alpha \), and the new character \( I = \int r^2\,dm \) — moment of inertia — is mass weighted by the SQUARE of its distance from the axis. The square is everything: the same kilograms placed twice as far resist spin-up four times harder. A solid disc carries \( I = \tfrac12 mr^2 \); a thin hoop, with all mass at the rim, carries \( I = mr^2 \) — double, at equal mass and radius. Distribution, not just quantity, decides rotational reluctance, which is why the statics course's area moment (resisting bending) and this mass moment (resisting angular acceleration) share a name and a square: same mathematics, different physics.

02 · Rotational energy and the flywheel

A spinning body banks kinetic energy \( KE = \tfrac12 I\omega^2 \), and the \( \omega^2 \) makes speed the cheap lever: doubling spin quadruples storage. That is the flywheel's entire business plan — presses and punches draw their violent, brief demands from a wheel kept spinning by a modest motor, smoothing the load the way the machinery course will later formalize. A body that rolls carries both accounts at once, translational \( \tfrac12 mv^2 \) plus rotational \( \tfrac12 I\omega^2 \), which is why rolling objects arrive at ramp bottoms slower than sliding ones: part of the height budget went into spin.

03 · Angular momentum and its conservation

Momentum's rotational sibling is \( L = I\omega \), conserved whenever no external torque acts. Because \( I \) depends on mass placement, a system that redistributes its own mass trades \( I \) against \( \omega \) at constant product — the figure skater's spin-up, and equally the reason a machine that throws a mass outward slows its own rotation. Torque is the rate of change of angular momentum, \( \tau = dL/dt \), completing the rotational translation dictionary: every linear law has now crossed over.

04 · The rotational dynamics working method

Solve rotating problems by the same audit sequence as linear ones, with the dictionary open. Identify the axis and get \( I \) about THAT axis — from the standard shapes, or by the parallel-axis transfer when the spin axis is offset. Sum torques about the axis, signs by a declared positive sense, and apply \( \tau_{net} = I\alpha \). Chain to angular kinematics for speeds and angles, and to \( \tfrac12 I\omega^2 \) when the question is energetic. Audit with limits — at \( \alpha = 0 \) the torque balance must collapse to statics — and with the distribution instinct: between two candidates of equal mass, the one with mass farther out always spins up slower, coasts longer, and stores more at speed. The machines this feeds are immediate: every motor start is \( \tau = I\alpha \) against the driven train's inertia, and every coast-down time is the same equation read backward — which is exactly how the condition-monitoring course will one day read it.

Lesson 11 · Oscillations first contact

01 · The restoring recipe

Oscillation needs exactly two ingredients: a restoring force that pulls toward equilibrium in proportion to displacement, and inertia that overshoots it. The spring–mass system is the archetype — \( F = -kx \) feeding \( m\ddot{x} \) — and its motion is simple harmonic: \( x(t) = A\sin(\omega_n t) \), a sine forever, with amplitude \( A \) set by how the motion started and angular frequency set by the system itself: \( \omega_n = \sqrt{k/m} \). Stiffness accelerates the cycle; mass slows it; amplitude, remarkably, does not enter — small or large swings keep the same beat, which is what makes natural frequency a property of the MACHINE, not of the disturbance.

02 · The pendulum and the family of oscillators

The pendulum repeats the recipe with gravity as the spring: \( T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g} \) for small swings — mass absent, length and gravity only. Every oscillator in the family carries the same signature quantities: period \( T = 2\pi/\omega_n \), frequency \( f = 1/T \) in hertz, and the energy sloshing between kinetic (maximum at equilibrium, where speed peaks at \( v_{max} = A\omega_n \)) and potential (maximum at the extremes, where motion pauses). Reading \( x(t) = A\sin(\omega t) \) fluently — amplitude from the coefficient, frequency from the argument — is reading the native language of every vibration trace the later courses will put on screen.

03 · Why machines care: resonance previewed

Every machine element has natural frequencies, and rotating machinery supplies periodic forcing at running speed and its multiples. When forcing frequency approaches natural frequency, amplitude grows dramatically — resonance — and structures that seemed rigid begin to breathe. This lesson only introduces the actors; the vibrations course will quantify their meeting. But the design instinct starts now: know your \( \omega_n \), know your running speeds, and keep them apart.

04 · The oscillation working method

Identify the stiffness and the inertia — every oscillator has both, whatever it looks like — and form \( \omega_n = \sqrt{k/m} \) (or the pendulum's \( \sqrt{g/L} \)). Convert consciously among \( \omega_n \), \( f \), and \( T \): the \( 2\pi \) that separates rad/s from Hz is this chapter's border crossing, as rpm's was the last one's. Extract amplitude and frequency from any given \( x(t) \) by inspection, and derive the speed and acceleration peaks as \( A\omega \) and \( A\omega^2 \). Audit with the scaling instincts: quadrupling stiffness only doubles frequency (the square root taxes enthusiasm), adding mass always slows the beat, and amplitude never changes the clock. Where a real trace disagrees with these instincts — frequency shifting with load, beat patterns, growth over time — the disagreement is information, and the vibrations course will teach you to read it. Here, the assignment is fluency with the clean case that all the diagnostics measure against — the sine every analyzer decomposes real signals into, and the natural frequency every balancing report quotes. First courses end where careers begin; this one ends on the exact frequency.

DRW 102 — Engineering Drawing & CAD

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Why drawings rule the plant

01 · The drawing is the contract

A drawing is not a picture of a part; it is a contract about a part. It binds a designer's intent, a fabricator's obligation, an inspector's acceptance criteria, and — years later — a maintenance engineer's only honest witness to what was actually built. When the drawing and the machine disagree, work stops until somebody finds out which one is lying.

02 · Revision control — the drawing's chain of custody

Every drawing carries a title block (who, what, when, scale, projection symbol, drawing number) and a revision table (what changed, when, approved by whom). The revision letter is the single most important character on the sheet: torque a coupling per Rev B when the shop built Rev D and the bolts may be the wrong length. The discipline is absolute: work only from the revision the document register calls current, and treat uncontrolled prints — the ones taped inside panel doors — as rumors.

03 · As-built, as-designed, as-found

Plants drift away from their drawings. Nozzles get relocated, supports added, impellers trimmed. An as-built drawing claims to capture reality after construction; in practice the claim decays from the day it's stamped. The maintenance engineer's habit: verify the two or three dimensions that matter against the metal before cutting steel, and red-line the drawing when reality wins. A marked-up print returned to document control is a gift to the next engineer; a silent discrepancy is a trap armed for them.

Closing. Drawings rule because they are the only durable, arguable record of intent. Respect the revision letter, distrust uncontrolled prints, and feed corrections back — the drawing system is a living asset exactly as much as the machines it describes.

Lesson 02 · Projection theory and orthographic views

01 · Projection — flattening three dimensions honestly

Orthographic projection maps a 3-D object onto 2-D planes with parallel rays, preserving true lengths and angles in each view. The object is imagined inside a glass box; each face of the box receives one view. Unfold the box and the views land in fixed positions relative to each other — that fixed arrangement is what lets a trained reader reconstruct the solid.

02 · First angle versus third angle

Two unfolding conventions rule the world. In first angle (ISO default, most of Europe and Asia), the object sits between observer and plane: the top view lands below the front view. In third angle (ASME, North America), the plane sits between observer and object: the top view lands above. The truncated-cone symbol in the title block declares which convention the sheet uses — read it first, every sheet, because a first-angle drawing read with third-angle eyes puts every feature on the wrong side of the part.

03 · Choosing and reading views

The front view carries the most shape information; additional views exist only to remove ambiguity. Hidden lines (dashed) show edges behind material; center lines (chain) declare axes and symmetry. A competent drawing shows the minimum views that fully define the part — and a competent reader cross-references features between views until the solid clicks into place mentally. That click is the skill; it comes only from reps.

Closing. Orthographic views are a lossless code for shape. Learn the two unfolding conventions, check the symbol before reading, and practice the mental refold until drawings become pictures.

Lesson 03 · Isometric and pictorial views

01 · Pictorials — communication, not measurement

Isometric and other pictorial views show three faces at once, trading measurability for immediacy. In isometric projection the three axes sit 120° apart and equal lengths along each axis draw equal — but diagonals and circles distort: a hole becomes an ellipse, a square face a rhombus. Pictorials excel at telling a fitter which way parts face; they are the wrong place to scale a dimension, ever.

02 · Where pictorials mislead

Isometric distortion hides interferences and flattens depth: two edges that clear by millimetres can appear to collide, and coaxial features can appear offset. The professional rule: sight the pictorial for orientation and sequence, then verify geometry in the orthographic views. Exploded isometrics — parts strung along assembly axes — are the fitter's map for teardown order, which is why parts catalogs live on them.

03 · Freehand sketching — the engineer's fastest tool

A proportioned freehand sketch remains the fastest CAD in existence: boxing in overall proportions first, then carving features, keeping the three isometric axes disciplined. On the floor you will sketch worn parts for the machine shop, mark up failure locations, and capture as-found configurations — with a pencil beating a laptop to every one of these tasks. Proportion matters more than beauty: a sketch with honest ratios and three good dimensions makes a part; a pretty sketch with vague ratios makes scrap.

Closing. Pictorials orient, orthographics measure, and a disciplined freehand sketch is a professional instrument — practice all three deliberately.

Lesson 04 · Sections and conventions

01 · Cutting the part open on paper

Interior features drawn as forests of hidden lines become unreadable fast. A section view slices the part with an imaginary cutting plane and draws what the knife exposes: solid material carries hatching; voids stay clear. The cutting-plane line in the parent view — with arrows showing viewing direction and letters naming the section — is the key that connects slice to view.

02 · The section family

Full sections cut straight across. Half sections show half-in, half-out on symmetric parts. Offset sections bend the cutting plane to pass through features that don't share a line — the bends are never drawn in the section itself. Revolved and removed sections drop a cross-section onto or beside a long member; broken-out sections peel a small window. Choosing the right family is drawing craft: the goal is always the fewest, clearest slices.

03 · Conventions that lie honestly

Ribs, webs, shafts, bolts, and keys are conventionally not hatched even when the plane slices them — hatching them would suggest massive solidity and hide the part's real structure. Similarly, spokes rotate into the plane, and lugs align by convention. These are deliberate, standardized departures from literal truth that make drawings more truthful about function. Knowing them separates readers from decoders.

Closing. Sections replace hidden-line archaeology with visible truth; the conventions — unhatched ribs and fasteners above all — are shared grammar, not license.

Lesson 05 · Dimensioning practice

01 · Dimensions carry function

A dimension is not decoration; it encodes which distances matter and from where. Good practice dimensions each feature once, from datums that mean something to the part's function, placed in the view where the feature shows true shape. Duplicated or over-complete dimensioning is an error, not generosity: two paths to the same edge will one day disagree, and the shop must guess which one you meant.

02 · Chains, baselines, and the accumulation problem

Chain dimensioning strings features end-to-end: each step is controlled, but the position of the last feature accumulates every tolerance in the chain — three steps at ±0.1 can put the final hole ±0.3 from the first. Baseline dimensioning measures every feature from one datum: each position is held directly, and accumulation dies. The choice is functional: chain when step-to-step spacing matters (mating hole pairs), baseline when positions from a reference matter (mounting patterns).

03 · The grammar of callouts

⌀ before a value means diameter; R radius; ( ) marks a reference dimension (information, not control); a boxed dimension is theoretically exact — controlled elsewhere, usually by GD&T. Units are declared once in the title block; mixed units on a face of a drawing are a defect. Reading a dimension means reading its status: controlled, reference, or basic — three different levels of promise.

Closing. Dimension once, from functional datums, chain or baseline by intent, and read every callout's status before trusting it. The dimensions are the part's legal description; everything else is scenery.

Lesson 06 · Tolerances, fits, and limits

01 · Tolerance is a purchase

No process makes exact dimensions; tolerance is how much inexactness the function can afford. Tightening a tolerance buys precision with money — grinding instead of turning, gauging instead of measuring. The engineer's discipline is to tolerance functionally: as loose as the function allows, as tight as it demands, feature by feature. Blanket tight tolerances are a tax the shop invoices back.

02 · The ISO fit system

Cylindrical fits are standardized as a letter-number pair on each member: the letter locates the tolerance zone (A…H clearance side for holes, h…z for shafts), the number (IT grade) sets its width. The hole-basis convention keeps the hole at H (zone starting at nominal) and moves the shaft:

\[ \text{H7/g6: running clearance} \qquad \text{H7/k6: transition} \qquad \text{H7/p6: interference} \tag{1} \]

For ⌀45 H7/g6: hole 45.000/45.025, shaft 44.991/44.975 — clearance between 0.009 and 0.050 mm: a shaft that turns freely with minimal slop, the classic bearing-journal recipe.

03 · Reading limits like a fitter

Every fit resolves to four numbers — hole max/min, shaft max/min — and two extremes: tightest (min hole, max shaft) and loosest (max hole, min shaft). Compute both before assembly decisions: will it press, slide, or rattle? Bearing catalogs, coupling manuals, and seal datasheets all speak this language; the fitter's micrometer settles the argument.

Closing. Tolerances price function; the ISO letter-number system encodes fits compactly; and four limit numbers per joint tell you tight, loose, and everything a rebuild needs to know.

Lesson 07 · GD&T essentials

01 · Why coordinates aren't enough

Plus-minus tolerances on coordinates control a hole's X and Y separately — but function cares about the hole's position as a whole, its axis attitude, the flatness of the face it lives on. Geometric Dimensioning & Tolerancing (GD&T) tolerances geometry directly: form (flatness, straightness), orientation (perpendicularity, parallelism), location (position, concentricity-family), and runout. Each control is a feature-control frame: symbol, tolerance zone, and the datums it's measured from.

02 · Datums — the measurement contract

A datum reference frame (A|B|C) declares the exact sequence of surfaces the part sits on during measurement: primary A arrests three degrees of freedom, secondary B two, tertiary C one. The same part, gauged from different datums, yields different verdicts — so the drawing fixes the frame once, and design, manufacture, and inspection all argue inside it. Datums are chosen from function: the faces that seat, the bores that locate.

03 · Runout — the rotating machine's own control

Circular runout limits the wobble of each circular element as the part turns on its datum axis — one dial reading per section. Total runout sweeps the whole surface axially and radially at once, controlling taper and coning that circular runout cannot see. For couplings, sleeves, and seal faces, runout tolerances are the drawing's way of promising your dial indicator a quiet needle — and Lesson 6's fits plus today's runout are the complete language of a shaft drawing.

Closing. GD&T tolerances what function feels — form, orientation, location, runout — from declared datums. Read the frame, find the datums, and the drawing tells you exactly how the inspector will set the part up.

Lesson 08 · Fasteners, welds, and surface symbols

01 · Symbols — high-density engineering language

Drawings compress whole procedures into symbols. Three families dominate plant work: threads and fasteners, welds, and surface finish. Misread any of them and the error is built, not drawn. This lesson is deliberately a reading lesson: the symbols carry law.

02 · Threads and welds

M16×2 6g reads: ISO metric thread, 16 mm nominal, 2 mm pitch, external tolerance class 6g. Coarse pitch is default; fine pitch is specified. For welds, the ISO/AWS symbol hangs instructions on a reference line: the symbol below the line applies to the arrow side of the joint, above the line to the other side; numbers left of the symbol give throat or leg size, right of it length and pitch; a flag means field weld; a circle means weld-all-around. A fillet symbol with 6 below the line and 6 above commands both sides at 6 mm — miss the top symbol and half the strength stays undelivered.

03 · Surface finish

The checkmark symbol carries roughness (Ra in µm), sometimes process notes and lay direction. Ra 3.2 is honest machining; 1.6 careful turning; 0.8 grinding territory; 0.4 and below, lapping and money. Finish symbols on seal journals, gasket faces, and bearing seats are functional specifications: a seal lip on an Ra 3.2 journal wears out years early, a gasket on a too-smooth flange face slides instead of gripping. The number is a friction, sealing, and fatigue decision — never cosmetic.

Closing. Thread callouts, weld symbols, and finish marks are compressed contracts. Read them slowly once, and the shop reads your intent forever.

Lesson 09 · Parametric CAD: parts

01 · Parametric means rebuildable

Parametric CAD stores recipes, not shapes: a sketch with dimensions, then features — extrude, revolve, hole, fillet — stacked in a history tree. Change a dimension and the recipe re-executes. The power is enormous and conditional: a model rebuilt from a disciplined recipe updates cleanly; a model built on accidental geometry collapses into red errors the first time anyone touches it.

02 · Design intent — the recipe's grammar

Design intent is deciding what should stay true when things change. A hole 10 mm from the edge (offset intent) behaves differently from a hole at the plate's midline (symmetry intent) when the plate grows. The discipline: fully define every sketch (no under-constrained geometry drifting at a click), reference datums and axes rather than face edges that may vanish, name features, and order the tree so machining-like operations come last. Model as if the next engineer will change the one dimension you least expect — because they will.

03 · From model to manufacture

The model is master; drawings, CAM paths, and 3-D prints are projections of it. Materials, mass properties, and revision metadata live on the model. For the maintenance engineer, the skill is bidirectional: read OEM models when you have them, and rebuild worn parts as clean parametric models when you don't — Lesson 3's field sketch becomes tonight's model and tomorrow's machined spare.

Closing. Parametric CAD is programming with geometry: fully defined sketches, functional references, an ordered tree — that is what separates a model from a liability.

Lesson 10 · Parametric CAD: assemblies and drawings

01 · Assemblies — constraints as kinematics

An assembly model positions parts with mates: coincident faces, concentric axes, distance offsets. Every mate removes degrees of freedom; the art is removing exactly the right ones. A shaft concentric to a bore and seated on a shoulder should still spin — leave that freedom alive and the model tells you how the machine moves; over-constrain it and the model fights itself with warnings that mean nothing and hide the one that does.

02 · The BOM is the assembly's ledger

The bill of materials — item numbers ballooned on the drawing, quantities, part numbers, materials — is what stores, procurement, and the fitter actually consume. Balloon 7 on the exploded view is line 7 in the table is the box on the shelf. Structure matters: sub-assemblies roll up (a bearing unit is one line here, four lines in its own BOM), and phantom items (bulk grease, thread-locker) appear where policy puts them. A drawing whose balloons and BOM disagree is a stores error factory.

03 · Drawings from models — still a craft

CAD auto-generates views, but choosing them is still Lesson 2's judgment: which section shows the fits, where the BOM table sits, which dimensions the fitter needs at the machine (coupling gap, seal compression, bolt torque table). Auto-ballooning follows assembly structure; verify it — a regenerated drawing can renumber balloons silently between revisions, and a fitter working from memory of "item 3" will draw the wrong part.

Closing. Mates encode kinematics, BOMs encode logistics, and the assembly drawing binds them for the person with the spanner. Model the motion, audit the balloons.

Lesson 11 · Reading plant drawings

01 · The plant's drawing ecosystem

Beyond part and assembly drawings, a plant runs on: GA (general arrangement) drawings — equipment in space, with footprints, elevations, and maintenance clearances; P&IDs — process logic, every pipe, valve, and instrument as symbols (INS 352 goes deep); isometrics — pipe runs dimensioned for fabrication; foundation and structural drawings — what the equipment stands on; and electrical single-lines — power distribution's family tree. Each answers one question well; the reader's skill is knowing which sheet answers today's question.

02 · Navigating a GA

A GA is read in three passes. Pass one: orientation — north arrow, column grid, elevation datums. Pass two: the equipment item and its immediate space — footprint, anchor pattern, withdrawal clearances (tube bundle pull, rotor removal envelope, crane hook travel). Pass three: the cross-references — detail drawings, foundation sheets, vendor drawings called up by number. The grid reference (column line C-7) is the plant's postal address system; learn to speak it and rigging studies stop being arguments.

03 · The drawing package as a system

A pump replacement touches the GA (space and clearances), the foundation drawing (bolts and grout), the vendor GA (nozzle loads, base details), the P&ID (what it connects to), an isometric (how the pipe reaches it), and the single-line (its feeder). The engineer's product is often not a drawing but a package: the current revisions of all of these, cross-checked. Lesson 1's register discipline scales up here — a package with one stale sheet is a stale package.

Closing. The course ends where it began: drawings as the plant's contracts. You can now read the whole family — parts, assemblies, GAs — with revision discipline, datum eyes, and symbol fluency. DRW 102's exam is every job pack you'll ever assemble.

Lesson 01 · Forces, vectors, and resultants

01 · Force is a vector, and the arrow is the contract

A force has magnitude, direction, and a point of application — drop any of the three and the analysis is fiction. In 2-D we work in components: a force \( F \) at angle \( \theta \) above the horizontal carries \( F_x = F\cos\theta \) and \( F_y = F\sin\theta \). The reverse trip is just as routine:

\[ R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}, \qquad \theta = \tan^{-1}\!\frac{R_y}{R_x} \tag{1} \]

Resultants are found by summing components, never by adding magnitudes — two 10 kN forces can combine to anything from 0 to 20 kN depending on their directions.

02 · Weight is not mass

Mass is the amount of matter, in kilograms; weight is the gravitational force on that mass, \( W = mg \) with \( g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), in newtons. A 250 kg motor does not load its sling with "250 kg" — it loads it with \( 250 \times 9.81 = 2452.5\ \mathrm{N} \approx 2.45\ \mathrm{kN} \). Bolt-load calculations, sling charts, and pressure-area checks all fail the same way when someone feeds a mass where a force belongs. Carry units through every line; they are the cheapest error detector you will ever own.

03 · Cables and struts resolve the same way

A taut cable pulls along its own axis; a straight two-force strut pushes or pulls along its axis. Both therefore hand you a force whose direction is known from geometry — only the magnitude is unknown. Resolving such members into components is the mechanical habit this whole course is built on, and the Worked Examples tab drills it on a crane cable and a motor lift.

04 · The component working method

Every force problem in this course, and most in the courses after it, runs through one mechanical procedure — run it the same way every time and errors lose their hiding places. Choose axes deliberately: horizontal-vertical by default, but rotate the axes onto an incline the moment most forces align with it — the algebra shrinks. Build a sign table: before any arithmetic, write each force's components with signs from the geometry, in a small table, so every \( \cos \) and \( \sin \) is committed once and visibly. Sum, then recompose: add the columns for \( \Sigma F_x \) and \( \Sigma F_y \), recompose into magnitude and angle — and state the quadrant from the signs of the sums, not from the calculator's arctangent, which cheerfully reports the wrong quadrant half the time.

Then run the two closing audits. Recomposition: resolve your resultant back into components; if they don't reproduce the sums, a sign slipped. Magnitude sense: the resultant can never exceed the sum of the magnitudes and can never be less than the largest minus the rest — an answer outside that band is wrong before any rechecking. Ten disciplined seconds close out a page of arithmetic.

Closing. Components in, resultant out, units carried, weight computed from mass — four small disciplines that remove most of the errors this course will otherwise spend weeks catching.

Lesson 02 · Moments and couples

01 · Moment: the turning effect of a force

The moment of a force about a point measures its tendency to rotate a body about that point:

\[ M = F d \tag{1} \]

where \( d \) is the perpendicular distance from the point to the force's line of action — not the distance to where the force happens to be applied. A 500 N pull on a 0.6 m wrench gives 300 N·m only if the pull is perpendicular to the handle; at \( 60^\circ \) to it, the effective arm shrinks and \( M = 500 \times 0.6 \sin 60^\circ = 259.8\ \mathrm{N \cdot m} \). Sign convention: pick counterclockwise positive and never renegotiate mid-problem.

02 · Varignon's theorem

The moment of a force equals the sum of the moments of its components. This is not a curiosity — it is the standard labor-saving device. Rather than hunt for a perpendicular distance through awkward geometry, resolve the force into \( F_x \) and \( F_y \) at its point of application and take each component's moment with its own easy arm. The wrench case above: \( F_y = 500\sin 60^\circ \) acting 0.6 m from the nut gives the same 259.8 N·m in one line.

03 · Couples: pure rotation

Two equal, opposite, non-collinear forces form a couple. Their force resultant is zero — a couple does not push a body anywhere — but their moment \( M = Fd \) (\( d \) the separation) is the same about every point in the plane. That free-floating property is why we may slide a couple anywhere on a rigid body, and why any force system reduces to one force plus one couple at a chosen point — the standard equivalent system used from here to machine design.

04 · The moment working method

Moments punish improvisation, so fix the routine. Declare the convention first — counterclockwise positive, written at the top of the page — and never renegotiate it mid-problem. Choose the moment center tactically: the point that kills the most unknowns, usually where reaction lines cross; the moment of a force through the chosen point is zero, and that choice is free information. Pick the computation route per force: if the perpendicular distance is visible, \( M = Fd \) is fastest; if the geometry is angled and awkward, split the force into components at its point of application and let Varignon's theorem sum the pieces — components times their own clean arms beat one force times a distance you can't see.

Treat couples as the privileged objects they are: a couple's moment is the same about every point, so it moves freely around the diagram without recalculation — the one moment that never asks "about where?". And keep the units honest: newton-metres, force times length, always stated. A moment reported as a bare number is an invitation to the classic error of adding it to a force — dimensional homogeneity, learned in the mathematics course, polices this line for free.

Closing. Moment is force times perpendicular arm; Varignon converts geometry into components; couples turn without pushing and belong to no particular point. These three facts carry every equilibrium equation that follows.

Lesson 03 · The free-body diagram

01 · Isolate, replace, label

A free-body diagram is built in three deliberate moves. Isolate: draw the body alone, severed from every support and contact. Replace: at each cut, draw the force or moment that the removed part exerted on the body. Label: every force gets a symbol, a direction, and — for knowns — a value with units. The most common omission is the body's own weight, acting at its center of gravity; the second most common is a forgotten contact force at a surface the sketch made look innocent.

02 · Supports are force vocabularies

Each support type supplies a fixed menu of reactions. A roller or smooth surface: one force, normal to the rolling surface. A pin: two force components, unknown in direction until solved. A fixed (built-in) support: two force components plus a moment — that resisting moment is what a pin cannot supply, and it is why a flagpole stays up while a pinned pole would fall. A taut cable: one pull, along the cable, never a push.

03 · Every equilibrium error is an FBD error

When a statics answer comes out wrong, the algebra is rarely the culprit; the diagram is. A force drawn at the wrong point, a reaction assumed in a direction the support cannot supply, an internal force shown on a body that was never cut — each corrupts the equations before they are written. The professional habit is to treat the FBD as a deliverable in itself: a diagram a colleague could take and write the equations from without asking you anything. the Worked Examples tab applies the discipline to a simply supported beam and a cantilever.

04 · The FBD checklist

The free-body diagram is a procedure, not an artwork. Run the checklist in order. One — isolate: redraw ONLY the chosen body, stripped of everything it touches. Two — replace every contact: each support, cable, surface, and neighbor becomes the force set it can transmit — a cable becomes one pull along its line, a smooth surface one normal push, a pin two components. Three — add the field forces: weight at the center of gravity, always. Four — name every unknown with a symbol and an assumed direction; a wrong assumption costs nothing — the algebra answers with a minus sign. Five — count: unknowns versus available equations, before solving; a mismatch caught here saves a page of doomed algebra.

Two disciplines keep diagrams honest. Forces the body exerts on other things never appear — an FBD shows only what acts on the body, and mixing the action–reaction pair onto one diagram is the classic corruption. And when a problem involves several connected bodies, draw a separate FBD per body plus, where helpful, one of the whole assembly — the pair of views cross-checks itself, because the internal forces must cancel in the assembly view.

Closing. Isolate, replace, label; know each support's force menu; never write \( \Sigma F = 0 \) until the picture is finished. The FBD is not preparation for the analysis — it is the analysis.

Lesson 04 · Equilibrium of a particle

01 · Concurrent forces, one point, two (or three) equations

A particle in statics is any body whose forces all pass through one point — a hook, a ring, a connection node. Equilibrium then needs only force balance:

\[ \Sigma F_x = 0, \qquad \Sigma F_y = 0 \tag{1} \]

(add \( \Sigma F_z = 0 \) in 3-D). No moment equation exists to help: concurrent forces have no arm about their common point. Two equations means two unknowns per 2-D node — count before solving, and if unknowns exceed equations, another FBD is required, not more algebra.

02 · Components or geometry — choose deliberately

With exactly three concurrent forces in equilibrium, the vectors must close into a triangle tip-to-tail; the law of sines then hands over the answer quickly and the geometric fact is worth having on its own. With four or more forces, or awkward angles, components (1) win every time and never mislead. The professional default is components; the triangle is a fast lane you take when the problem is small and the geometry clean.

03 · Cables, rings, and pulleys

Cable systems are particle equilibrium in the field. Each cable pulls along its axis; a frictionless pulley changes a cable's direction but not its tension — the same \( T \) runs through. A single movable pulley therefore halves the holding force: two rope parts share the load. These idealizations (massless cable, frictionless sheave) are stated assumptions, not truths — real sheave friction adds a few percent, which is why rigging tables carry efficiency factors. the Worked Examples tab solves the classic two-cable suspension and a movable-pulley hold.

04 · The particle equilibrium recipe

Concurrent-force problems yield to a fixed sequence. FBD of the point — the ring, hook, or knot where the forces meet — with every cable's pull drawn along its own line. Axes and components: resolve each force with the sign table from Lesson 1. Write both equations: \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \) and \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \), giving two equations for at most two unknowns. Solve simultaneously — substitution is usually cleanest: express one tension from the x-equation, feed it to the y-equation. Check physically: substitute the answers back until both sums close to zero within rounding, and confirm every cable came out in tension — a negative cable force is not a solution, it is a diagnosis that the geometry cannot hang that load that way.

Read the answers like an engineer afterward. The steeper cable always carries more of a hung load's weight; as a cable approaches horizontal its tension grows without bound — which is why "just pull it a little tighter" is how straight spans of cable snap hardware. The recipe produces numbers; the reading of them produces judgment, and the judgment is what the courses downstream — and the plant floor — actually pay for.

Closing. One point, force balance only, unknowns counted against equations, triangle for three clean forces, components for everything else. Particle equilibrium is small — and it is the kernel of every truss joint in Lesson 6.

Lesson 05 · Equilibrium of rigid bodies

01 · The full toolkit

A rigid body in plane equilibrium obeys three independent equations:

\[ \Sigma F_x = 0, \qquad \Sigma F_y = 0, \qquad \Sigma M_O = 0 \tag{1} \]

with the moment point \( O \) yours to choose — and choosing it on an unknown force's line of action removes that unknown from the equation. The craft of rigid-body statics is mostly the craft of choosing moment points that make equations fall apart into one-unknown pieces.

02 · Two- and three-force members

A member loaded at exactly two points carries force only along the line joining them — anything else would leave an unbalanced moment. Recognizing a two-force member converts a two-component unknown into a single magnitude along a known direction, which is a large discount. A three-force member's forces must be concurrent (or all parallel); that fact locates unknown directions geometrically. Both shortcuts come from \( \Sigma M = 0 \) applied with intent.

03 · Determinate or not

Three equations solve three unknowns. A beam on a pin and a roller (3 unknowns) is statically determinate: statics alone finishes it. Add a second pin (4 unknowns) and equilibrium no longer decides how the supports share — the structure is statically indeterminate, and the answer depends on stiffness, which is Strength of Materials' opening business. Recognizing indeterminacy early saves you from hunting for a fourth equation that does not exist.

04 · The rigid-body recipe

Bodies with size add one resource and one obligation: moments. The sequence is fixed. FBD with the support table: replace each support by its reaction set — pin two, roller one, fixed three — and count; a planar body offers exactly three equations, so more than three unknowns stops the problem before it starts. Take moments first, and tactically: choose the moment center where the most unknown reaction lines intersect — usually a support — so one \( \Sigma M = 0 \) line yields one unknown immediately. Finish with the force sums: \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \), \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \) deliver the remaining reactions. Audit with a spare moment equation: a moment sum about a different point, computed with the solved numbers, must close to zero — the free self-check almost nobody uses and every professional should.

Two readings matter on the way out. Reactions must add up to carry the total applied load — vertically and horizontally — or something is lost. And the load-sharing should make physical sense: a point load near one support loads that support proportionally harder, in inverse ratio of the distances — lever arms doing exactly what levers do.

One caution for the field: supports in drawings are idealizations of hardware. A "pin" is a bolted cleat that also resists a little moment; a "roller" is a slotted hole or a bearing pad that can bind. The three-equation arithmetic stands on those idealizations, so when a measured reaction disagrees stubbornly with the calculation, inspect the support before doubting the algebra — the hardware may have quietly changed its type.

Closing. Three equations, moment points chosen to kill unknowns, two-force members spotted on sight, and the determinacy count run before solving. That is rigid-body statics in one paragraph; the rest is practice.

Lesson 06 · Trusses

01 · A truss is a set of two-force members

The truss idealization: straight members, frictionless pin joints, loads applied only at joints. Every member is then a two-force member — pure tension or pure compression along its axis. That single assumption converts a structure into bookkeeping: find each member's axial force and its sense. Real gusset-plated, bolted trusses are not pinned, but the idealization prices member forces remarkably well and is the standard first analysis of any roof truss or braced rack.

02 · Method of joints, method of sections

The method of joints isolates one pin at a time and applies particle equilibrium — two equations per joint, so start at a joint with at most two unknowns (usually a support) and walk the structure. The method of sections cuts clean through up to three members and treats one whole side as a rigid body; a moment taken about the intersection of two cut members isolates the third in a single line. Joints when you want everything; sections when you want one member deep inside the span without solving half the truss to reach it.

03 · Zero-force members and reading the load path

At an unloaded joint of two non-collinear members, both are zero-force; at an unloaded joint of three members where two are collinear, the third is zero-force. Spotting these by inspection prunes the work — and teaches the deeper habit: a truss is a load path. Follow the applied load through diagonals and chords to the supports and the force pattern (top chord compression, bottom chord tension in a simply supported truss under gravity load) stops being memorized and starts being seen.

04 · Choosing the attack: joints or sections

Truss problems reward a decision made before any arithmetic. Sweep for zero-force members first — the two-collinear-plus-one pattern at unloaded joints — and strike them out; trusses routinely shrink by a quarter before any equation is written. Ask what the problem actually wants. Every member force? Method of joints: find a starting joint with at most two unknowns (support ends and loaded tips qualify), solve it, and let each solved member unlock the next joint. Three specific member forces in a big truss? Method of sections: cut through at most three members including the targets, treat either half as a rigid body, and take a moment about the point where the two unwanted cut members intersect — the target force falls out in one line.

Keep the bookkeeping uniform: assume every member in tension, let negative answers declare compression, and record T or C beside every result — downstream design cares about the difference more than the magnitude, because compression members buckle and tension members don't. Final audit: at any solved joint, the component sums must close; thirty seconds of checking buys back the whole truss.

Closing. Two-force members, joints for the census, sections for the surgical strike, zero-force members pruned on sight. A truss drawing is a picture of forces waiting to be read.

Lesson 07 · Frames and machines

01 · Frames are not trusses

A frame contains at least one multi-force member — a member loaded at three or more points, or loaded between its pins. Its members carry shear and bending, not just axial force, so the truss shortcut (force along the member) is off the table except for any genuine two-force members hiding inside. Identify those first: every one you spot replaces two unknowns with one.

02 · Dismember at the pins — and pay Newton his due

The method is systematic: draw an FBD of the whole frame for the external reactions, then dismember — separate the members at their connecting pins and draw each member alone. At every pin, the force one member exerts on the other appears on both diagrams, equal and opposite. The classic error is drawing pin components in inconsistent directions on the two sides, which silently violates \( \Sigma F = 0 \) for the pin itself. Adopt a convention, draw the action–reaction pair explicitly, and let negative answers correct your guesses.

03 · Machines: frames that multiply force

A machine is a frame with moving parts designed to transmit and transform force — pliers, clamps, linkages, toggles. The same dismemberment analysis yields the mechanical advantage: output force per input force, set by the lever arms. A toggle clamp near its dead-center position multiplies force enormously because the geometry sends the output arm toward zero — the reason a modest hand force can hold a workpiece against machining loads. the Worked Examples tab runs a gripping tool and a loaded two-member frame.

04 · The disassembly working method

Frames surrender to a fixed sequence, and improvising it is how pin forces get lost. Survey for two-force members first — straight, loaded only at their two pins — because each one found replaces two unknown components with a single force along the member line. Solve the assembly as one rigid body where possible, harvesting the external reactions before any disassembly. Then pull the pins: draw a separate FBD for every member, and at each shared pin write the force ONCE, applying it to the two members as an equal-and-opposite pair — the bookkeeping backbone of the whole method. Choose which member's diagram to solve first by counting unknowns: there is almost always one member with only two or three, and solving it feeds the others like the truss's joint order did.

For machines, the same disassembly answers the practical question — what does the jaw, blade, or clamp deliver for a given hand force? Moments about the pivot of the handle member give the multiplication directly: output over input equals input arm over output arm. When the answer looks too good, check whether friction and pin clearances were ignored — they were, and real tools deliver a little less than the statics promises.

Closing. Spot the two-force members, dismember at pins with honest action–reaction pairs, and read machines as frames whose geometry is doing force arithmetic. Statics of frames is Newton's third law made procedural.

Lesson 08 · Internal forces: shear and bending moment

01 · Cut the beam, expose the invisible

External reactions are only half the story; design needs the forces inside the member. Cut a beam at any section and, on the exposed face, three internal resultants restore equilibrium: normal force \( N \), shear force \( V \), and bending moment \( M \). Sign convention (keep it for life): positive shear rotates the element clockwise; positive moment sags the beam — compression on top, tension on the bottom. The convention is arbitrary; consistency is not.

02 · Diagrams, and the calculus that draws them

Shear and moment vary along the span, so we plot them. Two differential relations do most of the drawing:

\[ \frac{dV}{dx} = -w(x), \qquad \frac{dM}{dx} = V \tag{1} \]

Between point loads, \( V \) is constant and \( M \) runs straight; under a uniform load, \( V \) slopes linearly and \( M \) curves parabolically. A point load steps \( V \); a couple steps \( M \). And the operational headline: the bending moment is extremal where the shear crosses zero — because \( dM/dx = V = 0 \) there. That is where beams break, and where you look first.

03 · Why these diagrams are the product

Strength of Materials will convert \( M \) into bending stress and \( V \) into shear stress; every beam, shaft, and rack girder calculation starts from these two plots. Drawing them is not an academic exercise before the real work — for the structural side of plant engineering, the diagram is the deliverable that the stress check consumes. the Worked Examples tab builds both canonical cases: the central point load and the uniform load.

04 · Building the diagrams without tears

Internal-force diagrams are constructed, not guessed, and the construction has a grammar. Reactions first — no diagram survives wrong supports. Walk the beam from the left, cutting just after each load or support: shear jumps by each point force, slopes down under distributed load at exactly the load intensity, and the moment's slope at every station IS the shear there — \( dM/dx = V \). That one relation is the whole craft: where shear crosses zero, the moment peaks; where shear is constant, the moment runs straight; where the load is uniform, the moment curves as a parabola. Close the audit at the far end: both diagrams must return to zero (or to the end reaction/moment) — a diagram that doesn't close is announcing an arithmetic error at the exact spot it diverges.

Keep the benchmark cases in your head as calibration: a central point load peaks at \( PL/4 \); a uniform load at \( wL^2/8 \). Any computed maximum should be sanity-checked against the nearest benchmark before it goes near a design table — the courses on strength and machine design will consume these diagrams, and they inherit every error made here.

Closing. Cut, expose \( N, V, M \), plot with the two differential relations, and read the maximum moment where shear crosses zero. Beams are analyzed exactly once you can draw these two pictures without hesitation.

Lesson 09 · Friction

01 · Dry friction and the threshold

A stationary block resists sliding with whatever friction force equilibrium demands — up to a ceiling:

\[ F \le \mu_s N \tag{1} \]

Equality holds only at impending motion; below it, friction is found from equilibrium, not from (1). Once sliding starts, kinetic friction \( \mu_k N \) (with \( \mu_k < \mu_s \)) takes over — which is why things lurch. The resultant of \( N \) and limiting \( F \) leans at the friction angle \( \phi = \tan^{-1}\mu_s \) from the normal; the cone it sweeps contains every possible contact reaction. If the applied load's line falls inside the cone, no sliding — geometry answering a force question.

02 · Wedges and screws

A wedge is friction's canonical machine: driving it must beat friction on both faces, and the friction directions always oppose the impending relative motion — set them by asking "which way does each surface slide?" before writing equations. A power screw is a wedge wrapped around a cylinder: the thread's helix angle plays the wedge angle, and self-locking (the jack that holds under load) occurs when the friction angle exceeds the helix angle. Every jack, clamp, and threaded fastener trades on this.

03 · Belt friction: the exponential surprise

A belt or rope wrapped on a drum can hold a large tension with a small one. Integrating the friction on each element of wrap gives the capstan equation:

\[ \frac{T_2}{T_1} = e^{\mu \beta} \tag{2} \]

with \( \beta \) the wrap angle in radians. The ratio is exponential in wrap — each added turn multiplies. Three turns of rope on a bollard hold a ship; the same law sets how much power a conveyor drive pulley can transmit before the belt slips.

04 · The friction working method

Friction problems have one trap and one discipline. The trap: writing \( F = \mu_s N \) by reflex. Static friction is adaptive — it supplies exactly what equilibrium demands, UP TO the ceiling \( \mu_s N \), and equals the ceiling only at impending motion. So the method is: assume equilibrium, solve for the friction force required, then compare with the available maximum. Required below available — the body holds, and the actual friction is the required value, not the ceiling. Required above available — motion begins, and kinetic friction \( \mu_k N \) takes over at its lower, fixed value.

The discipline: recompute \( N \) from equilibrium every time the loading changes. A downward push on a crate raises N and buys holding capacity; an upward-angled pull robs it — the same pull that helps horizontally hurts vertically, and the trade is decided by the numbers, not intuition. Complete the method by checking the second failure mode: tall narrow bodies may TIP before they slide, so compare the moment of the push about the tipping edge with the restoring moment of the weight. Slip-or-tip is answered by whichever threshold arrives first, and rigging, racking, and machine-skid problems ask it weekly.

Treat published coefficients as estimates, not constants. Surface condition, moisture, paint, and dust move \( \mu \) by tens of percent, which is why lift plans and skid calculations carry margin on top of the friction arithmetic. When a job genuinely hinges on friction, the professional move is to measure — the tilt test that reads \( \mu_s \) straight off the angle of repose — and then still design as if the measured number were optimistic.

Closing. Friction is an inequality until motion impends; wedges and screws are friction geometrized; and wrap buys holding power exponentially. Get the impending-motion direction right and the rest is bookkeeping.

Lesson 10 · Centroids and centers of gravity

01 · The average position of area or weight

The centroid is the area-weighted average position of a shape:

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{\int x\, dA}{\int dA}, \qquad \bar{y} = \frac{\int y\, dA}{\int dA} \tag{1} \]

The center of gravity is the same construction weighted by weight; for uniform density and gravity they coincide. Integration handles curved boundaries once; engineering practice handles everything else by composite parts — split the shape into rectangles, triangles, and circles whose centroids are known, then take the weighted average. Holes enter the sum as negative areas: same formula, signed.

02 · The tabular method

Composite work is done in a table: one row per part, columns for \( A \), \( \bar{x}_i \), \( \bar{y}_i \), \( A\bar{x}_i \), \( A\bar{y}_i \); sum the columns, divide. The discipline matters more than it looks — a consistent datum (pick one corner, state it) and signed areas prevent the classic errors: measuring one part from a different origin, or forgetting that a cut-out subtracts moment as well as area. The same table extends without modification to weights: replace \( A \) with \( W \) and the result is a center of gravity.

03 · Where the hook must sit

Lift any rigid load from a single hook and it hangs with its center of gravity vertically below the hook. If the CG is off the hook line, the load tilts until they align — swinging tools, spilling contents, and shifting sling angles on the way. A lifting plan therefore starts with a CG calculation: composite masses of pump, motor, and baseplate, exactly the table of 2 with kilograms in the area column. The hook, or the sling spread, is then placed to put the CG under the crane's line of pull. the Worked Examples tab runs one steel section and one equipment skid.

04 · The tabular working method

Centroid calculations live or die by bookkeeping, so industrialize it. Decompose the section into shapes whose centroids are known — rectangles, triangles, circles. Fix one reference corner for the whole table and never move it mid-problem; half of all centroid errors are two shapes measured from two different origins. Tabulate \( A_i \), \( \bar{x}_i \), \( \bar{y}_i \), \( A_i\bar{x}_i \), \( A_i\bar{y}_i \) — holes entering as negative areas with negative first moments — sum the columns, divide. The table is not scaffolding to discard later: the moment-of-inertia lesson extends the same table by one column, and CAD packages print the same quantities under the same names, so fluency here reads straight into the tools.

Audit the answer geometrically before accepting it: the centroid must sit inside the convex hull of the section (though possibly outside the material, as in an L or a channel — that is legal and common), and symmetry must be honored — any axis of symmetry the section has, the centroid sits on. An answer violating either rule is wrong with certainty, no recomputation required.

Closing. One averaging formula, executed by table, with signed areas and a declared datum. From steel sections to lifting plans, it is the same arithmetic wearing different units.

Lesson 11 · Moments of inertia

01 · The second moment: area times distance squared

The second moment of area about the x-axis is

\[ I_x = \int y^2\, dA \tag{1} \]

Distance enters squared: area far from the axis counts disproportionately. For the workhorse rectangle about its own centroid, \( I = bh^3/12 \) — the cubed depth is the single most consequential exponent in structural engineering. \( I \) is not inertia in the dynamic sense; it is a purely geometric measure of how a section's area is deployed against bending.

02 · The parallel-axis theorem

Centroidal values are tabulated; real composite parts sit away from the composite centroid. The transfer rule:

\[ I = \bar{I} + A d^2 \tag{2} \]

adds \( Ad^2 \) for a part whose own centroid lies distance \( d \) from the axis in question. Two habits keep it honest: the theorem transfers only from a centroidal axis (never between two non-centroidal axes directly), and in composite work the \( Ad^2 \) terms usually dwarf the parts' own \( \bar{I} \) — flanges of an I-beam earn their keep almost entirely through \( Ad^2 \).

03 · Why sections look the way they do

An I-beam is the parallel-axis theorem drawn in steel: put most of the area in two flanges far from the neutral axis (huge \( d \)), keep a thin web to hold them apart and carry shear. the Worked Examples tab computes a small I-section at \( I = 4.49 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \) — then shows a solid square of the same area manages only \( 0.65 \times 10^6 \): the shape, not the steel, is doing seven times the work. Strength of Materials will divide bending moment by exactly this \( I \) to get stress; every section table you will ever open is a catalog of this one integral.

04 · The transfer working method

Composite moments of inertia extend the centroid table by exactly one discipline: the parallel-axis transfer. The sequence never varies. Find the composite centroid first — Lesson 10's table verbatim. For each part, write its own centroidal \( \bar{I}_i \) from the standard formulas, measure the distance \( d_i \) from the part's centroid to the COMPOSITE centroid, and transfer: \( I_i = \bar{I}_i + A_i d_i^2 \). Sum. Holes ride the same table with negative \( A \) and negative \( \bar{I} \). The classic error is transferring between two arbitrary axes — the theorem only moves I away from a shape's OWN centroid, never between two off-centroid axes; when in doubt, route through the centroid in two steps.

Read the result with the \( h^3 \) instinct: material far from the axis does the work, which is why doubling a beam's depth multiplies its bending stiffness eightfold while doubling its width merely doubles it, and why the I-beam exists at all. When a computed composite I comes out smaller than its largest part's contribution, the arithmetic is wrong — transfers only ever add for real areas. These numbers feed the flexure formula in Strength of Materials directly; the audit habits travel with them.

Closing. \( y^2 \) rewards distance, \( bh^3/12 \) rewards depth, and \( Ad^2 \) explains the I-beam. Statics ends by manufacturing the geometric property that strength calculations spend.

MAT 104 — Materials Science I

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Why materials fail the plant

01 · Four families, four bargains

Every engineering material belongs to one of four families, and each family is a bargain with physics. Metals: stiff, strong, tough, conductive — but dense and corrodible. Ceramics: hard, refractory, chemically inert — but brittle, and brittle without apology. Polymers: light, cheap, formable, corrosion-immune — but soft, creep-prone, and temperature-limited. Composites: property combinations the families cannot reach alone — bought with cost and joining headaches. No family is best; each is best at something, and the selection question is always "what does this application refuse to forgive?"

02 · The spine of the course

Materials science stands on one chain:

\[ \text{processing} \rightarrow \text{structure} \rightarrow \text{properties} \rightarrow \text{performance} \tag{1} \]

Processing sets the internal structure — from atomic bonding through crystal defects to microstructure; structure sets the properties; properties decide whether the part survives service. The chain runs both directions: read it left-to-right to design a material, right-to-left to diagnose a failure. Every lesson in this course is one link examined closely.

03 · The failed part is a materials question

A snapped bolt, a cracked impeller, a swollen seal — the maintenance floor sees these as events; this course teaches you to see them as structure speaking. Was the fracture surface woody and deformed (ductile, overload) or flat and crystalline (brittle, or fatigue)? Did the polymer harden and craze (thermal aging) or soften and smear (chemical attack)? Chain (1) run backwards is failure analysis, and it begins with knowing which family the part belongs to and what that family's bargain was.

04 · The failure-reading working method

Materials thinking has a procedure, and it starts at the broken part, not the catalog. Name the failure mode first — overload, fatigue, corrosion, wear, creep — because each mode names its own culprit property and its own fix; a fatigue failure is not solved by a stronger material if the problem is a sharp shoulder. Walk the causal chain backward: performance failed → which property fell short → which structure produced that property → which processing produced that structure. The chain runs in both directions, and every lesson in this course adds one link. Quantify before judging: \( \sigma = F/A \) turns "the bolt looked thin" into a number — a 20% section loss is a 25% stress rise at constant load, because the divisor shrank — and numbers survive meetings that adjectives do not.

Close every reading with the honest questions: what environment, what load spectrum, what life, what cost? A material is never good or bad — it is suited or unsuited to those four declared inputs. The professional habit this course builds is refusing to discuss materials until the four inputs are on the table, and refusing to blame a material until the failure mode has a name.

Keep one more discipline from day one: data provenance. Handbook numbers are typical values with real scatter; a delivered batch's certificate is the number that actually matters, and downstream lessons will teach you to read one. Where the certificate and the handbook disagree, the certificate wins — the material in the rack outranks the material in the book.

Closing. Four families, one chain, two reading directions. Everything that follows — bonding, crystals, defects, strengthening — is the machinery inside the word "structure".

Lesson 02 · Atomic bonding

01 · Bonds are springs with a depth

Two atoms settle at the separation \( r_0 \) where attraction and repulsion balance — the bottom of the bond-energy well. Two features of that well govern macroscopic behavior. Its curvature at the bottom is the stiffness of the interatomic spring: deep, narrow wells give high elastic modulus. Its depth is the bond energy: deep wells mean high melting points and — because a deep well is also more symmetric near its floor — low thermal expansion. Modulus, melting point, and expansion coefficient travel together, and the bond-energy curve is why.

02 · The four bonding types

Metallic: ion cores in a shared electron sea. The sea conducts (electrons free to drift) and the non-directional bonding lets planes of atoms slide without shattering — conductivity and ductility from one picture. Ionic: electron transfer, then Coulomb attraction between ions; strong but directionally rigid — slide a plane one spacing and like charges meet, so ceramics fracture rather than flow. Covalent: shared electron pairs, strongly directional; diamond's hardness and silicon's brittleness. Secondary (van der Waals / hydrogen): weak dipole attractions — the reason polymer chains, strongly covalent along their backbones, still slide past each other at modest temperature.

03 · Mixed character, measured

Real compounds sit between poles. Electronegativity difference estimates the ionic fraction of a bond; Pauling's relation \( \%IC = [1 - e^{-0.25(X_A - X_B)^2}] \times 100 \) puts MgO at ~73% ionic while SiC is ~12% — one behaves as a classic ionic ceramic, the other as a covalent one, and their fracture and conduction behavior follow suit. the Worked Examples tab computes both, plus the raw Coulomb force inside an ion pair.

04 · Reading properties off the bond

The bond-energy curve is a property-prediction machine if read with method. Well depth is cohesion: deeper wells mean higher melting points, higher stiffness, lower vapor pressure — which is why tungsten's deep metallic well melts at 3400 °C while polyethylene's van-der-Waals-held chains soften in boiling water. Well curvature at the bottom is elastic modulus: a steep, narrow well resists stretching hard. Well asymmetry is thermal expansion: shallow, lopsided wells let atoms ride up the soft side as temperature climbs — polymers expand roughly ten times more than metals per degree for exactly this reason.

Work the scale conversion fluently: table values arrive per mole, atoms bond one at a time, so divide by Avogadro's number — a 640 kJ/mol bond is about 1.1 aJ, or 6.6 eV, per bond. And keep the bonding families honest in memory: primary bonds (ionic, covalent, metallic) are hundreds of kJ/mol and set the ceiling properties; secondary bonds are tens and set the softening behavior of everything held together by them. When a datasheet surprises you, the bond picture is the first sanity check: no van-der-Waals solid will ever serve at 500 °C, whatever the brochure implies.

Closing. Stiffness, melting, and expansion live in the well's shape; ductility versus brittleness lives in whether the bonding forgives atomic-plane slide. Metals forgive; ceramics do not; polymers are covalent ropes held by weak handshakes.

Lesson 03 · Crystal structures

01 · Three cells carry most of metallurgy

Most engineering metals crystallize into one of three unit cells. FCC (face-centered cubic): atoms at corners and face centers — copper, aluminum, nickel, austenitic stainless. BCC (body-centered cubic): corners plus one center atom — ferritic iron, chromium, tungsten. HCP (hexagonal close-packed): zinc, magnesium, titanium. Geometry first: in FCC atoms touch along the face diagonal, so \( a = 2R\sqrt{2} \) with 4 atoms per cell; in BCC they touch along the body diagonal, \( a = 4R/\sqrt{3} \), 2 atoms per cell.

02 · Packing predicts behavior

The atomic packing factor — fraction of cell volume occupied — is 0.74 for FCC and HCP against 0.68 for BCC. FCC's close packing comes with close-packed planes on which slip is easy in many directions at any temperature: FCC metals stay ductile from cryogenic cold to near melting. BCC's slightly open structure makes slip thermally assisted — cool a BCC steel enough and slip stalls before fracture does, the root of the ductile–brittle transition that Lesson 8 prices in joules. HCP's single close-packed plane family gives few slip systems and the notorious formability limits of magnesium and zinc.

03 · Density from first principles

The unit cell converts crystallography into a checkable number:

\[ \rho = \frac{nA}{V_C N_A} \tag{1} \]

\( n \) atoms per cell, \( A \) the atomic weight, \( V_C \) the cell volume, \( N_A \) Avogadro's number. the Worked Examples tab runs copper and lands within 1% of the handbook value — a satisfying audit that the atomic picture is not a metaphor but a measurement.

04 · The unit-cell bookkeeping method

Crystal arithmetic rewards the same tabular discipline as statics. Fix the cell geometry first: for FCC, atoms touch along the face diagonal, so \( a = 2R\sqrt{2} \); for BCC, along the body diagonal, \( a = 4R/\sqrt{3} \) — derive the relation from the touching direction rather than memorizing it, and it cannot betray you. Count atoms the honest way: corners contribute an eighth each, faces a half, body atoms a whole — 4 per FCC cell, 2 per BCC. Then let the density formula audit everything: \( \rho = nA/(V_C N_A) \) predicts copper at 8.90 g/cm³ from nothing but a radius and an atomic mass; agreement with the handbook to within a percent certifies the whole chain of geometry, counting, and units in one number.

Use the same audit in reverse on real parts: a measured density falling meaningfully below the theoretical value is porosity talking — castings and additive parts confess their voids this way, and the quality lab's densitometer is running this lesson's equation as a routine instrument. Structure is not an abstraction; it is a number you can weigh — and the first crystal-level measurement most engineers ever make is a bathroom-scale argument about atoms, which is precisely what makes it trustworthy on a shop floor.

Closing. Three cells, two touching rules, one packing-factor gap that explains why austenitic steel stays tough in liquid nitrogen while ferritic steel turns to glass in a cold snap. Crystal structure is destiny, slightly exaggerated — but only slightly.

Lesson 04 · Crystallographic directions and planes

01 · Naming directions and planes

Crystals are anisotropic objects, so we need coordinates. Directions [uvw]: take the vector's components along the cell axes, clear fractions to smallest integers, wrap in square brackets, mark negatives with an overbar. Planes (hkl), the Miller indices: take the plane's intercepts on the axes in units of the lattice parameters, invert them, clear fractions, wrap in parentheses — an intercept at infinity (plane parallel to an axis) inverts to a clean zero. Families of equivalent directions and planes get angle and brace brackets: ⟨110⟩, {111}.

02 · Why these labels earn their keep

Slip — plastic deformation — happens on the densest planes along the densest directions, because those are the paths where atoms ride over each other with the least protest. In FCC that means {111} planes and ⟨110⟩ directions: 12 combinations, the slip systems that make copper and aluminum so obligingly ductile. Linear and planar densities (atoms per length, per area) are the quantitative versions, and computing them is how the claim "close-packed = slip-prone" stops being a slogan.

03 · Anisotropy leaves the lab

A single crystal's modulus can differ by a factor of two between directions. Polycrystals average this out — unless processing lines the grains up. Rolling does exactly that: plate develops a crystallographic texture plus elongated grains and aligned inclusions, so strength, ductility, and especially toughness differ between the rolling direction and the transverse. Standards respond by specifying test-bar orientation, and fabricators respond — or should — by orienting bends relative to the rolling direction. The Miller machinery of 1 is the language in which all of this is written.

04 · The indexing working method

Miller indices are a procedure, and running it identically every time is the entire skill. Planes: read the intercepts in cell-edge units, take reciprocals, clear fractions, bracket as \( (hkl) \) — a plane parallel to an axis intercepts at infinity and earns a zero. Directions: head coordinates minus tail coordinates, clear to smallest integers, bracket as \( [uvw] \). Negative components wear an overbar, and families collect all symmetry-equivalent members: \( \{111\} \) means every close-packed plane in FCC at once. The classic error is mixing conventions mid-problem — intercepts for directions or vector components for planes produce plausible-looking nonsense, which is why the two recipes should be written at the top of the page until they are reflex.

Then put the indices to work, because they exist for the packing arithmetic: linear density along a close-packed direction is \( 1/(2R) \) — atoms touching in a row — and the densest planes with the densest directions form the slip systems where plasticity lives. FCC's twelve well-fed slip systems are the geometric reason copper draws into wire without complaint; HCP's grudging three are why some alloys crack where others flow. The indices are the language; slip is the sentence they were invented to write.

Closing. Directions in brackets, planes in parentheses, slip on the close-packed combinations, and rolled products carrying their processing history as directionality. The labels are dry; what they index is not.

Lesson 05 · Defects in solids

01 · The perfect crystal is a useful fiction

Every real crystal is defective, and the defects are not blemishes on the theory — they are the theory of real behavior. By dimensionality: point defects — vacancies (missing atoms), interstitials (atoms squeezed into gaps), and substitutional or interstitial solute atoms; line defects — dislocations, edge and screw; planar defects — grain boundaries, twin boundaries, free surfaces. Each class controls a different family of properties, and the rest of this course is largely the story of engineering their populations.

02 · Vacancies obey thermodynamics

Vacancies are not accidents; equilibrium demands them. Their fraction rises exponentially with temperature:

\[ \frac{N_v}{N} = \exp\!\left(-\frac{Q_v}{kT}\right) \tag{1} \]

Near the melting point roughly one site in ten thousand is empty; at room temperature the fraction is astronomically smaller. This matters because vacancies are diffusion's vehicle — atoms move mainly by hopping into adjacent empty sites — so (1) is the deep reason every diffusion-controlled process in Lesson 6 accelerates exponentially with temperature.

03 · Dislocations: why metals are a thousand times weaker than they should be

Shearing a perfect crystal means breaking every bond across a plane simultaneously — the theoretical strength, orders of magnitude above anything measured. A dislocation breaks the bonds one row at a time, like moving a carpet by running a wrinkle across it rather than dragging the whole rug. Metals deform at realistic stresses because dislocations exist; they strengthen when we obstruct dislocation motion — with solutes, boundaries, and other dislocations, the entire agenda of Lesson 9. Grain boundaries, meanwhile, are both obstacles (strength) and fast-diffusion highways (creep, corrosion sensitivity): planar defects always charge rent both ways.

04 · Reading the defect inventory

Perfection is not an option in real crystals, so the working method is inventory management. Know each defect's dimension: point defects (vacancies, interstitials, substitutionals), line defects (dislocations), planar defects (grain boundaries, surfaces) — and match each to the property it governs: vacancies to diffusion, dislocations to plasticity, boundaries to strength and to creep. Respect the exponential: the vacancy fraction \( e^{-Q_v/kT} \) is astronomically sensitive to temperature — around \( 3\times10^{-5} \) at 1000 K for a typical metal and essentially zero at room temperature — so processes that need vacancies (diffusion, annealing, creep) switch on with heat and freeze out without it. That single exponential explains why heat treatment works at temperature and why parts are stable at service conditions.

The professional reading: defects are not flaws to lament but levers to pull. Carbon interstitials ARE steel; substitutional chromium IS stainless; grain boundaries ARE the strengthening in fine-grained plate. The materials engineer's job is rarely to remove defects — it is to choose which defects, at what population, on purpose. The next two lessons pull exactly those levers.

A note on scale keeps the inventory honest: even a heavily defective crystal is mostly perfect. At the 1000 K figure computed in this lesson's examples, fewer than three sites in a hundred thousand stand vacant — and yet that trace population carries all of diffusion. Materials behavior is routinely governed by its rarest constituents, which is why counting them, rather than rounding them to zero, is a professional skill.

Closing. Point defects carry diffusion, line defects carry plasticity, planar defects referee both. "Defect" is the field's least fortunate word — these are the control knobs.

Lesson 06 · Diffusion

01 · Fick's laws, with atoms attached

Diffusion is atoms hopping site to site, biased downhill in concentration. Steady state is Fick's first law, \( J = -D\, dC/dx \): flux proportional to gradient, \( D \) the diffusion coefficient. Transient cases — the plant's usual reality — obey Fick's second law:

\[ \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2} \tag{1} \]

whose solutions for a surface held at constant concentration follow the error function. The engineering headline hiding in those solutions: any characteristic penetration depth scales as

\[ x \sim \sqrt{Dt} \tag{2} \]

Double the depth costs four times the time. That square root governs carburizing, decarburizing, homogenization, and every soak-time argument you will ever have with a furnace schedule.

02 · Temperature is the big lever

The diffusion coefficient is Arrhenius:

\[ D = D_0 \exp\!\left(-\frac{Q_d}{RT}\right) \tag{3} \]

with activation energy \( Q_d \) set by the migration mechanism — interstitials like carbon in iron dart between gaps cheaply; substitutional atoms must wait for vacancies and pay more. Because \( T \) sits in an exponential, modest temperature moves swamp heroic time extensions: in the Worked Examples tab, 200 K of extra furnace temperature buys a factor of ~19 in \( D \), which no affordable soak time matches.

03 · Reading processes through (2) and (3)

Case-hardening a gear: carbon in from the surface, case depth by \( \sqrt{Dt} \), temperature chosen high enough for speed but below distortion limits. Homogenizing a segregated casting: the same mathematics with the dendrite arm spacing as the distance to erase. Even degradation reads this way — hydrogen charging into a weld, carburization of furnace tubes — diffusion is one mathematics wearing many overalls.

04 · The diffusion estimating method

Most industrial diffusion questions are answered by one scaling law before any full solution: penetration grows as \( x \sim \sqrt{Dt} \). Run it as a ratio and the unknown constants cancel — if 4 hours of carburizing buys a 1 mm case, a 2 mm case costs \( 2^2 = 4 \) times as long: 16 hours, not 8. The square root is the story: doubling depth always quadruples time, which is why deep cases are expensive and why homogenizing a badly segregated casting can be economically hopeless. Temperature is the other lever, and it is exponential: \( D = D_0 e^{-Q_d/RT} \) — a modest temperature rise can buy what hours of extra soak cannot, which is why furnace setpoints, not timers, dominate diffusion process design.

Keep the mechanisms straight when judging feasibility: small interstitials like carbon hop between gaps and move fast; substitutional atoms must wait for a vacancy to swap with and move orders of magnitude slower at the same temperature. That is why case-hardening with carbon is an overnight job while chromizing is a specialist's slow art — and why "just diffuse it in" is a plan that needs this lesson's arithmetic before it becomes a work order.

The same estimating method audits vendors: a heat-treat certificate quoting an improbably deep case for the stated hours and temperature is either running hotter than declared or measuring generously — and \( \sqrt{Dt} \), evaluated on the back of the certificate itself, is enough to ask the question with numbers instead of suspicion.

Closing. Gradient drives, \( \sqrt{Dt} \) paces, Arrhenius accelerates. Three relations, and most furnace-floor arithmetic is already in hand.

Lesson 07 · Mechanical properties I: elasticity and the tension test

01 · One test to rule them

Pull a standard bar to fracture, record load and elongation, and normalize away the geometry: engineering stress \( \sigma = F/A_0 \), engineering strain \( \epsilon = \Delta L/L_0 \), both referred to original dimensions. The resulting curve is the most quoted document in mechanical engineering, and four numbers summarize it: the elastic modulus \( E \) (initial slope, Hooke's law \( \sigma = E\epsilon \)); the yield strength \( \sigma_y \) (0.2% offset, where permanent set begins); the ultimate tensile strength (the curve's peak); and ductility, as percent elongation or reduction of area.

02 · What each number is for

\( E \) is a bonding property — recall Lesson 2's well curvature — essentially untouchable by heat treatment: all steels share ~200 GPa. \( \sigma_y \) is the designer's boundary; machines are sized so working stress stays below it with margin. UTS matters for what happens beyond intent — overload margins and, through empirical correlations, hardness checks. Ductility is the forgiveness budget: the plastic reserve that lets a part yield locally, redistribute, and warn before it breaks.

03 · True stress, and the necking illusion

Past the UTS the engineering curve droops — but the material is not softening. Necking has begun: area shrinks locally, and dividing load by the original area hides it. True stress \( \sigma_T = F/A \) (instantaneous area) keeps rising to fracture; with volume constancy the conversions in the plastic range are

\[ \sigma_T = \sigma(1+\epsilon), \qquad \epsilon_T = \ln(1+\epsilon) \tag{1} \]

Metal forming lives on true quantities — a rolling pass or a drawing die sees real areas, not test-report conventions. the Worked Examples tab extracts all four report numbers from raw data, then converts a point through (1).

04 · Reading a tension test like a professional

The tensile curve is read in a fixed order, and each stop answers one design question. The elastic slope first: Young's modulus — and the professional expectation that it barely varies within an alloy family; every carbon steel runs near 200 GPa, so a certificate claiming otherwise indicts the test, not the steel. Yield next, by the 0.2% offset construction when the knee is gradual: draw the line parallel to the elastic slope from \( \varepsilon = 0.002 \) and read the intersection — this is the number design codes divide by safety factors. Ultimate strength marks where necking begins: beyond it, the ENGINEERING curve droops only because the true area is shrinking faster than the load — the material itself is still hardening, and knowing that resolves the classic "why does the curve fall?" confusion. Ductility last: percent elongation, the formability budget manufacturing will spend.

The working method for any calculation is unit discipline on \( \sigma = F/A_0 \) and \( \varepsilon = \Delta L/L_0 \): kilonewtons over square millimetres give megapascals directly, and strain is always dimensionless — a strain quoted in millimetres is a gauge length away from being a number. Verify computed moduli against the family expectation before reporting; the check costs nothing and catches decimal slips every time.

Closing. One bar, one pull, four numbers, and one conversion that keeps the story honest after necking. Every mill certificate you will ever read is this lesson in tabular form.

Lesson 08 · Mechanical properties II: hardness and toughness

01 · Hardness: the field's favorite proxy

Hardness is resistance to localized plastic indentation — push a standard indenter with a standard load, measure the dent. Brinell (10 mm ball, the impression's diameter), Vickers (diamond pyramid, works from foils to carbides on one scale), Rockwell (depth-based, direct-reading, the production floor's default). Conversions between scales are empirical, tabulated, and alloy-specific — treat cross-scale comparisons as estimates, not measurements.

02 · Why hardness tracks strength — and when it lies

An indentation is a small, constrained plasticity experiment, so hardness rides the same dislocation resistance as yield strength. For steels the workhorse correlation is

\[ UTS\ (\mathrm{MPa}) \approx 3.45 \times HB \tag{1} \]

good enough to audit a heat treatment without cutting the part — which is precisely how the field uses it. The proxy lies when the assumptions break: heavily cold-worked surfaces, decarburized skins, coarse multi-phase structures sampled by a small indenter, or non-ferrous alloys wearing a steel correlation. Hardness answers "what is the strength here, at this spot" — sometimes that locality is the feature, sometimes the trap.

03 · Toughness, and the transition that sank ships

Strength says what stress a material bears; toughness says what energy it absorbs breaking — measured crudely and usefully by the Charpy impact test: a notched bar, a swinging pendulum, joules read off the follower. BCC steels hold an unpleasant surprise: impact energy collapses below a ductile–brittle transition temperature, Lesson 3's thermally-assisted slip arriving as fracture. Liberty ships cracked in cold seas on exactly this physics. FCC metals — austenitic stainless, aluminum, copper — have no transition, which is why cryogenic plant is built from them.

04 · Choosing and converting hardness numbers honestly

Hardness testing is the plant's favorite mechanical test because it is fast, cheap, and almost nondestructive — but its numbers demand method. Choose the scale by the job: Brinell's big ball averages over coarse structures (castings, weld HAZ surveys); Vickers's diamond reads thin cases and small zones; Rockwell's direct-reading dial rules production floors. Convert with respect: scales measure different deformations, so conversions between them are empirical tables, not algebra — and the steel-only estimate \( UTS \approx 3.45 \times HB \) is a field tool for triage, never a substitute for a tensile certificate when the number matters contractually.

Toughness closes the lesson because it is the property strength tests miss: the area under the stress–strain curve — strength AND ductility multiplied into energy. Two materials of equal strength can differ tenfold in toughness, and the Charpy machine exists to catch the difference under shock, including the ductile-to-brittle transition that ambushes BCC steels in cold service. The working rule: specify strength for the load, hardness for the wear, toughness for the accident — and never let one number impersonate the other two. And when a service temperature dips below freezing, ask the Charpy question explicitly: transition behavior is found by testing across temperatures, never by extrapolating a single room-temperature result.

Closing. Hardness is portable strength, within its correlation's jurisdiction; Charpy is energy accounting with a notch. Between them they answer the floor's two commonest questions: is it hard enough, and will it shatter when the weather turns.

Lesson 09 · Dislocations and strengthening

01 · One enemy, many obstacles

Plastic flow is dislocation traffic (Lesson 5), so all strengthening is one strategy: obstruct the traffic. Every industrial method is a different obstacle wearing a different price tag — and each purchase of strength is paid for, almost always, in ductility. Four mechanisms cover the metallurgy you will meet in practice.

02 · The four mechanisms

Grain refinement. A boundary forces slip to renegotiate direction; more boundaries, more negotiations. Quantitatively, the Hall–Petch relation:

\[ \sigma_y = \sigma_0 + k_y\, d^{-1/2} \tag{1} \]

— fine grains strengthen, and (uniquely among the four) usually improve toughness too, which is why fine grain is the closest metallurgy comes to a free lunch. Solid solution. Solute atoms strain the lattice locally; dislocations wade through the strain fields. Every alloying addition does some of this. Strain hardening. Deform the metal and dislocations multiply until they obstruct each other — quantified by percent cold work, spent by every rolling pass and drawing die. Precipitation. Fine second-phase particles force dislocations to cut or bow around them; the strongest aluminum alloys and nickel superalloys live here.

03 · Choosing among them

The mechanisms stack — a drawn, alloyed, fine-grained wire uses three at once — but they differ in temperature stability (strain hardening anneals away; precipitates coarsen), in cost, and in what they do to toughness and conductivity. A copper conductor is the cautionary tale: solutes that strengthen it also scatter electrons, so cable copper is strengthened by drawing, not doping. the Worked Examples tab prices mechanism one with real Hall–Petch constants and mechanism three on a drawn rod.

04 · The strengthening decision method

Every strengthening mechanism is the same sentence with a different subject: something obstructs dislocations. The engineering skill is choosing WHICH something, and the choice runs on three questions. What temperature will the part see? Strain hardening anneals away — it cannot serve hot; grain-boundary strengthening fades as boundaries begin to slide at creep temperatures; solid-solution and precipitation strengthening hold higher. What process route is available? Cold work arrives free with rolling and drawing; fine grain arrives through controlled rolling and normalizing; precipitates demand the full solution-quench-age cycle of the heat-treatment lessons ahead. What does the trade cost? Every mechanism buys strength with ductility — the strengthened metal forms less willingly, and a drawing schedule that ignores this snaps wire.

Run Hall–Petch as the worked example of quantitative thinking: \( \sigma_y = \sigma_0 + k\,d^{-1/2} \) turns a micrograph's grain size into a yield prediction, and its inverse square root explains why halving grain size buys a meaningful but diminishing return. The mechanisms stack — commercial alloys routinely deploy three at once — and reading a datasheet is recognizing which levers its producer pulled. A dual-phase automotive steel, for instance, runs fine grains, solute, and a hard second phase simultaneously; its certificate is a three-lever chord, and hearing the chord is what this lesson trains.

Closing. Strength is obstructed dislocation motion — by boundaries, solutes, tangles, or particles. Name the obstacle in any datasheet's "condition" column and you have read the alloy's biography.

Lesson 10 · Recovery, recrystallization, and grain growth

01 · What cold work leaves behind

A cold-worked metal is a loaded spring at the microscale: dislocation density up a thousandfold, grains flattened and rotated, a few percent of the deformation energy locked in as internal strain. That stored energy is the driving force for everything in this lesson — heat the metal and it will spend that energy reorganizing, in three acts.

02 · Recovery, recrystallization, grain growth

Recovery (lowest temperatures): dislocations climb, annihilate, and tidy into low-energy walls. Residual stress falls and conductivity returns, but strength barely moves — the stress-relief anneal lives here. Recrystallization: new, strain-free grains nucleate in the deformed matrix and consume it wholesale; strength drops toward the annealed baseline and ductility floods back. The temperature where this completes in about an hour — the recrystallization temperature — runs roughly

\[ T_R \approx 0.4\, T_m \quad (\mathrm{in\ kelvins}) \tag{1} \]

and falls with heavier prior cold work (more stored energy, more driving force). Grain growth: left hot after recrystallization finishes, boundaries migrate to cut boundary area, grains coarsen, and Hall–Petch (Lesson 9) runs in reverse. Annealing schedules end when recrystallization does, not later, for exactly this reason.

03 · Hot working, defined properly

Hot working is deformation above the recrystallization temperature — the metal recrystallizes as fast as the rolls harden it, so it never accumulates cold work. Cold working is deformation below \( T_R \). The boundary is homologous, not absolute: by (1), lead recrystallizes below room temperature — a fitter bending lead sheet is hot-working it at 25 °C — while tungsten at a red-hot 800 °C can still be cold-working. "Hot" belongs to the metal's melting point, not the thermometer.

04 · The annealing decision method

Cold work and annealing form a cycle the mill walks deliberately, and the walking has rules. Track the stored energy: each cold pass banks dislocations, raising strength and dropping ductility toward the cracking threshold; the annealing furnace spends that stored energy on new, strain-free grains. Locate the temperature honestly: recrystallization begins near \( 0.4\,T_m \) in kelvins — the boundary that separates cold work from hot work by definition, not by whether the shop felt warm. Below it, recovery alone relaxes residual stresses while keeping most of the strength: the stress-relief anneal that fabricators order by that exact name. Respect the clock after the reset: grain growth never stops at temperature — \( d^2 - d_0^2 = Kt \) keeps consuming boundaries — so an over-soaked anneal trades the fine grain the previous lesson prized for coarse, weak structure. Schedules end on time for a reason.

The method in practice: choose the cold-work total for the final strength, insert intermediate anneals where cumulative reduction approaches the alloy's formability limit, and finish either work-hardened (springy temper) or annealed (soft temper) to order. Temper designations on certificates are precisely this cycle's bookkeeping.

Closing. Stored strain energy drives three acts — tidy, replace, coarsen — and \( 0.4\,T_m \) marks the stage door. Every anneal on every line is a decision about which act to stop after.

Lesson 11 · Solidification and cast structure

01 · Nucleation: the reluctant start

A melt below its freezing point does not freeze at once — a solid nucleus must first reach a critical size before growing is cheaper than redissolving. The balance of surface energy paid against volume energy won sets the critical radius:

\[ r^* = \frac{2\gamma T_m}{\Delta H_f\, \Delta T} \tag{1} \]

— inversely proportional to the undercooling \( \Delta T \). Homogeneous nucleation needs implausible undercoolings; real melts nucleate heterogeneously on mold walls and inoculant particles, which is why foundries seed melts deliberately: more nuclei, finer grains, better castings.

02 · The three zones of a casting

Read a sectioned billet or ingot and solidification history is written in its grains. Chill zone: a skin of fine equiaxed grains, nucleated in a rush against the cold mold. Columnar zone: long grains growing inward along the heat-flow direction, competing until the best-aligned survive. Central equiaxed zone: grains nucleated in the cooling interior liquid. The proportions are process knobs — pouring superheat, mold cooling, stirring — and they matter because columnar structures are anisotropic and their convergence planes are weak.

03 · Segregation: solidification's chemical fingerprint

Solids accept less solute than liquids, so a growing crystal rejects solute into the melt ahead of it. At dendrite scale this leaves microsegregation — solute-lean dendrite cores, solute-rich interdendritic pockets, quantified by the lever rule on the phase diagram and erasable only by homogenization (Lesson 6's \( \sqrt{Dt} \), with the arm spacing as the distance). At full-ingot scale, macrosegregation — centerline enrichment in continuously cast products — cannot be diffused away at any industrial timescale; it must be managed in the caster. Coring survives because solid-state diffusion is slow: the fingerprint outlives the process that made it.

04 · Reading a casting's history from its structure

A sectioned casting is a recording of its own solidification, and the working method is to read it zone by zone. The chill zone at the surface — fine random grains — says the mold quenched the first metal fast. The columnar zone — long grains aimed at the center — says heat left through the walls, and its aligned boundaries are weak paths that matter for load direction. The equiaxed center says nucleation won the race in the last, slowest-cooling liquid. Alongside the zones, read the defects as verbs: centerline shrinkage means feeding failed; segregation bands mean solute was pushed ahead of the fronts; gas porosity's round voids mean dissolved gas came out of solution.

Then run the arithmetic that controls all of it: Chvorinov's rule, \( t = B(V/A)^2 \), prices each region's solidification time by its modulus \( V/A \) — and the whole craft of risering is making the riser's modulus larger than the casting's, so the riser freezes LAST and feeds shrinkage instead of hosting it. The same square law says thin sections freeze fast and fine, heavy bosses freeze slow and coarse — so properties vary across one casting by design necessity, a fact the machine-design courses will inherit and respect.

Closing. Nucleation sets the grain count, growth writes the three zones, rejection writes the chemistry. A casting's cross-section is a recording — this lesson teaches you to play it back.

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Why engineers script

01 · Why engineers script

A spreadsheet hides its logic inside cells; a script lays it out as readable text. That single difference buys the three things engineering calculation needs most: repeatability — run the script tomorrow on new data and the identical logic executes; auditability — a reviewer reads the calculation top to bottom, every assumption visible; and scale — the loop that processes one reading processes a million without new effort. Spreadsheets remain excellent scratchpads, but the moment a calculation matters, recurs, or grows, the professional tool is a script — and the habit of writing one is what this course installs.

02 · The toolchain, plainly

Python is an interpreter: it reads statements and executes them immediately. You can feed it lines interactively — the REPL, ideal for trying an idea — or hand it a file of statements, a script, which is the durable product. A script is just text: openable in any editor, storable beside the data it processed, and — crucially — diffable, so yesterday's version and today's can be compared line by line. That property makes scripts the natural citizens of version control and the natural evidence in an engineering audit trail.

03 · Anatomy of a calculation script

Every calculation script worth keeping has the same skeleton: named inputs at the top, with units in the names or comments (\( \texttt{flow\_m3h = 100} \)); the computation in clear steps, mirroring how you would write it on paper; and output that states units — \( \texttt{print(f"shaft power = \{P/1000:.1f\} kW")} \). Arithmetic operators behave as expected, with three worth flagging now: \( \texttt{**} \) is exponentiation, \( \texttt{/} \) always returns a decimal, and \( \texttt{//} \) divides and discards the remainder. A pump-power script in this shape is eight lines — and it replaces the back-of-envelope forever, because it IS the envelope, kept.

04 · The scripting working method

Write scripts the way the mathematics course taught you to check units: as a discipline, not an afterthought. Name variables with their units — ambiguity between bar and kPa has sunk more calculations than any syntax error ever will. State inputs once, at the top, never buried mid-logic. Print results WITH units and sensible precision. Keep one calculation per script while learning; later lessons modularize. And before trusting any script, run it against a case you have verified by hand — the machine-verification habit this whole platform practices on you is the habit you are now learning to practice on your own code. A script that has never met a hand-checked case is an opinion with syntax.

Finally, keep scripts with their evidence. Store the script beside the data it processed and note the date and source of both — the calculation, its inputs, and its result then travel as one reviewable package. Months later, when a result is questioned, the answer is a rerun rather than an archaeology project; and when the data changes, the same package regenerates every downstream number identically. That closed loop — data, script, result, rerunnable — is what engineering computing means by an audit trail.

Lesson 02 · Variables, types, and expressions

01 · Types: what a value is

Every value in Python has a type, and the type decides what operations mean. Mechanics uses four constantly: int — whole numbers, exact; float — decimals, approximate by nature; str — text, including text that LOOKS like a number; and bool — True or False, the currency of decisions. The classic trap sits between str and float: data arrives from files and instruments as text, and "42.5" will not do arithmetic — "42.5" * 2 is not 85. Conversion is explicit: float("42.5") crosses the border, and crossing it consciously is the habit.

02 · Floats tell the truth approximately

A float is a binary approximation of a decimal number, and the approximation shows: \( 0.1 + 0.2 \) is not exactly \( 0.3 \) in a computer, and testing equality between computed floats fails unpredictably. The professional pattern is the tolerance test — \( \texttt{abs(a - b) < 1e-9} \) — precisely the pattern every quiz verifier on this platform uses. Roundoff also accumulates: sum a million small floats and the last digits drift. None of this is a defect to fix; it is a property to respect, the numerical equivalent of instrument uncertainty.

03 · Presenting numbers like an engineer

Computed values arrive with fifteen digits; engineering answers carry units and honest precision. The f-string is the tool: f"{p/1000:.2f} kW" renders 8175 W as \( \texttt{8.18 kW} \) — converted, rounded, and labeled in one expression. Precision in the format is a claim about accuracy, exactly as significant figures were in the physics course: print three decimals only if the calculation deserves them. The output line of a script is its public face; make it state value, precision, and unit together, every time.

04 · The type-discipline working method

Treat every value's type as consciously as its unit. At the borders of the script — reading files, taking input — convert text to numbers explicitly and immediately, so the interior works entirely in numeric types. Compare floats only with tolerances; reserve equality for ints and strings. Name booleans as assertions — is_running, above_alarm — so conditions read as sentences. And when a result looks absurd, check types before logic: half the beginner's "impossible" outputs are a string where a number belonged, silently concatenated instead of added. The interpreter always does exactly what the types tell it to; the working method is making sure that is also what you meant.

Keep the int/float division of labor deliberate as well. Counts, indices, and loop variables are integers by nature — a plant has 14 pumps, never 14.0 — while every measured quantity is a float carrying its approximation honestly. Mixing them is legal (Python promotes to float), but REPORTING a count as a decimal reads as sloppiness, and rounding a float into an index without deciding between truncation and rounding is how off-by-one errors are born. When precision genuinely matters — currency totals, long accumulations — know that specialized decimal types exist and that the ordinary float is a measuring instrument, not a ledger. For this course's engineering arithmetic, floats with tolerance comparisons and honest formatting carry every calculation; the discipline is knowing which type each value IS, the same way the physics course always knew each quantity's unit — a habit checked once per variable and repaid on every line after.

Lesson 03 · Control flow

01 · Decisions: if, elif, else

Programs earn their keep when behavior depends on data, and the instrument is the conditional. An if tests a boolean expression; elif chains further tests in order; else catches the remainder. Order matters and is a design decision: an alarm classifier tests the most severe threshold first, so a reading of 95 °C reports "trip" rather than the also-true "warning". Comparisons (\( \texttt{< <= == >= >} \)) and the boolean connectives (\( \texttt{and, or, not} \)) compose the tests; indentation — four spaces — is not decoration but syntax, defining exactly which lines each branch owns.

02 · Repetition: for and while

The for loop walks a known collection — every reading in a list, every line in a file, every integer in range(n) (which yields 0 through \( n-1 \)) — and is the workhorse of data processing: initialize an accumulator, add each item, report. The while loop repeats as long as a condition holds and serves the open-ended questions: keep compounding the filter's pressure drop until it crosses the alarm line, and report how many weeks that took. Choose by the question's shape — known collection, for; unknown horizon, while — and give every while a guaranteed exit, because a condition that never turns false runs forever.

03 · Guard clauses: refusing bad input early

Real calculations meet impossible inputs — negative flows, zero efficiencies, text where numbers belong — and the professional pattern rejects them at the door: if eta <= 0: raise ValueError("efficiency must be positive"). The guard clause converts a silent wrong answer into a loud, named failure at the exact line where the assumption broke. The mathematics course called this domain discipline — functions have domains, and \( \eta = 0 \) is not in this one; the guard writes the domain into the code.

04 · The control-flow working method

Translate procedures the way you would explain them aloud. Write the decision table first — conditions in severity order, outcomes named — then transcribe it as an if/elif/else chain and test EVERY branch once, including the else. For loops, name the accumulator for what it accumulates (total_kwh, not s), initialize it visibly, and hand-trace the first two iterations on paper before trusting a thousand — the two-minute trace that catches off-by-one and wrong-order errors while they are still cheap. Guard first, compute second, report third: scripts organized in that order read as procedures, fail loudly at their borders, and hold up when the data turns hostile, which production data always eventually does.

Two loop hygienes complete the method. Keep the loop body honest about what it touches — a loop that both accumulates a total AND mutates the list it walks is two jobs tangled, and tangled jobs breed the subtle bugs; split them. And when a while-loop's exit depends on a computed quantity, add a hard iteration cap as a guard — if weeks > 1000: raise RuntimeError(...) — so a modeling mistake produces a named failure in milliseconds instead of a silent forever. Loops are where scripts spend their lives; discipline there is discipline everywhere.

Lesson 04 · Functions

01 · Functions: calculations with names

A function packages a calculation behind a name and an interface: def shaft_power(q_m3s, head_m, eta=0.7): declares what goes in, return declares what comes out, and everything between is the calculation, written once and reused forever. The docstring — a quoted paragraph directly under the def — states what the function does AND its units, which turns the function into a self-documenting engineering tool: help(shaft_power) answers the reviewer before the reviewer asks. Default arguments (the eta=0.7) encode the usual case while keeping the unusual case one keyword away.

02 · Return, not print

A function that PRINTS its result has spent it; a function that RETURNS it hands the value to the caller for further use — feeding the next calculation, a comparison, a plot, a test. Printing is presentation, the script's outer layer; returning is plumbing, the composable interior. The distinction becomes visible the first time you try to use a printed result in arithmetic and find you hold None. Compose functions the way the chain rule composed rates: small functions, each doing one nameable thing, wired by their returns — \( \texttt{gap(c\_to\_k(90), c\_to\_k(30))} \) reads like the sentence it implements.

03 · Scope: what happens in a function stays there

Variables born inside a function are local: invisible outside, gone when the call ends. This is a feature — ten functions can each use x without collision — and a discipline: a function should receive what it needs as arguments and deliver what it produces as returns, never reaching sideways into the script's globals. Functions written that way are testable in isolation, which is exactly how the next lesson will test them.

04 · The function-design working method

Extract a function whenever a calculation is used twice, or once but nameable. Name it by what it returns (hydraulic_power_w), carry units in parameter names, and write the docstring FIRST — if you cannot state the contract in a sentence, the function is not yet one thing. Keep functions small enough to hand-verify: one formula, one decision, one transformation each. Then apply Lesson 1's rule at the function scale: every function meets a hand-checked case before it is trusted, and the check lives beside the function as next lesson's assertion. A personal library of such functions — built, checked, and reused across this course — is the deliverable this working method is quietly assembling.

Resist the two classic function pathologies while the habits are forming. The do-everything function — read the file, convert the units, compute the power, print the report — cannot be tested except by running the world; split it at every nameable seam and each piece becomes checkable alone. And the copy-paste twin — the same formula pasted in three places with one variable renamed — guarantees that a future correction reaches two of the three; extraction into one function makes the fix land everywhere by construction. Functions are the unit of reuse, of testing, and of trust: size them so all three work.

Lesson 05 · Testing and debugging engineering code

01 · Why engineers test code

This platform machine-verifies every numeric answer before publishing it; this lesson hands you the same discipline for your own scripts. A test is a claim made executable: run the function on a case whose answer you know, and let the computer confirm agreement — today, tomorrow, and after every future edit. Untested code decays silently: an innocent change to one line shifts a result three functions away, and without tests nobody notices until the wrong number is in a report. With tests, the same mistake fails loudly in seconds, at your desk, before it costs anything.

02 · The assertion: a claim with teeth

The core tool is one statement: assert condition, "message" — silent when the condition holds, a named failure when it breaks. For engineering functions the pattern is the tolerance form from Lesson 2: assert abs(area_m2(0.1) - 0.007854) < 1e-6 — the hand-checked case (a 100 mm circle's \( \pi d^2/4 \)) pinned beside the function it certifies. Three or four assertions per function — a typical case, an edge (zero, the domain boundary), and a case exercising each branch — form a small permanent test suite that reruns in milliseconds. The habit converts "I checked it once when I wrote it" into "it is checked on every run", which is the difference between a memory and a guarantee.

03 · Exceptions and honest failure

Guard clauses (Lesson 3) raise exceptions at broken assumptions; testing meets them from the other side. A function fed garbage should fail LOUDLY — ValueError with a message naming the violated assumption — never return a plausible wrong number. Debugging itself is hypothesis testing, the scientific method at a keyboard: reproduce the failure with the smallest possible input, form a hypothesis about the earliest wrong value, place a print or assertion just before the suspect line, and bisect until the bug has nowhere to hide. Staring is not a method; bisection is.

04 · The verification working method

Adopt the platform's own pipeline at personal scale. Write the function; write its hand-checked case as an assertion immediately — while the paper calculation is still on the desk; add the edge-case and branch assertions; keep the whole block at the bottom of the file or in a test script, and run it after EVERY change. When a bug surfaces in use, its fix begins by writing the failing case as a new assertion — so the same bug can never return unannounced. And respect the honest limit: tests prove the presence of agreement with your checked cases, not the absence of all error — which is precisely why the cases are chosen with engineering judgment: the typical, the extreme, and the branch nobody believes will ever run. That judgment is the engineering; the assertions merely keep it enforced. A dozen well-chosen assertions guarding a personal library of checked functions is a small thing to maintain and a large thing to own: it is the difference between code you wrote and code you can vouch for.

Lesson 06 · Lists, dictionaries, and files

01 · Lists: ordered data with a name

Engineering data arrives in sequences — readings over a route, temperatures over a shift — and the list is Python's sequence: readings = [2.1, 4.5, 3.2], ordered, indexable, growable. Indexing starts at ZERO — readings[0] is the first item — and negative indices count from the end, readings[-1] being the latest reading, an idiom worth owning immediately. Slices carve subranges (readings[1:3] yields items 1 and 2 — the stop index is excluded, mirroring range), append grows the list in place, and the built-ins len, sum, min, max answer the first questions any dataset is asked. The list comprehension compresses the transform loop to a phrase — [x * 0.746 for x in hp_values] — and reads like the sentence it implements.

02 · Dictionaries: records with named fields

Where lists order items, dictionaries name them: pump = {"tag": "P-101", "power_kw": 11.5} is an equipment record whose fields are looked up by key — pump["power_kw"] — not by position. Adding a field is one assignment; asking whether one exists is "tag" in pump. The two structures compose into the universal data shape of plant work: a LIST of DICTIONARIES — one dict per equipment item, one list per fleet — which is exactly what a CSV file of records becomes the moment Python reads it.

03 · Files: where real data lives

The CSV — comma-separated values, one record per line — is the lingua franca of every historian export, test-bench log, and maintenance system dump. Reading one is three idioms stacked: iterate the file line by line, split(",") each line into fields, and convert numeric fields with float() at the border, exactly as Lesson 2 demanded. The with open(...) as f: form opens the file and guarantees it closes. Everything after the read is Lessons 3–4: loops, accumulators, and functions, now fed by real data instead of typed constants.

04 · The data-structure working method

Choose the structure by the question the data answers. Ordered measurements → list; named fields of one thing → dict; a fleet of records → list of dicts; and when you catch yourself tracking parallel lists (tags in one, powers in another), that is the signal the records want to be dicts. Convert types at the file border, never downstream. Trust the built-ins before writing loops — sum, max, len are tested code you did not have to write. And hand-trace list operations on a three-item example before applying them to thirty thousand rows, exactly as the loop lesson traced its accumulators: the small case proves the pattern, the large case merely repeats it.

One caution as datasets grow: a structure choice made casually at three records becomes architecture at thirty thousand. The list-of-dicts shape scales gracefully because every operation this course teaches — loops, comprehensions, filters, summaries — walks it unchanged; ad-hoc shapes (nested lists of mixed meaning, parallel lists) accumulate special cases until the script is mostly exceptions. Choose the boring, regular shape early and the analysis lessons ahead inherit clean footing.

Lesson 07 · Arrays and numerical work

01 · The array idea: operate on all of it

Lists process element by element through loops; numerical arrays (NumPy's ndarray) operate on whole datasets at once: kw = hp * 0.746 converts every horsepower value in one expression — vectorization. The gains are two: the code READS as the mathematics (one formula, visibly applied to everything), and it runs orders of magnitude faster, because the looping happens in optimized machinery below. For engineering data — a year of hourly readings is 8,760 numbers, a vibration waveform 65,536 — the array is the natural container, and the loop becomes the exception rather than the habit.

02 · The reductions: from many numbers to a few

Analysis usually ends in reductions: mean, std, min/max, sum — the descriptive statistics of a dataset in one call each. The standard deviation deserves engineering respect: it is the honest width of the scatter, the number that turns "about 4.25" into "4.25 ± 0.30", and the seed of every control chart the metrology course will draw. Alongside them, argmax answers WHERE — the index of the worst reading — which is often the more actionable question: not "how bad" but "which machine".

03 · Masks: asking questions of data

Comparison on an array yields an array of booleans — a mask — and the mask selects: readings[readings > 4.0] is every reading above the alarm line, in one expression: no loop, no if, no accumulator. Count them with sum of the mask, average them, or feed them onward. The idiom — condition, mask, selection — is the data-analysis sentence structure, and it compounds: masks combine with \( \texttt{\&} \) and \( \texttt{|} \) into compound questions ("above alarm AND after the overhaul") that still read as one line.

04 · The vectorized working method

Reach for the array whenever data is homogeneous and numeric; keep lists for mixed records. Write transformations as whole-array expressions and CHECK them on a three-value array first — vectorized code is compact enough that a wrong formula corrupts everything uniformly, so the small trace matters doubly. Prefer the built-in reductions to hand-rolled loops (they are tested code, and faster), and when a reduction's answer surprises, look at the mask counts before doubting the data: how many values, how many above threshold, how many NaN — the three questions that catch most data-quality issues at the door. Then the discipline that spans every lesson: units ride the variable names (vel_mm_s), conversions happen once, visibly, at the border, and any result consumed downstream gets an assertion pinning a known case. Arrays change the scale of what one line does; they change nothing about what makes the line trustworthy.

The payoff arrives immediately in the lessons ahead: plotting consumes arrays whole, curve fitting reduces them to parameters, and simulations generate them by the thousand. Vectorized thinking — state the operation once, apply it to everything, summarize with a reduction — is also precisely how plant historians and analysis platforms work internally, so fluency here reads directly onto the tools the later automation courses will operate.

Lesson 08 · Plotting engineering data

01 · The plot is an argument

An engineering plot exists to support a decision, and its anatomy is the argument's grammar: axes labeled WITH UNITS (an unlabeled axis is a number with no meaning — the chart version of the unit sins every course here prosecutes), a title that states what is shown, and a legend when more than one series appears. The choice of plot type is the choice of claim: a LINE plot claims continuity — readings ordered in time, trends; a SCATTER plot claims only "these pairs were observed" — right for correlation questions and unordered data; a BAR chart compares discrete categories — machines, months, failure modes. Choosing a line where a scatter belongs invents continuity the data never promised; the plot type is the first honesty decision.

02 · Scales that reveal

Linear axes hide what logarithmic axes reveal. Exponential decay — the cooling curves and filter-loading trends of the mathematics course — plots as a curve on linear axes but as a STRAIGHT LINE on a semilog (log-y) plot, whose slope reads the time constant: plotting \( \ln(T - T_\infty) \) against t was exactly this move, done by hand. Data spanning decades of magnitude — vibration spectra, particle counts — needs log scales simply to be seen: on a linear axis, the small values flatten into the floor. The working question before every plot: does this data live on one scale, or several?

03 · Sampling honesty

Plotted data was SAMPLED, and sampling has physics. A signal changing faster than half the sampling rate does not disappear — it ALIASES, reappearing as a false slow signal: a 50 Hz vibration sampled at 60 Hz shows up as a phantom 10 Hz wobble (\( |50 - 60| \)). The instrumentation course will formalize Nyquist; the plotting lesson's version is the caution: before trusting a surprising slow oscillation in sampled data, ask what the sampling rate was. Equally, decimating data for plotting speed (every tenth point) is legitimate — STATED; silently, it is how spikes vanish from trend reviews.

04 · The plotting working method

Build every plot in the same order. State the question the plot must answer; choose the type by the claim (trend → line, correlation → scatter, comparison → bar); choose scales by the data's structure (exponential suspicion → semilog; decades of range → log); label axes with quantities AND units; then read the plot yourself before showing anyone — does it actually answer the question, and would a skeptic's eye find the axis tricks (truncated baselines, cherry-picked windows) that make weak cases look strong? The mathematics course called the plot the test of first-order behavior; this lesson generalizes: the plot is where data confesses its structure, provided the plotter asks honestly. A figure that survives your own skepticism is ready for the morning meeting — and a figure that decided a question honestly is worth saving beside the script and data that produced it, completing the audit trail this course keeps building.

Lesson 09 · Curve fitting and interpolation

01 · Two questions a table cannot answer alone

Engineering data arrives as points: rows of a steam table, samples on a pump test, readings from a calibration bench. The questions arrive between the points — the head at a flow the test never held, the transmitter output at a pressure between bench settings. Two distinct operations close that gap. Interpolation reads between the rows of data you trust, adding no opinion of its own. Fitting proposes a model — a line, a low-order polynomial — and asks the data to vote on its parameters. Keeping the two distinct is the first discipline of this lesson: interpolation honors every point; fitting deliberately smooths over their scatter, and each is wrong where the other is right.

02 · Interpolation: reading between the rows

Between two table rows \( (x_1, y_1) \) and \( (x_2, y_2) \), linear interpolation draws the chord: \( y = y_1 + \frac{x - x_1}{x_2 - x_1}\,(y_2 - y_1) \). The fraction is just "how far across the interval x sits", and the same fraction is applied to the y-interval. Property tables are published dense precisely so that this chord is an excellent stand-in for the true curve; np.interp(x, xs, ys) does the row hunting and the arithmetic in one call, provided the sample points xs increase monotonically. The method's honest boundary is the table's edge: extrapolation — reading beyond the last row — is a different act entirely, because past the data the curve owes you nothing. Worse, np.interp does not protest at the edge: it silently returns the endpoint value, so the guard clause of Lesson 3 — assert the query lies inside the table's range — is part of every lookup that matters.

03 · Least squares: the best line through scatter

Calibration data never lies on a line; it scatters around one. For each candidate line, every point carries a residual — measured minus predicted, \( r_i = y_i - (m x_i + b) \). Least squares chooses the \( m, b \) that minimize \( \sum r_i^2 \), and for a straight line the answer is closed-form: \( m = \frac{n\sum x y - \sum x \sum y}{n \sum x^2 - (\sum x)^2} \), with \( b = \bar{y} - m\bar{x} \). np.polyfit(x, y, 1) evaluates exactly this. The degree argument is a temptation: a degree-9 polynomial threads all ten of your points exactly — residuals zero, \( R^2 = 1 \) — and swings wildly everywhere between them. Adding parameters always improves the numbers and, past the physics, always degrades the model; the fit stops summarizing the data and starts memorizing its noise.

04 · The fitting working method

The routine that keeps fits honest: PLOT the data before fitting anything — the eye catches curvature, outliers, and regime changes that summary numbers hide. Fit the simplest model the physics justifies, then plot the RESIDUALS: random scatter of roughly constant width says the model has taken all the structure the data offers; any pattern — a bow, a trend, a fan — is physics the model missed, an instruction to think, not to raise the degree. Distrust \( R^2 \) as a verdict: it measures variance explained, and a badly bowed fit can still post 0.99; the residual plot, not the score, is the honesty check. Report parameters with their units and alongside the data's domain, because the fit is a summary of THAT range — using it outside is extrapolation wearing a calculator's confidence. And when the goal is only to read between trusted rows, skip the model entirely: interpolate. The habits are small, but they are the difference between a calibration you can defend and a curve that merely looks professional. Both return in force when Metrology fits gauge data and Reliability fits life data — this lesson is their rehearsal.

Lesson 10 · Small simulations

01 · The equations engineers actually meet

Strip the plant to its rates and first-order differential equations appear everywhere: a tank fills at the rate flow-in minus flow-out; a motor housing cools at a rate proportional to how far it sits above ambient; receiver pressure decays as the leak bleeds it down. Each says \( dy/dt = f(y) \): the state's rate of change depends on the state itself. A few such equations have textbook solutions; most industrial variants — level-dependent outflow, staged inputs, valves that open mid-run — do not. Simulation sidesteps the impasse: instead of solving the equation, STEP it. March time forward in small increments, updating the state by its current rate, and the trajectory emerges numerically. The computer becomes a cheap laboratory — one where a tank can overflow a hundred times before breakfast and nothing needs mopping.

02 · Euler's method: the honest workhorse

The simplest march is Euler's: \( y_{n+1} = y_n + \Delta t \cdot f(y_n) \) — new state equals old state plus rate times step. It is nothing more than "keep the rate constant for a little while", the same approximation a hand calculation makes, automated. Its price is a per-step error that accumulates in proportion to \( \Delta t \): halve the step, roughly halve the error at a given time. That buys a wonderfully practical convergence test needing no theory at all — run the simulation, halve \( \Delta t \), run again; if the answer you report did not change at the precision you report it, the step is small enough. If it moved, halve again. The experiment IS the error analysis, and it costs seconds.

03 · When the step lies: stability

Take cooling toward ambient, \( dT/dt = -k\,(T - T_\infty) \). One Euler step multiplies the deviation from ambient by the factor \( (1 - k\,\Delta t) \). For small steps that factor sits just under 1 and the temperature relaxes exponentially, as physics says. But let \( k\,\Delta t \) exceed 2 and the factor drops below −1: each step OVERSHOOTS ambient farther than it started, flipping sign and growing — the simulated temperature oscillates with expanding amplitude while the real housing simply cools. The boundary \( \Delta t < 2/k \) is the stability limit, and it teaches the lesson's sharpest distinction: instability is not sloppiness, it is a qualitatively false solution. Between stable and accurate stands the time constant \( \tau = 1/k \) — the system's natural clock; steps around \( \tau/10 \) resolve the physics, steps near the limit merely avoid explosion.

04 · The simulation working method

Write the rate as a named function f(y) — Lesson 4's contract — so the model sits in one testable place and the marching loop never changes. Anchor the code before trusting it: the plain cooling case has an exact solution, so assert your loop reproduces it; the tank has a steady state you can compute by setting the rates equal, so run long and assert the level settles there. Only then vary toward the case with no formula — the varying inflow, the staged setpoint — because a loop that reproduces the known case has EARNED extrapolation to the unknown one. Run the halve-the-step experiment as routine, plot whole trajectories rather than trusting endpoints (Lesson 8: the plot is the argument), and keep the model's assumptions written beside the code. That last habit is the deepest: a converged, stable simulation is faithful to its EQUATIONS, and if the equations omit real physics — the heat the sun adds, the second leak — the laboratory lies with perfect numerical composure. Step-size error you can halve away; model error you can only think away. The distinction returns at full scale in Heat Transfer, System Dynamics, and every digital-twin conversation of the later automation courses.

Lesson 11 · Project: a maintenance data mini-analysis

01 · One project, the whole course

The course closes the way real analysis begins: a work-order export lands — one CSV row per maintenance job, with machine tag, failure date, hours down, hours to repair, a cause code — and someone asks what it says. The pipeline that answers is this lesson: load the raw file, clean it defensively, compute a handful of KPIs, plot the story, and write four honest sentences on top. Every prior lesson reports for duty: files and dictionaries carry the records in, arrays vectorize the arithmetic, assertions pin the steps, plots make the argument. The deliverable is not a number — it is a SCRIPT that turns the raw file into the numbers, rerunnable by anyone, next month, unchanged.

02 · Cleaning: where the analysis is won or lost

Real exports are dirty in boringly predictable ways: duplicated rows from double entry, a duration keyed in minutes into an hours column, negative times from swapped dates, blank cause codes. Cleaning is not clerical — every fix is an engineering decision, and the discipline is to make each one explicit in code: drop exact duplicates and COUNT what was dropped; range-check every numeric column and quarantine violators rather than silently deleting them; log rows in versus rows out. The payoff is quantitative: one mis-keyed duration can multiply a mean by ten while the median barely moves — which is why the robust statistics of Lesson 7 are cleaning's instruments, and why "how many rows survived, and why" is the first line of any defensible report.

03 · KPIs with honest denominators

Three ratios summarize a machine's month. MTBF — uptime divided by failure count — how long it runs between stoppages. MTTR — downtime divided by failure count — how long a stoppage lasts. Availability — uptime over total period — the fraction of the calendar the machine was there when needed. Each is one line of arithmetic and one buried decision: the denominator. Calendar hours or scheduled hours? Do planned overhauls count as downtime? Neither answer is wrong; the UNSTATED answer is. The other closing instrument is the Pareto view: rank downtime by cause and the top two or three causes typically own most of the loss — the ranking that converts a data file into next month's priorities. A KPI without its denominator stated, or a ranking without its period, is decoration.

04 · The reproducibility working method

The habits that make the analysis an instrument rather than a performance: the raw file is READ-ONLY — the script loads it, transforms in memory, writes results to new files, and never edits the source, because the untouched original plus the script IS the audit trail; rerunning must reproduce every figure exactly. One script runs raw to figures with no manual steps in between — no hand-pasted cells, no "then I fixed one value in the editor", since a single silent hand-edit breaks the chain that lets a reviewer trust the output. Assertions stand guard at the borders: row counts after each cleaning stage, ranges on every computed KPI. Figures carry their period and filters in the caption. And the four sentences on top state what was computed, over what denominator, with what excluded — the analyst's equivalent of showing units. This pattern — raw, script, assertions, figures, statement — returns at full strength in Reliability Engineering, where the same work-order data feeds Weibull fits and failure forecasting. This project is the rehearsal, and the habits are the deliverable.
