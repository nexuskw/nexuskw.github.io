Forces, vectors, and resultants

Force as a vector; components and resultants in 2-D and 3-D. Units and the weight–mass distinction that still catches graduates. Resolving cable and strut forces.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why is weight not mass, and where does the confusion bite in bolt-load calculations?
  • Resolve a 10 kN cable force at 30° to horizontal into components.

01 · Force is a vector, and the arrow is the contract

A force has magnitude, direction, and a point of application — drop any of the three and the analysis is fiction. In 2-D we work in components: a force \( F \) at angle \( \theta \) above the horizontal carries \( F_x = F\cos\theta \) and \( F_y = F\sin\theta \). The reverse trip is just as routine:

\[ R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}, \qquad \theta = \tan^{-1}\!\frac{R_y}{R_x} \tag{1} \]

Resultants are found by summing components, never by adding magnitudes — two 10 kN forces can combine to anything from 0 to 20 kN depending on their directions.

02 · Weight is not mass

Mass is the amount of matter, in kilograms; weight is the gravitational force on that mass, \( W = mg \) with \( g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), in newtons. A 250 kg motor does not load its sling with "250 kg" — it loads it with \( 250 \times 9.81 = 2452.5\ \mathrm{N} \approx 2.45\ \mathrm{kN} \). Bolt-load calculations, sling charts, and pressure-area checks all fail the same way when someone feeds a mass where a force belongs. Carry units through every line; they are the cheapest error detector you will ever own.

03 · Cables and struts resolve the same way

A taut cable pulls along its own axis; a straight two-force strut pushes or pulls along its axis. Both therefore hand you a force whose direction is known from geometry — only the magnitude is unknown. Resolving such members into components is the mechanical habit this whole course is built on, and the Worked Examples tab drills it on a crane cable and a motor lift.

04 · The component working method

Every force problem in this course, and most in the courses after it, runs through one mechanical procedure — run it the same way every time and errors lose their hiding places. Choose axes deliberately: horizontal-vertical by default, but rotate the axes onto an incline the moment most forces align with it — the algebra shrinks. Build a sign table: before any arithmetic, write each force's components with signs from the geometry, in a small table, so every \( \cos \) and \( \sin \) is committed once and visibly. Sum, then recompose: add the columns for \( \Sigma F_x \) and \( \Sigma F_y \), recompose into magnitude and angle — and state the quadrant from the signs of the sums, not from the calculator's arctangent, which cheerfully reports the wrong quadrant half the time.

Then run the two closing audits. Recomposition: resolve your resultant back into components; if they don't reproduce the sums, a sign slipped. Magnitude sense: the resultant can never exceed the sum of the magnitudes and can never be less than the largest minus the rest — an answer outside that band is wrong before any rechecking. Ten disciplined seconds close out a page of arithmetic.

Closing. Components in, resultant out, units carried, weight computed from mass — four small disciplines that remove most of the errors this course will otherwise spend weeks catching.

Examples and Quiz

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Resolving a cable force

Problem. A crane cable applies \( F = 10\ \mathrm{kN} \) to a padeye, at \( 30^\circ \) above the horizontal. Find: the horizontal and vertical components.

Solution. (1) \( F_x = 10\cos 30^\circ = 8.66\ \mathrm{kN} \). (2) \( F_y = 10\sin 30^\circ = 5.00\ \mathrm{kN} \). (3) Check by recomposition: \( \sqrt{8.66^2 + 5.00^2} = 10.0\ \mathrm{kN} \) — the original magnitude returns.

Answer: \( F_x = 8.66\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( F_y = 5.00\ \mathrm{kN} \). Sanity check: at \( 30^\circ \) the force leans closer to horizontal than vertical, so \( F_x > F_y \) — as computed.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Weight, then sling tension

Problem. A 250 kg motor hangs from two identical sling legs, each at \( 60^\circ \) above the horizontal. Find: the weight and the tension per leg.

Solution. (1) Weight: \( W = mg = 250 \times 9.81 = 2452.5\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) Vertical equilibrium, two legs sharing symmetrically: \( 2T\sin 60^\circ = W \). (3) \( T = 2452.5 / (2 \times 0.866) = 1416\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: \( W \approx 2.45\ \mathrm{kN} \); each leg carries \( \approx 1.42\ \mathrm{kN} \). Sanity check: each leg carries more than \( W/2 = 1226\ \mathrm{N} \) because only the vertical component of the leg tension supports load — flattening the legs toward horizontal would drive \( T \) higher still, which is why rigging charts penalize shallow sling angles.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Three forces, one resultant

Problem. Three concurrent forces act on a padeye: 300 N along +x, 200 N along +y, and 150 N at 210° (measured CCW from +x). Find: the magnitude and direction of the resultant.

Solution. (1) Components: \( \Sigma F_x = 300 + 0 + 150\cos 210° = 300 - 129.9 = 170.1\ \mathrm{N} \); \( \Sigma F_y = 0 + 200 + 150\sin 210° = 200 - 75 = 125\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) Magnitude: \( R = \sqrt{170.1^2 + 125^2} = 211\ \mathrm{N} \). (3) Direction: both sums positive → first quadrant; \( \theta = \tan^{-1}(125/170.1) = 36.3° \) above +x.

Answer: \( R \approx 211\ \mathrm{N} \) at 36.3°. Sanity check: the magnitude band: largest force alone is 300 N and the sum of magnitudes is 650 N — but the 150 N force opposes the other two, so a resultant below 300 N is expected; recomposing 211 N at 36.3° returns (170.1, 125.0) exactly.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A 20 kN cable force acts at \( 60^\circ \) above the horizontal. What is its vertical component?

\( F_y = F\sin\theta = 20\sin 60^\circ = 17.3\ \mathrm{kN} \). Choosing 10.0 kN is the classic swap — that is \( 20\cos 60^\circ \), the horizontal component. The component along an axis uses the sine of the angle measured from the other axis; sketch the triangle before reaching for a formula.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Forces of 6 kN and 8 kN act at a point. Which resultant magnitude is impossible?

Two forces bound their resultant between their difference and their sum: \( |8-6| = 2 \) kN (opposing) up to \( 8+6 = 14 \) kN (aligned). 10 kN sits inside the range (it is the perpendicular case, \( \sqrt{6^2+8^2} \)). 16 kN exceeds the sum — no orientation can produce it.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A 100 N force acts at 60° above the horizontal. What are its horizontal and vertical components?

\( F_x = 100\cos 60° = 50.0\ \mathrm{N} \), \( F_y = 100\sin 60° = 86.6\ \mathrm{N} \). The swapped pair is the classic cosine–sine mix-up — at angles above 45° the vertical component must be the larger, which settles it without a calculator. The other options confuse the magnitude and the angle with components, which have force units both.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A crane load cell reads 500 (calibrated in kg). What force does the load apply to the hook?

\( W = mg = 500 \times 9.81 = 4905\ \mathrm{N} \approx 4.9\ \mathrm{kN} \). Kilograms measure mass; the hook feels newtons. Writing \"500 N\" understates the load nearly tenfold — the exact unit slip Lesson 1's vignette catches in a lift plan.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Two 100 N forces act on a bracket at right angles to each other. The resultant's magnitude is:

Perpendicular forces add vectorially: \( R = \sqrt{100^2 + 100^2} = 141\ \mathrm{N} \). Answering 200 N adds magnitudes algebraically — only legal when forces are collinear and aligned; 0 N would require them opposed. The resultant of two forces always lies between their difference and their sum: here between 0 and 200, and 141 sits exactly where the right angle puts it.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the scale reads kilograms, the sling feels newtons. You arrive at a scrap bay where the crane's load cell shows 4,800 on its display and a new engineer writes "4,800 N" into a lift plan for an electric-arc-furnace charge at a steel mill. The display is calibrated in kilograms; the sling load is \( 4800 \times 9.81 \approx 47.1\ \mathrm{kN} \) (figures representative). One unit slip understates the rigging load by a factor of ten. The fix costs one line — \( W = mg \), units written beside every number — which is precisely Lesson 1 doing its job on a real lift.
