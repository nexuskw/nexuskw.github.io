STA 103Year 1 · Semester 1

Engineering Statics

Equilibrium as a habit of mind: forces, moments, free-body diagrams, trusses and frames, friction, and centroids — the grammar under every strength-of-materials and machine-design calculation that follows.

11 lessons · 0 in the Core 60 · Primary texts:

  • MIT OCW 2.001 Mechanics & Materials I (ocw.mit.edu)
01

Forces, vectors, and resultants

Force as a vector; components and resultants in 2-D and 3-D. Units and the weight–mass distinction that still catches graduates. Resolving cable and strut forces.

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001

02

Moments and couples

Moment of a force about a point and an axis. Couples as pure turning effect. Varignon's theorem used to simplify real load cases.

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001

03

The free-body diagram

The single most important skill in this course: isolate, replace, label. Support types and their reactions. Every equilibrium error traces back to a bad FBD.

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001

04

Equilibrium of a particle

Concurrent force systems in 2-D and 3-D. Cable-and-pulley systems and spring balances. Solving cleanly with components versus geometry.

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001

05

Equilibrium of rigid bodies

The full ΣF = 0, ΣM = 0 toolkit. Two- and three-force members as shortcuts. Statically determinate versus indeterminate — and why the distinction matters later.

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001

06

Trusses

Method of joints and method of sections. Zero-force members by inspection. Reading a pipe-rack or roof truss as a load path, not a picture.

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001

07

Frames and machines

Multi-member structures with internal pins. Dismembering frames correctly — action and reaction at every pin. Mechanical advantage of linkages and clamps.

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001

08

Internal forces: shear and bending moment

Cutting a beam and exposing N, V, M. Shear and moment diagrams for point and distributed loads. The diagrams as the input strength-of-materials will demand.

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001

09

Friction

Dry friction, impending motion, and the friction cone. Wedges, screws, and belt friction — the capstan equation derived. Why belt tension ratios govern conveyor drives.

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001

10

Centroids and centers of gravity

Centroids by integration and by composite parts. Center of gravity of built-up sections and equipment skids. Lifting-plan relevance: where the hook must sit.

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001

11

Moments of inertia

Second moment of area by integration and parallel-axis theorem. Composite sections used in structural tables. The property that will set bending stress in Strength of Materials.

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.001
