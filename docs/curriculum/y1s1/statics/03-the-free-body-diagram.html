The free-body diagram

The single most important skill in this course: isolate, replace, label. Support types and their reactions. Every equilibrium error traces back to a bad FBD.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Name the three steps of a free-body diagram and the most common omission.
  • What reactions does a fixed support supply that a pin does not?

01 · Isolate, replace, label

A free-body diagram is built in three deliberate moves. Isolate: draw the body alone, severed from every support and contact. Replace: at each cut, draw the force or moment that the removed part exerted on the body. Label: every force gets a symbol, a direction, and — for knowns — a value with units. The most common omission is the body's own weight, acting at its center of gravity; the second most common is a forgotten contact force at a surface the sketch made look innocent.

02 · Supports are force vocabularies

Each support type supplies a fixed menu of reactions. A roller or smooth surface: one force, normal to the rolling surface. A pin: two force components, unknown in direction until solved. A fixed (built-in) support: two force components plus a moment — that resisting moment is what a pin cannot supply, and it is why a flagpole stays up while a pinned pole would fall. A taut cable: one pull, along the cable, never a push.

03 · Every equilibrium error is an FBD error

When a statics answer comes out wrong, the algebra is rarely the culprit; the diagram is. A force drawn at the wrong point, a reaction assumed in a direction the support cannot supply, an internal force shown on a body that was never cut — each corrupts the equations before they are written. The professional habit is to treat the FBD as a deliverable in itself: a diagram a colleague could take and write the equations from without asking you anything. the Worked Examples tab applies the discipline to a simply supported beam and a cantilever.

04 · The FBD checklist

The free-body diagram is a procedure, not an artwork. Run the checklist in order. One — isolate: redraw ONLY the chosen body, stripped of everything it touches. Two — replace every contact: each support, cable, surface, and neighbor becomes the force set it can transmit — a cable becomes one pull along its line, a smooth surface one normal push, a pin two components. Three — add the field forces: weight at the center of gravity, always. Four — name every unknown with a symbol and an assumed direction; a wrong assumption costs nothing — the algebra answers with a minus sign. Five — count: unknowns versus available equations, before solving; a mismatch caught here saves a page of doomed algebra.

Two disciplines keep diagrams honest. Forces the body exerts on other things never appear — an FBD shows only what acts on the body, and mixing the action–reaction pair onto one diagram is the classic corruption. And when a problem involves several connected bodies, draw a separate FBD per body plus, where helpful, one of the whole assembly — the pair of views cross-checks itself, because the internal forces must cancel in the assembly view.

Closing. Isolate, replace, label; know each support's force menu; never write \( \Sigma F = 0 \) until the picture is finished. The FBD is not preparation for the analysis — it is the analysis.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Beam on a pin and a roller

Problem. A 4 m beam is pinned at A and rests on a roller at B (right end). A 2 kN load acts 1.5 m from A. Neglect beam weight. Find: the reactions.

Solution. (1) FBD: pin at A gives \( A_x, A_y \); roller at B gives vertical \( R_B \); load 2 kN down at 1.5 m. (2) \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \Rightarrow A_x = 0 \) (no horizontal loads). (3) \( \Sigma M_A = 0 \): \( R_B \times 4 - 2 \times 1.5 = 0 \Rightarrow R_B = 0.75\ \mathrm{kN} \). (4) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \): \( A_y = 2 - 0.75 = 1.25\ \mathrm{kN} \).

Answer: \( A_y = 1.25\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( R_B = 0.75\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( A_x = 0 \). Sanity check: the load sits nearer A, so A carries more — 1.25 > 0.75, and the two sum to 2 kN. ✓

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Cantilever: the moment reaction earns its keep

Problem. A bracket is built into a wall (fixed support) and carries 3 kN downward at 2 m from the wall. Find: the wall reactions.

Solution. (1) FBD: at the wall draw \( V \) (vertical force), \( H \) (horizontal force), and moment \( M_w \). (2) \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \Rightarrow H = 0 \). (3) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \Rightarrow V = 3\ \mathrm{kN} \) upward. (4) \( \Sigma M_{wall} = 0 \Rightarrow M_w = 3 \times 2 = 6\ \mathrm{kN\cdot m} \), opposing the load's turning.

Answer: \( V = 3\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( M_w = 6\ \mathrm{kN\cdot m} \). Sanity check: replace the fixed support with a pin: no \( M_w \) is available, nothing balances the 6 kN·m, and the bracket rotates — the moment reaction is not optional bookkeeping, it is what "built-in" means.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Free body on an incline

Problem. A 50 kg crate rests on a smooth 25° incline, held by a rope parallel to the slope. Draw the free-body diagram and find the rope tension and the normal force. \( g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \).

Solution. (1) FBD: weight \( W = 50 \times 9.81 = 490.5\ \mathrm{N} \) vertically down; normal \( N \) perpendicular to the slope; tension \( T \) up the slope. Axes along and normal to the incline. (2) Along the slope: \( T = W\sin 25° = 490.5 \times 0.4226 = 207\ \mathrm{N} \). (3) Normal to it: \( N = W\cos 25° = 490.5 \times 0.9063 = 445\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: \( T \approx 207\ \mathrm{N} \), \( N \approx 445\ \mathrm{N} \). Sanity check: \( \sqrt{207^2 + 445^2} = 490.8 \approx W \) — the two components reassemble the weight, as the rotated axes demand; and a steeper slope would trade N for T, exactly as intuition says.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

Which support supplies a moment reaction?

Only the fixed support resists rotation, adding a moment to its two force components. A pin gives two force components but spins freely; a roller gives one normal force; a cable one axial pull. This menu is why a flagpole (fixed) stands while the same pole on a pin would fall.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

The most commonly omitted force on a free-body diagram is:

Self-weight, acting at the center of gravity, is invisible on the sketch and therefore chronically forgotten — the first item to check on any suspect FBD. Applied loads and support reactions are at least prompted by the drawing; gravity prompts nothing.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

Which force does NOT belong on the free-body diagram of a crate resting on the floor?

An FBD shows only forces acting on the chosen body. The crate-on-floor force acts on the floor — it is the Newton's-third-law partner of the normal force, and the pair never appears on one diagram together. Including it is the most common FBD corruption, and it silently doubles the contact force in the equations.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A taut cable attached to a body can exert on it:

Cables carry tension only — they pull along their own axis and buckle rather than push. Drawing a cable force as a push (or at an angle to the cable) is an FBD error that no amount of correct algebra downstream can repair.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

A planar body is supported by one pin and one roller. How many unknown reaction components appear on its FBD?

The pin contributes two components; the roller one, normal to its surface: three unknowns — exactly matching the three planar equilibrium equations, which is why pin-plus-roller is THE standard determinate support scheme. Four would demand more equations than statics owns; two would leave the body a mechanism.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the pipe support that was drawn wrong before it was built wrong. You arrive at a pipe-rack survey in a petrochemical process unit where a spring hanger is topped out and a nearby rigid support is cold. The as-built review starts exactly where this lesson does: draw the pipe segment as a free body, put in weight, the hanger pull, and each support's actual reaction menu — a rigid shoe pushes only up, a guide only sideways, the spring a set force. The diagram shows one support was assumed to share load it geometrically cannot see (figures representative). The correction was decided on the FBD before anyone touched a wrench.
