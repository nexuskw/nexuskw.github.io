Internal forces: shear and bending moment

Cutting a beam and exposing N, V, M. Shear and moment diagrams for point and distributed loads. The diagrams as the input strength-of-materials will demand.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What sign convention makes shear and moment diagrams consistent?
  • Where is bending moment maximum when shear crosses zero — and why?

01 · Cut the beam, expose the invisible

External reactions are only half the story; design needs the forces inside the member. Cut a beam at any section and, on the exposed face, three internal resultants restore equilibrium: normal force \( N \), shear force \( V \), and bending moment \( M \). Sign convention (keep it for life): positive shear rotates the element clockwise; positive moment sags the beam — compression on top, tension on the bottom. The convention is arbitrary; consistency is not.

02 · Diagrams, and the calculus that draws them

Shear and moment vary along the span, so we plot them. Two differential relations do most of the drawing:

\[ \frac{dV}{dx} = -w(x), \qquad \frac{dM}{dx} = V \tag{1} \]

Between point loads, \( V \) is constant and \( M \) runs straight; under a uniform load, \( V \) slopes linearly and \( M \) curves parabolically. A point load steps \( V \); a couple steps \( M \). And the operational headline: the bending moment is extremal where the shear crosses zero — because \( dM/dx = V = 0 \) there. That is where beams break, and where you look first.

03 · Why these diagrams are the product

Strength of Materials will convert \( M \) into bending stress and \( V \) into shear stress; every beam, shaft, and rack girder calculation starts from these two plots. Drawing them is not an academic exercise before the real work — for the structural side of plant engineering, the diagram is the deliverable that the stress check consumes. the Worked Examples tab builds both canonical cases: the central point load and the uniform load.

Closing. Cut, expose \( N, V, M \), plot with the two differential relations, and read the maximum moment where shear crosses zero. Beams are analyzed exactly once you can draw these two pictures without hesitation.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • Basic calculus — slopes and areas connect \( w \), \( V \), and \( M \).

Glossary

  • Shear force \( V \) — internal transverse force at a section.
  • Bending moment \( M \) — internal moment at a section; sagging positive.
  • Shear diagram — \( V(x) \) along the span; steps at point loads.
  • Moment diagram — \( M(x) \); slope equals shear; extremal where \( V = 0 \).
  • UDL — uniformly distributed load, in kN/m.

Problem 1 of 5 · solve, then check

Central point load

Problem. Simply supported beam, span \( L = 4\ \mathrm{m} \), point load \( P = 8\ \mathrm{kN} \) at midspan. Find: the shear and moment diagrams and the maximum moment.

Solution. (1) Reactions: symmetric, \( R = 4\ \mathrm{kN} \) each. (2) Shear: \( V = +4\ \mathrm{kN} \) from A to midspan, stepping down by 8 to \( -4\ \mathrm{kN} \) to B. (3) Moment: rises linearly (slope 4) to midspan, falls linearly after. (4) \( M_{max} = 4 \times 2 = 8\ \mathrm{kN\cdot m} \) — matching \( PL/4 = 8 \times 4/4 \).

Answer: \( V_{max} = 4\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( M_{max} = 8\ \mathrm{kN\cdot m} \) at midspan. Sanity check: \( M \) peaks exactly where \( V \) crosses zero (the load point). Diagram closes to zero at both ends — free ends carry no moment. ✓

Problem 2 of 5 · multiple choice

The bending moment in a beam is maximum where:

\( dM/dx = V \): the moment's slope is the shear, so \( M \) turns where \( V = 0 \). The largest load and the moment peak often do coincide — but the shear-zero rule is the reason, and the reliable locator when loading is mixed.

Problem 3 of 5 · multiple choice

A simply supported beam, span 10 m, carries 2 kN/m over its whole length. \( M_{max} \):

\( M_{max} = wL^2/8 = 2 \times 100 / 8 = 25\ \mathrm{kN\cdot m} \) at midspan. 50 uses \( wL^2/4 \) (confusing it with \( PL/4 \)); 100 forgets the 8 entirely. The two canonical formulas — \( PL/4 \) and \( wL^2/8 \) — are worth keeping straight for standing-up estimates.

Problem 4 of 5 · solve, then check

Uniform load

Problem. Simply supported beam, \( L = 6\ \mathrm{m} \), UDL \( w = 3\ \mathrm{kN/m} \). Find: diagrams and \( M_{max} \).

Solution. (1) Reactions: \( R = wL/2 = 9\ \mathrm{kN} \) each. (2) Shear: \( V(x) = 9 - 3x \) — linear, zero at \( x = 3\ \mathrm{m} \). (3) Moment: parabolic, \( M(x) = 9x - 1.5x^2 \). (4) \( M_{max} = M(3) = 27 - 13.5 = 13.5\ \mathrm{kN\cdot m} \) — matching \( wL^2/8 = 3 \times 36/8 \).

Answer: \( V_{max} = 9\ \mathrm{kN} \) at the supports; \( M_{max} = 13.5\ \mathrm{kN\cdot m} \) at midspan. Sanity check: shear crosses zero at midspan by symmetry, and the parabola peaks there. The two formulas \( PL/4 \) and \( wL^2/8 \) are worth memorizing — they bound most quick beam checks you will ever do standing up.

Problem 5 of 5 · multiple choice

At a downward point load, the shear diagram:

A concentrated load is a jump in \( V \) equal to its magnitude — the diagram is discontinuous there. Distributed loads produce the gradual slopes (\( dV/dx = -w \)); mixing up step versus slope is the fastest way to a wrong diagram.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the liquid-filled span. You arrive at a piping review at EQUATE where a line's support span was set when the service was gas; a process change now runs it liquid-full, multiplying the weight per metre (figures representative). The pipe between supports is Lesson 8's beam under a UDL: \( M_{max} = wL^2/8 \) at mid-span, growing with the square of span. The review question — add a support or accept the sag — is answered by exactly this diagram: double the weight doubles the moment, but halving the span quarters it. The support goes in at the shear-zero point's worst span.
