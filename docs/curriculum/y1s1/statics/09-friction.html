Friction

Dry friction, impending motion, and the friction cone. Wedges, screws, and belt friction — the capstan equation derived. Why belt tension ratios govern conveyor drives.

Lecture

  • Why does belt tension ratio grow exponentially with wrap angle?

01 · Dry friction and the threshold

A stationary block resists sliding with whatever friction force equilibrium demands — up to a ceiling:

\[ F \le \mu_s N \tag{1} \]

Equality holds only at impending motion; below it, friction is found from equilibrium, not from (1). Once sliding starts, kinetic friction \( \mu_k N \) (with \( \mu_k < \mu_s \)) takes over — which is why things lurch. The resultant of \( N \) and limiting \( F \) leans at the friction angle \( \phi = \tan^{-1}\mu_s \) from the normal; the cone it sweeps contains every possible contact reaction. If the applied load's line falls inside the cone, no sliding — geometry answering a force question.

02 · Wedges and screws

A wedge is friction's canonical machine: driving it must beat friction on both faces, and the friction directions always oppose the impending relative motion — set them by asking "which way does each surface slide?" before writing equations. A power screw is a wedge wrapped around a cylinder: the thread's helix angle plays the wedge angle, and self-locking (the jack that holds under load) occurs when the friction angle exceeds the helix angle. Every jack, clamp, and threaded fastener trades on this.

03 · Belt friction: the exponential surprise

A belt or rope wrapped on a drum can hold a large tension with a small one. Integrating the friction on each element of wrap gives the capstan equation:

\[ \frac{T_2}{T_1} = e^{\mu \beta} \tag{2} \]

with \( \beta \) the wrap angle in radians. The ratio is exponential in wrap — each added turn multiplies. Three turns of rope on a bollard hold a ship; the same law sets how much power a conveyor drive pulley can transmit before the belt slips.

04 · The friction working method

Friction problems have one trap and one discipline. The trap: writing \( F = \mu_s N \) by reflex. Static friction is adaptive — it supplies exactly what equilibrium demands, UP TO the ceiling \( \mu_s N \), and equals the ceiling only at impending motion. So the method is: assume equilibrium, solve for the friction force required, then compare with the available maximum. Required below available — the body holds, and the actual friction is the required value, not the ceiling. Required above available — motion begins, and kinetic friction \( \mu_k N \) takes over at its lower, fixed value.

The discipline: recompute \( N \) from equilibrium every time the loading changes. A downward push on a crate raises N and buys holding capacity; an upward-angled pull robs it — the same pull that helps horizontally hurts vertically, and the trade is decided by the numbers, not intuition. Complete the method by checking the second failure mode: tall narrow bodies may TIP before they slide, so compare the moment of the push about the tipping edge with the restoring moment of the weight. Slip-or-tip is answered by whichever threshold arrives first, and rigging, racking, and machine-skid problems ask it weekly.

Treat published coefficients as estimates, not constants. Surface condition, moisture, paint, and dust move \( \mu \) by tens of percent, which is why lift plans and skid calculations carry margin on top of the friction arithmetic. When a job genuinely hinges on friction, the professional move is to measure — the tilt test that reads \( \mu_s \) straight off the angle of repose — and then still design as if the measured number were optimistic.

Closing. Friction is an inequality until motion impends; wedges and screws are friction geometrized; and wrap buys holding power exponentially. Get the impending-motion direction right and the rest is bookkeeping.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Dragging a crate, and why angle helps

Problem. An 80 kg crate sits on a floor with \( \mu_s = 0.35 \). Find: the horizontal force to start it moving, and the force if pulling at \( 20^\circ \) above horizontal.

Solution. (1) \( W = 80 \times 9.81 = 784.8\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) Horizontal pull: \( P = \mu_s W = 0.35 \times 784.8 = 274.7\ \mathrm{N} \). (3) Angled pull: the vertical component unloads the floor. \( N = W - P\sin 20^\circ \), and impending motion demands \( P\cos 20^\circ = \mu_s N \). (4) \( P = \dfrac{\mu_s W}{\cos 20^\circ + \mu_s \sin 20^\circ} = \dfrac{274.7}{0.940 + 0.120} = 259.3\ \mathrm{N} \).

Answer: 274.7 N flat; 259.3 N at 20° — about 6% less. Sanity check: lifting-while-pulling reduces \( N \), hence friction; too steep an angle wastes pull on lift instead of advance, so an optimum angle exists (it equals the friction angle, \( \tan^{-1}0.35 \approx 19.3^\circ \) — our 20° is nearly optimal).

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Conveyor drive pulley

Problem. A belt wraps a drive pulley \( 180^\circ \) with \( \mu = 0.3 \); the slack side runs at \( T_1 = 1.2\ \mathrm{kN} \). Find: the maximum tight-side tension and transmittable belt pull before slip.

Solution. (1) Wrap: \( \beta = \pi\ \mathrm{rad} \). (2) Capstan: \( T_2/T_1 = e^{0.3\pi} = 2.566 \). (3) \( T_2 = 1.2 \times 2.566 = 3.08\ \mathrm{kN} \). (4) Transmittable pull: \( T_2 - T_1 = 1.88\ \mathrm{kN} \).

Answer: \( T_2 = 3.08\ \mathrm{kN} \); usable pull 1.88 kN. Sanity check: increase wrap to \( 270^\circ \) (snub pulley): ratio \( e^{0.3 \times 4.71} = 4.11 \) — the exponential means modest extra wrap buys disproportionate capacity, which is exactly why snub pulleys exist.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Will the crate move?

Problem. A 40 kg crate sits on a concrete floor, \( \mu_s = 0.35 \), \( \mu_k = 0.28 \). A horizontal push of 120 N is applied. Find: whether the crate moves, the actual friction force, and the push needed to start motion.

Solution. (1) \( N = W = 40 \times 9.81 = 392\ \mathrm{N} \); maximum static friction \( = 0.35 \times 392 = 137\ \mathrm{N} \). (2) Required friction for equilibrium = 120 N < 137 N → the crate stays put. (3) Actual friction = 120 N — what equilibrium demands, not the ceiling. (4) To start motion: push > 137 N; once moving, only \( 0.28 \times 392 = 110\ \mathrm{N} \) sustains it.

Answer: no motion; friction = 120 N; break-away at ≈137 N, then ≈110 N to keep sliding. Sanity check: the drop from 137 to 110 N is why a stuck skid lurches when it finally breaks free — the classic static-to-kinetic step, predicted entirely by the two coefficients.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A block presses on the floor with \( N = 200\ \mathrm{N} \), \( \mu_s = 0.4 \). A 50 N horizontal push is applied. The friction force is:

Below the threshold, friction equals whatever equilibrium demands: 50 N. The ceiling \( \mu_s N = 80\ \mathrm{N} \) applies only at impending motion — quoting 80 N for a 50 N push is the single most common friction error, and it fails the sanity check that the block would accelerate backwards.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Doubling a belt's wrap angle on a drum multiplies its holding ratio \( T_2/T_1 \) by:

\( T_2/T_1 = e^{\mu\beta} \), so \( \beta \to 2\beta \) squares the ratio: \( e^{\mu \cdot 2\beta} = (e^{\mu\beta})^2 \). Exponentials multiply where intuitions add — three turns on a bollard hold a ship precisely because each turn multiplies the previous total.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A crate needs 137 N to start sliding. It is pushed with 50 N and does not move. The friction force acting on it is:

Static friction is adaptive: it supplies exactly what equilibrium requires — here 50 N to balance the push — and not one newton more. The ceiling \( \mu_s N = 137\ \mathrm{N} \) is reached only at impending motion. Writing \( \mu_s N \) by reflex whenever friction appears is the single most common friction error, and this question is its vaccine.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A power screw is self-locking (holds its load with no applied torque) when:

Self-locking requires friction to beat the thread's slope: \( \phi > \alpha \). A jack that stays up under load is exactly this inequality. Lubrication reduces \( \phi \) and can therefore un-lock a marginal screw — the opposite of choice D's instinct.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

A crate just barely rests without sliding on a ramp inclined at 21.8°. Its coefficient of static friction is about:

At the angle of repose, \( \tan\phi = \mu_s \): \( \tan 21.8° = 0.40 \). This is also a practical field measurement — tilt a sample surface until sliding begins and read the coefficient off the angle. Choosing 0.37 uses \( \sin \) instead of \( \tan \); 0.22 mistakes the angle-over-degrees ratio for physics; 0.80 doubles the truth by using the wrong triangle side.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the capstan that is literally called a capstan. You arrive at a wire-drawing line in a cable plant where wire is hauled through dies by motor-driven capstan drums — the machine's name is the equation's name. Tension into the die must stay controlled: too little grip and the wire slips and scores; the fix on the floor is more wrap, because \( T_2/T_1 = e^{\mu\beta} \) grows exponentially with each added turn (figures representative). When a worn drum surface drops \( \mu \), the symptom — slip at the same wrap — walks straight out of Lesson 9's derivation.
