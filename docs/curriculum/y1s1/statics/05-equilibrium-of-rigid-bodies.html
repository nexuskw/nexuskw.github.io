Equilibrium of rigid bodies

The full ΣF = 0, ΣM = 0 toolkit. Two- and three-force members as shortcuts. Statically determinate versus indeterminate — and why the distinction matters later.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does a two-force member carry load only along its axis?
  • Statically determinate versus indeterminate: what changes in how you must solve?

01 · The full toolkit

A rigid body in plane equilibrium obeys three independent equations:

\[ \Sigma F_x = 0, \qquad \Sigma F_y = 0, \qquad \Sigma M_O = 0 \tag{1} \]

with the moment point \( O \) yours to choose — and choosing it on an unknown force's line of action removes that unknown from the equation. The craft of rigid-body statics is mostly the craft of choosing moment points that make equations fall apart into one-unknown pieces.

02 · Two- and three-force members

A member loaded at exactly two points carries force only along the line joining them — anything else would leave an unbalanced moment. Recognizing a two-force member converts a two-component unknown into a single magnitude along a known direction, which is a large discount. A three-force member's forces must be concurrent (or all parallel); that fact locates unknown directions geometrically. Both shortcuts come from \( \Sigma M = 0 \) applied with intent.

03 · Determinate or not

Three equations solve three unknowns. A beam on a pin and a roller (3 unknowns) is statically determinate: statics alone finishes it. Add a second pin (4 unknowns) and equilibrium no longer decides how the supports share — the structure is statically indeterminate, and the answer depends on stiffness, which is Strength of Materials' opening business. Recognizing indeterminacy early saves you from hunting for a fourth equation that does not exist.

04 · The rigid-body recipe

Bodies with size add one resource and one obligation: moments. The sequence is fixed. FBD with the support table: replace each support by its reaction set — pin two, roller one, fixed three — and count; a planar body offers exactly three equations, so more than three unknowns stops the problem before it starts. Take moments first, and tactically: choose the moment center where the most unknown reaction lines intersect — usually a support — so one \( \Sigma M = 0 \) line yields one unknown immediately. Finish with the force sums: \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \), \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \) deliver the remaining reactions. Audit with a spare moment equation: a moment sum about a different point, computed with the solved numbers, must close to zero — the free self-check almost nobody uses and every professional should.

Two readings matter on the way out. Reactions must add up to carry the total applied load — vertically and horizontally — or something is lost. And the load-sharing should make physical sense: a point load near one support loads that support proportionally harder, in inverse ratio of the distances — lever arms doing exactly what levers do.

One caution for the field: supports in drawings are idealizations of hardware. A "pin" is a bolted cleat that also resists a little moment; a "roller" is a slotted hole or a bearing pad that can bind. The three-equation arithmetic stands on those idealizations, so when a measured reaction disagrees stubbornly with the calculation, inspect the support before doubting the algebra — the hardware may have quietly changed its type.

Closing. Three equations, moment points chosen to kill unknowns, two-force members spotted on sight, and the determinacy count run before solving. That is rigid-body statics in one paragraph; the rest is practice.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Beam with mixed loading

Problem. A 6 m beam, pin at A (left), roller at B (right), carries a uniform load of 2 kN/m across its full length plus a 5 kN point load 2 m from A. Find: the reactions.

Solution. (1) Replace the UDL by its resultant: \( 2 \times 6 = 12\ \mathrm{kN} \) at midspan (3 m). (2) \( \Sigma M_A = 0 \): \( 6R_B - 12(3) - 5(2) = 0 \Rightarrow R_B = 46/6 = 7.67\ \mathrm{kN} \). (3) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \): \( R_A = 12 + 5 - 7.67 = 9.33\ \mathrm{kN} \). (4) Check with \( \Sigma M_B \): \( 9.33 \times 6 - 12 \times 3 - 5 \times 4 = 56 - 36 - 20 = 0 \). ✓

Answer: \( R_A = 9.33\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( R_B = 7.67\ \mathrm{kN} \). Sanity check: the point load sits toward A, so A carries the larger share; totals sum to 17 kN, the whole applied load. ✓

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

The two-force strut discount

Problem. A horizontal arm is pinned to a wall at A and carries 4 kN down at its 2 m free end. A strut, pinned at the arm 1.2 m from A and to the wall below, props it at \( 45^\circ \). Find: the strut force and pin reactions at A.

Solution. (1) The strut is a two-force member: its force \( T \) acts along its \( 45^\circ \) axis — one unknown, not two. (2) \( \Sigma M_A = 0 \): \( T\sin 45^\circ \times 1.2 = 4 \times 2 \Rightarrow T = 9.43\ \mathrm{kN} \) (compression — it props). (3) \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \): \( A_x = T\cos 45^\circ = 6.67\ \mathrm{kN} \), directed toward the wall. (4) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \): \( A_y = 4 - T\sin 45^\circ = 4 - 6.67 = -2.67\ \mathrm{kN} \) — the pin actually pulls the arm down.

Answer: strut 9.43 kN (compression); at A: 6.67 kN horizontal, 2.67 kN downward. Sanity check: the strut's vertical push (6.67 kN) exceeds the 4 kN load, so the pin must hold the arm down — a sign the arithmetic predicted and the negative \( A_y \) confirmed.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Reactions of a simply supported beam

Problem. A 6 m beam is pinned at A and rests on a roller at B. A 4 kN load acts 2 m from A. Find: both support reactions.

Solution. (1) FBD: \( A_x, A_y \) at the pin, \( B_y \) at the roller; no horizontal loads → \( A_x = 0 \). (2) Moments about A (killing both pin unknowns): \( \Sigma M_A = B_y \times 6 - 4 \times 2 = 0 \Rightarrow B_y = 8/6 = 1.33\ \mathrm{kN} \). (3) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \): \( A_y = 4 - 1.33 = 2.67\ \mathrm{kN} \).

Answer: \( A_y = 2.67\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( B_y = 1.33\ \mathrm{kN} \), \( A_x = 0 \). Sanity check: the load sits twice as close to A as to B, and A carries twice B's share \( (2.67 = 2\times1.33) \) — the lever ratio exactly; audit moment about B: \( 2.67\times6 - 4\times4 = 16 - 16 = 0 \) ✓.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A two-force member carries load:

With forces at only two points, moment equilibrium about either point forces both to lie along the joining line — otherwise an unbalanced couple remains. (Even a bent member obeys this: the force follows the line between the two pins, not the member's shape.)

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

An 8 m beam on a pin (A) and roller (B) carries 10 kN at 2 m from A. Reaction at B:

\( \Sigma M_A = 0: R_B \times 8 = 10 \times 2 \Rightarrow R_B = 2.5\ \mathrm{kN} \), leaving \( R_A = 7.5\ \mathrm{kN} \). The load sits near A, so A carries the lion's share — 5 kN each would only be right for a mid-span load.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

How many independent equilibrium equations does a single rigid body offer in a planar problem?

Two force sums and one moment sum: \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \), \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \), \( \Sigma M = 0 \). Writing moment equations about additional points produces true statements but not new information — any fourth equation is a combination of the first three. Six belongs to 3-D. This count is the budget every support scheme is measured against.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A plane structure has four unknown reactions. Equilibrium alone:

Plane statics supplies exactly three independent equations; a fourth unknown makes the structure statically indeterminate. No choice of moment points manufactures new information — the missing equation comes from deformation compatibility, which is Strength of Materials' business.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

A roller support on an inclined surface exerts a reaction:

A roller can only push normal to the surface it rolls on — that is its entire personality, and on an incline the reaction tilts with the surface. "Vertically upward" is the flat-ground habit misapplied; "any direction" describes a pin, not a roller. Reading the support's geometry before writing its reaction is the discipline this question rehearses.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the rack beam that had four supports on paper and three in fact. You arrive at a shop-fabricated pipe-rack beam bearing on four columns. Survey shows one bearing shimmed proud and a neighbor daylight-gapped: the beam is statically indeterminate, and fit-up — not the load table — decided how the columns share (figures representative). Lesson 5's determinacy count is the diagnostic: four vertical unknowns, three equations, so equilibrium alone never fixed the shares. The rectification is mechanical — re-shim to bearing — but the explanation of why a gap formed at all is this lesson's closing paragraph.
