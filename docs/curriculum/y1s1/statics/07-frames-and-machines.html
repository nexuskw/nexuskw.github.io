Frames and machines

Multi-member structures with internal pins. Dismembering frames correctly — action and reaction at every pin. Mechanical advantage of linkages and clamps.

Statics: Lesson 53 - Frame Problem with Two-Force MembersJEFF HANSON

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why must frames be dismembered at pins with action–reaction pairs?
  • What is mechanical advantage in a toggle clamp, statically speaking?

01 · Frames are not trusses

A frame contains at least one multi-force member — a member loaded at three or more points, or loaded between its pins. Its members carry shear and bending, not just axial force, so the truss shortcut (force along the member) is off the table except for any genuine two-force members hiding inside. Identify those first: every one you spot replaces two unknowns with one.

02 · Dismember at the pins — and pay Newton his due

The method is systematic: draw an FBD of the whole frame for the external reactions, then dismember — separate the members at their connecting pins and draw each member alone. At every pin, the force one member exerts on the other appears on both diagrams, equal and opposite. The classic error is drawing pin components in inconsistent directions on the two sides, which silently violates \( \Sigma F = 0 \) for the pin itself. Adopt a convention, draw the action–reaction pair explicitly, and let negative answers correct your guesses.

03 · Machines: frames that multiply force

A machine is a frame with moving parts designed to transmit and transform force — pliers, clamps, linkages, toggles. The same dismemberment analysis yields the mechanical advantage: output force per input force, set by the lever arms. A toggle clamp near its dead-center position multiplies force enormously because the geometry sends the output arm toward zero — the reason a modest hand force can hold a workpiece against machining loads. the Worked Examples tab runs a gripping tool and a loaded two-member frame.

04 · The disassembly working method

Frames surrender to a fixed sequence, and improvising it is how pin forces get lost. Survey for two-force members first — straight, loaded only at their two pins — because each one found replaces two unknown components with a single force along the member line. Solve the assembly as one rigid body where possible, harvesting the external reactions before any disassembly. Then pull the pins: draw a separate FBD for every member, and at each shared pin write the force ONCE, applying it to the two members as an equal-and-opposite pair — the bookkeeping backbone of the whole method. Choose which member's diagram to solve first by counting unknowns: there is almost always one member with only two or three, and solving it feeds the others like the truss's joint order did.

For machines, the same disassembly answers the practical question — what does the jaw, blade, or clamp deliver for a given hand force? Moments about the pivot of the handle member give the multiplication directly: output over input equals input arm over output arm. When the answer looks too good, check whether friction and pin clearances were ignored — they were, and real tools deliver a little less than the statics promises.

Closing. Spot the two-force members, dismember at pins with honest action–reaction pairs, and read machines as frames whose geometry is doing force arithmetic. Statics of frames is Newton's third law made procedural.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Gripping tool

Problem. A gripping tool's handles are squeezed with 150 N at 200 mm from the pivot pin; the jaws grip at 40 mm on the other side of the pivot. Find: the jaw force and mechanical advantage.

Solution. (1) FBD of one handle-jaw lever about the pivot. (2) \( \Sigma M_{pivot} = 0 \): \( 150 \times 200 = F_{jaw} \times 40 \). (3) \( F_{jaw} = 30000/40 = 750\ \mathrm{N} \). (4) \( MA = 750/150 = 5 \) — equal to the arm ratio \( 200/40 \).

Answer: 750 N grip; mechanical advantage 5. Sanity check: arms ratio 5:1 must give force ratio 5:1 — the lever law in its plainest form; the pivot pin itself carries \( 150 + 750 = 900\ \mathrm{N} \), the largest force in the tool, which is why pivot pins wear first.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Two-member frame under a side load

Problem. Members AC and BC are pinned together at C \( (2, 3)\ \mathrm{m} \), with A \( (0,0) \) pinned and B \( (4,0) \) on a roller. A 6 kN horizontal force pushes at C. Find: reactions and member forces.

Solution. (1) Whole frame, \( \Sigma M_A = 0 \): \( B_y \times 4 - 6 \times 3 = 0 \Rightarrow B_y = 4.5\ \mathrm{kN} \) up. (2) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \Rightarrow A_y = -4.5\ \mathrm{kN} \) (i.e., 4.5 kN down); \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \Rightarrow A_x = -6\ \mathrm{kN} \) (opposing the push). (3) Both members are two-force (loaded only at their end pins). At C, resolve along the member directions: each member's axis has length \( \sqrt{2^2+3^2} = 3.61\ \mathrm{m} \). (4) Solving joint C: \( F_{AC} = 5.41\ \mathrm{kN} \) tension, \( F_{BC} = 5.41\ \mathrm{kN} \) compression.

Answer: \( A = (6.0, 4.5)\ \mathrm{kN} \) resisting, \( B_y = 4.5\ \mathrm{kN} \); \( |F_{AC}| = |F_{BC}| = 5.41\ \mathrm{kN} \), tension and compression respectively. Sanity check: the side push tries to topple the frame toward B: the windward member (AC) stretches, the leeward (BC) squashes — signs agree with the picture.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

What the jaws deliver

Problem. A pair of pliers has handles gripped 180 mm from the pivot pin and jaws biting 45 mm from it, on the other side. A technician squeezes with 120 N per handle. Find: the jaw clamping force, treating each half as a lever about the pin.

Solution. (1) One handle as a rigid body pivoting at the pin: moments about the pin give \( F_{jaw} \times 45 = 120 \times 180 \). (2) \( F_{jaw} = 120 \times 180/45 = 480\ \mathrm{N} \). (3) Mechanical advantage: \( 180/45 = 4 \) — the arm ratio, exactly.

Answer: ≈480 N of clamp per 120 N of grip. Sanity check: the pin carries the sum of the transverse forces — \( 120 + 480 = 600\ \mathrm{N} \) — which is why the pivot pin, not the jaw, is where worn pliers fail first; the statics quietly predicts the tool's wear point.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A lever takes 100 N of hand force at 300 mm from the pivot; the output acts at 60 mm. Output force:

Moment balance: \( 100 \times 300 = F \times 60 \Rightarrow F = 500\ \mathrm{N} \) — mechanical advantage 5, the arm ratio. 20 N inverts the ratio (that lever would be a force reducer, useful for delicate work, not gripping).

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

What disqualifies a structure from truss analysis and makes it a frame?

Truss analysis rests on every member being two-force. A member loaded at a third point (or between its pins) carries shear and bending, so its force is no longer axial — dismemberment with action–reaction pin forces replaces the joint method.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

What distinguishes a frame from a truss?

Truss members are all two-force members — pure axial tension or compression. The moment a structure contains even one multi-force member (loaded between its joints, or at three-plus points), it is a frame, and the joint-equation shortcut dies: analysis proceeds by disassembling members and exposing pin-force pairs. Member count, load direction, and pin presence are all red herrings.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

When a frame is dismembered at a pin, the forces drawn on the two separated members must be:

Newton's third law: the pin force one member exerts on the other is mirrored exactly. Drawing the pair inconsistently — both components up on both members, say — silently violates equilibrium of the pin and corrupts every equation written afterward.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Two members of a frame share pin C. The pin force computed on member 1 at C is 850 N pointing up-right. On member 2 at C, the pin force is:

Newton's third law at the shared pin: whatever member 1 receives, member 2 receives exactly reversed. This pairing is the entire bookkeeping system of frame analysis — write the force once, apply it twice with opposite signs. Copying it in the same direction onto both members is the classic frame error, and it silently violates equilibrium of the pin itself.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the toggle clamp that holds a filling line's pace. You arrive at a dairy filling hall where a container-clamping fixture must hold product steady through a fill-and-cap cycle. The fixture is a toggle linkage: a small pneumatic cylinder input, links near dead-center at clamp, and a holding force many times the cylinder's output (figures representative). When a batch of misformed containers starts slipping, the maintenance question is Lesson 7's: has linkage wear moved the toggle geometry off dead-center, collapsing the mechanical advantage? A pin-by-pin dismemberment — not a bigger cylinder — locates the worn joint.
