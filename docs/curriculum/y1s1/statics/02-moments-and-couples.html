Moments and couples

Moment of a force about a point and an axis. Couples as pure turning effect. Varignon's theorem used to simplify real load cases.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What does a couple do that a single force cannot?
  • State Varignon's theorem and one situation where it saves work.

01 · Moment: the turning effect of a force

The moment of a force about a point measures its tendency to rotate a body about that point:

\[ M = F d \tag{1} \]

where \( d \) is the perpendicular distance from the point to the force's line of action — not the distance to where the force happens to be applied. A 500 N pull on a 0.6 m wrench gives 300 N·m only if the pull is perpendicular to the handle; at \( 60^\circ \) to it, the effective arm shrinks and \( M = 500 \times 0.6 \sin 60^\circ = 259.8\ \mathrm{N \cdot m} \). Sign convention: pick counterclockwise positive and never renegotiate mid-problem.

02 · Varignon's theorem

The moment of a force equals the sum of the moments of its components. This is not a curiosity — it is the standard labor-saving device. Rather than hunt for a perpendicular distance through awkward geometry, resolve the force into \( F_x \) and \( F_y \) at its point of application and take each component's moment with its own easy arm. The wrench case above: \( F_y = 500\sin 60^\circ \) acting 0.6 m from the nut gives the same 259.8 N·m in one line.

03 · Couples: pure rotation

Two equal, opposite, non-collinear forces form a couple. Their force resultant is zero — a couple does not push a body anywhere — but their moment \( M = Fd \) (\( d \) the separation) is the same about every point in the plane. That free-floating property is why we may slide a couple anywhere on a rigid body, and why any force system reduces to one force plus one couple at a chosen point — the standard equivalent system used from here to machine design.

04 · The moment working method

Moments punish improvisation, so fix the routine. Declare the convention first — counterclockwise positive, written at the top of the page — and never renegotiate it mid-problem. Choose the moment center tactically: the point that kills the most unknowns, usually where reaction lines cross; the moment of a force through the chosen point is zero, and that choice is free information. Pick the computation route per force: if the perpendicular distance is visible, \( M = Fd \) is fastest; if the geometry is angled and awkward, split the force into components at its point of application and let Varignon's theorem sum the pieces — components times their own clean arms beat one force times a distance you can't see.

Treat couples as the privileged objects they are: a couple's moment is the same about every point, so it moves freely around the diagram without recalculation — the one moment that never asks "about where?". And keep the units honest: newton-metres, force times length, always stated. A moment reported as a bare number is an invitation to the classic error of adding it to a force — dimensional homogeneity, learned in the mathematics course, polices this line for free.

Closing. Moment is force times perpendicular arm; Varignon converts geometry into components; couples turn without pushing and belong to no particular point. These three facts carry every equilibrium equation that follows.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Wrench moment, two ways

Problem. A 500 N force acts at the end of a 0.6 m wrench, at \( 60^\circ \) to the handle axis. Find: the moment about the nut, by perpendicular arm and by Varignon.

Solution. (1) Perpendicular arm: \( d = 0.6\sin 60^\circ = 0.520\ \mathrm{m} \), so \( M = 500 \times 0.520 = 259.8\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \). (2) Varignon: the component across the handle is \( 500\sin 60^\circ = 433.0\ \mathrm{N} \) with arm 0.6 m: \( M = 433.0 \times 0.6 = 259.8\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \). The along-handle component's line of action passes through the nut — zero arm, zero moment.

Answer: \( M = 259.8\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \) by both routes. Sanity check: the perpendicular case bounds it: \( 500 \times 0.6 = 300\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \) is the maximum possible, and \( 259.8 < 300 \). ✓

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

A couple on a valve handwheel

Problem. Two hands apply equal and opposite 200 N forces at diametrically opposite points of a 0.3 m-diameter handwheel. Find: the moment, and the moment about a point 1 m away from the wheel's center.

Solution. (1) Couple moment: \( M = Fd = 200 \times 0.3 = 60\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \). (2) About the distant point: a couple's moment is identical about every point in the plane — still \( 60\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \). The two forces' moments about that point differ individually, but their sum is unchanged.

Answer: \( 60\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \), everywhere. Sanity check: the force resultant is \( 200 - 200 = 0 \) — the wheel is turned, not translated, which is exactly what a handwheel is for.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

The angled pull on a valve wrench

Problem. A 250 N pull is applied at the end of a 0.6 m wrench arm, at 40° to the arm. Find: the moment about the pivot, and the pull angle that would maximize it.

Solution. (1) Only the component perpendicular to the arm turns it: \( M = F\,r\sin\theta = 250 \times 0.6 \times \sin 40° = 150 \times 0.643 = 96.4\ \mathrm{N\,m} \). (2) The maximum comes at \( \theta = 90° \): \( M_{max} = 250 \times 0.6 = 150\ \mathrm{N\,m} \) — pulling square to the arm.

Answer: 96.4 N·m; maximum 150 N·m at 90°. Sanity check: \( \sin 40° = 0.643 \) means the angled pull delivers 64% of the perpendicular pull's moment — exactly the fraction computed, and exactly why a fitter instinctively squares up on a stubborn valve.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A 400 N force acts perpendicular to a wrench, 0.25 m from the nut. The moment about the nut is:

\( M = Fd = 400 \times 0.25 = 100\ \mathrm{N\cdot m} \). Perpendicular application means the full 0.25 m is the moment arm — no sine factor needed. 1600 N·m divides instead of multiplying; 25 N·m forgets the force entirely.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

A couple acting on a bracket is evaluated about a point 2 m away instead of at the bracket. Its moment:

A couple's moment is the same about every point in the plane — its force resultant is zero, so moving the reference point adds equal and opposite contributions that cancel. That location-independence is what makes couples freely movable on a rigid body.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

Two 80 N forces form a couple with 0.5 m between their lines of action. The couple's moment about a point 3 m away from the pair is:

\( M = F s = 80 \times 0.5 = 40\ \mathrm{N\,m} \), and the defining property of a couple is that this value is identical about every point — the two forces' individual moments change with the point, but their sum never does. Growing the moment with distance imports single-force thinking; that is exactly the habit the couple concept exists to break.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

Varignon's theorem lets you replace \"find the perpendicular distance\" with:

The moment of a force equals the sum of the moments of its components — so resolve at the point of application and use each component's easy rectangular arm. (Forces whose line of action passes through the moment point do contribute zero, but that is a corollary, not the theorem.)

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Varignon's theorem lets you compute a force's moment by:

The moment of a force about a point equals the sum of the moments of its components about that point — which is why splitting an angled force at its point of application and using each component's clean perpendicular arm is both legal and usually faster. "Any convenient distance" is the classic error the theorem prevents: the arm must be perpendicular to each force line, and components make those arms visible.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the cheater bar that ruined a stud. You arrive at a bolted joint on a fabricated steel connection where a fitter has slipped a metre of pipe over a 300 mm wrench to break a seized nut — quadrupling the moment arm and taking the applied torque far beyond the stud's rating (figures representative). The stud yields, and the joint now needs drilling out. Lesson 2 is the whole story: \( M = Fd \) does not care whether the extra \( d \) came from an engineered tool or scavenged pipe. Torque wrenches exist to put a number on the moment; the calculation here is how you check that number is the right one.
