Moments of inertia

Second moment of area by integration and parallel-axis theorem. Composite sections used in structural tables. The property that will set bending stress in Strength of Materials.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What does the parallel-axis theorem add, and when must you use it?
  • Why does an I-beam's shape maximize second moment for its area?

01 · The second moment: area times distance squared

The second moment of area about the x-axis is

\[ I_x = \int y^2\, dA \tag{1} \]

Distance enters squared: area far from the axis counts disproportionately. For the workhorse rectangle about its own centroid, \( I = bh^3/12 \) — the cubed depth is the single most consequential exponent in structural engineering. \( I \) is not inertia in the dynamic sense; it is a purely geometric measure of how a section's area is deployed against bending.

02 · The parallel-axis theorem

Centroidal values are tabulated; real composite parts sit away from the composite centroid. The transfer rule:

\[ I = \bar{I} + A d^2 \tag{2} \]

adds \( Ad^2 \) for a part whose own centroid lies distance \( d \) from the axis in question. Two habits keep it honest: the theorem transfers only from a centroidal axis (never between two non-centroidal axes directly), and in composite work the \( Ad^2 \) terms usually dwarf the parts' own \( \bar{I} \) — flanges of an I-beam earn their keep almost entirely through \( Ad^2 \).

03 · Why sections look the way they do

An I-beam is the parallel-axis theorem drawn in steel: put most of the area in two flanges far from the neutral axis (huge \( d \)), keep a thin web to hold them apart and carry shear. the Worked Examples tab computes a small I-section at \( I = 4.49 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \) — then shows a solid square of the same area manages only \( 0.65 \times 10^6 \): the shape, not the steel, is doing seven times the work. Strength of Materials will divide bending moment by exactly this \( I \) to get stress; every section table you will ever open is a catalog of this one integral.

04 · The transfer working method

Composite moments of inertia extend the centroid table by exactly one discipline: the parallel-axis transfer. The sequence never varies. Find the composite centroid first — Lesson 10's table verbatim. For each part, write its own centroidal \( \bar{I}_i \) from the standard formulas, measure the distance \( d_i \) from the part's centroid to the COMPOSITE centroid, and transfer: \( I_i = \bar{I}_i + A_i d_i^2 \). Sum. Holes ride the same table with negative \( A \) and negative \( \bar{I} \). The classic error is transferring between two arbitrary axes — the theorem only moves I away from a shape's OWN centroid, never between two off-centroid axes; when in doubt, route through the centroid in two steps.

Read the result with the \( h^3 \) instinct: material far from the axis does the work, which is why doubling a beam's depth multiplies its bending stiffness eightfold while doubling its width merely doubles it, and why the I-beam exists at all. When a computed composite I comes out smaller than its largest part's contribution, the arithmetic is wrong — transfers only ever add for real areas. These numbers feed the flexure formula in Strength of Materials directly; the audit habits travel with them.

Closing. \( y^2 \) rewards distance, \( bh^3/12 \) rewards depth, and \( Ad^2 \) explains the I-beam. Statics ends by manufacturing the geometric property that strength calculations spend.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

I-section by composite parts

Problem. An I-section: two flanges 100 × 10 mm (top and bottom), web 10 × 80 mm, overall depth 100 mm, symmetric. Find: \( I \) about the horizontal centroidal axis.

Solution. (1) Symmetry puts the centroid at mid-depth; each flange centroid sits \( d = 45\ \mathrm{mm} \) away. (2) One flange: \( \bar{I} = \dfrac{100 \times 10^3}{12} = 8333\ \mathrm{mm^4} \); \( Ad^2 = 1000 \times 45^2 = 2\,025\,000\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). (3) Web (centroid on the axis, no transfer): \( \dfrac{10 \times 80^3}{12} = 426\,667\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). (4) Total: \( I = 2(8333 + 2\,025\,000) + 426\,667 = 4.49 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \).

Answer: \( I \approx 4.49 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). Sanity check: the flanges' own \( \bar{I} \) (8,333 each) is 0.4% of their \( Ad^2 \) — the transfer term carries the section, as 2 promised.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Same steel, different shape

Problem. Reshape the I-section's area (\( A = 2 \times 1000 + 800 = 2800\ \mathrm{mm^2} \)) into a solid square. Find: its \( I \) and the comparison.

Solution. (1) Square side: \( s = \sqrt{2800} = 52.9\ \mathrm{mm} \). (2) \( I = \dfrac{s^4}{12} = \dfrac{52.9^4}{12} = 0.653 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). (3) Ratio: \( 4.49/0.653 = 6.9 \).

Answer: the I-shape is ~6.9× stiffer in bending than a square of identical area. Sanity check: identical steel tonnage, sevenfold performance difference — pure geometry. This single ratio is why rolled sections exist, why racks are built from I-beams and channels, and why "add more steel" is the wrong first answer to a deflection problem.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

One rectangle, two axes

Problem. A 60 mm wide × 100 mm deep rectangular section. Find: its moment of inertia about its own centroidal horizontal axis and about its base — and reconcile the two with the parallel-axis theorem.

Solution. (1) Centroidal: \( \bar{I} = bh^3/12 = 60 \times 100^3/12 = 5.0 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). (2) About the base: \( I = bh^3/3 = 2.0 \times 10^7\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). (3) Parallel axis, \( d = 50\ \mathrm{mm} \): \( \bar{I} + Ad^2 = 5.0\times10^6 + 6000 \times 2500 = 2.0 \times 10^7 \) ✓ — the two formulas are one theorem apart.

Answer: \( 5.0 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \) centroidal; \( 2.0 \times 10^7\ \mathrm{mm^4} \) at the base. Sanity check: the base value is exactly four times the centroidal one for a rectangle — a ratio worth memorizing as a self-test whenever both are computed.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

The second moment of area \( I \) measures:

\( I = \int y^2 dA \): area counts by the square of its distance from the axis. Two sections of identical area can differ in \( I \) sevenfold — geometry, not quantity of material. Stress capacity involves \( I \) but also material strength; \( I \) itself is purely geometric.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

A 50 mm wide × 100 mm deep rectangle, about its horizontal centroidal axis: \( I \) =

\( I = bh^3/12 = 50 \times 100^3/12 = 4.17 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{mm^4} \). Choice A swaps \( b \) and \( h \) (\( 100 \times 50^3/12 \)) — a factor of 4 penalty for laying the section on its side, which is the whole lesson about depth cubed.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

The area moment of inertia carries units of:

\( I = \int y^2\,dA \): length squared times area — length to the fourth. mm² is area, mm³ is the section modulus (I divided by a distance — coming in Strength of Materials), N·mm is a moment of force: three different section quantities that beginners shuffle. Units are the fastest audit: any "moment of inertia" not in length⁴ is some other animal.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

The parallel-axis theorem \( I = \bar{I} + Ad^2 \) may be applied:

The theorem transfers from a part's own centroidal axis outward. Chaining it between two non-centroidal axes double-counts the offset — go back through the centroid instead. (Symmetry and the size of \( d \) are irrelevant to validity.)

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Doubling a rectangular beam's depth (h) while keeping its width multiplies its centroidal moment of inertia by:

\( I = bh^3/12 \propto h^3 \): doubling h gives \( 2^3 = 8 \) times the stiffness quantity. Doubling the WIDTH doubles I only linearly — which is the entire argument for deep, upright sections and for the I-beam's flanges-far-from-axis geometry. Choosing 4 squares instead of cubes; 16 belongs to \( h^4 \), a different integral.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the purlin that sagged and the catalog that fixed it. You arrive at a pre-engineered building retrofit where new cable trays load roof purlins beyond their original duty, and mid-span deflection is visible by eye (figures representative). The instinct on the floor is a heavier purlin; the catalog answer is a deeper one — \( I \) grows with depth cubed, so a modest depth increase outperforms a large weight increase. The engineer's comparison table is worked example 2 with real section numbers: same steel budget, different deployment of area, and the deflection problem disappears into \( bh^3/12 \).
