Trusses

Method of joints and method of sections. Zero-force members by inspection. Reading a pipe-rack or roof truss as a load path, not a picture.

Statics: Lesson 48 - Trusses, Method of JointsJEFF HANSON

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • How do you spot a zero-force member by inspection?
  • Method of sections: when does it beat method of joints, and why?

01 · A truss is a set of two-force members

The truss idealization: straight members, frictionless pin joints, loads applied only at joints. Every member is then a two-force member — pure tension or pure compression along its axis. That single assumption converts a structure into bookkeeping: find each member's axial force and its sense. Real gusset-plated, bolted trusses are not pinned, but the idealization prices member forces remarkably well and is the standard first analysis of any roof truss or braced rack.

02 · Method of joints, method of sections

The method of joints isolates one pin at a time and applies particle equilibrium — two equations per joint, so start at a joint with at most two unknowns (usually a support) and walk the structure. The method of sections cuts clean through up to three members and treats one whole side as a rigid body; a moment taken about the intersection of two cut members isolates the third in a single line. Joints when you want everything; sections when you want one member deep inside the span without solving half the truss to reach it.

03 · Zero-force members and reading the load path

At an unloaded joint of two non-collinear members, both are zero-force; at an unloaded joint of three members where two are collinear, the third is zero-force. Spotting these by inspection prunes the work — and teaches the deeper habit: a truss is a load path. Follow the applied load through diagonals and chords to the supports and the force pattern (top chord compression, bottom chord tension in a simply supported truss under gravity load) stops being memorized and starts being seen.

04 · Choosing the attack: joints or sections

Truss problems reward a decision made before any arithmetic. Sweep for zero-force members first — the two-collinear-plus-one pattern at unloaded joints — and strike them out; trusses routinely shrink by a quarter before any equation is written. Ask what the problem actually wants. Every member force? Method of joints: find a starting joint with at most two unknowns (support ends and loaded tips qualify), solve it, and let each solved member unlock the next joint. Three specific member forces in a big truss? Method of sections: cut through at most three members including the targets, treat either half as a rigid body, and take a moment about the point where the two unwanted cut members intersect — the target force falls out in one line.

Keep the bookkeeping uniform: assume every member in tension, let negative answers declare compression, and record T or C beside every result — downstream design cares about the difference more than the magnitude, because compression members buckle and tension members don't. Final audit: at any solved joint, the component sums must close; thirty seconds of checking buys back the whole truss.

Closing. Two-force members, joints for the census, sections for the surgical strike, zero-force members pruned on sight. A truss drawing is a picture of forces waiting to be read.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Method of joints on a triangular truss

Problem. A symmetric triangular truss spans 4 m with apex height 1.5 m; a 10 kN load hangs at the apex. Find: the forces in a sloping member and the bottom chord.

Solution. (1) By symmetry each support carries 5 kN. (2) Slope angle: \( \theta = \tan^{-1}(1.5/2) = 36.87^\circ \). (3) Joint at left support — vertical: \( F_{slope}\sin\theta = 5 \Rightarrow F_{slope} = 5/0.6 = 8.33\ \mathrm{kN} \), pushing into the joint: compression. (4) Horizontal: \( F_{chord} = F_{slope}\cos\theta = 8.33 \times 0.8 = 6.67\ \mathrm{kN} \), pulling: tension.

Answer: sloping members 8.33 kN (C); bottom chord 6.67 kN (T). Sanity check: the sloped member must exceed the 5 kN vertical it carries (only its sine works vertically): 8.33 > 5. ✓ Flatter trusses drive both numbers up — why shallow roofs need heavier members.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

One member by sections

Problem. A parallel-chord truss: span 8 m, height 2 m, four 2 m panels, 4 kN at each of the three interior bottom-chord joints. Find: the bottom-chord force in the second panel.

Solution. (1) Reactions: total 12 kN, symmetric, \( R = 6\ \mathrm{kN} \) each. (2) Cut vertically through the second panel (between \( x = 2 \) and \( x = 4 \) m); keep the left portion. (3) Moment about the top joint at \( x = 2\ \mathrm{m} \) (height 2 m) — the cut top chord and diagonal pass through it, vanishing from the equation. The 4 kN load at \( x = 2 \) also has zero arm. (4) \( \Sigma M = 0 \): \( F_{BC} \times 2 = 6 \times 2 \Rightarrow F_{BC} = 6\ \mathrm{kN} \) (tension).

Answer: 6 kN, tension. Sanity check: one cut, one moment equation, one member — against walking three joints to reach the same number. Sections earn their keep exactly here.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

First joint of a roof truss

Problem. At a truss joint, a diagonal member rises at 45° and a bottom chord runs horizontally; the joint carries a 10 kN downward load. Find: both member forces and their sense (T or C).

Solution. (1) Assume both members in tension (pulling away from the joint). (2) \( \Sigma F_y = 0 \): \( F_d\sin 45° - 10 = 0 \Rightarrow F_d = 14.14\ \mathrm{kN} \)… but the diagonal must push up to hold the load, so with the tension assumption the sign works out via geometry: taking the diagonal rising away from the joint, \( F_d = +14.14\ \mathrm{kN} \) along the member toward the joint = compression when the diagonal sits above the load. (3) \( \Sigma F_x = 0 \): \( F_c = F_d\cos 45° = 10\ \mathrm{kN} \), pulling away — tension.

Answer: diagonal ≈ 14.1 kN (C), chord = 10.0 kN (T). Sanity check: the diagonal's vertical component \( 14.14\sin45° = 10 \) exactly balances the load, and its horizontal component \( 14.14\cos45° = 10 \) is exactly what the chord resists — the classic strut-and-tie pair every roof truss repeats.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

At an unloaded truss joint, two members meet at an angle (non-collinear). Their forces are:

Resolve perpendicular to either member: only the other member has a component there, so it must be zero — and then the first must be zero too. This inspection rule prunes trusses before any arithmetic; the members still matter for stability and other load cases.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

A simply supported truss carries gravity loads. Typically its chords are:

The truss sags like a beam: the top shortens (compression), the bottom stretches (tension) — the same pattern a bending-moment diagram implies. Reading this without calculation is the \"load path\" habit; a cantilevered truss reverses it, which is the standard trap.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

At an unloaded truss joint, two members are collinear and a third meets them at an angle. The third member's force is:

Sum forces perpendicular to the collinear pair: only the angled member has a component there, and with no load at the joint that component — hence the whole force — must vanish. That is the zero-force-member rule, and sweeping for it first routinely deletes a quarter of a truss before any equations. The member still matters structurally (it braces against buckling and load cases not analyzed) — zero force is not zero purpose.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

At a support carrying 5 kN vertically, the only non-horizontal member is a diagonal at \( 30^\circ \) above horizontal. Its force is:

The diagonal alone must supply the 5 kN vertical: \( F\sin 30^\circ = 5 \Rightarrow F = 10\ \mathrm{kN} \). The shallow angle is the punishment — at 30°, only half the member force works vertically. 8.66 kN mistakes sine for cosine; 2.5 kN multiplies instead of divides.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Why does the method of sections cut through at most three members of a plane truss?

The cut half of the truss is treated as one rigid body, which offers exactly three planar equations — so at most three unknown cut-member forces are solvable. Cut four and the section alone cannot finish (though a clever moment point may still extract one target). The limit is the equation budget, not the saw: nothing is physically cut at all.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — reading the brace, not the drawing. You arrive at a pre-engineered building bay where a fitter asks whether a wind-bay diagonal can be dropped to pass a duct. The frame drawing shows geometry; Lesson 6 reads it as a load path: under lateral load that diagonal is the tension path to the foundation, and several adjacent members are zero-force only because it is there (figures representative). Remove it and the zero-force members inherit work they were never sized for. The duct moves; the diagonal stays. The decision took a joint sketch, not software.
