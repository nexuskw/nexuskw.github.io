Taylor series and approximation

Linear and quadratic approximation of functions near an operating point. Where sin θ ≈ θ comes from and when it lies. Error terms as an engineering honesty clause.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • Derivatives of all orders (Lessons 3–5) — coefficients of (1) are derivatives at a point.
  • Factorials and powers — the series' bookkeeping.
  • Radians (Lesson 2) — (2) is a radian-only privilege.

Glossary

  • Taylor series — the local polynomial replica of a smooth function.
  • Linearization — first-order truncation; tangent-line model.
  • Truncation error — the tail you dropped; sized by the first dropped term.
  • Operating point — the \( a \) around which the plant model is expanded.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Where does sin θ ≈ θ come from, and at roughly what angle does the lie exceed 1%?
  • Why does linearization around an operating point underpin control engineering?

01 · Every smooth function is locally a polynomial

Zoom into any smooth curve and it straightens; zoom with more care and it bends like a parabola. Taylor's theorem makes this precise: near \( x = a \),

\[ f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + \frac{f''(a)}{2}(x-a)^2 + \cdots \tag{1} \]

The first two terms are the tangent line — linearization — and the third begins to track curvature. Engineering lives on truncations of (1): every "small-angle," "small-signal," and "about the operating point" argument you will ever meet is a Taylor series with the tail cut off.

02 · The classic small approximations

\[ \sin\theta \approx \theta - \frac{\theta^3}{6}, \qquad \cos\theta \approx 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2}, \qquad e^x \approx 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2} \tag{2} \]

At \( \theta = 10° = 0.1745 \) rad, \( \sin\theta = 0.17365 \): the one-term approximation \( \theta \) errs by 0.5%, and adding the cubic term shrinks the error to 0.0004%. This is why pendulum periods, beam deflections, and control models replace \( \sin\theta \) with \( \theta \) below ~15° and nobody blushes.

03 · Knowing the size of the tail

An approximation without an error bound is a wish. The first neglected term estimates the error: truncating after \( \theta \) leaves \( \sim\theta^3/6 \). Professional habit: state the operating range, evaluate the first dropped term at the range's edge, and write both down. That habit is what separates "we linearized about 50% flow" from hand-waving — and it is exactly how instrument nonlinearity specs and control-model validity limits are written.

Closing. Taylor series turn smooth functions into local polynomials; linearization is its two-term truncation; the first dropped term prices the honesty of the approximation. This lesson closes the course by naming the approximation culture the whole curriculum will use.

Worked Examples

Worked example 1 — pricing the small-angle lie

Problem. A pendulum-style level float swings ±12°. The controls model uses \( \sin\theta \approx \theta \). Find: the worst-case percentage error of that model across the swing.

Solution. (1) \( 12° = 0.2094 \) rad; \( \sin(0.2094) = 0.20791 \). (2) Error \( = (0.2094 - 0.20791)/0.20791 = 0.72\% \). (3) First-dropped-term estimate: \( \theta^3/6 = 0.00153 \) → \( 0.00153/0.2079 = 0.74\% \) — the bound predicts the actual error almost exactly.

Answer: ≤0.75% across the swing — negligible against a ±2% level transmitter. Sanity check: error grows with \( \theta^2 \) relative terms; at 24° it would be ~3% — still quotable, but now worth stating.

Worked example 2 — quick exponential estimates

Problem. Estimate \( e^{0.1} \) with two and three terms of (2), against the true 1.10517.

Solution. (1) Two terms: \( 1 + 0.1 = 1.1 \) — error 0.47%. (2) Three terms: \( 1 + 0.1 + 0.005 = 1.105 \) — error 0.015%.

Answer: 1.1 (coarse), 1.105 (fine). Sanity check: next term \( x^3/6 = 0.000167 \) predicts the residual error of the three-term estimate — matches the 0.00017 shortfall exactly.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the transmitter that was "linear enough." You arrive at a metering review: a DP flow transmitter infers flow from \( Q \propto \sqrt{\Delta P} \), but the legacy scaling block linearized the square root around 70% of range. Below 30% flow the plant has been over-reading for years. Taylor's first dropped term, evaluated at the low end, prices the distortion at 4% — precisely the unexplained loss the mass balance kept showing. The fix is a proper square-root block; the diagnosis was a truncation-error estimate on one line of a notepad. Approximations are fine; unpriced approximations are not.
