Functions, units, and engineering magnitudes

What a function is when its argument is a temperature or a flow rate. Unit discipline and dimensional homogeneity as the first error-check every engineer owns. Orders of magnitude and sanity estimates.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why must every term of a physical equation share the same dimensions, and what error does this catch first?
  • A pump's flow is 'about 100' — cubic metres per hour or per day? Show how an order-of-magnitude check settles it.

01 · Numbers that carry units

Engineering mathematics begins one step before calculus: every quantity you will ever differentiate or integrate carries a unit, and the units obey algebra just as the numbers do. When a pump curve says head is 42 m, that number is meaningless until you know it multiplies through \( P = \rho g Q H \) to give watts:

\[ [P] = \frac{\mathrm{kg}}{\mathrm{m}^3}\cdot\frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}^2}\cdot\frac{\mathrm{m}^3}{\mathrm{s}}\cdot \mathrm{m} = \frac{\mathrm{kg\,m^2}}{\mathrm{s}^3} = \mathrm{W} \tag{1} \]

Check the units first and half of all calculation errors die before they are born. This is the dimensional analysis discipline: both sides of any physical equation must carry identical dimensions, and arguments of \( \sin \), \( \ln \), \( e^x \) must be dimensionless.

02 · Functions as machine behavior

A function \( f(x) \) is a machine's behavior written down: give it a flow, it returns a pressure drop; give it a time, it returns a temperature. The pump curve \( H(Q) \), the motor torque curve \( T(\omega) \), the cooling curve \( T(t) \) — the whole plant is a library of functions. Domain matters physically: \( H(Q) \) exists only for \( Q \ge 0 \), and beyond runout flow the curve is not merely undefined but forbidden.

03 · Magnitudes and scales

An engineer estimates before calculating. Powers of ten are the estimating language: a 100 m³/h pump moves \(\sim\)28 L/s; a kilowatt is ten 100 W lamps; a bar is \(\sim\)10 m of water column. On the plant floor this means you can smell a wrong answer: if a small transfer pump computes to 300 kW, the arithmetic is wrong before you re-check it — the machine on the skid is the size of a suitcase, and suitcases don't dissipate 300 kW.

04 · The unit-check working method

Knowing that units must balance is not the same as having a method. Use this three-step protocol on every equation you meet, in this course and every one after it. Step one: write the equation symbolically and substitute units, not numbers — run the algebra on the units alone and confirm both sides match, exactly as equation (1) did for pump power. Step two: convert every input to base SI before any arithmetic: flows to m³/s, pressures to Pa, lengths to m. Most real-world calculation errors are unit errors, and most unit errors happen when kPa meets Pa or m³/h meets m³/s in the same line. Step three: after computing, translate the answer back into the working units of the trade — kW, bar, m³/h — and test it against an order-of-magnitude estimate before you write it down.

Two traps deserve names. The gauge trap: gauge and absolute pressure differ by one atmosphere, and equations built on absolute pressure quietly fail when fed gauge readings. The prefix trap: a dropped factor of 1000 from k, M, or m survives every later step and grows more embarrassing the longer it lives. Scientific notation is the antidote — write \( 2.5\times10^5 \) Pa and exponents stay visible and checkable.

Closing. Units are algebra, functions are machine behavior, and order-of-magnitude sense is the cheapest error detector you will ever own. Every lesson that follows leans on all three.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • Algebraic manipulation — solving \( P=\rho g Q H \) for any one symbol.
  • SI prefixes — k, M, m, µ span nine orders of magnitude; conversion is exponent bookkeeping.
  • Scientific notation — \( 3.2\times10^4 \) beats 32000 for error-catching.

Glossary

Dimensional homogeneity\( [\mathrm{LHS}] = [\mathrm{RHS}] \)Run on every equation before numbers go in; both sides must carry identical dimensions.
Hydraulic power\( P = \rho g Q H \)Turning a pump duty (flow, head) into watts — with \( Q \) in m³/s, never m³/h.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Hydraulic and shaft power

Problem. A cooling-water pump at a Gulf petrochemical utility area delivers \( Q = 120\ \mathrm{m^3/h} \) of water against a total head \( H = 42\ \mathrm{m} \). Pump efficiency is 65%. Given: \( \rho = 1000\ \mathrm{kg/m^3},\ g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \). Find: hydraulic power and shaft power.

Solution. (1) Convert flow: \( Q = 120/3600 = 0.0333\ \mathrm{m^3/s} \). (2) Hydraulic power: \( P_h = \rho g Q H = 1000 \times 9.81 \times 0.0333 \times 42 = 13.7\ \mathrm{kW} \). (3) Shaft power: \( P_s = P_h/\eta = 13.7/0.65 = 21.1\ \mathrm{kW} \).

Answer: \( P_h \approx 13.7\ \mathrm{kW},\ P_s \approx 21.1\ \mathrm{kW} \). Sanity check: a 22 kW motor frame on a 120 m³/h duty is entirely ordinary — had we forgotten the 3600, we'd have gotten 49 MW, a power-station number.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Unit conversion under pressure

Problem. A compressed-air header reads 6.0 bar(g). Find: the gauge pressure in kPa and psi, and the approximate equivalent water column.

Solution. (1) \( 6.0\ \mathrm{bar} = 600\ \mathrm{kPa} \). (2) \( 600 \times 0.145 = 87\ \mathrm{psi} \). (3) Water column: \( h = P/\rho g = 600{,}000 / (1000 \times 9.81) = 61.2\ \mathrm{m} \).

Answer: 600 kPa ≈ 87 psi ≈ 61 m H₂O. Sanity check: the 10 m-per-bar rule gives 60 m — our exact figure lands within 2%.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Scaling a transmitter signal

Problem. A level transmitter on a 2.5 m measuring range outputs a linear 4–20 mA signal: 0 m maps to 4 mA, 2.5 m maps to 20 mA. Find: the function \( L(I) \) converting current to level, the level at a reading of 11.2 mA, and the function's physical domain.

Solution. (1) The span is 16 mA for 2.5 m, so \( L(I) = 2.5\,\dfrac{I - 4}{16} \) metres, with \( I \) in mA. (2) At \( I = 11.2 \): \( L = 2.5 \times 7.2/16 = 1.125\ \mathrm{m} \). (3) Domain: \( 4 \le I \le 20 \) mA — readings below 4 mA are not levels but fault signals, which is precisely why the live-zero convention exists.

Answer: \( L(I) = 2.5(I-4)/16 \); 1.125 m at 11.2 mA; domain 4–20 mA. Sanity check: 11.2 mA sits at \( 7.2/16 = 45\% \) of the current span, and 45% of 2.5 m is 1.125 m — the function agrees with the proportion.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A transfer pump delivers \( Q = 90\ \mathrm{m^3/h} \) of water (\( \rho = 1000\ \mathrm{kg/m^3} \)) against a total head \( H = 25\ \mathrm{m} \), with pump efficiency \( \eta = 75\% \). Taking \( g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), what shaft power must the motor supply?

Convert the flow first: \( Q = 90/3600 = 0.025\ \mathrm{m^3/s} \), so the hydraulic power is \( P_h = \rho g Q H = 1000 \times 9.81 \times 0.025 \times 25 = 6.13\ \mathrm{kW} \), and the shaft power is \( P_s = P_h/\eta = 6.13/0.75 = 8.2\ \mathrm{kW} \). Efficiency divides rather than multiplies: the motor must supply more power than the water receives, which eliminates 4.6 kW (the classic multiply-by-\( \eta \) error) and 6.1 kW (efficiency ignored). The 29.4 MW figure comes from leaving \( Q \) in \( \mathrm{m^3/h} \) — an order-of-magnitude check rejects it at once, since a small transfer pump is not a power station.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

A tank cooling curve is modelled as \( T(t) = T_\infty + (T_0 - T_\infty)\,e^{-t/\tau} \), where \( t \) is time in seconds. What does dimensional homogeneity require of the constant \( \tau \)?

The argument of \( e^x \) — like those of \( \sin \) and \( \ln \) — must be dimensionless, so \( \tau \) must share the dimension of \( t \): time. The kelvin answer commits the classic error of matching the constant to the equation's output units instead of to the argument it sits inside; the temperature dimensions are already balanced by the prefactor \( (T_0 - T_\infty) \). No function absorbs units, and constants in physical equations routinely carry dimensions — homogeneity is checked term by term and argument by argument.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A water pump moves \( 100\ \mathrm{m^3/h} \) against 30 m of head, with pump efficiency around 70%. Which shaft-power magnitude is plausible?

Hydraulic power is \( \rho g Q H = 1000 \times 9.81 \times (100/3600) \times 30 \approx 8.2\ \mathrm{kW} \); dividing by the 0.70 efficiency gives ≈12 kW of shaft power. The 120 W and 1.2 MW answers are the classic unit slips — forgetting the 3600 in the flow conversion moves the result by exactly these factors. Order-of-magnitude sense is the fastest error detector: a pump this size is a two-person lift, not a power-station item.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A chilled-water line gauge reads \( 250\ \mathrm{kPa} \). Using \( \rho = 1000\ \mathrm{kg/m^3} \) and \( g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), what height of water column does this pressure correspond to?

Convert to base units before substituting: \( h = P/(\rho g) = 250000/(1000 \times 9.81) = 25.5\ \mathrm{m} \). The rule of thumb that 1 bar is roughly 10 m of water confirms it: 250 kPa is 2.5 bar, hence about 25 m. The 0.025 m answer is the classic slip of feeding kPa into an SI formula without converting to Pa, and 250 m follows from misconverting 250 kPa into 25 bar — both fail the order-of-magnitude sanity check for a building chilled-water loop.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

An energy report lists a consumption of 3.6 MJ. What is that in kilowatt-hours?

One kilowatt-hour is 1000 W sustained for 3600 s: \( 1000 \times 3600 = 3.6\times10^6\ \mathrm{J} = 3.6\ \mathrm{MJ} \) — so 3.6 MJ is exactly 1.0 kWh. Choosing 3.6 kWh treats MJ and kWh as the same unit with different labels, the classic energy-billing slip; the two differ by precisely the 3600 seconds in an hour.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette 1 — the suspicious kilowatt. You arrive at a pump house during an energy audit and the datasheet claims a 15 kW motor delivers 260 m³/h at 35 m. Equation (1) says the water alone needs \( 1000 \times 9.81 \times 0.0722 \times 35 \approx 24.8\ \mathrm{kW} \) — more than the motor can give before efficiency. The datasheet is internally impossible; either the duty or the nameplate was transcribed wrong. Ten seconds of unit algebra just saved a warranty dispute.

Vignette 2 — reading a foreign drawing. A packaged unit arrives documented in psi and US gpm. Before the coordination meeting you convert the relief setting — 150 psi → 10.3 bar — and flag that the plant's 10 bar-rated header cannot legally receive it (ASME B31.3 pressure design basis). The catch costs one email now instead of a rework order later.
