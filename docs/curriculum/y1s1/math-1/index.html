MTH 101Year 1 · Semester 1

Engineering Mathematics I

Single-variable calculus rebuilt as a working tool: limits, derivatives, integrals, and first differential equations, always pointed at rates, areas, and physical balances you will meet again in thermodynamics and vibrations.

11 lessons · 0 in the Core 60 · Primary texts:

  • MIT OCW 18.01 Single Variable Calculus (ocw.mit.edu)
01

Functions, units, and engineering magnitudes

What a function is when its argument is a temperature or a flow rate. Unit discipline and dimensional homogeneity as the first error-check every engineer owns. Orders of magnitude and sanity estimates.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01

02

Limits and continuity

The limit as the formal version of 'what happens as we approach'. Continuity and why physical quantities are usually, but not always, continuous. One-sided limits at switching events.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01

03

The derivative as a rate

Definition from first principles, then the derivative as velocity, heat-up rate, and wear rate. Differentiability versus continuity. Reading slopes off real trend data.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01

04

Differentiation rules and the chain rule

Product, quotient, and chain rules with mechanical fluency as the goal. Implicit differentiation. The chain rule as the engine behind every related-rates problem in plant contexts.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01

05

Exponentials, logarithms, and growth/decay

e and ln as the natural language of charging, cooling, and decay processes. Log scales for spanning decades of data. Semilog reading of exponential trends — the shape behind Newton cooling and RC circuits.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01

06

Extrema, curve behavior, and optimization

First and second derivative tests. Framing a physical question as minimize-cost or maximize-output with constraints stated honestly. Classic geometry-of-a-tank optimizations as rehearsal for design trade-offs.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01

07

The integral as accumulation

Riemann sums to the definite integral. Accumulated flow from a rate curve, energy from a power trace. The Fundamental Theorem as the bridge between rates and totals.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01

08

Integration techniques

Substitution, integration by parts, and simple partial fractions. Choosing a technique by recognizing structure. Numerical fallback when no closed form exists.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01

09

Applications: areas, volumes, work, and averages

Solids of revolution for vessels and shafts. Work integrals for pumping and lifting. Mean value of a fluctuating signal — the first meeting with RMS thinking.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01

10

First-order differential equations

Separable and linear first-order ODEs. Tank mixing, Newton cooling, and first-order sensor lag solved end to end. Time constants as the physical meaning of the solution.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01

11

Taylor series and approximation

Linear and quadratic approximation of functions near an operating point. Where sin θ ≈ θ comes from and when it lies. Error terms as an engineering honesty clause.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01
