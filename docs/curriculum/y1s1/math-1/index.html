MTH 101 · YEAR 1 · SEMESTER 1

Engineering Mathematics I

Single-variable calculus rebuilt as a working tool: limits, derivatives, integrals, and first differential equations, always pointed at rates, areas, and physical balances you will meet again in thermodynamics and vibrations.

01

Functions, units, and engineering magnitudesFull lesson

What a function is when its argument is a temperature or a flow rate. Unit discipline and dimensional homogeneity as the first error-check every engineer owns. Orders of magnitude and sanity estimates.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why must every term of a physical equation share the same dimensions, and what error does this catch first?
  • A pump's flow is 'about 100' — cubic metres per hour or per day? Show how an order-of-magnitude check settles it.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01 Watch lectures ▶

02

Limits and continuityFull lesson

The limit as the formal version of 'what happens as we approach'. Continuity and why physical quantities are usually, but not always, continuous. One-sided limits at switching events.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Give a plant quantity that genuinely jumps discontinuously, and one that only appears to.
  • What does a one-sided limit describe at a relay switching instant?

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01 Watch lectures ▶

03

The derivative as a rateFull lesson

Definition from first principles, then the derivative as velocity, heat-up rate, and wear rate. Differentiability versus continuity. Reading slopes off real trend data.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A bearing temperature trend rises 2 °C/hour: what derivative statement is that, and toward what does it point?
  • Why can a function be continuous yet not differentiable at a point — and what would that look like on a trend?

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01 Watch lectures ▶

04

Differentiation rules and the chain ruleFull lesson

Product, quotient, and chain rules with mechanical fluency as the goal. Implicit differentiation. The chain rule as the engine behind every related-rates problem in plant contexts.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Tank level depends on flow, flow on valve position: which rule chains the sensitivities together?
  • Differentiate x·e^x and explain which rule did the work.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01 Watch lectures ▶

05

Exponentials, logarithms, and growth/decayFull lesson

e and ln as the natural language of charging, cooling, and decay processes. Log scales for spanning decades of data. Semilog reading of exponential trends — the shape behind Newton cooling and RC circuits.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why do cooling curves plot straight on semilog axes, and what does the slope encode?
  • What does a time constant of 5 minutes say about how long until a sensor reads ~63% of a step change?

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01 Watch lectures ▶

06

Extrema, curve behavior, and optimizationFull lesson

First and second derivative tests. Framing a physical question as minimize-cost or maximize-output with constraints stated honestly. Classic geometry-of-a-tank optimizations as rehearsal for design trade-offs.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A closed tank must hold fixed volume with minimum surface: set up (don't solve) the optimization.
  • What distinguishes a local maximum from the global one on a cost curve, and why does the difference matter?

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01 Watch lectures ▶

07

The integral as accumulationFull lesson

Riemann sums to the definite integral. Accumulated flow from a rate curve, energy from a power trace. The Fundamental Theorem as the bridge between rates and totals.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Power drawn over a shift is a curve: what integral gives energy consumed, and in what units?
  • State the Fundamental Theorem in one sentence about rates and totals.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01 Watch lectures ▶

08

Integration techniquesFull lesson

Substitution, integration by parts, and simple partial fractions. Choosing a technique by recognizing structure. Numerical fallback when no closed form exists.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Which technique handles ∫x·e^x dx, and why does substitution alone fail?
  • When is a numerical integral the honest choice over an analytic one?

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01 Watch lectures ▶

09

Applications: areas, volumes, work, and averagesFull lesson

Solids of revolution for vessels and shafts. Work integrals for pumping and lifting. Mean value of a fluctuating signal — the first meeting with RMS thinking.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does RMS, not the plain mean, represent the heating effect of a fluctuating current?
  • Set up the work integral for pumping a tank's contents up 10 m — what varies inside the integral?

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01 Watch lectures ▶

10

First-order differential equationsFull lesson

Separable and linear first-order ODEs. Tank mixing, Newton cooling, and first-order sensor lag solved end to end. Time constants as the physical meaning of the solution.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Newton cooling: write the ODE and say what each symbol claims physically.
  • Two mixing tanks differ only in volume: which has the longer time constant, and why?

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.01 Watch lectures ▶

11

Taylor series and approximationFull lesson

Linear and quadratic approximation of functions near an operating point. Where sin θ ≈ θ comes from and when it lies. Error terms as an engineering honesty clause.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Where does sin θ ≈ θ come from, and at roughly what angle does the lie exceed 1%?
  • Why does linearization around an operating point underpin control engineering?
