Applications: areas, volumes, work, and averages

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does RMS, not the plain mean, represent the heating effect of a fluctuating current?
  • Set up the work integral for pumping a tank's contents up 10 m — what varies inside the integral?

01 · Average value — what the meter should have read

The average of a continuously varying quantity is its integral spread evenly across the interval:

\[ \bar f = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(t)\,dt \tag{1} \]

Average demand, average flow, average temperature — every KPI that begins with the word "average" is equation (1), whether computed honestly or not. When someone averages three spot readings taken at convenient times, you now know precisely what they did wrong.

02 · RMS — the average that heats

Electrical heating goes as the square of current, so the meaningful "average" for AC is the root of the mean of the square:

\[ f_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T f^2(t)\,dt}, \qquad \text{for } f = A\sin\omega t:\ f_{rms} = \frac{A}{\sqrt 2} \tag{2} \]

The sine result follows because \( \sin^2 \) averages to exactly ½ over a cycle. This is why 230 V mains has a 325 V peak, and why vibration severity standards quote RMS velocity: RMS tracks energy, and energy does the damage.

03 · Work by integration

When force varies along the path, work is accumulated force-times-distance: \( W = \int F\,dx \). Pumping a tank empty is the signature case: each layer of liquid at depth \( y \) has weight \( \rho g A\,dy \) and must rise a different height. Summing layers is an integral, executed in the Worked Examples tab for a real tank. The same layer-summing pattern computes hydrostatic forces on gates and centroids of plates — one method, many billables.

04 · Choosing the right average — a field guide

Three different averages answer three different questions, and choosing the wrong one is a quiet, expensive error. The plain mean — equation (1) — answers "how much per unit time on the invoice": billing, throughput, duty totals. The RMS — equation (2) — answers "what steady level would heat, stress, or wear the same": electrical heating, vibration severity, fatigue-relevant loading, because damage physics runs on squares. The peak answers "will it survive the worst instant": trips, relief sizing, insulation. A signal has all three numbers at once, and the crest factor — peak over RMS — is the single figure that says how far apart they live: near 1.4 for a clean sine, climbing whenever impacts and spikes enter, which is why analysts watch it as an early bearing-damage flag.

On sampled data the integrals become sums without changing meaning: mean the samples for equation (1), mean the squares and take the root for equation (2), weighting by interval when the sampling is uneven. For work-by-layers problems, the recipe is mechanical: draw the layer, write its weight, write its travel distance as a function of position, integrate over the geometry — then check against the centroid shortcut, which agrees every time the geometry is uniform and catches the setup when it isn't.

The same layer-summing pattern prices hydrostatic force on a gate, the metal in a tapered column, and the moment of a distributed load — one method, many invoices. What changes between problems is only what each layer carries: weight, pressure, or load per metre. Master the pattern once, in the tank problem, and every later course that says "integrate over the geometry" is asking for a rerun.

Closing. Integrals define honest averages (1), energy-true averages (2), and path-dependent work — three tools that turn "roughly" into "defensibly."

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

RMS of a sine, derived

Problem. Show \( f_{rms} = A/\sqrt2 \) for \( f = A\sin\omega t \) over one period.

Solution. (1) \( f^2 = A^2\sin^2\omega t = \frac{A^2}{2}(1 - \cos 2\omega t) \). (2) The cosine term integrates to zero over a full period, so \( \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T f^2 dt = \frac{A^2}{2} \). (3) Square root: \( f_{rms} = A/\sqrt2 \approx 0.707A \).

Answer: \( A/\sqrt2 \). Sanity check: 230 V RMS × √2 = 325 V peak — the familiar mains numbers reproduce.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Emptying a tank over the rim

Problem. A vertical cylindrical water tank, radius 1.5 m, holds 4.0 m of water. Find: the minimum work to pump the entire contents over the rim (at the 4.0 m level).

Solution. (1) Layer at height \( y \) (from the bottom), thickness \( dy \): volume \( A\,dy \) with \( A = \pi(1.5)^2 = 7.07\ \mathrm{m^2} \); lift height \( (4-y) \). (2) \( W = \rho g A \int_0^4 (4-y)\,dy = 1000 \times 9.81 \times 7.07 \times \left[4y - \tfrac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^4 \). (3) Bracket: \( 16 - 8 = 8 \). So \( W = 69{,}300 \times 8 = 555\ \mathrm{kJ} \).

Answer: ≈555 kJ (≈0.154 kWh). Sanity check: total mass 28.3 t lifted an average of 2 m: \( 28{,}300 \times 9.81 \times 2 = 555\ \mathrm{kJ} \) — the centroid shortcut confirms the integral exactly.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Mean versus RMS on a duty cycle

Problem. A motor draws 40 A for 2 s, then 10 A for 8 s, repeating every 10 s. Find: the mean current and the RMS current, and state which one sizes the motor thermally.

Solution. (1) Mean: \( (40 \times 2 + 10 \times 8)/10 = 160/10 = 16\ \mathrm{A} \). (2) RMS: mean of squares \( = (40^2 \times 2 + 10^2 \times 8)/10 = (3200 + 800)/10 = 400 \), so \( I_{rms} = 20\ \mathrm{A} \). (3) Heating goes as \( I^2R \), so the 20 A figure — not 16 A — is the thermally equivalent steady current.

Answer: mean 16 A, RMS 20 A; thermal sizing uses RMS. Sanity check: RMS ≥ mean always, with equality only for constant signals; a motor sized on the 16 A average would run 56% hot on an \( I^2 \) basis \( (400/256 = 1.56) \) — the exact mistake the RMS concept exists to prevent.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A reactor jacket temperature follows \( T(t) = 20 + 4t - t^2 \) (in Celsius, with \( t \) in hours) over a 3-hour batch, \( 0 \le t \le 3 \). What is the true mean temperature of the batch?

The mean value is \( \bar T = \frac{1}{3}\int_0^3 (20 + 4t - t^2)\,dt = \frac{1}{3}\left[20t + 2t^2 - \tfrac{t^3}{3}\right]_0^3 = \frac{69}{3} = 23.0\ ^\circ\mathrm{C} \). Averaging only the start and end readings gives 21.5 °C — the spot-reading shortcut the lecture warns against, which misses everything between the samples. Choosing 69 °C is the integral itself, forgetting to divide by the interval width \( b-a \); 23.75 °C is a single reading at the midpoint, another form of spot sampling.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

A vertical cylindrical tank of radius 1.0 m holds water (\( \rho = 1000\ \mathrm{kg/m^3} \)) to a depth of 3.0 m. Taking \( g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2} \), what is the minimum work required to pump the entire contents over the rim at the 3.0 m level?

A layer at height \( y \) has weight \( \rho g A\,dy \) with \( A = \pi(1.0)^2 \) and must rise \( (3-y) \), so \( W = \rho g A \int_0^3 (3-y)\,dy = 1000 \times 9.81 \times \pi \times 4.5 \approx 139\ \mathrm{kJ} \). The centroid shortcut confirms it: 9.42 t of water rising 1.5 m gives the same 139 kJ. Choosing 277 kJ lifts the whole mass through the full 3.0 m instead of the centroid height — the classic error of ignoring that each layer travels a different distance; 14.1 kJ drops \( g \), leaving mass-metres rather than joules.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

What is the mean value of \( f(t) = 6t^2 \) over \( [0, 2] \)?

\( \bar f = \tfrac{1}{2}\int_0^2 6t^2 dt = \tfrac{1}{2}\left[2t^3\right]_0^2 = \tfrac{16}{2} = 8 \). Answering 16 is the integral without dividing by the width — a total, not an average; 24 is the endpoint value \( f(2) \), the spot-reading error; 4 halves the correct answer by dividing twice. Mean value is always integral over width: the two operations are inseparable.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

Why does the RMS value, rather than the plain time-average, represent the heating effect of an AC current in a resistor?

Power in a resistor is \( i^2 R \), so the physically meaningful average must be taken on \( i^2 \); the square root then restores current units, giving the DC-equivalent amplitude that produces the same heating. The \( \sqrt{2} \) relation in the second choice is the classic overgeneralisation — peak divided by \( \sqrt{2} \) holds only for pure sinusoids, and the crest factor of other waveforms differs. The third choice confuses RMS with the rectified (mean-absolute) average, a distinct and generally smaller quantity.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Which signal has a crest factor of exactly \( \sqrt{2} \approx 1.41 \)?

For a sine, peak \( = A \) and RMS \( = A/\sqrt2 \), so peak/RMS \( = \sqrt2 \). A constant and a square wave both have RMS equal to their peak — crest factor 1 — while impulsive signals push the ratio well above 2, which is precisely why a rising crest factor on a bearing's vibration flags early impact-type damage while the RMS still looks calm. Crest factor is the one number that says how "spiky" a signal is.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the average that wasn't. You arrive at a demand-charge dispute: the utility bills on average kW over each 15-minute window, and the plant's spreadsheet — three manual readings a shift — says the plant never crosses the contract limit. The historian's one-minute data, run through equation (1) per window, says otherwise: startup windows average 8% over. The spreadsheet sampled politely between the peaks. Billing is an integral; sampling is an opinion. The plant reschedules two big starts and the penalty disappears next cycle.
