The derivative as a rate

Definition from first principles, then the derivative as velocity, heat-up rate, and wear rate. Differentiability versus continuity. Reading slopes off real trend data.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A bearing temperature trend rises 2 °C/hour: what derivative statement is that, and toward what does it point?
  • Why can a function be continuous yet not differentiable at a point — and what would that look like on a trend?

01 · From average rate to instantaneous rate

Between Monday and Friday a bearing housing warmed from 62 °C to 74 °C: average rate 3 °C/day. But right now, this hour, how fast is it heating? Squeeze the interval and the average rate converges — Lesson 2's limit — to the instantaneous rate:

\[ \frac{dT}{dt} = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0}\frac{T(t+\Delta t)-T(t)}{\Delta t} \tag{1} \]

That is the derivative: the local slope of the trend, the speed of change at an instant. Units divide: °C per hour, m³ per second, mm/s of vibration per week. A derivative always has units, and always tells a maintenance story.

02 · The derivative as the trend's confession

Condition monitoring is derivative-reading as a profession. A flat vibration trend with \( dV/dt \approx 0 \) buys you time; the same amplitude with a steep positive slope buys you a work order. Two machines at identical 4 mm/s are in different worlds if one arrived there over three years and the other over three weeks. Value tells you where it is; the derivative tells you where it's going.

03 · Computing from a model

Given a fitted trend \( T(t) = 40 + 0.8t + 0.05t^2 \) (°C, hours), the power rule (proved next lesson) gives \( T'(t) = 0.8 + 0.1t \): the heating accelerates, because the rate itself grows. Set \( T'(t) \) against an alarm rate and you get a time-to-act, not just a number: solve \( 0.8+0.1t = 2.5 \) → \( t = 17\ \mathrm{h} \). The derivative converts a curve into a deadline.

04 · Computing derivatives from data — the working method

Real trends arrive as samples, not formulas, so the working derivative is a finite difference. From the two latest points you can only form the forward-looking secant \( (f_{now} - f_{prev})/\Delta t \); mid-record, prefer the central difference \( (f_{i+1} - f_{i-1})/(2\Delta t) \), which cancels the first layer of error and tracks the true tangent far better on smooth data. Three disciplines make the estimates trustworthy. State the units — a slope is always something per something, and writing mm/s per week forces the physical meaning into view. Respect noise: differentiation amplifies it, so difference over intervals long enough for the real change to dominate the scatter; a derivative computed from two noisy readings an hour apart is an opinion, not a measurement. Distinguish model from data: when a curve has been fitted, differentiate the model symbolically and use the data-side difference as its check — when the two disagree badly, the fit, the data, or the machine has changed, and every one of those is worth knowing.

Then read the result the way 2 taught: the value says where the machine is, the derivative says where it is going, and the difference between those two sentences is the difference between recording a number and making a decision.

Closing. The derivative is a limit of average rates, it carries units, and on the plant floor it is the difference between watching a number and reading its intent.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • Limits (Lesson 2) — the derivative is literally a limit; no limit, no slope.
  • Slope of a line — rise over run; the derivative generalizes it to curves.
  • Function notation (Lesson 1) — \( T'(t) \) is itself a new function of time.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Time-to-alarm from a fitted trend

Problem. A gearbox housing temperature trend fits \( T(t) = 40 + 0.8t + 0.05t^2 \) (°C, \(t\) in hours since the shift began). Site practice flags any heating rate above 2.5 °C/h. Find: the rate at \( t=10\ \mathrm{h} \) and the time the rate limit is reached.

Solution. (1) Differentiate: \( T'(t) = 0.8 + 0.1t \). (2) At \( t=10 \): \( T'(10) = 0.8 + 1.0 = 1.8\ \mathrm{°C/h} \) — under the limit. (3) Set \( 0.8 + 0.1t = 2.5 \Rightarrow t = 17\ \mathrm{h} \).

Answer: 1.8 °C/h now; the 2.5 °C/h threshold arrives at \( t = 17\ \mathrm{h} \). Sanity check: the rate grows 0.1 °C/h every hour; from 1.8 at \(t=10\), seven more hours adds 0.7 → 2.5. Consistent.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Filling rate from level

Problem. A vertical cylindrical tank, diameter 3.0 m, shows level rising at 0.40 m per minute. Find: the inflow in m³/h.

Solution. (1) \( V = A h \) with \( A = \pi D^2/4 = \pi (3.0)^2/4 = 7.07\ \mathrm{m^2} \). (2) Chain of rates: \( \dot V = A \, \dot h = 7.07 \times 0.40 = 2.83\ \mathrm{m^3/min} \). (3) Convert: \( 2.83 \times 60 = 170\ \mathrm{m^3/h} \).

Answer: ≈170 m³/h. Sanity check: the tank gains 24 m³ every 10 minutes — about 3.4 m of level per hour, which a 3 m-diameter receiving tank plausibly does during transfer. Units chain cleanly: m² × m/min → m³/min.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

A derivative from historian samples

Problem. A vibration trend is sampled weekly: \( V = 2.0,\ 2.3,\ 2.9 \) mm/s at \( t = 0, 1, 2 \) weeks. Find: the rate of change at \( t = 1 \) week by forward difference and by central difference, with units, and state which estimate to trust on a smooth trend.

Solution. (1) Forward difference: \( (2.9 - 2.3)/1 = 0.60 \) mm/s per week. (2) Central difference: \( (2.9 - 2.0)/2 = 0.45 \) mm/s per week. (3) On smooth data the central difference is the better tangent estimate — its first-order errors cancel; the forward difference looks half a step into the future and overstates a rising, accelerating trend.

Answer: forward 0.60, central 0.45 mm/s per week; prefer the central estimate mid-record. Sanity check: the weekly increments are 0.3 then 0.6 — the instantaneous rate at \( t=1 \) should sit between them, and 0.45 does.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A machine's vibration trend has been fitted as \( V(t) = 1.2 + 0.03t^2 \) (mm/s, with \( t \) in weeks since installation). What is the instantaneous rate of change of vibration at \( t = 5 \) weeks?

Differentiating gives \( V'(t) = 0.06t \), so \( V'(5) = 0.30 \) mm/s per week. The distractor 0.15 comes from dropping the factor of 2 when differentiating \( t^2 \), and 1.95 is the value \( V(5) \) itself — confusing where the trend is with how fast it is moving. Answering 0.06 means the derivative was formed correctly but never evaluated at \( t = 5 \).

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

A pump bearing's temperature follows the fitted trend \( T(t) = 35 + 1.2t + 0.04t^2 \) (°C, \( t \) in hours since start-up). Site practice requires intervention once the heating rate reaches 3.0 °C/h. At what time does the rate reach that limit?

The rate is \( T'(t) = 1.2 + 0.08t \); setting \( 1.2 + 0.08t = 3.0 \) gives \( t = 1.8/0.08 = 22.5 \) h — the derivative turns the curve into a deadline. The distractor 45 h comes from differentiating \( 0.04t^2 \) as \( 0.04t \) (dropping the factor of 2), and 37.5 h comes from dividing 3.0 by 0.08 without first subtracting the constant rate term 1.2 °C/h.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A bearing temperature \( T(t) \) is logged with \( T \) in °C and \( t \) in hours. What are the units of \( T'(t) \)?

The derivative is a limit of \( \Delta T/\Delta t \), so its units are the ratio of the units: °C per hour. A derivative always carries units, and stating them is not pedantry — it is what turns "the trend is 1.8" into the actionable "the housing is heating at 1.8 °C every hour." Answering °C confuses the rate with the reading; dimensionless confuses mathematics with the pure-number world it models.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A recorded trend is continuous at \( t = t_0 \) but shows a sharp corner there, like \( |t - t_0| \)-shaped behaviour after a sudden load change. Which statement is correct?

At a corner the limit defining the derivative fails: the left and right difference quotients approach different values, yet the function value has no jump — continuous but not differentiable. The classic error is reversing the implication: differentiability implies continuity, never the converse. Averaging the two one-sided slopes is a tempting repair, but the defining limit simply does not exist, so no derivative can be assigned.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Between \( t = 2 \) h and \( t = 6 \) h a trend rises from 10 to 22 units — an average rate of 3 units/h. What can be said about the instantaneous rate at \( t = 4 \) h?

An average over an interval constrains nothing at any particular interior instant: the trend could be rising at 6 units/h at \( t=4 \) and crawling elsewhere, or vice versa. (The mean value theorem promises the instantaneous rate equals 3 somewhere in the interval — but not at any point you get to choose.) Reading an interval average as a point value is the classic trend-review error this lesson exists to retire.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — two identical numbers, one work order. You arrive for the monthly route at a cable plant's drawing line and both gearboxes read 3.9 mm/s. The history tells them apart: unit A has sat between 3.7 and 4.0 for two years (\( dV/dt \approx 0 \)); unit B was at 2.1 six weeks ago — a slope of ~0.3 mm/s per week and climbing. Same value, different derivative, different decision: A stays on route, B gets an inspection window before the next production campaign. When you defend that call in the morning meeting, the argument is equation (1), spoken in plain language: it isn't where the number is, it's how fast it's moving.
