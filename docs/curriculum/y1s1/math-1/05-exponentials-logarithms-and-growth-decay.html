Exponentials, logarithms, and growth/decay

e and ln as the natural language of charging, cooling, and decay processes. Log scales for spanning decades of data. Semilog reading of exponential trends — the shape behind Newton cooling and RC circuits.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why do cooling curves plot straight on semilog axes, and what does the slope encode?
  • What does a time constant of 5 minutes say about how long until a sensor reads ~63% of a step change?

§1 · The function that is its own derivative

One function grows at a rate equal to its own size: \( \frac{d}{dt}e^{t} = e^{t} \). Scale time by a constant and \( \frac{d}{dt}e^{kt} = k e^{kt} \): growth or decay proportional to the current amount. That single property makes \( e^{kt} \) the native language of charging capacitors, cooling castings, decaying vibration transients, draining accumulators, and compounding costs.

§2 · Decay with an offset — the plant's favorite curve

A hot object doesn't decay to zero; it decays to ambient. The working form is

\[ T(t) = T_\infty + (T_0 - T_\infty)\,e^{-t/\tau} \tag{1} \]

where \( \tau \) is the time constant: after one \( \tau \), 63% of the gap to ambient is closed; after 3\( \tau \), 95%; after 5\( \tau \), it's over. Ask "how many time constants?" and any first-order transient becomes readable at a glance.

§3 · Logarithms — solving for time

The logarithm inverts the exponential; it is how you extract when:

\[ t = \tau \,\ln\!\frac{T_0 - T_\infty}{T(t) - T_\infty} \tag{2} \]

Logs also compress scale (decibels, pH, Richter) and straighten exponentials: plot \( \ln(T - T_\infty) \) against \( t \) and first-order data falls on a line whose slope is \( -1/\tau \). Fitting a straight line to a log plot is how you measure a time constant from plant data.

Closing. \( e^{kt} \) encodes self-proportional change; \( \tau \) makes transients countable; logarithms solve for time and straighten curves so data confesses its constants.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • Chain rule (Lesson 4) — \( \frac{d}{dt}e^{-t/\tau} = -\tfrac{1}{\tau}e^{-t/\tau} \).
  • Log laws — \( \ln(ab) = \ln a + \ln b \), \( \ln e^x = x \); equation (2) is these laws at work.
  • Limits (Lesson 2) — \( e^{-t/\tau}\to 0 \): the transient's destiny is ambient.

Glossary

  • Time constant \( \tau \) — time to close 63% of the remaining gap; the transient's fingerprint.
  • Asymptote — the value approached but never crossed; here, ambient.
  • Half-life — time to halve; \( t_{1/2} = \tau \ln 2 \approx 0.693\,\tau \).
  • Semilog plot — log axis that renders exponentials straight.

Worked Examples

Worked example 1 — billet cooling window

Problem. A steel billet leaves a cooling bed model at \( T_0 = 900\ \mathrm{°C} \) into \( T_\infty = 30\ \mathrm{°C} \) surroundings with \( \tau = 45 \) min (representative first-order fit). Find: temperature after 60 min, and the time to reach 100 °C for safe handling.

Solution. (1) \( T(60) = 30 + 870\,e^{-60/45} = 30 + 870\,e^{-1.333} \). (2) \( e^{-1.333} = 0.264 \Rightarrow T(60) = 30 + 229 = 259\ \mathrm{°C} \). (3) For 100 °C, equation (2): \( t = 45 \ln\frac{870}{70} = 45 \ln 12.43 = 45 \times 2.52 = 113\ \mathrm{min} \).

Answer: ≈259 °C after an hour; ≈113 min (≈2.5\( \tau \)) to 100 °C. Sanity check: 100 °C sits 92% of the way to ambient, and 2.5 time constants closes ~92% of the gap. Internally consistent.

Worked example 2 — doubling time from a growth rate

Problem. Plant electricity tariff escalates 3% per year. Find: the doubling time of the bill at constant consumption.

Solution. (1) Growth: \( C = C_0(1.03)^n \). Doubling when \( (1.03)^n = 2 \). (2) \( n = \ln 2 / \ln 1.03 = 0.6931 / 0.02956 = 23.4 \) years.

Answer: ≈23 years. Sanity check: the rule of 70 gives \( 70/3 \approx 23.3 \) — the shortcut and the logarithm agree.

Kuwait Floor

Vignette — how long until we can touch it? You arrive at a melt-shop planning meeting: a repair crew needs billets on the cooling bed handled "as soon as possible." The shift engineer guesses; you fit instead. Two historian points — 620 °C at 30 min, 259 °C at 60 min — on a semilog plot of \( T-30 \) give a straight line whose slope yields \( \tau \approx 45 \) min, and equation (2) turns that into a committed answer: just under two hours to 100 °C. Scheduling gets a number with a derivation behind it instead of a shrug, and nobody gloves up against a 260-degree surface.
