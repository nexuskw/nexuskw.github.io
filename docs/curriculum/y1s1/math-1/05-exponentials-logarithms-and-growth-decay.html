Exponentials, logarithms, and growth/decay

e and ln as the natural language of charging, cooling, and decay processes. Log scales for spanning decades of data. Semilog reading of exponential trends — the shape behind Newton cooling and RC circuits.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why do cooling curves plot straight on semilog axes, and what does the slope encode?
  • What does a time constant of 5 minutes say about how long until a sensor reads ~63% of a step change?

01 · The function that is its own derivative

One function grows at a rate equal to its own size: \( \frac{d}{dt}e^{t} = e^{t} \). Scale time by a constant and \( \frac{d}{dt}e^{kt} = k e^{kt} \): growth or decay proportional to the current amount. That single property makes \( e^{kt} \) the native language of charging capacitors, cooling castings, decaying vibration transients, draining accumulators, and compounding costs.

02 · Decay with an offset — the plant's favorite curve

A hot object doesn't decay to zero; it decays to ambient. The working form is

\[ T(t) = T_\infty + (T_0 - T_\infty)\,e^{-t/\tau} \tag{1} \]

where \( \tau \) is the time constant: after one \( \tau \), 63% of the gap to ambient is closed; after 3\( \tau \), 95%; after 5\( \tau \), it's over. Ask "how many time constants?" and any first-order transient becomes readable at a glance.

03 · Logarithms — solving for time

The logarithm inverts the exponential; it is how you extract when:

\[ t = \tau \,\ln\!\frac{T_0 - T_\infty}{T(t) - T_\infty} \tag{2} \]

Logs also compress scale (decibels, pH, Richter) and straighten exponentials: plot \( \ln(T - T_\infty) \) against \( t \) and first-order data falls on a line whose slope is \( -1/\tau \). Fitting a straight line to a log plot is how you measure a time constant from plant data.

04 · The transient protocol

Every first-order transient yields to the same four questions, asked in order. What is the destination? Identify \( T_\infty \) — ambient, setpoint, asymptote — because everything is measured as a gap from it. What is the gap? Compute the current excess over the destination; the transient's whole story is this gap shrinking. What is the tempo? Get \( \tau \) — from a datasheet, from a fit, or from data via the semilog method of 3: plot \( \ln(\text{gap}) \) against time, and the slope is \( -1/\tau \). When? Turn any target into time with \( t = \tau\,\ln(\text{gap}_0/\text{gap}_{target}) \), or count in the coarse currency — one \( \tau \) closes 63% of the gap, three close 95%, five end the story.

The same machinery runs upward for growth. Compounding at rate \( r \) per period doubles in \( \ln 2/\ln(1+r) \) periods — the honest version of the rule of 70 — and any quantity that grows in proportion to itself, from an escalating tariff to a fouling deposit, is the exponential wearing different clothes. When data arrives, straighten it first: if the semilog plot is not a line, the process is not first-order, and no amount of \( \tau \)-talk will make it one. The plot is the test; the constants come after.

Know the honest failure modes too. A curve on the semilog plot usually means two time constants stacked — a fast surface effect riding on a slow bulk one — and a flat early section means deadtime: transport delay before the response begins. Both are information, not nuisance. Report what the data shows, fit the region the model genuinely describes, and say so; a stated approximation is engineering, an unstated one is luck.

Closing. \( e^{kt} \) encodes self-proportional change; \( \tau \) makes transients countable; logarithms solve for time and straighten curves so data confesses its constants.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • Chain rule (Lesson 4) — \( \tfrac{d}{dt}e^{-t/\tau} = -\tfrac{1}{\tau}e^{-t/\tau} \).
  • Log laws — \( \ln(ab) = \ln a + \ln b \), \( \ln e^x = x \).
  • Limits (Lesson 2) — \( e^{-t/\tau}\to 0 \): the transient's destiny is ambient.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Billet cooling window

Problem. A steel billet leaves a cooling bed model at \( T_0 = 900\ \mathrm{°C} \) into \( T_\infty = 30\ \mathrm{°C} \) surroundings with \( \tau = 45 \) min (representative first-order fit). Find: temperature after 60 min, and the time to reach 100 °C for safe handling.

Solution. (1) \( T(60) = 30 + 870\,e^{-60/45} = 30 + 870\,e^{-1.333} \). (2) \( e^{-1.333} = 0.264 \Rightarrow T(60) = 30 + 229 = 259\ \mathrm{°C} \). (3) For 100 °C, equation (2): \( t = 45 \ln\frac{870}{70} = 45 \ln 12.43 = 45 \times 2.52 = 113\ \mathrm{min} \).

Answer: ≈259 °C after an hour; ≈113 min (≈2.5\( \tau \)) to 100 °C. Sanity check: 100 °C sits 92% of the way to ambient, and 2.5 time constants closes ~92% of the gap. Internally consistent.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Doubling time from a growth rate

Problem. Plant electricity tariff escalates 3% per year. Find: the doubling time of the bill at constant consumption.

Solution. (1) Growth: \( C = C_0(1.03)^n \). Doubling when \( (1.03)^n = 2 \). (2) \( n = \ln 2 / \ln 1.03 = 0.6931 / 0.02956 = 23.4 \) years.

Answer: ≈23 years. Sanity check: the rule of 70 gives \( 70/3 \approx 23.3 \) — the shortcut and the logarithm agree.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

A fouling filter on the calendar

Problem. A filter's differential pressure grows 5% per week from a clean-condition \( \Delta P_0 = 0.30 \) bar. The changeout alarm is set at 0.60 bar. Find: how many weeks until the alarm, assuming the compounding trend holds.

Solution. (1) Model: \( \Delta P = 0.30\,(1.05)^n \). Alarm when \( (1.05)^n = 2 \). (2) Take logarithms: \( n = \ln 2/\ln 1.05 = 0.6931/0.04879 = 14.2 \) weeks.

Answer: ≈14 weeks — a doubling time, read straight off the growth rate. Sanity check: the rule of 70 gives \( 70/5 = 14 \) — shortcut and logarithm agree, so the maintenance plan can commit to a changeout window around week 13 with margin.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A thermocouple with first-order time constant \( \tau = 8\ \mathrm{s} \) reads 20 °C when it is plunged into an oil bath held at 80 °C. What does it read 12 s later?

The first-order response is \( T(t) = T_\infty + (T_0 - T_\infty)e^{-t/\tau} = 80 - 60\,e^{-1.5} \approx 66.6 \) °C. The 57.8 °C distractor applies the one-time-constant 63% rule to an interval of \( 1.5\tau \); that figure holds only at exactly one \( \tau \). 110 °C extrapolates the initial rate linearly, ignoring that the driving gap shrinks as the reading approaches the bath, and 33.4 °C swaps the roles of \( T_0 \) and \( T_\infty \).

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

A casting's cooling transient is logged and \( \ln(T - T_\infty) \) is plotted against time; the points fall on a straight line of slope \( -0.040\ \mathrm{min^{-1}} \). What is the half-life of the transient, i.e. the time for the remaining temperature gap to halve?

The semilog slope equals \( -1/\tau \), so \( \tau = 1/0.040 = 25 \) min, and the half-life is \( t_{1/2} = \tau \ln 2 \approx 17.3 \) min. Choosing 25 min confuses the time constant (63% of the gap closed) with the half-life (50% closed). 36.1 min inverts the relation as \( \tau/\ln 2 \), and 12.5 min guesses \( \tau/2 \) in place of \( \tau \ln 2 \).

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A first-order transient has run for exactly three time constants. Roughly what fraction of the initial gap to the destination remains?

The remaining fraction decays as \( e^{-t/\tau} \); at \( t = 3\tau \) that is \( e^{-3} \approx 0.050 \) — about 5% left, 95% closed. Answering 63% or 37% swaps the closed and remaining fractions at one time constant into the wrong slot; 95% states what is done, not what remains. The 63/95/99 ladder at \( 1\tau/3\tau/5\tau \) is worth owning cold — it is the transient-reading currency of every discipline downstream.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

You must confirm that a cooling trend is first-order and extract its time constant graphically. Which plot renders the data straight, and what does its slope encode?

Since \( T - T_\infty = (T_0 - T_\infty)e^{-t/\tau} \), taking logarithms gives \( \ln(T - T_\infty) = \ln(T_0 - T_\infty) - t/\tau \): a straight line in \( t \) with slope \( -1/\tau \). Plotting \( \ln T \) without subtracting ambient is the classic error — the offset \( T_\infty \) leaves the curve bent, because the pure exponential is the gap, not the temperature itself. A slope of \( -\tau \) inverts the fingerprint, and \( T \) versus \( \ln t \) straightens logarithmic trends, not exponential ones.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

For a first-order transient, how long does closing 99% of the gap take, expressed in time constants?

Requiring \( e^{-t/\tau} = 0.01 \) gives \( t = \tau\ln 100 \approx 4.6\,\tau \) — the origin of the field rule that five time constants end the story. \( \tau\ln 10 \) closes only 90%; \( \tau\ln 2 \) is the half-life; and \( 99\tau \) misreads the exponential as linear, the very misreading the time-constant idea exists to prevent.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — how long until we can touch it? You arrive at a melt-shop planning meeting: a repair crew needs billets on the cooling bed handled "as soon as possible." The shift engineer guesses; you fit instead. Two historian points — 620 °C at 30 min, 259 °C at 60 min — on a semilog plot of \( T-30 \) give a straight line whose slope yields \( \tau \approx 45 \) min, and equation (2) turns that into a committed answer: just under two hours to 100 °C. Scheduling gets a number with a derivation behind it instead of a shrug, and nobody gloves up against a 260-degree surface.
