First-order differential equations

Separable and linear first-order ODEs. Tank mixing, Newton cooling, and first-order sensor lag solved end to end. Time constants as the physical meaning of the solution.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Newton cooling: write the ODE and say what each symbol claims physically.
  • Two mixing tanks differ only in volume: which has the longer time constant, and why?

01 · Equations that contain their own rates

A differential equation relates a quantity to its own rate of change. The plant's archetype: a hot mass losing heat in proportion to its excess over ambient,

\[ \frac{dT}{dt} = -\frac{T - T_\infty}{\tau} \tag{1} \]

Read it aloud: the hotter it is above ambient, the faster it cools. Most first-order plant dynamics — thermal soak, tank draining, filter loading, RC circuits, instrument response — are equation (1) with different nouns.

02 · Solving by separation

Separable equations split variables to opposite sides and integrate:

\[ \int\frac{dT}{T-T_\infty} = -\int\frac{dt}{\tau} \;\Rightarrow\; \ln|T-T_\infty| = -\frac{t}{\tau}+C \;\Rightarrow\; T = T_\infty + (T_0-T_\infty)e^{-t/\tau} \tag{2} \]

Lesson 5's cooling curve wasn't handed down; it is derived here, in three lines, from a physical statement. The constant of integration is fixed by the initial condition \( T(0) = T_0 \) — every first-order ODE needs one known point to pin its trajectory.

03 · Reading dynamics without solving

Much of an ODE's story is available without solution. Set the rate to zero and you find equilibria (here \( T = T_\infty \)). The sign of the rate tells direction of travel; its size sets speed. The coefficient \( 1/\tau \) is the speed of the whole approach — measure it from data and you know the system's tempo. Engineers read ODEs more often than they solve them; the solving builds the reading instinct.

04 · From balance statement to equation — the modeling method

First-order models are not found; they are written, and the writing follows one template. State the balance: rate of change of the stored quantity equals rate in minus rate out. Express each rate in the state variable: outflow proportional to level, heat loss proportional to excess temperature, contaminant leaving at concentration times flow. Divide through until the equation reads \( \dot{y} = -(y - y_\infty)/\tau \), and read \( \tau \) directly from the coefficients — \( V/Q \) for a flushed tank, thermal mass over loss coefficient for a cooling body. The physics enters in the rates; the mathematics is the same three lines every time.

Respect the sign conventions and the model's edges. A negative rate coefficient decays toward equilibrium; a positive one runs away, and recognizing which you have written is a stability judgment made before any solving. Step inputs — a heater switching on, a setpoint moving — reuse the same solution with the roles renamed: the response climbs \( 1 - e^{-t/\tau} \) toward its new destination. And when the data refuses the model — curved semilog plots, delayed starts — the honest conclusion is a second time constant or a deadtime, not a forced fit. Write what the balance supports; report what the data shows.

This template is the most reused page in the course. The same three lines will reappear as the RC circuit in the electrical course, the thermal soak in heat transfer, the flushed volume in fluid mechanics, and the instrument lag in controls — identical mathematics under different nouns. Learn to see the balance statement through the surface vocabulary and every one of those courses starts a lesson ahead.

Closing. First-order ODEs encode self-referential change; separation solves the classics; initial conditions select the one real trajectory among the many possible.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Flushing a tank clean

Problem. A 10 m³ well-mixed tank of contaminated water (concentration \( C_0 \)) is flushed with clean water at 0.5 m³/min, overflowing at the same rate. Find: \( C(t) \) and the time to reach 10% of \( C_0 \).

Solution. (1) Mass balance: \( V\frac{dC}{dt} = -QC \Rightarrow \frac{dC}{dt} = -\frac{Q}{V}C = -0.05\,C \) (per minute). (2) Separate and solve: \( C = C_0 e^{-0.05t} \). (3) Set \( 0.1 = e^{-0.05t} \Rightarrow t = \ln 10 / 0.05 = 46.1\ \mathrm{min} \).

Answer: \( C(t)=C_0e^{-0.05t} \); ≈46 minutes to 10%. Sanity check: \( \tau = V/Q = 20 \) min; 10% requires \( \ln 10 = 2.30 \) time constants → 46 min. Two roads, one number.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Time constant from two data points

Problem. A motor winding, tripped at \( t=0 \), reads 95 °C at \( t=10 \) min and 65 °C at \( t=30 \) min; ambient is 35 °C. Assuming first-order cooling, find \( \tau \) and the temperature at \( t=60 \) min.

Solution. (1) Excess temperatures: 60 °C and 30 °C — halved in 20 minutes. (2) Half-life relation: \( t_{1/2} = \tau\ln 2 \Rightarrow \tau = 20/0.693 = 28.9\ \mathrm{min} \). (3) At \( t=60 \): excess \( = 60 \cdot e^{-(60-10)/28.9} = 60\,e^{-1.732} = 60 \times 0.177 = 10.6 \) °C → \( T = 45.6\ \mathrm{°C} \).

Answer: \( \tau \approx 29 \) min; ≈46 °C an hour after trip. Sanity check: from 30 min to 60 min is ~one half-life plus a bit: 30 °C excess → ~10.6 °C. Consistent with halving each 20 minutes.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Heating toward an asymptote

Problem. A motor winding heats toward a steady temperature rise of 80 K above ambient with time constant \( \tau = 40 \) min after a loaded start, following \( \theta(t) = 80\,(1 - e^{-t/\tau}) \). Find: when the rise reaches 60 K, and the rise after one hour.

Solution. (1) \( 60 = 80(1 - e^{-t/40}) \Rightarrow e^{-t/40} = 0.25 \Rightarrow t = 40\ln 4 = 55.4\ \mathrm{min} \). (2) At \( t = 60 \): \( \theta = 80(1 - e^{-1.5}) = 80(1 - 0.223) = 62.1\ \mathrm{K} \).

Answer: 60 K at ≈55 min; ≈62 K after an hour. Sanity check: 60 K is 75% of the step, and 75% completion should take about \( 1.4\tau \) \( (\ln 4 = 1.386) \) — 55 minutes on a 40-minute time constant. The step response is the cooling curve read upward: same equation, destination above instead of below.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A temperature sensor behaves as a first-order system with time constant \( \tau = 4\ \mathrm{s} \). At \( t = 0 \) it is plunged from one steady bath into another, a step change in its input. How long until the reading has completed 95% of the step?

The unfinished fraction of the step decays as \( e^{-t/\tau} \), so 95% completion requires \( e^{-t/\tau} = 0.05 \), giving \( t = \tau\ln 20 \approx 3\tau = 12\ \mathrm{s} \). Choosing 4.0 s commits the classic error of reading \( \tau \) itself as the settling time; one time constant completes only 63.2% of the step. The 20 s choice applies the five-time-constant criterion, which corresponds to 99.3%, stricter than what was asked.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

After a trip, a pump casing's excess temperature above ambient is observed to halve every 15 minutes. Assuming first-order (Newton) cooling, what is the time constant \( \tau \)?

For a first-order decay the half-life satisfies \( t_{1/2} = \tau\ln 2 \), so \( \tau = 15/\ln 2 \approx 21.6\ \mathrm{min} \). Taking \( \tau = 15 \) min equates the half-life with the time constant — the classic slip, since one time constant leaves \( e^{-1} \approx 36.8\% \) of the excess, not 50%. The 10.4 min choice inverts the relation by computing \( 15 \times \ln 2 \) instead of dividing by it.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A body cools per \( \dfrac{dT}{dt} = -\dfrac{T - 25}{10} \) (°C, minutes). At the instant \( T = 45 \) °C, what is the cooling rate?

Substitute directly: \( dT/dt = -(45 - 25)/10 = -2.0 \) °C/min — a 20-degree excess divided by a 10-minute time constant, falling. The positive option drops the sign that makes it cooling; −4.5 divides the full temperature 45 by 10, forgetting that the equation runs on the excess over ambient; −0.5 inverts excess and time constant. Reading rates straight off the ODE, no solving, is the skill this equation rewards.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

For the cooling equation \( \frac{dT}{dt} = -\frac{T - T_\infty}{\tau} \), what does setting the rate to zero identify, and how does the solution behave with respect to it?

Zeroing the rate gives \( T = T_\infty \), the equilibrium: because the rate shrinks in proportion to the remaining excess, the trajectory decays exponentially toward it and never crosses or reaches it in finite time. The second choice commits the classic error of treating \( \tau \) as an arrival time — at \( t = \tau \) only about 63% of the excess has decayed. The initial condition comes from a measured starting point, not from zeroing the rate; it selects which trajectory occurs, not where trajectories rest.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

The general solution of a first-order ODE is a family of curves. What does the initial condition do?

The equation fixes the shape of all possible histories; the initial condition picks the one that passes through the known starting state — that is why every real prediction needs a measured starting point. The equilibrium and the time constant are properties of the equation itself (its coefficients), not of where you start; and the exponential term is the transient, removed only by waiting, not by initial conditions.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — when can we megger it? You arrive after a 400 kW motor trip; electrical wants winding temperature below 50 °C before insulation-resistance testing, and production wants the answer now. Two thermocouple readings, the half-life trick from worked example 2, and equation (2) give a defensible ETA — about an hour — instead of somebody's guess. You post the cooling curve on the permit desk; the test starts on schedule; nobody meggered a hot winding and nobody stood idle an extra shift. One separable ODE, one better afternoon.
