Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Functions, units, and engineering magnitudes

01 · Numbers that carry units

Engineering mathematics begins one step before calculus: every quantity you will ever differentiate or integrate carries a unit, and the units obey algebra just as the numbers do. When a pump curve says head is 42 m, that number is meaningless until you know it multiplies through \( P = \rho g Q H \) to give watts:

\[ [P] = \frac{\mathrm{kg}}{\mathrm{m}^3}\cdot\frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}^2}\cdot\frac{\mathrm{m}^3}{\mathrm{s}}\cdot \mathrm{m} = \frac{\mathrm{kg\,m^2}}{\mathrm{s}^3} = \mathrm{W} \tag{1} \]

Check the units first and half of all calculation errors die before they are born. This is the dimensional analysis discipline: both sides of any physical equation must carry identical dimensions, and arguments of \( \sin \), \( \ln \), \( e^x \) must be dimensionless.

02 · Functions as machine behavior

A function \( f(x) \) is a machine's behavior written down: give it a flow, it returns a pressure drop; give it a time, it returns a temperature. The pump curve \( H(Q) \), the motor torque curve \( T(\omega) \), the cooling curve \( T(t) \) — the whole plant is a library of functions. Domain matters physically: \( H(Q) \) exists only for \( Q \ge 0 \), and beyond runout flow the curve is not merely undefined but forbidden.

03 · Magnitudes and scales

An engineer estimates before calculating. Powers of ten are the estimating language: a 100 m³/h pump moves \(\sim\)28 L/s; a kilowatt is ten 100 W lamps; a bar is \(\sim\)10 m of water column. On the plant floor this means you can smell a wrong answer: if a small transfer pump computes to 300 kW, the arithmetic is wrong before you re-check it — the machine on the skid is the size of a suitcase, and suitcases don't dissipate 300 kW.

04 · The unit-check working method

Knowing that units must balance is not the same as having a method. Use this three-step protocol on every equation you meet, in this course and every one after it. Step one: write the equation symbolically and substitute units, not numbers — run the algebra on the units alone and confirm both sides match, exactly as equation (1) did for pump power. Step two: convert every input to base SI before any arithmetic: flows to m³/s, pressures to Pa, lengths to m. Most real-world calculation errors are unit errors, and most unit errors happen when kPa meets Pa or m³/h meets m³/s in the same line. Step three: after computing, translate the answer back into the working units of the trade — kW, bar, m³/h — and test it against an order-of-magnitude estimate before you write it down.

Two traps deserve names. The gauge trap: gauge and absolute pressure differ by one atmosphere, and equations built on absolute pressure quietly fail when fed gauge readings. The prefix trap: a dropped factor of 1000 from k, M, or m survives every later step and grows more embarrassing the longer it lives. Scientific notation is the antidote — write \( 2.5\times10^5 \) Pa and exponents stay visible and checkable.

Closing. Units are algebra, functions are machine behavior, and order-of-magnitude sense is the cheapest error detector you will ever own. Every lesson that follows leans on all three.

Lesson 02 · Limits and continuity

01 · Why limits exist in a plant

A limit answers one question: what value does a function approach as its input approaches a point? Formally, \( \lim_{x\to a} f(x) = L \) means we can force \( f(x) \) arbitrarily close to \( L \) by taking \( x \) close enough to \( a \). The definition sounds abstract; the plant meaning is concrete: what does the sensor settle toward, what does the ratio become, what happens at the operating edge?

02 · The ratio that defines everything

The most important limit in this course is a ratio of vanishing quantities,

\[ \lim_{\theta\to 0}\frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} = 1 \tag{1} \]

Try it numerically: at \( \theta = 0.1 \) rad the ratio is 0.9983; at 0.01, 0.99998. The two quantities shrink together but their ratio converges — that is the whole mental model for the derivative in the next lesson, where a vanishing rise over a vanishing run converges to a slope.

03 · Continuity — and what it means for signals

\( f \) is continuous at \( a \) when \( \lim_{x\to a} f(x) = f(a) \): no jump, no hole, no blow-up. Physical processes — temperature, level, pressure — are continuous; their measurements need not be. A thermocouple that jumps 40 °C between scans hasn't witnessed magic; it has witnessed electrical noise, a loose connection, or a step change in what is being touched. Knowing which physical variables can and cannot jump is a diagnostic tool: pressure can step (valve slam), metal temperature cannot.

One-sided limits close the lesson: a relay approaching its trip point from below and from above sees two different worlds, \( \lim_{x\to a^-} \) and \( \lim_{x\to a^+} \). Hysteresis is one-sided limits wearing work clothes.

04 · How to evaluate a limit — the decision tree

Faced with a limit, work a fixed sequence. First, substitute. If the function is continuous there, the limit is the value and you are done — most limits in practice end here. Second, if substitution returns 0/0, the form is telling you a common factor is hiding: factor polynomials, or multiply by the conjugate when square roots appear, cancel, and substitute again. Third, check the sides. When the expression involves absolute values, piecewise definitions, or a physical threshold, compute the left and right limits separately; the two-sided limit exists only when they agree. Fourth, verify numerically. Evaluate the expression at inputs marching toward the point — 0.1, 0.01, 0.001 away — and watch the trend. This is not cheating; it is how an engineer confirms an algebraic result the same way a technician confirms a calibration: with converging evidence.

Use continuity as a diagnostic tool the same disciplined way. Before blaming a sensor or believing a spike, ask which physical variables can genuinely jump (pressure, force, current) and which are integrals of finite flows and therefore cannot (metal temperature, tank level, accumulated volume). The mathematics sorts plant events from instrument events before the first work order is written.

Closing. Limits formalize approach; continuity formalizes no surprises. Tomorrow they combine into the derivative.

Lesson 03 · The derivative as a rate

01 · From average rate to instantaneous rate

Between Monday and Friday a bearing housing warmed from 62 °C to 74 °C: average rate 3 °C/day. But right now, this hour, how fast is it heating? Squeeze the interval and the average rate converges — Lesson 2's limit — to the instantaneous rate:

\[ \frac{dT}{dt} = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0}\frac{T(t+\Delta t)-T(t)}{\Delta t} \tag{1} \]

That is the derivative: the local slope of the trend, the speed of change at an instant. Units divide: °C per hour, m³ per second, mm/s of vibration per week. A derivative always has units, and always tells a maintenance story.

02 · The derivative as the trend's confession

Condition monitoring is derivative-reading as a profession. A flat vibration trend with \( dV/dt \approx 0 \) buys you time; the same amplitude with a steep positive slope buys you a work order. Two machines at identical 4 mm/s are in different worlds if one arrived there over three years and the other over three weeks. Value tells you where it is; the derivative tells you where it's going.

03 · Computing from a model

Given a fitted trend \( T(t) = 40 + 0.8t + 0.05t^2 \) (°C, hours), the power rule (proved next lesson) gives \( T'(t) = 0.8 + 0.1t \): the heating accelerates, because the rate itself grows. Set \( T'(t) \) against an alarm rate and you get a time-to-act, not just a number: solve \( 0.8+0.1t = 2.5 \) → \( t = 17\ \mathrm{h} \). The derivative converts a curve into a deadline.

04 · Computing derivatives from data — the working method

Real trends arrive as samples, not formulas, so the working derivative is a finite difference. From the two latest points you can only form the forward-looking secant \( (f_{now} - f_{prev})/\Delta t \); mid-record, prefer the central difference \( (f_{i+1} - f_{i-1})/(2\Delta t) \), which cancels the first layer of error and tracks the true tangent far better on smooth data. Three disciplines make the estimates trustworthy. State the units — a slope is always something per something, and writing mm/s per week forces the physical meaning into view. Respect noise: differentiation amplifies it, so difference over intervals long enough for the real change to dominate the scatter; a derivative computed from two noisy readings an hour apart is an opinion, not a measurement. Distinguish model from data: when a curve has been fitted, differentiate the model symbolically and use the data-side difference as its check — when the two disagree badly, the fit, the data, or the machine has changed, and every one of those is worth knowing.

Then read the result the way 2 taught: the value says where the machine is, the derivative says where it is going, and the difference between those two sentences is the difference between recording a number and making a decision.

Closing. The derivative is a limit of average rates, it carries units, and on the plant floor it is the difference between watching a number and reading its intent.

Lesson 04 · Differentiation rules and the chain rule

01 · The rules — machinery for slopes

Yesterday's limit definition is correct and slow. Today we industrialize it. For constants \(c\), functions \(u, v\):

\[ (cu)' = cu', \qquad (u+v)' = u' + v', \qquad (x^n)' = nx^{n-1} \tag{1} \] \[ (uv)' = u'v + uv', \qquad \left(\frac{u}{v}\right)' = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2} \tag{2} \]

Each is proved once from the limit and used forever. The power rule follows from expanding \( (x+\Delta x)^n \) and watching every term above first order vanish with \( \Delta x \) — the same "higher-order terms die" argument that engineering approximation lives on.

02 · The chain rule — rates through a chain of causes

Plant variables cause each other in chains: valve position sets flow, flow sets level, level sets pressure. If \( y = f(u) \) and \( u = g(x) \):

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx} \tag{3} \]

Rates multiply along the chain. This single rule powers every sensitivity argument you will make: how much does discharge pressure move per percent of speed? Differentiate through the chain of dependencies and multiply the local sensitivities.

03 · Relative rates — the engineer's favorite

Divide a derivative by its function and you get relative sensitivity. For a fan law \( P = kN^3 \), logarithmic differentiation gives \( \frac{dP}{P} = 3\frac{dN}{N} \): a 2% speed increase costs ≈6% more power ((1.02)³ = 1.061 exactly). This is why slowing a fan or pump slightly is the cheapest energy project in any plant — the cube works for you on the way down.

04 · Choosing the rule — structure before symbols

Differentiation errors rarely come from forgetting a rule; they come from misreading the structure of the expression. So classify before you differentiate. Is the expression a sum of powers? The power rule handles each term independently — after rewriting roots and reciprocals as powers, \( \sqrt{x} = x^{1/2} \), \( 1/x = x^{-1} \). Is it two varying quantities multiplied — force times velocity, pressure times flow? That is a genuine product: product rule. A ratio with the variable in the denominator? Quotient rule, with the subtraction order fixed. A function of a function — something raised to a power, an exponential of an expression? Chain rule, differentiating outer times inner, working from the outside in.

For the power laws that fill engineering — \( P = kN^3 \), \( \Delta P = kQ^2 \) — logarithmic differentiation earns its keep: take logs, differentiate, and relative rates appear directly, \( dP/P = n\,dN/N \). That one line is the professional's sensitivity language: percent out per percent in. Keep the honest distinction from 3 visible — the relative-rate figure is a first-order estimate, exact only in the limit; for large changes, compute the ratio exactly and let the linear estimate serve as its sanity check.

Finally, make numerical verification a reflex. Any symbolic derivative can be audited in ten seconds: evaluate the function at \( x \) and at \( x + 0.001 \), divide the difference by 0.001, and compare with the formula's prediction. Agreement to three figures means the algebra survived; disagreement means a dropped inner derivative or a sign error is hiding in the work. Professionals check their derivatives the way welders check their fit-up — before the error is buried under everything built on top of it.

Closing. Rules (1)–(2) mechanize differentiation; the chain rule (3) walks rates through cause-and-effect; relative rates turn calculus into percentage talk that managers act on.

Lesson 05 · Exponentials, logarithms, and growth/decay

01 · The function that is its own derivative

One function grows at a rate equal to its own size: \( \frac{d}{dt}e^{t} = e^{t} \). Scale time by a constant and \( \frac{d}{dt}e^{kt} = k e^{kt} \): growth or decay proportional to the current amount. That single property makes \( e^{kt} \) the native language of charging capacitors, cooling castings, decaying vibration transients, draining accumulators, and compounding costs.

02 · Decay with an offset — the plant's favorite curve

A hot object doesn't decay to zero; it decays to ambient. The working form is

\[ T(t) = T_\infty + (T_0 - T_\infty)\,e^{-t/\tau} \tag{1} \]

where \( \tau \) is the time constant: after one \( \tau \), 63% of the gap to ambient is closed; after 3\( \tau \), 95%; after 5\( \tau \), it's over. Ask "how many time constants?" and any first-order transient becomes readable at a glance.

03 · Logarithms — solving for time

The logarithm inverts the exponential; it is how you extract when:

\[ t = \tau \,\ln\!\frac{T_0 - T_\infty}{T(t) - T_\infty} \tag{2} \]

Logs also compress scale (decibels, pH, Richter) and straighten exponentials: plot \( \ln(T - T_\infty) \) against \( t \) and first-order data falls on a line whose slope is \( -1/\tau \). Fitting a straight line to a log plot is how you measure a time constant from plant data.

04 · The transient protocol

Every first-order transient yields to the same four questions, asked in order. What is the destination? Identify \( T_\infty \) — ambient, setpoint, asymptote — because everything is measured as a gap from it. What is the gap? Compute the current excess over the destination; the transient's whole story is this gap shrinking. What is the tempo? Get \( \tau \) — from a datasheet, from a fit, or from data via the semilog method of 3: plot \( \ln(\text{gap}) \) against time, and the slope is \( -1/\tau \). When? Turn any target into time with \( t = \tau\,\ln(\text{gap}_0/\text{gap}_{target}) \), or count in the coarse currency — one \( \tau \) closes 63% of the gap, three close 95%, five end the story.

The same machinery runs upward for growth. Compounding at rate \( r \) per period doubles in \( \ln 2/\ln(1+r) \) periods — the honest version of the rule of 70 — and any quantity that grows in proportion to itself, from an escalating tariff to a fouling deposit, is the exponential wearing different clothes. When data arrives, straighten it first: if the semilog plot is not a line, the process is not first-order, and no amount of \( \tau \)-talk will make it one. The plot is the test; the constants come after.

Know the honest failure modes too. A curve on the semilog plot usually means two time constants stacked — a fast surface effect riding on a slow bulk one — and a flat early section means deadtime: transport delay before the response begins. Both are information, not nuisance. Report what the data shows, fit the region the model genuinely describes, and say so; a stated approximation is engineering, an unstated one is luck.

Closing. \( e^{kt} \) encodes self-proportional change; \( \tau \) makes transients countable; logarithms solve for time and straighten curves so data confesses its constants.

Lesson 06 · Extrema, curve behavior, and optimization

01 · Where curves turn

A smooth function can only reverse direction where its derivative is zero. Those points — where \( f'(x) = 0 \) — are the critical points, and among them live the maxima and minima that engineering actually cares about: least material, least energy, most throughput. The second derivative classifies them: \( f'' > 0 \) cups upward (minimum), \( f'' < 0 \) caps downward (maximum).

02 · The optimization recipe

Every optimization in this course follows one recipe:

\[ \text{model} \to \text{constraint} \to \text{single-variable objective} \to f'(x)=0 \to \text{check ends} \tag{1} \]

Write the quantity to optimize; use the constraint to eliminate variables; differentiate; solve; then — the step everyone forgets — check the boundary. In a plant, optima frequently live at the constraint (valve fully open, maximum allowed speed) rather than at an interior zero of the derivative. The mathematics is honest about this only if you ask.

03 · A worked shape decision

Minimum-surface tanks, minimum-loss pipe runs, maximum-volume boxes from fixed sheet — these classic problems all reduce to recipe (1). In the Worked Examples tab we size an open-top square tank for 4 m³ with least plate area — the kind of trade a fabrication shop quotes weekly — and find the optimum has height equal to half the base side, a proportion worth memorizing.

04 · From words to optimum — the translation discipline

The calculus in recipe (1) is the easy half; the profession lives in the translation that precedes it. Practice the sequence deliberately. Name the objective — the single quantity to minimize or maximize — and write its formula with every variable it depends on. Name the constraint — the fixed volume, the required duty, the forbidden region — as an equation, and use it to eliminate variables until the objective depends on one. Differentiate, set to zero, solve — then classify with the second derivative, and walk the boundary: evaluate the objective at every end of the allowed interval, because constrained optima are the plant's normal case, not its exception. Finally, sanity-check the geometry of the answer: optima usually land on proportions — height equals half the base, cost terms equal at the minimum — and a result that looks arbitrary is a result worth re-deriving.

Two honest cautions. A mathematical optimum is only as good as the model's costs: a plate that is cheapest by area may not be cheapest by welding length, and writing the model down makes that assumption visible and challengeable. And when the objective is nearly flat near its optimum — as it often is — precision fetishism wastes effort: report the flat region, not a false fourth digit.

Real decision variables are also rarely continuous. Batches come in integers, plate comes in stock sizes, speeds come in motor steps. The calculus still earns its keep: find the continuous optimum, then evaluate the objective at the feasible values on either side of it and take the better — the curve's flatness near the optimum usually makes the rounding cost negligible, and computing it proves that instead of assuming it.

Closing. Derivative zero finds candidates; the second derivative sorts them; the boundary check keeps you honest. Optimization is calculus with a purchase order attached.

Lesson 07 · The integral as accumulation

01 · Accumulation is the other half of calculus

The derivative asks: how fast? The integral asks the inverse question: how much, in total? A flow meter reads m³/h instant by instant; the invoice wants m³. Adding up a changing rate over time is integration:

\[ V = \int_{0}^{T} Q(t)\,dt \tag{1} \]

Geometrically this is the area under the \( Q(t) \) curve — approximated by stacking thin rectangles \( Q \cdot \Delta t \) and letting \( \Delta t \to 0 \). Every totalizer in the plant is performing equation (1) in silicon, all day long.

02 · The Fundamental Theorem — rates and totals are inverses

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus says accumulation and differentiation undo each other: if \( F(t) = \int_a^t f(x)\,dx \), then \( F' = f \). Consequently

\[ \int_a^b f(t)\,dt = F(b) - F(a) \quad\text{where } F' = f \tag{2} \]

Find any antiderivative, evaluate at the ends, subtract. The theorem is why energy (kWh) is the antiderivative of power (kW), why distance is the antiderivative of speed, and why a totalizer can be checked against two readings of a cumulative register.

03 · Reading areas like an engineer

Area under a pump's power trend = energy consumed. Area under a flow curve = volume transferred. Area between inflow and outflow curves = inventory change. When the curve is data rather than formula, the integral is done numerically — trapezoids on historian samples — which is Lesson 9's business. The concept is identical: total = area.

04 · Setting up an integral — the working method

Every accumulation problem is set up the same way, and the setup is where the marks are won or lost. Identify the rate — the quantity per time (or per length, per angle) being accumulated — and write its units down. Fix the interval: the start and end of the accumulation, in the rate's own time variable. Integrate, and check the units of the result: a rate in m³/h integrated over hours must land in m³, and if it doesn't, the setup — not the arithmetic — is wrong. Then exploit the Fundamental Theorem in both directions. Given a formula, find an antiderivative and evaluate at the ends. Given a cumulative register — a totalizer, an energy meter, an odometer — the theorem says the accumulation over any window is simply the difference of two readings: engineering's most underrated free lunch.

Sketch first, always. A rough graph of the rate shows whether the answer should be near the peak-times-width rectangle or well under it, whether any region runs negative, and where the accumulation concentrates. The sketch is the estimate, the integral is the precision, and disagreement between them is the error alarm that catches transposed limits and dropped signs before they reach a report.

One caution completes the method: an integral answers exactly the question its limits ask, no more. A total over a shift says nothing about which hour did the accumulating, and a net accumulation of zero can hide furious movement in both directions. Keep the question and the interval welded together when you present the number, and totals stop being ambushed by follow-up questions.

Closing. Integrals accumulate rates into totals; the Fundamental Theorem makes them computable from antiderivatives; and the plant's totalizers, energy meters, and inventory balances are integrals with tag numbers.

Lesson 08 · Integration techniques

01 · Substitution — the chain rule reversed

Most integrals you meet are chain-rule results wearing a disguise. Substitution unmasks them: set \( u = \) the inner function, trade \( dx \) for \( du \), integrate, return.

\[ \int x\,(x^2+1)^3 dx \;\xrightarrow{\,u = x^2+1,\ du = 2x\,dx\,}\; \tfrac12\int u^3 du = \frac{(x^2+1)^4}{8} + C \tag{1} \]

The skill is seeing the derivative of the inside sitting beside it. Flow, decay, and geometry integrals in plant work overwhelmingly fall to substitution.

02 · Integration by parts — the product rule reversed

When the integrand is a genuine product — a polynomial times an exponential, say — parts applies:

\[ \int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du \tag{2} \]

Choose \( u \) to simplify when differentiated, \( dv \) to stay tame when integrated. The workhorse case \( \int t\,e^{-t}dt \) models energy in decaying transients and first-moment calculations, and the Worked Examples tab runs it end to end.

03 · Knowing when to stop

Real plant data rarely hands you a clean antiderivative. The professional sequence is: try substitution; try parts; recognize a table form; otherwise integrate numerically — trapezoids or Simpson on sampled data — and say so. A numerically honest answer beats a heroic symbolic detour every time. Software does symbolic work well; your job is to set the integral up correctly and sanity-check its output.

04 · Choosing the technique — a decision procedure

Integration is pattern recognition with a fallback ladder, and the ladder has exactly four rungs. Rung one: is the derivative of an inner function sitting beside it? If the integrand contains \( g(x) \) inside another function and \( g'(x) \) — up to a constant — as a factor, substitution finishes it in one line. Train the eye on the constant: in \( \int x\,e^{x^2}dx \), the \( x \) outside is \( \tfrac12 \) of the needed \( 2x \), and the half simply rides along. Rung two: a genuine product of unrelated factors — polynomial times exponential, polynomial times sine — goes to parts, choosing \( u \) as the factor that simplifies when differentiated. Rung three: recognize a table form and write the answer. Rung four: integrate numerically — trapezoids on the samples you have — and say so plainly.

Two habits turn technique into reliability. Verify every antiderivative by differentiating it — ten seconds that catch dropped halves and sign slips before they propagate. And when limits are involved in a substitution, transform them with the variable so the evaluation happens in \( u \)-space and nothing needs converting back. The techniques are few; the discipline around them is what makes the answers dependable.

Know also what the ladder is for. In professional work, symbolic software integrates faster than any human — but it cannot decide whether the integral was set up correctly, whether the limits mean what the question meant, or whether the answer's magnitude is plausible. The techniques you practice here build exactly that judgment: someone who has integrated by hand recognizes a wrong machine answer on sight, the way a machinist who has cut threads manually reads a bad CNC part at a glance. Learn the moves to own the judgment, then delegate the labor freely.

Closing. Substitution reverses the chain rule, parts reverses the product rule, and numerical integration is the licensed fallback — not a defeat.

Lesson 09 · Applications: areas, volumes, work, and averages

01 · Average value — what the meter should have read

The average of a continuously varying quantity is its integral spread evenly across the interval:

\[ \bar f = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(t)\,dt \tag{1} \]

Average demand, average flow, average temperature — every KPI that begins with the word "average" is equation (1), whether computed honestly or not. When someone averages three spot readings taken at convenient times, you now know precisely what they did wrong.

02 · RMS — the average that heats

Electrical heating goes as the square of current, so the meaningful "average" for AC is the root of the mean of the square:

\[ f_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T f^2(t)\,dt}, \qquad \text{for } f = A\sin\omega t:\ f_{rms} = \frac{A}{\sqrt 2} \tag{2} \]

The sine result follows because \( \sin^2 \) averages to exactly ½ over a cycle. This is why 230 V mains has a 325 V peak, and why vibration severity standards quote RMS velocity: RMS tracks energy, and energy does the damage.

03 · Work by integration

When force varies along the path, work is accumulated force-times-distance: \( W = \int F\,dx \). Pumping a tank empty is the signature case: each layer of liquid at depth \( y \) has weight \( \rho g A\,dy \) and must rise a different height. Summing layers is an integral, executed in the Worked Examples tab for a real tank. The same layer-summing pattern computes hydrostatic forces on gates and centroids of plates — one method, many billables.

04 · Choosing the right average — a field guide

Three different averages answer three different questions, and choosing the wrong one is a quiet, expensive error. The plain mean — equation (1) — answers "how much per unit time on the invoice": billing, throughput, duty totals. The RMS — equation (2) — answers "what steady level would heat, stress, or wear the same": electrical heating, vibration severity, fatigue-relevant loading, because damage physics runs on squares. The peak answers "will it survive the worst instant": trips, relief sizing, insulation. A signal has all three numbers at once, and the crest factor — peak over RMS — is the single figure that says how far apart they live: near 1.4 for a clean sine, climbing whenever impacts and spikes enter, which is why analysts watch it as an early bearing-damage flag.

On sampled data the integrals become sums without changing meaning: mean the samples for equation (1), mean the squares and take the root for equation (2), weighting by interval when the sampling is uneven. For work-by-layers problems, the recipe is mechanical: draw the layer, write its weight, write its travel distance as a function of position, integrate over the geometry — then check against the centroid shortcut, which agrees every time the geometry is uniform and catches the setup when it isn't.

The same layer-summing pattern prices hydrostatic force on a gate, the metal in a tapered column, and the moment of a distributed load — one method, many invoices. What changes between problems is only what each layer carries: weight, pressure, or load per metre. Master the pattern once, in the tank problem, and every later course that says "integrate over the geometry" is asking for a rerun.

Closing. Integrals define honest averages (1), energy-true averages (2), and path-dependent work — three tools that turn "roughly" into "defensibly."

Lesson 10 · First-order differential equations

01 · Equations that contain their own rates

A differential equation relates a quantity to its own rate of change. The plant's archetype: a hot mass losing heat in proportion to its excess over ambient,

\[ \frac{dT}{dt} = -\frac{T - T_\infty}{\tau} \tag{1} \]

Read it aloud: the hotter it is above ambient, the faster it cools. Most first-order plant dynamics — thermal soak, tank draining, filter loading, RC circuits, instrument response — are equation (1) with different nouns.

02 · Solving by separation

Separable equations split variables to opposite sides and integrate:

\[ \int\frac{dT}{T-T_\infty} = -\int\frac{dt}{\tau} \;\Rightarrow\; \ln|T-T_\infty| = -\frac{t}{\tau}+C \;\Rightarrow\; T = T_\infty + (T_0-T_\infty)e^{-t/\tau} \tag{2} \]

Lesson 5's cooling curve wasn't handed down; it is derived here, in three lines, from a physical statement. The constant of integration is fixed by the initial condition \( T(0) = T_0 \) — every first-order ODE needs one known point to pin its trajectory.

03 · Reading dynamics without solving

Much of an ODE's story is available without solution. Set the rate to zero and you find equilibria (here \( T = T_\infty \)). The sign of the rate tells direction of travel; its size sets speed. The coefficient \( 1/\tau \) is the speed of the whole approach — measure it from data and you know the system's tempo. Engineers read ODEs more often than they solve them; the solving builds the reading instinct.

04 · From balance statement to equation — the modeling method

First-order models are not found; they are written, and the writing follows one template. State the balance: rate of change of the stored quantity equals rate in minus rate out. Express each rate in the state variable: outflow proportional to level, heat loss proportional to excess temperature, contaminant leaving at concentration times flow. Divide through until the equation reads \( \dot{y} = -(y - y_\infty)/\tau \), and read \( \tau \) directly from the coefficients — \( V/Q \) for a flushed tank, thermal mass over loss coefficient for a cooling body. The physics enters in the rates; the mathematics is the same three lines every time.

Respect the sign conventions and the model's edges. A negative rate coefficient decays toward equilibrium; a positive one runs away, and recognizing which you have written is a stability judgment made before any solving. Step inputs — a heater switching on, a setpoint moving — reuse the same solution with the roles renamed: the response climbs \( 1 - e^{-t/\tau} \) toward its new destination. And when the data refuses the model — curved semilog plots, delayed starts — the honest conclusion is a second time constant or a deadtime, not a forced fit. Write what the balance supports; report what the data shows.

This template is the most reused page in the course. The same three lines will reappear as the RC circuit in the electrical course, the thermal soak in heat transfer, the flushed volume in fluid mechanics, and the instrument lag in controls — identical mathematics under different nouns. Learn to see the balance statement through the surface vocabulary and every one of those courses starts a lesson ahead.

Closing. First-order ODEs encode self-referential change; separation solves the classics; initial conditions select the one real trajectory among the many possible.

Lesson 11 · Taylor series and approximation

01 · Every smooth function is locally a polynomial

Zoom into any smooth curve and it straightens; zoom with more care and it bends like a parabola. Taylor's theorem makes this precise: near \( x = a \),

\[ f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + \frac{f''(a)}{2}(x-a)^2 + \cdots \tag{1} \]

The first two terms are the tangent line — linearization — and the third begins to track curvature. Engineering lives on truncations of (1): every "small-angle," "small-signal," and "about the operating point" argument you will ever meet is a Taylor series with the tail cut off.

02 · The classic small approximations

\[ \sin\theta \approx \theta - \frac{\theta^3}{6}, \qquad \cos\theta \approx 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2}, \qquad e^x \approx 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2} \tag{2} \]

At \( \theta = 10° = 0.1745 \) rad, \( \sin\theta = 0.17365 \): the one-term approximation \( \theta \) errs by 0.5%, and adding the cubic term shrinks the error to 0.0004%. This is why pendulum periods, beam deflections, and control models replace \( \sin\theta \) with \( \theta \) below ~15° and nobody blushes.

03 · Knowing the size of the tail

An approximation without an error bound is a wish. The first neglected term estimates the error: truncating after \( \theta \) leaves \( \sim\theta^3/6 \). Professional habit: state the operating range, evaluate the first dropped term at the range's edge, and write both down. That habit is what separates "we linearized about 50% flow" from hand-waving — and it is exactly how instrument nonlinearity specs and control-model validity limits are written.

04 · Building a linearization you can defend

Linearization is a contract with three clauses, written in this order. Choose the operating point deliberately — the flow, load, or angle the system actually lives at — because the model is exact there and degrades with distance. Compute the two numbers: the function's value and its derivative at that point; the tangent model is just \( f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) \), a straight line wearing the local slope. Price the error before anyone asks: evaluate the first dropped term, \( \tfrac12 f''(a)(x-a)^2 \), at the edge of the range you intend to use, and write both the range and the error into the work. A linearization delivered with its validity range and worst-case error is engineering; delivered bare, it is a hope with a slope.

The quadratic error growth is the practical lever: halve the excursion and the error quarters, which is why tight-range instruments tolerate linear scaling that wide-range ones cannot. Run the contract in reverse to audit existing systems — find where a legacy scaling block or control model was linearized, evaluate the dropped term at today's operating range, and you have a defensible number for how much the old approximation now costs. The habit generalizes: every model is an approximation; professionals differ only in whether the error is priced.

Carry that sentence out of the course. Instrument linearity specifications, control-model validity ranges, and datasheet accuracy classes are all Taylor-series contracts written by someone else — read them by asking where the expansion point sits and how far the promised range strays from it. The engineer who can price an approximation can also audit one.

Closing. Taylor series turn smooth functions into local polynomials; linearization is its two-term truncation; the first dropped term prices the honesty of the approximation. This lesson closes the course by naming the approximation culture the whole curriculum will use.
