Differentiation rules and the chain rule

Product, quotient, and chain rules with mechanical fluency as the goal. Implicit differentiation. The chain rule as the engine behind every related-rates problem in plant contexts.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Tank level depends on flow, flow on valve position: which rule chains the sensitivities together?
  • Differentiate x·e^x and explain which rule did the work.

01 · The rules — machinery for slopes

Yesterday's limit definition is correct and slow. Today we industrialize it. For constants \(c\), functions \(u, v\):

\[ (cu)' = cu', \qquad (u+v)' = u' + v', \qquad (x^n)' = nx^{n-1} \tag{1} \] \[ (uv)' = u'v + uv', \qquad \left(\frac{u}{v}\right)' = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2} \tag{2} \]

Each is proved once from the limit and used forever. The power rule follows from expanding \( (x+\Delta x)^n \) and watching every term above first order vanish with \( \Delta x \) — the same "higher-order terms die" argument that engineering approximation lives on.

02 · The chain rule — rates through a chain of causes

Plant variables cause each other in chains: valve position sets flow, flow sets level, level sets pressure. If \( y = f(u) \) and \( u = g(x) \):

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx} \tag{3} \]

Rates multiply along the chain. This single rule powers every sensitivity argument you will make: how much does discharge pressure move per percent of speed? Differentiate through the chain of dependencies and multiply the local sensitivities.

03 · Relative rates — the engineer's favorite

Divide a derivative by its function and you get relative sensitivity. For a fan law \( P = kN^3 \), logarithmic differentiation gives \( \frac{dP}{P} = 3\frac{dN}{N} \): a 2% speed increase costs ≈6% more power ((1.02)³ = 1.061 exactly). This is why slowing a fan or pump slightly is the cheapest energy project in any plant — the cube works for you on the way down.

04 · Choosing the rule — structure before symbols

Differentiation errors rarely come from forgetting a rule; they come from misreading the structure of the expression. So classify before you differentiate. Is the expression a sum of powers? The power rule handles each term independently — after rewriting roots and reciprocals as powers, \( \sqrt{x} = x^{1/2} \), \( 1/x = x^{-1} \). Is it two varying quantities multiplied — force times velocity, pressure times flow? That is a genuine product: product rule. A ratio with the variable in the denominator? Quotient rule, with the subtraction order fixed. A function of a function — something raised to a power, an exponential of an expression? Chain rule, differentiating outer times inner, working from the outside in.

For the power laws that fill engineering — \( P = kN^3 \), \( \Delta P = kQ^2 \) — logarithmic differentiation earns its keep: take logs, differentiate, and relative rates appear directly, \( dP/P = n\,dN/N \). That one line is the professional's sensitivity language: percent out per percent in. Keep the honest distinction from 3 visible — the relative-rate figure is a first-order estimate, exact only in the limit; for large changes, compute the ratio exactly and let the linear estimate serve as its sanity check.

Finally, make numerical verification a reflex. Any symbolic derivative can be audited in ten seconds: evaluate the function at \( x \) and at \( x + 0.001 \), divide the difference by 0.001, and compare with the formula's prediction. Agreement to three figures means the algebra survived; disagreement means a dropped inner derivative or a sign error is hiding in the work. Professionals check their derivatives the way welders check their fit-up — before the error is buried under everything built on top of it.

Closing. Rules (1)–(2) mechanize differentiation; the chain rule (3) walks rates through cause-and-effect; relative rates turn calculus into percentage talk that managers act on.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • The derivative as limit (Lesson 3) — today's rules are shortcuts to that limit.
  • Exponent algebra — \( x^{-1} \), \( x^{1/2} \) so the power rule covers roots and reciprocals.
  • Composite functions — recognizing \( f(g(x)) \) before differentiating it.

Glossary

TermEquationWhen to use
Power rule\( (x^n)' = n\,x^{n-1} \)Sums of powers — after rewriting roots and reciprocals as powers.
Product rule\( (uv)' = u'v + uv' \)Two varying factors multiplied: force × velocity, pressure × flow.
Quotient rule\( (u/v)' = (u'v - uv')/v^2 \)Ratios with the variable below the line; the subtraction order is fixed.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Chain rule, straight up

Problem. \( y = (3x^2+2)^4 \). Find: \( y'(1) \).

Solution. (1) Outer: \( \frac{d}{du}u^4 = 4u^3 \) with \( u = 3x^2+2 \). (2) Inner: \( u' = 6x \). (3) Chain: \( y' = 4(3x^2+2)^3 \cdot 6x = 24x(3x^2+2)^3 \). (4) At \( x=1 \): \( 24 \times 5^3 = 24 \times 125 = 3000 \).

Answer: 3000. Sanity check: numerically, \( y(1.001) - y(1) \approx 628.0\!-\!625 = 3.0 \) over \( \Delta x = 0.001 \) → slope ≈ 3000. Confirmed.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

The affinity-law energy argument

Problem. A 90 kW cooling fan runs at fixed speed with dampers throttling. A VFD retrofit would slow it 10%. Using the fan law \( P = kN^3 \), find the approximate and exact power saving.

Solution. (1) Relative rates: \( dP/P = 3\,dN/N = 3 \times (-10\%) = -30\% \) (first-order estimate). (2) Exact: \( (0.9)^3 = 0.729 \) → 27.1% saving. (3) Power: \( 90 \times 0.271 = 24.4\ \mathrm{kW} \) saved continuously.

Answer: ≈24 kW (exact 27.1%, linear estimate 30%). Sanity check: the linear estimate overshoots because the cube curve bends — for a 10% change the two agree within a few points, exactly what a first-order method promises.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Power through the product rule

Problem. During a controlled winch lower-away, cable tension falls as \( F(t) = 200 - 5t \) N while payout speed rises as \( v(t) = 2 + 0.3t \) m/s. Mechanical power is \( P = Fv \). Find: \( P'(10) \) and interpret its sign.

Solution. (1) Product rule: \( P' = F'v + Fv' = (-5)(2 + 0.3t) + (200 - 5t)(0.3) \). (2) At \( t = 10 \): \( (-5)(5) + (150)(0.3) = -25 + 45 = +20 \) W/s. (3) Interpretation: tension is falling, but the speed growth outweighs it — power is still rising at 20 watts per second.

Answer: \( P'(10) = +20 \) W/s; power increasing. Sanity check: \( P(10) = 150 \times 5 = 750 \) W and \( P(11) = 145 \times 5.3 = 768.5 \) W — a rise of 18.5 W over the next second, consistent with a +20 W/s instantaneous rate that is itself easing off.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

Let \( y = (2x^3 + 1)^5 \). Compute \( y'(1) \).

By the chain rule, the outer derivative is \( 5u^4 \) with \( u = 2x^3 + 1 \) and the inner derivative is \( 6x^2 \), so \( y' = 30x^2(2x^3+1)^4 \), which at \( x = 1 \) gives \( 30 \times 3^4 = 2430 \). The distractor 405 is the classic error of differentiating only the outer power and forgetting to multiply by the inner derivative \( 6x^2 \). Writing the outer derivative as \( 5u^5 \) instead of \( 5u^4 \) — failing to reduce the exponent — produces 7290.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

For differentiable functions \( u(x) \) and \( v(x) \) with \( v(x) \neq 0 \), which expression equals \( \left( \dfrac{u}{v} \right)' \)?

The quotient rule is \( (u/v)' = (u'v - uv')/v^2 \): the term with the differentiated numerator comes first, and the subtraction order is fixed. Reversing the numerator to \( uv' - u'v \) is the classic sign error and yields exactly the negative of the true derivative. The plus-sign version transplants the product-rule pattern where it does not apply, and \( u'/v' \) — differentiating top and bottom separately — already fails on a case as simple as \( x/x^2 \).

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

Let \( f(x) = 4x^{3/2} \). What is \( f'(9) \)?

Rewrite and apply the power rule: \( f'(x) = 4 \cdot \tfrac{3}{2}x^{1/2} = 6\sqrt{x} \), so \( f'(9) = 6 \times 3 = 18 \). Choosing 12 drops the \( 3/2 \) exponent factor; 162 forgets to lower the power (using \( x^{3/2} \) instead of \( x^{1/2} \)); 108 is \( f(9) \) itself — the value-versus-slope confusion. The power rule covers roots the moment you write them as fractional powers.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

A centrifugal pump absorbs shaft power according to the affinity law \( P = kN^3 \), where \( N \) is rotational speed. A variable-frequency drive slows the pump by 8%. What is the exact percentage reduction in absorbed power?

The exact power ratio is \( (0.92)^3 = 0.7787 \), so the saving is \( 1 - 0.7787 = 22.1\% \). The relative-rate estimate \( dP/P = 3\,dN/N = 24\% \) is only a first-order linearization; quoting it as the exact value is the classic error behind that distractor, and it overshoots because the cubic curve bends. Using the square law (which governs head, \( \propto N^2 \)) instead of the cube law for power gives 15.4%, while assuming power simply proportional to speed gives 8%.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Tank level responds to volume as \( dL/dV = 0.14 \) m per m³, and volume grows at \( dV/dt = 2.5 \) m³/min. How fast is level rising?

The chain rule multiplies rates along the dependency chain: \( dL/dt = (dL/dV)(dV/dt) = 0.14 \times 2.5 = 0.35 \) m/min. Dividing (17.9) or adding (2.64) the sensitivities are the classic chain-rule confusions; 0.056 inverts one factor. When causes chain — valve to flow, flow to volume, volume to level — their local sensitivities multiply, which is the entire content of equation (3).

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — selling the VFD with one line of calculus. You arrive at an energy review where a utilities fan farm runs throttled year-round. Management wants payback numbers, not mathematics. You write one line on the whiteboard — power moves three percent for every percent of speed — and translate: 10% slower ≈ 25–30% less power, per fan, forever. The chain rule and the cube law did the argument; the spreadsheet that follows merely confirms what equation (3) already promised. The retrofit gets scoped that week.
