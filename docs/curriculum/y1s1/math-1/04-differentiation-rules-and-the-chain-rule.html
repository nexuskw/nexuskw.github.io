Differentiation rules and the chain rule

Product, quotient, and chain rules with mechanical fluency as the goal. Implicit differentiation. The chain rule as the engine behind every related-rates problem in plant contexts.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Tank level depends on flow, flow on valve position: which rule chains the sensitivities together?
  • Differentiate x·e^x and explain which rule did the work.

§1 · The rules — machinery for slopes

Yesterday's limit definition is correct and slow. Today we industrialize it. For constants \(c\), functions \(u, v\):

\[ (cu)' = cu', \qquad (u+v)' = u' + v', \qquad (x^n)' = nx^{n-1} \tag{1} \] \[ (uv)' = u'v + uv', \qquad \left(\frac{u}{v}\right)' = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2} \tag{2} \]

Each is proved once from the limit and used forever. The power rule follows from expanding \( (x+\Delta x)^n \) and watching every term above first order vanish with \( \Delta x \) — the same "higher-order terms die" argument that engineering approximation lives on.

§2 · The chain rule — rates through a chain of causes

Plant variables cause each other in chains: valve position sets flow, flow sets level, level sets pressure. If \( y = f(u) \) and \( u = g(x) \):

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx} \tag{3} \]

Rates multiply along the chain. This single rule powers every sensitivity argument you will make: how much does discharge pressure move per percent of speed? Differentiate through the chain of dependencies and multiply the local sensitivities.

§3 · Relative rates — the engineer's favorite

Divide a derivative by its function and you get relative sensitivity. For a fan law \( P = kN^3 \), logarithmic differentiation gives \( \frac{dP}{P} = 3\frac{dN}{N} \): a 2% speed increase costs ≈6% more power ((1.02)³ = 1.061 exactly). This is why slowing a fan or pump slightly is the cheapest energy project in any plant — the cube works for you on the way down.

Closing. Rules (1)–(2) mechanize differentiation; the chain rule (3) walks rates through cause-and-effect; relative rates turn calculus into percentage talk that managers act on.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • The derivative as limit (Lesson 3) — today's rules are shortcuts to that limit.
  • Exponent algebra — \( x^{-1} \), \( x^{1/2} \) so the power rule covers roots and reciprocals.
  • Composite functions — recognizing \( f(g(x)) \) before differentiating it.

Glossary

  • Product/quotient rule — derivatives of combined effects; equation (2).
  • Chain rule — derivative through nested dependence; equation (3).
  • Sensitivity — output change per unit input change; a derivative with a job.
  • Relative (log) sensitivity — percent-per-percent; \( d\ln y / d\ln x \).

Worked Examples

Worked example 1 — chain rule, straight up

Problem. \( y = (3x^2+2)^4 \). Find: \( y'(1) \).

Solution. (1) Outer: \( \frac{d}{du}u^4 = 4u^3 \) with \( u = 3x^2+2 \). (2) Inner: \( u' = 6x \). (3) Chain: \( y' = 4(3x^2+2)^3 \cdot 6x = 24x(3x^2+2)^3 \). (4) At \( x=1 \): \( 24 \times 5^3 = 24 \times 125 = 3000 \).

Answer: 3000. Sanity check: numerically, \( y(1.001) - y(1) \approx 628.0\!-\!625 = 3.0 \) over \( \Delta x = 0.001 \) → slope ≈ 3000. Confirmed.

Worked example 2 — the affinity-law energy argument

Problem. A 90 kW cooling fan runs at fixed speed with dampers throttling. A VFD retrofit would slow it 10%. Using the fan law \( P = kN^3 \), find the approximate and exact power saving.

Solution. (1) Relative rates: \( dP/P = 3\,dN/N = 3 \times (-10\%) = -30\% \) (first-order estimate). (2) Exact: \( (0.9)^3 = 0.729 \) → 27.1% saving. (3) Power: \( 90 \times 0.271 = 24.4\ \mathrm{kW} \) saved continuously.

Answer: ≈24 kW (exact 27.1%, linear estimate 30%). Sanity check: the linear estimate overshoots because the cube curve bends — for a 10% change the two agree within a few points, exactly what a first-order method promises.

Kuwait Floor

Vignette — selling the VFD with one line of calculus. You arrive at an energy review where a utilities fan farm runs throttled year-round. Management wants payback numbers, not mathematics. You write one line on the whiteboard — power moves three percent for every percent of speed — and translate: 10% slower ≈ 25–30% less power, per fan, forever. The chain rule and the cube law did the argument; the spreadsheet that follows merely confirms what equation (3) already promised. The retrofit gets scoped that week.
