Integration techniques

Substitution, integration by parts, and simple partial fractions. Choosing a technique by recognizing structure. Numerical fallback when no closed form exists.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • Chain rule (Lesson 4) and product rule — today's methods are their inverses.
  • Antiderivatives (Lesson 7) — the target object of every technique.
  • Exponentials (Lesson 5) — \( \int e^{kt}dt = e^{kt}/k + C \) appears constantly.

Glossary

  • Substitution — change of variable matching the chain rule's pattern.
  • Integration by parts — equation (2); trades one integral for a simpler one.
  • Table form — a memorized standard integral.
  • Numerical integration — trapezoid/Simpson sums on data; the honest fallback.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Which technique handles ∫x·e^x dx, and why does substitution alone fail?
  • When is a numerical integral the honest choice over an analytic one?

01 · Substitution — the chain rule reversed

Most integrals you meet are chain-rule results wearing a disguise. Substitution unmasks them: set \( u = \) the inner function, trade \( dx \) for \( du \), integrate, return.

\[ \int x\,(x^2+1)^3 dx \;\xrightarrow{\,u = x^2+1,\ du = 2x\,dx\,}\; \tfrac12\int u^3 du = \frac{(x^2+1)^4}{8} + C \tag{1} \]

The skill is seeing the derivative of the inside sitting beside it. Flow, decay, and geometry integrals in plant work overwhelmingly fall to substitution.

02 · Integration by parts — the product rule reversed

When the integrand is a genuine product — a polynomial times an exponential, say — parts applies:

\[ \int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du \tag{2} \]

Choose \( u \) to simplify when differentiated, \( dv \) to stay tame when integrated. The workhorse case \( \int t\,e^{-t}dt \) models energy in decaying transients and first-moment calculations, and the Worked Examples tab runs it end to end.

03 · Knowing when to stop

Real plant data rarely hands you a clean antiderivative. The professional sequence is: try substitution; try parts; recognize a table form; otherwise integrate numerically — trapezoids or Simpson on sampled data — and say so. A numerically honest answer beats a heroic symbolic detour every time. Software does symbolic work well; your job is to set the integral up correctly and sanity-check its output.

Closing. Substitution reverses the chain rule, parts reverses the product rule, and numerical integration is the licensed fallback — not a defeat.

Worked Examples

Worked example 1 — substitution with limits

Problem. Evaluate \( \displaystyle\int_0^2 x\,(x^2+1)^3\,dx \).

Solution. (1) \( u = x^2+1 \), \( du = 2x\,dx \); limits map \( x{=}0 \to u{=}1 \), \( x{=}2 \to u{=}5 \). (2) Integral becomes \( \tfrac12 \int_1^5 u^3 du = \tfrac12 \cdot \frac{u^4}{4}\Big|_1^5 \). (3) \( = \frac{625 - 1}{8} = 78 \).

Answer: 78. Sanity check: the integrand at \( x=2 \) is \( 2 \times 125 = 250 \), at 0 it's 0; a rough average of ~40 over width 2 gives ~80 — same neighborhood as 78.

Worked example 2 — parts on a decaying transient

Problem. A quantity accumulates as \( \int_0^2 t\,e^{-t}\,dt \) (dimensionless model of energy delivered by a decaying pulse). Find: its value.

Solution. (1) Parts with \( u = t,\ dv = e^{-t}dt \Rightarrow du = dt,\ v = -e^{-t} \). (2) \( \int t e^{-t} dt = -t e^{-t} + \int e^{-t} dt = -t e^{-t} - e^{-t} \). (3) Evaluate 0→2: \( \left[-(t+1)e^{-t}\right]_0^2 = -(3)(0.1353) + 1 = 1 - 0.406 = 0.594 \).

Answer: ≈0.594. Sanity check: the full integral to infinity equals 1 exactly; by \( t=2 \) (two time constants) most of the pulse has landed — 59% here, with the tail's remainder consistent with \( (t+1)e^{-t} \) at \( t=2 \) being 0.406.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the totalizer that lied politely. You arrive to commission a batching skid whose vendor totalizer disagrees with the tank dip by 4% every batch. The vendor's firmware integrates flow with coarse rectangles at a slow scan; the batch flow rises and falls steeply. You re-integrate a logged batch with trapezoids — thirty minutes in a spreadsheet — and land within 0.5% of the dip. The finding isn't "the meter is bad"; it's "the integration method under-resolves the ramps." The vendor pushes a firmware setting, scan tightens, and the totalizer and the tank finally tell one story. Technique matters even when silicon does the arithmetic.
