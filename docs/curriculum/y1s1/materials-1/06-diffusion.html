Diffusion

Fick's first and second laws with the physical picture of jumping atoms. Temperature dependence through the Arrhenius form. Carburizing depth as a worked application — the √Dt scaling that recurs everywhere.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does case-hardening depth scale with √(Dt)?
  • What does the Arrhenius form say about doubling temperature versus doubling time?

01 · Fick's laws, with atoms attached

Diffusion is atoms hopping site to site, biased downhill in concentration. Steady state is Fick's first law, \( J = -D\, dC/dx \): flux proportional to gradient, \( D \) the diffusion coefficient. Transient cases — the plant's usual reality — obey Fick's second law:

\[ \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2} \tag{1} \]

whose solutions for a surface held at constant concentration follow the error function. The engineering headline hiding in those solutions: any characteristic penetration depth scales as

\[ x \sim \sqrt{Dt} \tag{2} \]

Double the depth costs four times the time. That square root governs carburizing, decarburizing, homogenization, and every soak-time argument you will ever have with a furnace schedule.

02 · Temperature is the big lever

The diffusion coefficient is Arrhenius:

\[ D = D_0 \exp\!\left(-\frac{Q_d}{RT}\right) \tag{3} \]

with activation energy \( Q_d \) set by the migration mechanism — interstitials like carbon in iron dart between gaps cheaply; substitutional atoms must wait for vacancies and pay more. Because \( T \) sits in an exponential, modest temperature moves swamp heroic time extensions: in the Worked Examples tab, 200 K of extra furnace temperature buys a factor of ~19 in \( D \), which no affordable soak time matches.

03 · Reading processes through (2) and (3)

Case-hardening a gear: carbon in from the surface, case depth by \( \sqrt{Dt} \), temperature chosen high enough for speed but below distortion limits. Homogenizing a segregated casting: the same mathematics with the dendrite arm spacing as the distance to erase. Even degradation reads this way — hydrogen charging into a weld, carburization of furnace tubes — diffusion is one mathematics wearing many overalls.

04 · The diffusion estimating method

Most industrial diffusion questions are answered by one scaling law before any full solution: penetration grows as \( x \sim \sqrt{Dt} \). Run it as a ratio and the unknown constants cancel — if 4 hours of carburizing buys a 1 mm case, a 2 mm case costs \( 2^2 = 4 \) times as long: 16 hours, not 8. The square root is the story: doubling depth always quadruples time, which is why deep cases are expensive and why homogenizing a badly segregated casting can be economically hopeless. Temperature is the other lever, and it is exponential: \( D = D_0 e^{-Q_d/RT} \) — a modest temperature rise can buy what hours of extra soak cannot, which is why furnace setpoints, not timers, dominate diffusion process design.

Keep the mechanisms straight when judging feasibility: small interstitials like carbon hop between gaps and move fast; substitutional atoms must wait for a vacancy to swap with and move orders of magnitude slower at the same temperature. That is why case-hardening with carbon is an overnight job while chromizing is a specialist's slow art — and why "just diffuse it in" is a plan that needs this lesson's arithmetic before it becomes a work order.

The same estimating method audits vendors: a heat-treat certificate quoting an improbably deep case for the stated hours and temperature is either running hotter than declared or measuring generously — and \( \sqrt{Dt} \), evaluated on the back of the certificate itself, is enough to ask the question with numbers instead of suspicion.

Closing. Gradient drives, \( \sqrt{Dt} \) paces, Arrhenius accelerates. Three relations, and most furnace-floor arithmetic is already in hand.

Examples and Quiz

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Case depth and the square root's tax

Problem. Carburizing at 950 °C with \( D \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-11}\ \mathrm{m^2/s} \) (representative for carbon in austenite). Find: the \( \sqrt{Dt} \) penetration scale after 4 h, and the time to double it.

Solution. (1) \( t = 4 \times 3600 = 14\,400\ \mathrm{s} \). (2) \( \sqrt{Dt} = \sqrt{1.6 \times 10^{-11} \times 14\,400} = \sqrt{2.30 \times 10^{-7}} = 4.8 \times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{m} \approx 0.48\ \mathrm{mm} \). (3) Doubling depth requires \( 4t \): 16 h.

Answer: ≈0.5 mm scale at 4 h; 16 h to reach ≈1 mm. Sanity check: the second half-millimetre costs three times what the first did — the \( \sqrt{t} \) law is why deep cases are expensive and why specifications state case depth with tolerance, not ambition.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

What 200 K buys

Problem. Carbon in FCC iron: \( D_0 = 2.3 \times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{m^2/s} \), \( Q_d = 148\ \mathrm{kJ/mol} \) (Callister & Rethwisch). Find: \( D \) at 1000 K and 1200 K.

Solution. (1) \( D_{1000} = 2.3 \times 10^{-5} \exp[-148\,000/(8.314 \times 1000)] = 4.3 \times 10^{-13}\ \mathrm{m^2/s} \). (2) \( D_{1200} = 2.3 \times 10^{-5} \exp[-148\,000/(8.314 \times 1200)] = 8.3 \times 10^{-12}\ \mathrm{m^2/s} \). (3) Ratio: ≈19×.

Answer: a 200 K raise multiplies \( D \) by ~19. Sanity check: to match that with time at 1000 K you would need a 19× longer soak — days for hours. Temperature is the lever; time is the fine adjustment. (Distortion and grain growth are the counter-forces that keep furnaces from simply running hotter.)

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Pricing a deeper case

Problem. A carburizing furnace produces a 1.0 mm case depth in 4 hours at temperature. Production asks for 2.0 mm. Find: the required time at the same temperature, using the penetration scaling law.

Solution. (1) Penetration scales as \( x \sim \sqrt{Dt} \), so \( t \propto x^2 \) at fixed temperature. (2) \( t_2 = t_1 (x_2/x_1)^2 = 4 \times (2.0/1.0)^2 = 16\ \mathrm{h} \).

Answer: 16 hours — quadruple, not double. Sanity check: the square-root law means each additional millimetre costs more than the one before; halfway through the 16 hours the case is at \( \sqrt{8/16} \times 2.0 = 1.41\ \mathrm{mm} \), not 1.0 — diffusion front-loads its progress, and the last fraction of depth is the expensive part.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A carburizing cycle gives 0.5 mm case depth in 4 h. Reaching 1.0 mm at the same temperature takes about:

Depth scales as \( \sqrt{Dt} \): doubling depth quadruples time — 16 h. The linear instinct (8 h) is the classic furnace-scheduling error; deep cases are bought with disproportionate hours, which is why case depth is specified with tolerance, not generosity.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Carbon diffuses through iron much faster than nickel does because carbon:

Small interstitial atoms dart between lattice gaps; substitutional atoms like nickel must wait for a vacancy to arrive next door — a rarer event with a higher activation energy. Mechanism, not atomic weight, sets the pace.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

Doubling a carburized case depth at fixed temperature requires:

\( x \sim \sqrt{Dt} \Rightarrow t \propto x^2 \): depth doubles only when time quadruples. Linear thinking (double time) is the classic scheduling error, and it under-books the furnace by half. Carbon potential shifts surface concentration, not the square-root march of the front — no dial removes the exponent.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

To speed a diffusion process meaningfully, the strongest single lever is:

\( D = D_0 e^{-Q_d/RT} \): temperature sits in the exponent. In this course's worked example, +200 K multiplied \( D \) by ~19 — matching that with time means a 19× longer soak. Time enters only under a square root; temperature enters exponentially.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Why does carbon diffuse through iron orders of magnitude faster than chromium does?

Mechanism decides tempo. Interstitial diffusers always have empty neighboring gaps; substitutional diffusers move only when an adjacent vacancy appears — and vacancies are the scarce commodity the previous lesson counted. Reactivity and mass are red herrings at this scale, and while boundaries do offer fast paths, both species use them; the bulk mechanism gap is the real story.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the replacement gear's thin skin. You arrive at a petrochemical plant's machine shop reviewing a failed spare: a case-hardened gear that pitted in months where the original ran years. Hardness traverse tells the story — the replacement's case reads 0.3 mm where the drawing calls for 0.8–1.0 mm (figures representative). Someone bought case depth with a short furnace cycle, and \( \sqrt{Dt} \) does not negotiate: half the depth means a quarter of the time, and the Hertzian contact stresses below the thin case found soft core. The purchasing lesson is Lesson 6: case depth is furnace hours, and certificates should say so.
