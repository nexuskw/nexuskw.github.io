Crystallographic directions and planes

Miller indices for directions and planes. Close-packed planes and slip systems. Anisotropy: why rolled plate has direction-dependent properties.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What makes close-packed planes the natural slip planes?
  • Why does rolled plate have direction-dependent properties?

01 · Naming directions and planes

Crystals are anisotropic objects, so we need coordinates. Directions [uvw]: take the vector's components along the cell axes, clear fractions to smallest integers, wrap in square brackets, mark negatives with an overbar. Planes (hkl), the Miller indices: take the plane's intercepts on the axes in units of the lattice parameters, invert them, clear fractions, wrap in parentheses — an intercept at infinity (plane parallel to an axis) inverts to a clean zero. Families of equivalent directions and planes get angle and brace brackets: ⟨110⟩, {111}.

02 · Why these labels earn their keep

Slip — plastic deformation — happens on the densest planes along the densest directions, because those are the paths where atoms ride over each other with the least protest. In FCC that means {111} planes and ⟨110⟩ directions: 12 combinations, the slip systems that make copper and aluminum so obligingly ductile. Linear and planar densities (atoms per length, per area) are the quantitative versions, and computing them is how the claim "close-packed = slip-prone" stops being a slogan.

03 · Anisotropy leaves the lab

A single crystal's modulus can differ by a factor of two between directions. Polycrystals average this out — unless processing lines the grains up. Rolling does exactly that: plate develops a crystallographic texture plus elongated grains and aligned inclusions, so strength, ductility, and especially toughness differ between the rolling direction and the transverse. Standards respond by specifying test-bar orientation, and fabricators respond — or should — by orienting bends relative to the rolling direction. The Miller machinery of 1 is the language in which all of this is written.

04 · The indexing working method

Miller indices are a procedure, and running it identically every time is the entire skill. Planes: read the intercepts in cell-edge units, take reciprocals, clear fractions, bracket as \( (hkl) \) — a plane parallel to an axis intercepts at infinity and earns a zero. Directions: head coordinates minus tail coordinates, clear to smallest integers, bracket as \( [uvw] \). Negative components wear an overbar, and families collect all symmetry-equivalent members: \( \{111\} \) means every close-packed plane in FCC at once. The classic error is mixing conventions mid-problem — intercepts for directions or vector components for planes produce plausible-looking nonsense, which is why the two recipes should be written at the top of the page until they are reflex.

Then put the indices to work, because they exist for the packing arithmetic: linear density along a close-packed direction is \( 1/(2R) \) — atoms touching in a row — and the densest planes with the densest directions form the slip systems where plasticity lives. FCC's twelve well-fed slip systems are the geometric reason copper draws into wire without complaint; HCP's grudging three are why some alloys crack where others flow. The indices are the language; slip is the sentence they were invented to write.

Closing. Directions in brackets, planes in parentheses, slip on the close-packed combinations, and rolled products carrying their processing history as directionality. The labels are dry; what they index is not.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Indexing a direction and a plane

Problem. (a) Index the direction from the origin to the point \( (1, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1) \) in cell coordinates. (b) Index the plane with intercepts \( x = \tfrac{1}{2} \), \( y = 1 \), \( z = \infty \).

Solution. (a) Components \( (1, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1) \); clear fractions ×2 → [212]. (b) Reciprocals: \( 1/\tfrac{1}{2} = 2 \), \( 1/1 = 1 \), \( 1/\infty = 0 \) → (210).

Answer: [212] and (210). Sanity check: (210) is parallel to the z-axis — its zero third index says so directly; a plane's zero always marks the axis it never meets. ✓

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Linear density along ⟨110⟩ in copper

Problem. Copper is FCC with \( R = 0.128\ \mathrm{nm} \). Find: the linear density of atoms along [110].

Solution. (1) [110] runs along a face diagonal — in FCC, the close-packed direction where atoms touch continuously. (2) The face diagonal has length \( 4R \) and passes through 2 atom diameters' worth of centers: corner atom (½) + face atom (1) + corner atom (½) = 2 atoms. (3) \( LD = \dfrac{2}{4R} = \dfrac{1}{2R} = \dfrac{1}{0.256\ \mathrm{nm}} = 3.91\ \mathrm{atoms/nm} \).

Answer: 3.91 atoms/nm — the maximum possible for copper: atoms in contact the whole way. Sanity check: no direction can pack tighter than touching spheres, so every other direction in the crystal must come out below 3.91 — [100], for instance, carries \( 1/a = 2.76 \) atoms/nm. Slip picks the 3.91 highway, not the 2.76 side road.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Atoms per nanometre along the slip direction

Problem. In FCC copper (R = 0.128 nm), the close-packed directions are \( \langle 110 \rangle \), where atoms touch in a continuous row. Find: the linear density along this direction.

Solution. (1) Along a close-packed row, centers sit \( 2R \) apart. (2) Linear density \( = 1/(2R) = 1/(2 \times 0.128) = 3.91 \) atoms/nm. (3) This is the maximum possible for the element — no direction packs tighter, which is precisely why slip chooses it.

Answer: ≈3.9 atoms per nanometre. Sanity check: the face diagonal of the cell \( (a\sqrt{2} = 0.512\ \mathrm{nm}) \) hosts exactly 2 atom spacings \( (2 \times 2R = 0.512) \) — the geometry closes on itself, confirming both the lattice parameter and the density.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A plane intercepts the x-axis at 1 and is parallel to both y and z axes. Its Miller indices:

Intercepts \( (1, \infty, \infty) \) invert to \( (1, 0, 0) \). Parallel-to-axis always inverts to zero — the zeros in an index are a map of what the plane never touches.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Slip occurs preferentially on:

Close-packed planes are smooth at the atomic scale — widest spacing between planes, shortest slip step along dense directions, least bond disruption per increment of slide. In FCC that is {111}⟨110⟩: 12 systems, and the geometric reason FCC metals form so well.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

A plane intercepts the crystal axes at 1, ∞, ∞ (in cell-edge units). Its Miller indices are:

Reciprocals of 1, ∞, ∞ are 1, 0, 0 — the (100) plane, a cell face. Infinite intercept means parallel to that axis and earns the zero; forgetting the reciprocal step (or which axis was intercepted) produces the other options. The recipe — intercepts, reciprocals, clear — runs identically every time, which is its entire virtue.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

Rolled plate is stronger and tougher along the rolling direction than across it because:

Rolling elongates grains, develops crystallographic texture, and strings out inclusions along the rolling direction — the microstructure becomes directional, so properties follow. This is why specs fix test-bar orientation and why press-brake bends run across the grain where possible.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

FCC metals are generally more ductile than HCP metals because FCC offers:

Ductility needs slip systems — close-packed planes carrying close-packed directions — available in every loading orientation. FCC provides twelve; basic HCP only three, so many grains in an HCP polycrystal sit awkwardly to the load and crack rather than flow. Bond strength, atom size, and density are the wrong level of explanation: geometry of slip is the mechanism.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the bend that cracked across the grain. You arrive at a steel fabricator's press-brake station where identical flange bends crack on one batch of plate but only when the bend line runs parallel to the rolling direction (figures representative). The plate is anisotropic: rolling elongated its grains and strung out its inclusions, so bending across the grain flow stretches strong material while bending along it opens the weak, delaminating direction. The shop fix is layout discipline — mark the rolling direction on every sheet and orient bends across it — which is Lesson 4's anisotropy translated into chalk lines.
