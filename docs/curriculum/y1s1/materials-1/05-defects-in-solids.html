Defects in solids

Vacancies, interstitials, and substitutional atoms; dislocations edge and screw; grain boundaries. The defect population as the true design variable. Perfect crystals are a textbook fiction — usefully so.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why are dislocations, not perfect lattices, the reason metals deform at realistic stresses?
  • Name the point, line, and planar defects and one property each controls.

01 · The perfect crystal is a useful fiction

Every real crystal is defective, and the defects are not blemishes on the theory — they are the theory of real behavior. By dimensionality: point defects — vacancies (missing atoms), interstitials (atoms squeezed into gaps), and substitutional or interstitial solute atoms; line defects — dislocations, edge and screw; planar defects — grain boundaries, twin boundaries, free surfaces. Each class controls a different family of properties, and the rest of this course is largely the story of engineering their populations.

02 · Vacancies obey thermodynamics

Vacancies are not accidents; equilibrium demands them. Their fraction rises exponentially with temperature:

\[ \frac{N_v}{N} = \exp\!\left(-\frac{Q_v}{kT}\right) \tag{1} \]

Near the melting point roughly one site in ten thousand is empty; at room temperature the fraction is astronomically smaller. This matters because vacancies are diffusion's vehicle — atoms move mainly by hopping into adjacent empty sites — so (1) is the deep reason every diffusion-controlled process in Lesson 6 accelerates exponentially with temperature.

03 · Dislocations: why metals are a thousand times weaker than they should be

Shearing a perfect crystal means breaking every bond across a plane simultaneously — the theoretical strength, orders of magnitude above anything measured. A dislocation breaks the bonds one row at a time, like moving a carpet by running a wrinkle across it rather than dragging the whole rug. Metals deform at realistic stresses because dislocations exist; they strengthen when we obstruct dislocation motion — with solutes, boundaries, and other dislocations, the entire agenda of Lesson 9. Grain boundaries, meanwhile, are both obstacles (strength) and fast-diffusion highways (creep, corrosion sensitivity): planar defects always charge rent both ways.

04 · Reading the defect inventory

Perfection is not an option in real crystals, so the working method is inventory management. Know each defect's dimension: point defects (vacancies, interstitials, substitutionals), line defects (dislocations), planar defects (grain boundaries, surfaces) — and match each to the property it governs: vacancies to diffusion, dislocations to plasticity, boundaries to strength and to creep. Respect the exponential: the vacancy fraction \( e^{-Q_v/kT} \) is astronomically sensitive to temperature — around \( 3\times10^{-5} \) at 1000 K for a typical metal and essentially zero at room temperature — so processes that need vacancies (diffusion, annealing, creep) switch on with heat and freeze out without it. That single exponential explains why heat treatment works at temperature and why parts are stable at service conditions.

The professional reading: defects are not flaws to lament but levers to pull. Carbon interstitials ARE steel; substitutional chromium IS stainless; grain boundaries ARE the strengthening in fine-grained plate. The materials engineer's job is rarely to remove defects — it is to choose which defects, at what population, on purpose. The next two lessons pull exactly those levers.

A note on scale keeps the inventory honest: even a heavily defective crystal is mostly perfect. At the 1000 K figure computed in this lesson's examples, fewer than three sites in a hundred thousand stand vacant — and yet that trace population carries all of diffusion. Materials behavior is routinely governed by its rarest constituents, which is why counting them, rather than rounding them to zero, is a professional skill.

Closing. Point defects carry diffusion, line defects carry plasticity, planar defects referee both. "Defect" is the field's least fortunate word — these are the control knobs.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Vacancy census

Problem. For a metal with vacancy formation energy \( Q_v = 0.9\ \mathrm{eV} \) (representative), compare the equilibrium vacancy fraction at 1000 K and at 500 K. \( k = 8.62 \times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{eV/K} \).

Solution. (1) At 1000 K: \( N_v/N = \exp[-0.9/(8.62 \times 10^{-5} \times 1000)] = e^{-10.44} = 2.9 \times 10^{-5} \). (2) At 500 K: \( N_v/N = e^{-20.88} = 8.5 \times 10^{-10} \). (3) Ratio: \( \approx 34{,}000 \times \) more vacancies at the higher temperature.

Answer: \( 2.9 \times 10^{-5} \) versus \( 8.5 \times 10^{-10} \). Sanity check: halving absolute temperature squares the (tiny) Boltzmann factor — the exponential's signature. This 34,000× swing in diffusion's vehicle fleet is why annealing happens in furnaces, not on benches.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Weight percent to atom percent

Problem. Cartridge brass is 70 wt% Cu, 30 wt% Zn (\( A_{Cu} = 63.55 \), \( A_{Zn} = 65.38\ \mathrm{g/mol} \)). Find: the composition in atom percent.

Solution. (1) Per 100 g: \( n_{Cu} = 70/63.55 = 1.101\ \mathrm{mol} \); \( n_{Zn} = 30/65.38 = 0.459\ \mathrm{mol} \). (2) \( at\%\ Cu = \dfrac{1.101}{1.101 + 0.459} \times 100 = 70.6\% \); \( at\%\ Zn = 29.4\% \).

Answer: 70.6 at% Cu, 29.4 at% Zn. Sanity check: copper and zinc have nearly equal atomic masses, so weight and atom percent nearly coincide — the conversion only bites when masses differ strongly (carbon in iron: 0.4 wt% C is ~1.8 at%). Knowing when the distinction matters is the actual skill.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Counting the missing atoms

Problem. A metal has a vacancy formation energy of 0.9 eV. Find: the equilibrium vacancy fraction at 1000 K \( (k = 8.62\times10^{-5}\ \mathrm{eV/K}) \), and comment on room temperature.

Solution. (1) \( N_v/N = e^{-Q_v/kT} = e^{-0.9/(8.62\times10^{-5} \times 1000)} = e^{-10.44} \). (2) \( = 2.9\times10^{-5} \) — about 29 vacancies per million sites. (3) At 300 K the exponent triples to −34.8: fraction \( \sim 10^{-16} \) — effectively none.

Answer: \( \approx 3\times10^{-5} \) at 1000 K; vanishing at room temperature. Sanity check: eleven orders of magnitude between furnace and floor from one exponential — exactly why diffusion-driven processes run hot and why parts hold their microstructure in service.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

Raising temperature increases a metal's equilibrium vacancy fraction:

\( N_v/N = e^{-Q_v/kT} \) — a Boltzmann factor, hence exponential. Halving absolute temperature squares the (small) fraction. Vacancies are thermodynamically required, continuously created and annihilated, not frozen-in accidents.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Metals deform at stresses far below the theoretical bond-breaking strength because:

A dislocation localizes the slip event: only one row of bonds breaks and re-forms at each step — the carpet moved by running a wrinkle across it. This thousandfold discount is why plasticity exists at workshop stresses, and why obstructing dislocations is how alloys are strengthened.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

Raising temperature from 300 K to 1000 K changes a metal's equilibrium vacancy population by roughly:

\( N_v/N = e^{-Q_v/kT} \): the temperature sits in an exponent, so tripling T moves the fraction from \( \sim10^{-16} \) toward \( \sim10^{-5} \) — about eleven orders of magnitude for a typical 0.9 eV formation energy. Linear intuition (×3, ×2) is exactly what the exponential exists to overthrow, and the difference is why annealing works hot and microstructures hold cold.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

With \( Q_v = 0.9 \) eV at 1000 K (\( k = 8.62 \times 10^{-5} \) eV/K), the vacancy fraction is about:

\( \exp[-0.9/(8.62\times10^{-5} \times 1000)] = e^{-10.4} \approx 2.9 \times 10^{-5} \) — roughly one site in 34,000 empty. \( 10^{-9} \) is the 500 K answer: the exponential's steepness is the whole point.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Carbon dissolved in iron occupies which kind of site?

The carbon atom is small enough to squeeze into the interstices of the iron lattice — and strain them, which is the root of steel's strengthening and of martensite's ferocity later in the course. Chromium and nickel, iron-sized, substitute instead. The site choice also sets diffusion speed: gap-hopping carbon moves orders of magnitude faster than site-swapping chromium at any temperature.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — two kinds of defect, one inspection report. You arrive at a fabrication shop as an NDT crew sentences a weld: porosity beyond code, cut it out. On the same bench sits the metallurgical irony of Lesson 5 — the sound weld metal beside the pores is itself a carnival of defects: vacancies from solidification, dislocations by the billion from thermal contraction, fresh grain boundaries everywhere (figures representative). Those defects are why the weld metal has useful strength and ductility at all. NDT polices macro-defects; materials science engineers micro-defects. Same word, opposite verdicts — and an engineer should keep the two ledgers straight.
