Recovery, recrystallization, and grain growth

What annealing actually does to a cold-worked metal. Recrystallization temperature and its rule-of-thumb relation to melting point. The hot-working/cold-working boundary defined properly.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What does recrystallization undo, and what does its temperature roughly track?
  • Define hot working without mentioning absolute temperature.

01 · What cold work leaves behind

A cold-worked metal is a loaded spring at the microscale: dislocation density up a thousandfold, grains flattened and rotated, a few percent of the deformation energy locked in as internal strain. That stored energy is the driving force for everything in this lesson — heat the metal and it will spend that energy reorganizing, in three acts.

02 · Recovery, recrystallization, grain growth

Recovery (lowest temperatures): dislocations climb, annihilate, and tidy into low-energy walls. Residual stress falls and conductivity returns, but strength barely moves — the stress-relief anneal lives here. Recrystallization: new, strain-free grains nucleate in the deformed matrix and consume it wholesale; strength drops toward the annealed baseline and ductility floods back. The temperature where this completes in about an hour — the recrystallization temperature — runs roughly

\[ T_R \approx 0.4\, T_m \quad (\mathrm{in\ kelvins}) \tag{1} \]

and falls with heavier prior cold work (more stored energy, more driving force). Grain growth: left hot after recrystallization finishes, boundaries migrate to cut boundary area, grains coarsen, and Hall–Petch (Lesson 9) runs in reverse. Annealing schedules end when recrystallization does, not later, for exactly this reason.

03 · Hot working, defined properly

Hot working is deformation above the recrystallization temperature — the metal recrystallizes as fast as the rolls harden it, so it never accumulates cold work. Cold working is deformation below \( T_R \). The boundary is homologous, not absolute: by (1), lead recrystallizes below room temperature — a fitter bending lead sheet is hot-working it at 25 °C — while tungsten at a red-hot 800 °C can still be cold-working. "Hot" belongs to the metal's melting point, not the thermometer.

Closing. Stored strain energy drives three acts — tidy, replace, coarsen — and \( 0.4\,T_m \) marks the stage door. Every anneal on every line is a decision about which act to stop after.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • Strain hardening (Lesson 9) — this lesson is its undo operation.
  • Dislocations and boundaries (Lesson 5) — the populations being reorganized.
  • Diffusion (Lesson 6) — all three acts are diffusion-paced, hence Arrhenius.

Glossary

  • Recovery — dislocation tidying; stress relief without strength loss.
  • Recrystallization — new strain-free grains replacing the cold-worked structure.
  • Recrystallization temperature \( T_R \) — ≈0.4 \( T_m \) (K); lower with more cold work.
  • Grain growth — boundary-area-driven coarsening; strength's slow leak.
  • Hot / cold working — deformation above / below \( T_R \).

Problem 1 of 5 · solve, then check

Two metals, one rule of thumb

Problem. Estimate recrystallization temperatures for copper (\( T_m = 1358\ \mathrm{K} \)) and lead (\( T_m = 601\ \mathrm{K} \)).

Solution. (1) Copper: \( T_R \approx 0.4 \times 1358 = 543\ \mathrm{K} = 270\ \mathrm{^\circ C} \). (2) Lead: \( T_R \approx 0.4 \times 601 = 240\ \mathrm{K} = -33\ \mathrm{^\circ C} \).

Answer: Cu ≈ 270 °C; Pb ≈ −33 °C. Sanity check: lead's \( T_R \) sits below any room you will ever stand in — lead cannot be cold-worked at ambient and never strain-hardens on the bench, which any plumber has observed without naming. Copper's 270 °C is why annealing furnaces on wire lines run at moderate red heat and why drawn copper stays hard in service. (Rule-of-thumb figures; alloying and prior cold work shift them.)

Problem 2 of 5 · multiple choice

Nickel melts at 1728 K. Its recrystallization temperature is roughly:

\( T_R \approx 0.4\,T_m = 0.4 \times 1728 = 691\ \mathrm{K} = 418\ \mathrm{^\circ C} \). Choice B quotes the kelvin figure as celsius — the classic unit slip in homologous-temperature work. (Rule of thumb; alloying and prior cold work shift it.)

Problem 3 of 5 · multiple choice

A stress-relief anneal that must not soften the part should stop at:

Recovery lets dislocations tidy into low-energy arrangements — residual stress falls, conductivity returns, but strength barely moves. Recrystallization replaces the strengthened structure wholesale and erases cold work; grain growth then makes things worse. Anneal schedules are decisions about which act to stop after.

Problem 4 of 5 · solve, then check

Is 500 °C hot working for brass?

Problem. Brass (\( T_m \approx 1188\ \mathrm{K} \), representative) is formed at 500 °C. Find: the homologous temperature and the classification.

Solution. (1) \( T = 773\ \mathrm{K} \). (2) Homologous: \( T/T_m = 773/1188 = 0.65 \). (3) \( 0.65 > 0.4 \): comfortably above the recrystallization threshold — hot working.

Answer: \( T/T_m = 0.65 \); hot working. Sanity check: at 0.65 homologous the metal recrystallizes during deformation itself, so the press sees low, steady flow stress and the part emerges strain-free — the entire commercial point of working hot. The same 500 °C applied to tungsten (\( T_m = 3695\ \mathrm{K} \), homologous 0.21) would be cold work, restating 3: hot is a ratio, not a reading.

Problem 5 of 5 · multiple choice

A fitter bends lead sheet at room temperature. Metallurgically, this is:

Lead's \( T_R \approx 0.4 \times 601 = 240\ \mathrm{K} = -33\ \mathrm{^\circ C} \): any workshop is a hot-working mill for lead, which is why it never strain-hardens on the bench. \"Hot\" is homologous — a ratio to the melting point, not a thermometer reading.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the mill that anneals as it rolls. You arrive at the Kuwait Steel rolling mill where reheated billets thread the stands glowing — deformation at well above half the melting temperature, recrystallizing between and during passes, which is why a bar can take enormous cumulative reduction without ever cracking (figures representative). The finishing stands and the cooling bed then double as heat treatment: roll temperatures and cooling rates are scheduled so the final recrystallized grain size lands where the rebar spec's strength and bendability need it. Lesson 10 is not adjacent to a rolling mill; it is the operating principle of one.
