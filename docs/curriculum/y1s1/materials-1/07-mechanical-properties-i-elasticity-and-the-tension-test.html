Mechanical properties I: elasticity and the tension test

Engineering stress and strain; modulus, yield, UTS, ductility from the tensile curve. True versus engineering quantities. The tension test as the industry's reference experiment.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • From a tensile curve, extract modulus, yield, UTS, and ductility — where does each live?
  • Why does true stress keep rising while engineering stress falls after necking?

01 · One test to rule them

Pull a standard bar to fracture, record load and elongation, and normalize away the geometry: engineering stress \( \sigma = F/A_0 \), engineering strain \( \epsilon = \Delta L/L_0 \), both referred to original dimensions. The resulting curve is the most quoted document in mechanical engineering, and four numbers summarize it: the elastic modulus \( E \) (initial slope, Hooke's law \( \sigma = E\epsilon \)); the yield strength \( \sigma_y \) (0.2% offset, where permanent set begins); the ultimate tensile strength (the curve's peak); and ductility, as percent elongation or reduction of area.

02 · What each number is for

\( E \) is a bonding property — recall Lesson 2's well curvature — essentially untouchable by heat treatment: all steels share ~200 GPa. \( \sigma_y \) is the designer's boundary; machines are sized so working stress stays below it with margin. UTS matters for what happens beyond intent — overload margins and, through empirical correlations, hardness checks. Ductility is the forgiveness budget: the plastic reserve that lets a part yield locally, redistribute, and warn before it breaks.

03 · True stress, and the necking illusion

Past the UTS the engineering curve droops — but the material is not softening. Necking has begun: area shrinks locally, and dividing load by the original area hides it. True stress \( \sigma_T = F/A \) (instantaneous area) keeps rising to fracture; with volume constancy the conversions in the plastic range are

\[ \sigma_T = \sigma(1+\epsilon), \qquad \epsilon_T = \ln(1+\epsilon) \tag{1} \]

Metal forming lives on true quantities — a rolling pass or a drawing die sees real areas, not test-report conventions. the Worked Examples tab extracts all four report numbers from raw data, then converts a point through (1).

04 · Reading a tension test like a professional

The tensile curve is read in a fixed order, and each stop answers one design question. The elastic slope first: Young's modulus — and the professional expectation that it barely varies within an alloy family; every carbon steel runs near 200 GPa, so a certificate claiming otherwise indicts the test, not the steel. Yield next, by the 0.2% offset construction when the knee is gradual: draw the line parallel to the elastic slope from \( \varepsilon = 0.002 \) and read the intersection — this is the number design codes divide by safety factors. Ultimate strength marks where necking begins: beyond it, the ENGINEERING curve droops only because the true area is shrinking faster than the load — the material itself is still hardening, and knowing that resolves the classic "why does the curve fall?" confusion. Ductility last: percent elongation, the formability budget manufacturing will spend.

The working method for any calculation is unit discipline on \( \sigma = F/A_0 \) and \( \varepsilon = \Delta L/L_0 \): kilonewtons over square millimetres give megapascals directly, and strain is always dimensionless — a strain quoted in millimetres is a gauge length away from being a number. Verify computed moduli against the family expectation before reporting; the check costs nothing and catches decimal slips every time.

Closing. One bar, one pull, four numbers, and one conversion that keeps the story honest after necking. Every mill certificate you will ever read is this lesson in tabular form.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Reading the test

Problem. A standard bar, \( d_0 = 12.8\ \mathrm{mm} \), \( L_0 = 50.8\ \mathrm{mm} \): elastic point (150 MPa at \( \epsilon = 0.00075 \)); yield load 35 kN; final gauge length 60.14 mm. Find: \( E \), \( \sigma_y \), %EL.

Solution. (1) \( A_0 = \pi(12.8)^2/4 = 128.7\ \mathrm{mm^2} \). (2) \( E = 150/0.00075 = 200\,000\ \mathrm{MPa} = 200\ \mathrm{GPa} \). (3) \( \sigma_y = 35\,000/128.7 = 272\ \mathrm{MPa} \). (4) \( \%EL = \dfrac{60.14 - 50.8}{50.8} \times 100 = 18.4\% \).

Answer: \( E = 200\ \mathrm{GPa} \), \( \sigma_y = 272\ \mathrm{MPa} \), %EL = 18.4 — a garden-variety structural steel. Sanity check: \( E \) landing on 200 GPa flags the material as steel before anyone reads the cert header; a result of 70 GPa would have said aluminum, whatever the paperwork claimed.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Engineering to true

Problem. At its UTS a specimen shows engineering stress 400 MPa at engineering strain 0.15. Find: the true stress and true strain (uniform deformation, volume constant).

Solution. (1) \( \sigma_T = \sigma(1+\epsilon) = 400 \times 1.15 = 460\ \mathrm{MPa} \). (2) \( \epsilon_T = \ln(1.15) = 0.140 \).

Answer: 460 MPa at true strain 0.140. Sanity check: true stress exceeds engineering (the bar is thinner than \( A_0 \) pretends) while true strain trails engineering — both inequalities always hold in tension, a two-second validity test on any converted data point.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Modulus from the elastic leg

Problem. A tensile specimen of cross-section 128.7 mm² and gauge length 50 mm carries 25.74 kN in the elastic range, elongating 0.050 mm. Find: the stress, the strain, and Young's modulus — and judge the result.

Solution. (1) \( \sigma = 25{,}740/128.7 = 200\ \mathrm{MPa} \). (2) \( \varepsilon = 0.050/50 = 0.0010 \). (3) \( E = \sigma/\varepsilon = 200/0.0010 = 200{,}000\ \mathrm{MPa} = 200\ \mathrm{GPa} \).

Answer: 200 MPa; 0.001; \( E = 200\ \mathrm{GPa} \). Sanity check: 200 GPa is exactly the carbon-steel family value — the specimen behaves as steel should. Had the arithmetic produced 20 or 2000 GPa, the physics (not the steel) would be under suspicion: modulus is the most constant number in the alloy family, which makes it the best error detector in the test.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

A 50 kN load on a 100 mm² bar produces an engineering stress of:

\( \sigma = F/A_0 = 50\,000\ \mathrm{N} / 100\ \mathrm{mm^2} = 500\ \mathrm{N/mm^2} = 500\ \mathrm{MPa} \). The N/mm² = MPa identity is the unit fluency this course assumes from here on.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

Quenching and tempering a steel changes all of these except:

Modulus is bond stiffness — a property of the iron lattice itself, ~200 GPa for every steel regardless of heat treatment. Strength, hardness, and ductility all ride the microstructure, which is exactly what heat treatment rearranges. \"Stiffer steel via heat treat\" is a myth this question retires.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

The 0.2% offset construction exists because:

Aluminum alloys and many steels bend smoothly from elastic to plastic with no drop or plateau — so the convention draws a line parallel to the elastic slope, offset by 0.002 strain, and defines yield at the intersection. It is a convention, chosen for reproducibility, not a physical event at exactly 0.2% — and the same part number measured by two labs agrees because both run the same construction.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

Past the UTS, the engineering stress–strain curve falls because:

Deformation localizes into a neck; load drops, but true stress \( F/A \) keeps rising to fracture. The droop is an artifact of dividing by the original area — the material is still strain-hardening. True-stress conversion (\( \sigma_T = \sigma(1+\epsilon) \) while uniform) keeps the record honest.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

Past the ultimate tensile strength, the engineering stress–strain curve falls. The material at the neck is actually:

Engineering stress divides by the ORIGINAL area; once necking localizes, the true area collapses while true stress keeps rising to fracture. The drooping tail is an artifact of the \( A_0 \) convention, not material weakness — the classic misreading this question retires. True-stress curves, which divide by current area, rise monotonically to the end.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — the certificate is a tensile curve in a table. You arrive at a steel fabricator's receiving inspection where a plate delivery's mill certificate lists yield 355 MPa, UTS 510 MPa, elongation 22% (figures representative). Lesson 7 is the decoder: those three numbers are the tensile curve compressed for shipping. The estimator reads 355 for design margin, the press-brake foreman reads 22% for bendability, and the welding engineer reads the yield-to-UTS gap as work-hardening reserve. One test in a distant mill, one table, three trades making decisions from it — which is why certs are checked against the order, not filed unread.
