MAT 104 · YEAR 1 · SEMESTER 1

Materials Science I

Atoms to microstructure: bonding, crystal structures, defects, diffusion, and mechanical behavior — the vocabulary in which every later conversation about steels, failure, and heat treatment is conducted.

Syllabus

01

Why materials fail the plantFull lesson

Materials classes — metals, ceramics, polymers, composites — and what each trades away. Structure–property–processing–performance as the course's spine. Reading a failed component as a materials question.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 1

Checkpoint questions
  • Rank metals, ceramics, polymers by stiffness and by toughness — what trades where?
  • State the structure–property–processing–performance chain for one familiar part.
02

Atomic bondingFull lesson

Metallic, ionic, covalent, and secondary bonding. Bond energy curves and where stiffness and thermal expansion come from. Why metals conduct and deform while ceramics shatter.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 2

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do metals conduct and deform while ceramics insulate and shatter — from bonding alone?
  • What does a deep, narrow bond-energy well predict about stiffness and expansion?
03

Crystal structuresFull lesson

BCC, FCC, and HCP cells; coordination and packing factors. Density calculations from lattice data. Why FCC metals form and slip differently from BCC — a fact with cold-weather consequences.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 3

Checkpoint questions
  • Compute-style: which is denser at equal atomic mass, FCC or BCC, and why?
  • Why do BCC steels turn brittle in cold weather while FCC metals don't?
04

Crystallographic directions and planesFull lesson

Miller indices for directions and planes. Close-packed planes and slip systems. Anisotropy: why rolled plate has direction-dependent properties.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 3

Checkpoint questions
  • What makes close-packed planes the natural slip planes?
  • Why does rolled plate have direction-dependent properties?
05

Defects in solidsFull lesson

Vacancies, interstitials, and substitutional atoms; dislocations edge and screw; grain boundaries. The defect population as the true design variable. Perfect crystals are a textbook fiction — usefully so.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 4

Checkpoint questions
  • Why are dislocations, not perfect lattices, the reason metals deform at realistic stresses?
  • Name the point, line, and planar defects and one property each controls.
06

DiffusionFull lesson

Fick's first and second laws with the physical picture of jumping atoms. Temperature dependence through the Arrhenius form. Carburizing depth as a worked application — the √Dt scaling that recurs everywhere.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 5

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does case-hardening depth scale with √(Dt)?
  • What does the Arrhenius form say about doubling temperature versus doubling time?
07

Mechanical properties I: elasticity and the tension testFull lesson

Engineering stress and strain; modulus, yield, UTS, ductility from the tensile curve. True versus engineering quantities. The tension test as the industry's reference experiment.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 6

Checkpoint questions
  • From a tensile curve, extract modulus, yield, UTS, and ductility — where does each live?
  • Why does true stress keep rising while engineering stress falls after necking?
08

Mechanical properties II: hardness and toughnessFull lesson

Hardness scales — Brinell, Vickers, Rockwell — and their conversions and abuses. Impact energy and the ductile-brittle transition. Hardness as the maintenance engineer's field instrument.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 6

Checkpoint questions
  • Why is hardness a proxy for strength, and when does the proxy break?
  • What does the ductile–brittle transition mean for winter impact loads?
09

Dislocations and strengtheningFull lesson

Slip as dislocation motion. Strengthening by grain refinement, solid solution, strain hardening, and precipitates — all as obstacles to dislocations. Why 'harder' usually costs 'tougher'.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 7

Checkpoint questions
  • Explain grain-boundary strengthening in one sentence about dislocations.
  • Why does strain hardening raise strength but cost ductility?
10

Recovery, recrystallization, and grain growthFull lesson

What annealing actually does to a cold-worked metal. Recrystallization temperature and its rule-of-thumb relation to melting point. The hot-working/cold-working boundary defined properly.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 7

Checkpoint questions
  • What does recrystallization undo, and what does its temperature roughly track?
  • Define hot working without mentioning absolute temperature.
11

Solidification and cast structureFull lesson

Nucleation and growth; chill, columnar, and equiaxed zones in a casting. Segregation at the scale of dendrites and of ingots. The materials-science half of the casting lectures in MFG 105.

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 9; Flemings, Solidification Processing

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do castings show chill, columnar, and equiaxed zones?
  • Where does microsegregation live, and why does it survive solidification?
  • Callister & Rethwisch, Materials Science and Engineering: An Introduction (Wiley)

Career outlook — where this course pays

Materials literacy — reading certificates, hardness numbers, and microstructures — is the quiet qualification behind quality, inspection, and plant-engineering roles across sectors:

  • Infrastructure & utilities — conductor and insulation metallurgy is the daily business of cable producers such as Gulf Cable: wire-drawing work hardening, annealing schedules, and polymer insulation behavior are Lessons 8–10 of this course running as a production line.
  • Automotive & heavy equipment — workshop and parts-quality engineers at dealerships and fleet operators live on hardness triage, fastener grades, and failed-component reading: the fracture-surface and heat-treatment literacy built here decides warranty arguments.
  • Global technology & OEM — industrial-technology houses such as Schneider Electric hire materials-aware engineers for contact materials, polymer enclosures, and supplier quality — roles where a certificate audit and a density check are day-one skills.

Interview angle: the recurring test is a part on the table and the question "why did this fail, and what would you check first?" — the failure-mode-first method of Lesson 1, spoken aloud. Candidates who name the mode before naming an alloy pass.
