Dislocations and strengthening

Slip as dislocation motion. Strengthening by grain refinement, solid solution, strain hardening, and precipitates — all as obstacles to dislocations. Why 'harder' usually costs 'tougher'.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Explain grain-boundary strengthening in one sentence about dislocations.
  • Why does strain hardening raise strength but cost ductility?

01 · One enemy, many obstacles

Plastic flow is dislocation traffic (Lesson 5), so all strengthening is one strategy: obstruct the traffic. Every industrial method is a different obstacle wearing a different price tag — and each purchase of strength is paid for, almost always, in ductility. Four mechanisms cover the metallurgy you will meet in practice.

02 · The four mechanisms

Grain refinement. A boundary forces slip to renegotiate direction; more boundaries, more negotiations. Quantitatively, the Hall–Petch relation:

\[ \sigma_y = \sigma_0 + k_y\, d^{-1/2} \tag{1} \]

— fine grains strengthen, and (uniquely among the four) usually improve toughness too, which is why fine grain is the closest metallurgy comes to a free lunch. Solid solution. Solute atoms strain the lattice locally; dislocations wade through the strain fields. Every alloying addition does some of this. Strain hardening. Deform the metal and dislocations multiply until they obstruct each other — quantified by percent cold work, spent by every rolling pass and drawing die. Precipitation. Fine second-phase particles force dislocations to cut or bow around them; the strongest aluminum alloys and nickel superalloys live here.

03 · Choosing among them

The mechanisms stack — a drawn, alloyed, fine-grained wire uses three at once — but they differ in temperature stability (strain hardening anneals away; precipitates coarsen), in cost, and in what they do to toughness and conductivity. A copper conductor is the cautionary tale: solutes that strengthen it also scatter electrons, so cable copper is strengthened by drawing, not doping. the Worked Examples tab prices mechanism one with real Hall–Petch constants and mechanism three on a drawn rod.

04 · The strengthening decision method

Every strengthening mechanism is the same sentence with a different subject: something obstructs dislocations. The engineering skill is choosing WHICH something, and the choice runs on three questions. What temperature will the part see? Strain hardening anneals away — it cannot serve hot; grain-boundary strengthening fades as boundaries begin to slide at creep temperatures; solid-solution and precipitation strengthening hold higher. What process route is available? Cold work arrives free with rolling and drawing; fine grain arrives through controlled rolling and normalizing; precipitates demand the full solution-quench-age cycle of the heat-treatment lessons ahead. What does the trade cost? Every mechanism buys strength with ductility — the strengthened metal forms less willingly, and a drawing schedule that ignores this snaps wire.

Run Hall–Petch as the worked example of quantitative thinking: \( \sigma_y = \sigma_0 + k\,d^{-1/2} \) turns a micrograph's grain size into a yield prediction, and its inverse square root explains why halving grain size buys a meaningful but diminishing return. The mechanisms stack — commercial alloys routinely deploy three at once — and reading a datasheet is recognizing which levers its producer pulled. A dual-phase automotive steel, for instance, runs fine grains, solute, and a hard second phase simultaneously; its certificate is a three-lever chord, and hearing the chord is what this lesson trains.

Closing. Strength is obstructed dislocation motion — by boundaries, solutes, tangles, or particles. Name the obstacle in any datasheet's "condition" column and you have read the alloy's biography.

Examples and Quiz

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

Hall–Petch, priced in microns

Problem. For 70/30 brass, \( \sigma_0 = 70\ \mathrm{MPa} \), \( k_y = 0.74\ \mathrm{MPa \cdot m^{1/2}} \) (representative constants, Callister & Rethwisch). Find: yield strength at grain sizes 25 µm and 8 µm.

Solution. (1) \( d = 25\ \mathrm{\mu m} = 2.5 \times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{m} \): \( d^{-1/2} = 200\ \mathrm{m^{-1/2}} \); \( \sigma_y = 70 + 0.74 \times 200 = 218\ \mathrm{MPa} \). (2) \( d = 8\ \mathrm{\mu m} \): \( d^{-1/2} = 354 \); \( \sigma_y = 70 + 0.74 \times 354 = 332\ \mathrm{MPa} \).

Answer: 218 → 332 MPa: a 3× grain refinement buys ~50% more yield strength. Sanity check: the square root means returns diminish — the next 50% costs refining toward the micron scale, which is why Hall–Petch is a strategy, not an infinite staircase.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Cold work in a drawing die

Problem. A rod is drawn from 10 mm to 8 mm diameter. Find: the percent cold work.

Solution. (1) Areas scale with diameter squared: \( A_0 \propto 100 \), \( A_d \propto 64 \). (2) \( \%CW = \dfrac{100 - 64}{100} \times 100 = 36\% \).

Answer: 36% cold work. Sanity check: a 20% diameter reduction is a 36% area reduction — area, not diameter, is what the dislocation population responds to. At this level of cold work a brass or copper would show markedly higher strength and visibly less tensile ductility; whether that is product or problem depends on whether an anneal comes next — Lesson 10's question.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Yield strength from a micrograph

Problem. A steel follows Hall–Petch with \( \sigma_0 = 70\ \mathrm{MPa} \) and \( k = 0.74\ \mathrm{MPa\sqrt{m}} \). Metallography shows 25 µm grains. Find: the yield strength, and the value if processing refines the grains to 6.25 µm.

Solution. (1) \( d^{-1/2} = (25\times10^{-6})^{-1/2} = 200\ \mathrm{m^{-1/2}} \). (2) \( \sigma_y = 70 + 0.74 \times 200 = 218\ \mathrm{MPa} \). (3) At 6.25 µm: \( d^{-1/2} = 400 \Rightarrow \sigma_y = 70 + 296 = 366\ \mathrm{MPa} \).

Answer: ≈218 MPa; refining grains fourfold lifts yield to ≈366 MPa. Sanity check: quartering the grain size doubles \( d^{-1/2} \) — the square root working exactly as written — and the gain (+148 MPa) exceeds the base lattice strength itself: grain refinement is the one strengthening lever that also IMPROVES toughness, which is why structural steels chase it.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

Every metallic strengthening mechanism works by:

Plastic flow is dislocation traffic; boundaries, solute strain fields, dislocation tangles, and precipitates are four kinds of roadblock. Bond stiffness (modulus) barely moves — which is why strengthened alloys are stronger but not stiffer, echoing Lesson 7's modulus question.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

With \( \sigma_0 = 70 \) MPa and \( k_y = 0.74\ \mathrm{MPa\sqrt{m}} \), refining grains to 10 µm gives a yield strength of about:

\( d^{-1/2} = (10^{-5})^{-1/2} = 316\ \mathrm{m^{-1/2}} \); \( \sigma_y = 70 + 0.74 \times 316 = 304\ \mathrm{MPa} \). Hall–Petch rewards fine grains through the square root — and uniquely among the mechanisms it usually improves toughness at the same time.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

Cold-rolled sheet is stronger than annealed sheet of the same steel because rolling:

Plastic deformation manufactures dislocations by the billions; they tangle and block one another, so further slip needs more stress — strain hardening. The intuition that working metal 'removes defects' is exactly backwards: cold work is defect FARMING, on purpose. Composition is untouched; the strengthening lives entirely in the defect population, which is why an anneal can erase it.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

Percent cold work is computed from the change in:

\( \%CW = (A_0 - A_d)/A_0 \times 100 \). A 10 → 8 mm draw looks like \"20%\" by diameter but is 36% by area — and area is what the dislocation population responds to. Hardness rises because of cold work; it is the effect, not the measure.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

According to Hall–Petch, at room temperature a finer grain size makes a metal:

Each boundary forces dislocations to stop and restart in the next grain, so more boundary per volume means higher yield: \( \sigma_y = \sigma_0 + k d^{-1/2} \). The 'defects weaken' instinct inverts at room temperature — though it returns at creep temperatures, where boundaries slide. Uniquely among strengthening levers, grain refinement usually improves toughness too, which is why it is the structural-steel favorite.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Vignette — strength you can watch accumulating. You arrive at a cable plant's wire-drawing line where copper rod enters at one diameter and leaves several dies later markedly thinner, stiffer, and springier — strain hardening in real time, worked example 2 running continuously (figures representative). The wire must end soft for stranding and conductivity, yet every die pass hardens it: the line therefore alternates drawing with annealing, spending Lesson 9's mechanism and Lesson 10's cure in a scheduled rhythm. The process engineer's die schedule is, in effect, a %CW budget — spent, cleared, and spent again.
