Solidification and cast structure

Nucleation and growth; chill, columnar, and equiaxed zones in a casting. Segregation at the scale of dendrites and of ingots. The materials-science half of the casting lectures in MFG 105.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why do castings show chill, columnar, and equiaxed zones?
  • Where does microsegregation live, and why does it survive solidification?

01 · Nucleation: the reluctant start

A melt below its freezing point does not freeze at once — a solid nucleus must first reach a critical size before growing is cheaper than redissolving. The balance of surface energy paid against volume energy won sets the critical radius:

\[ r^* = \frac{2\gamma T_m}{\Delta H_f\, \Delta T} \tag{1} \]

— inversely proportional to the undercooling \( \Delta T \). Homogeneous nucleation needs implausible undercoolings; real melts nucleate heterogeneously on mold walls and inoculant particles, which is why foundries seed melts deliberately: more nuclei, finer grains, better castings.

02 · The three zones of a casting

Read a sectioned billet or ingot and solidification history is written in its grains. Chill zone: a skin of fine equiaxed grains, nucleated in a rush against the cold mold. Columnar zone: long grains growing inward along the heat-flow direction, competing until the best-aligned survive. Central equiaxed zone: grains nucleated in the cooling interior liquid. The proportions are process knobs — pouring superheat, mold cooling, stirring — and they matter because columnar structures are anisotropic and their convergence planes are weak.

03 · Segregation: solidification's chemical fingerprint

Solids accept less solute than liquids, so a growing crystal rejects solute into the melt ahead of it. At dendrite scale this leaves microsegregation — solute-lean dendrite cores, solute-rich interdendritic pockets, quantified by the lever rule on the phase diagram and erasable only by homogenization (Lesson 6's \( \sqrt{Dt} \), with the arm spacing as the distance). At full-ingot scale, macrosegregation — centerline enrichment in continuously cast products — cannot be diffused away at any industrial timescale; it must be managed in the caster. Coring survives because solid-state diffusion is slow: the fingerprint outlives the process that made it.

04 · Reading a casting's history from its structure

A sectioned casting is a recording of its own solidification, and the working method is to read it zone by zone. The chill zone at the surface — fine random grains — says the mold quenched the first metal fast. The columnar zone — long grains aimed at the center — says heat left through the walls, and its aligned boundaries are weak paths that matter for load direction. The equiaxed center says nucleation won the race in the last, slowest-cooling liquid. Alongside the zones, read the defects as verbs: centerline shrinkage means feeding failed; segregation bands mean solute was pushed ahead of the fronts; gas porosity's round voids mean dissolved gas came out of solution.

Then run the arithmetic that controls all of it: Chvorinov's rule, \( t = B(V/A)^2 \), prices each region's solidification time by its modulus \( V/A \) — and the whole craft of risering is making the riser's modulus larger than the casting's, so the riser freezes LAST and feeds shrinkage instead of hosting it. The same square law says thin sections freeze fast and fine, heavy bosses freeze slow and coarse — so properties vary across one casting by design necessity, a fact the machine-design courses will inherit and respect.

Closing. Nucleation sets the grain count, growth writes the three zones, rejection writes the chemistry. A casting's cross-section is a recording — this lesson teaches you to play it back.

Problem 1 of 8 · solve, then check

The critical nucleus for iron

Problem. For pure iron (representative values, Callister & Rethwisch): \( \gamma = 0.255\ \mathrm{J/m^2} \), \( \Delta H_f = 1.85 \times 10^9\ \mathrm{J/m^3} \), \( T_m = 1811\ \mathrm{K} \), homogeneous undercooling \( \Delta T = 295\ \mathrm{K} \). Find: \( r^* \).

Solution. (1) \( r^* = \dfrac{2 \times 0.255 \times 1811}{1.85 \times 10^9 \times 295} \). (2) Numerator: 923.6; denominator: \( 5.46 \times 10^{11} \). (3) \( r^* = 1.69 \times 10^{-9}\ \mathrm{m} \approx 1.7\ \mathrm{nm} \).

Answer: ≈1.7 nm — a cluster of only a few thousand atoms. Sanity check: the required undercooling, ~295 K, is enormous — real ladles never see it, confirming 1: industrial solidification is heterogeneous, nucleating on walls and particles at a few degrees of undercooling instead.

Problem 2 of 8 · solve, then check

Lever rule on a freezing alloy

Problem. A Cu–35 wt% Ni alloy sits in the two-phase field where the liquid contains 32 wt% Ni and the solid 43 wt% Ni (compositions from the Cu–Ni diagram, Callister & Rethwisch). Find: the phase fractions.

Solution. (1) Lever rule, liquid fraction: \( W_L = \dfrac{C_s - C_0}{C_s - C_L} = \dfrac{43 - 35}{43 - 32} = \dfrac{8}{11} = 0.73 \). (2) Solid: \( W_S = \dfrac{35 - 32}{11} = 0.27 \). Sum: 1.00. ✓

Answer: 73% liquid, 27% solid. Sanity check: the first solid (43% Ni) is markedly richer than the average (35%) — the rejection of 3 in numbers. Freeze fast and that difference is buried as coring in every dendrite; the lever rule is segregation's arithmetic before it becomes homogenization's problem.

Problem 3 of 8 · solve, then check

Sizing the freeze with Chvorinov

Problem. A plate casting has volume-to-area modulus \( V/A = 0.02\ \mathrm{m} \), and the mold's Chvorinov constant is \( B = 2.5\times10^{6}\ \mathrm{s/m^2} \). Find: the solidification time, and the modulus a riser needs to freeze after the casting.

Solution. (1) \( t = B\,(V/A)^2 = 2.5\times10^{6} \times (0.02)^2 = 2.5\times10^{6} \times 4\times10^{-4} = 1000\ \mathrm{s} \approx 17\ \mathrm{min} \). (2) A riser must freeze later: its modulus must exceed the casting's — practice sizes risers at \( (V/A)_{riser} \approx 1.2 \times (V/A)_{casting} = 0.024\ \mathrm{m} \), giving \( t \approx 1440\ \mathrm{s} \), a 44% margin.

Answer: ≈1000 s (17 min); riser modulus ≥ ~0.024 m. Sanity check: the square law means the 20% modulus margin becomes a 44% time margin \( (1.2^2 = 1.44) \) — geometry's gift to the riser designer, and the reason modulus, not volume, is the quantity risering tables tabulate.

Problem 4 of 8 · multiple choice

Increasing the undercooling \( \Delta T \) of a melt makes the critical nucleus radius \( r^* \):

\( r^* = 2\gamma T_m/(\Delta H_f\, \Delta T) \) — undercooling sits in the denominator. Colder melts accept smaller nuclei, so nucleation gets easier and grains finer. Inoculants exploit the same idea from the other side, by making heterogeneous sites abundant.

Problem 5 of 8 · multiple choice

From mold wall to center, a casting's grain zones run:

The cold wall nucleates a fine chill zone; survivors grow inward as columnar grains along the heat-flow direction; the last liquid nucleates the central equiaxed zone. The proportions are process knobs — superheat, cooling, stirring — and a macro-etch reads them back like a chart recorder.

Problem 6 of 8 · multiple choice

Per Chvorinov's rule, a riser feeds a casting successfully when the riser has:

\( t = B(V/A)^2 \): freezing order follows modulus, not size. A riser with the greater \( V/A \) stays liquid longest and feeds the casting's shrinkage; a big-but-thin riser (large volume, larger area) can freeze first and feed nothing. Modulus is the quantity risering practice tabulates — the rule's square makes even modest modulus margins into comfortable time margins.

Problem 7 of 8 · multiple choice

An alloy at 35 wt% Ni sits between liquid (32) and solid (43) on the Cu–Ni diagram. The liquid fraction is:

Lever rule: \( W_L = (43-35)/(43-32) = 8/11 = 0.73 \). Note the arm you use is the one on the far side of the average — the commonest lever-rule slip is grabbing the near arm, which gives the 27% solid fraction instead.

Problem 8 of 8 · multiple choice

The long, aligned grains found just inside a casting's surface are the:

After the chill zone's fine surface grains, survivors grow inward along the thermal gradient — long, parallel, and directional: the columnar zone. Its aligned boundaries are anisotropic weak paths, which matters when loads cross them. The equiaxed center forms last where cooling is slow and nucleation wins. Reading the three zones in order IS reading the casting's cooling history.

The metallurgist reads it like a chart recorder: superheat a touch high (long columnar zone), stirring earning its keep (healthy equiaxed core), casting speed near the segregation limit.
