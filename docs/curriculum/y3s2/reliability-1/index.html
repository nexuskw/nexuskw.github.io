Reliability Engineering I

Quantified failure: reliability mathematics, Weibull analysis of real data, system modeling, and FMEA/FMECA — the analytical core of the reliability engineer role this pathway targets.

01

Reliability functions

Reliability, CDF, PDF, and hazard rate defined and interrelated with derivations. The bathtub curve as hazard-rate biography. Reading each function's story from the others.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Relate R(t), F(t), f(t), and h(t): which pair determines the rest?
  • What does the bathtub curve's middle actually claim?

The exponential model

Constant hazard rate and its memoryless consequence proven. MTBF's honest meaning under the exponential. Where the model applies — and the electronics-versus-wearout boundary.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Prove memorylessness for the exponential in one line.
  • Why is MTBF alone a misleading purchase criterion?

03

Weibull analysisCore 60 · in production

The Weibull distribution with shape and scale parameters read as physics: β<1 infant mortality, β≈1 random, β>1 wearout. Median-rank plotting of a real failure dataset, censored points handled correctly. The analysis that turns a failure log into a maintenance strategy argument.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A seal population fits Weibull β = 0.7: what regime is that, and is scheduled replacement justified?
  • β = 3.2, η = 18 months: why plan intervention well before the MTBF, and roughly when?
  • Why must suspensions (censored units) enter the fit rather than being discarded?

Taught from — CORE 60 · C2 · NPTEL: Reliability Engineering alternative (enable captions)

04

Fitting and interpreting failure data

Data collection sins: mixed failure modes, missing suspensions, bad timestamps. Goodness of fit with judgment. Confidence bounds that keep conclusions honest.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Name three data sins that corrupt Weibull fits.
  • What do confidence bounds add to a fitted line?

Taught from — NPTEL: Reliability Engineering alternative (enable captions)

05

System reliability modelsCore 60 · in production

Series, parallel, and k-out-of-n structures derived from probability first principles; reliability block diagrams of real equipment trains. Redundancy's diminishing returns and common-cause caveat. A pumping station modeled end to end as the condensed example.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Two pumps in parallel, each R = 0.90, either sufficient: system reliability?
  • Three series components at R = 0.95 each: system reliability?
  • Why does adding redundancy do nothing against common-cause failures?

06

Availability and maintainability analysis

Repairable-system measures; availability from failure and repair distributions. Maintainability as design property — access, modularity, error-proofing. Where MTTR is really spent, measured.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Availability versus reliability: a machine with poor MTBF but great MTTR — what results?
  • Where does maintainability get designed in?

07

FMEA/FMECACore 60 · in production

Failure modes and effects analysis built properly: functions, functional failures, modes, effects, severity–occurrence–detection scoring, and the RPN's known abuses. Criticality analysis as the FMECA extension. One equipment FMEA worked as the condensed example — the template interviews ask you to narrate.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Two failure modes both score RPN 72 — one from severity 9, one from occurrence 9. Why must they be treated differently?
  • For a leaking mechanical seal, give one failure mode, one cause, and one effect — correctly sorted.
  • What does a detection score of 9 actually assert about your monitoring capability?

Taught from — CORE 60 · C4 · NPTEL: Reliability Engineering alternative (enable captions)

After this lesson you can answer:

09

RCM principlesCore 60 · in production

Reliability-centered maintenance's seven questions and its decision logic: condition-based first, time-based where age matters, run-to-failure where consequences permit, redesign where nothing works. Hidden failures and failure-finding intervals. The framework that disciplines every PM program worth the name.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does RCM route hidden failures to failure-finding tasks, and what makes a failure 'hidden'?
  • Under what two conditions does RCM legitimately assign run-to-failure?
  • Explain how a blanket monthly-PM policy over- and under-maintains the same plant simultaneously.
