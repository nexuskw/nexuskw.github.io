INS 352 · YEAR 3 · SEMESTER 2

Instrumentation & Process Control

The control loop as plumbing and paperwork: P&ID literacy, transmitters and control valves, loop tuning in the field, and the safety instrumented systems that sit above regulation — EQUATE-style process-plant territory.

Syllabus

01

The process control loop end to end

Sensor, transmitter, controller, and final element as one signal chain. Loop drawings versus P&IDs. Every element's failure mode inventoried.

Taught from — NPTEL: Process Control

Checkpoint questions
  • Trace a level loop end to end: name every element's failure mode.
  • Loop drawing versus P&ID: what does each give the troubleshooter?
02

P&ID readingCore 60 · in production

ISA-style symbols and tag grammar decoded — the identification letters, loop numbers, and line conventions that make a P&ID legible. Equipment, instruments, and interlocks traced through a real sheet. The drawing a process plant hands you on day one, read without flinching.

Taught from — CORE 60 · G3 · NPTEL: Process Control; Industrial Instrumentation

Checkpoint questions
  • Decode the tag FIC-201: what does each letter say, and what does the number point to?
  • How does a P&ID tell you a control valve's failure position, and why does it matter in a trip?
  • What distinguishes instrument signal lines from process lines on the drawing?
03

Control valves

Globe, ball, and butterfly bodies; inherent versus installed characteristic derived from the pressure-ratio argument. Cv sizing in outline; cavitation and flashing in valves. Positioners and their diagnostics.

Taught from — NPTEL: Process Control

Checkpoint questions
  • Inherent versus installed valve characteristic: what bends the curve?
  • Why does an equal-percentage valve suit most flow loops?
04

Transmitter engineering in the field

Ranging, elevation/suppression, and impulse-line practice consolidated. Smart transmitter diagnostics used properly. Calibration versus validation on live plants.

Taught from — NPTEL: Industrial Instrumentation alternative (enable captions)

Checkpoint questions
  • Elevation versus suppression in DP level: when does each apply?
  • Calibration versus validation on a live plant: what differs?
05

Loop tuning in practice

Process identification from bump tests: gain, time constant, dead time. Lambda and reaction-curve tuning applied to level, flow, and temperature archetypes. Dead time as the real enemy, named.

Taught from — NPTEL: Process Control

Checkpoint questions
  • From a bump test, extract gain, time constant, and dead time.
  • Why is dead time the real enemy of tight control?
06

Multiloop structures

Cascade, ratio, and feedforward — each derived from a disturbance argument. Split-range and override control where hardware demands it. Reading these structures off a P&ID.

Taught from — NPTEL: Process Control

Checkpoint questions
  • When does cascade control earn its complexity?
  • What disturbance does feedforward kill that feedback cannot?
07

DCS and control-room architecture

DCS versus PLC/SCADA delineated honestly. Controllers, I/O, and HMI stations in a process-plant architecture. Alarm management at plant scale — the rationalization discipline.

Taught from — NPTEL: Process Control

Checkpoint questions
  • DCS versus PLC/SCADA: what genuinely differs?
  • What does alarm rationalization remove and preserve?
08

Safety instrumented systems

The protection layers above control; SIS separated from BPCS by design. SIL concepts and proof testing in outline. Trip logic read from cause-and-effect diagrams.

Taught from — NPTEL: Process Control

Checkpoint questions
  • Why must SIS be independent of the BPCS?
  • What does a proof test prove?
09

Analyzers and special measurements

pH, conductivity, and gas analyzers in outline; sample systems as the real failure point. Analyzer maintenance economics. Where analyzers gate product release.

Taught from — NPTEL: Industrial Instrumentation alternative (enable captions)

Checkpoint questions
  • Why do sample systems fail more than analyzers?
  • What does analyzer dead time do to control?
10

Instrument maintenance and reliability

Calibration programs, drift trending, and smart diagnostics as condition monitoring for instruments. Instrument criticality ranking. The instrument technician–reliability engineer interface.

Taught from — NPTEL: Industrial Instrumentation alternative (enable captions)

Checkpoint questions
  • What makes instrument drift a condition-monitoring signal?
  • Rank instrument criticality: which loops earn tight calibration intervals?
11

Loop package project

For one control loop: P&ID extract, instrument datasheets, valve sizing sketch, tuning plan, and test procedure. The document set of a small management-of-change. Instrumentation practiced as engineering, not adjustment.

Taught from — NPTEL: Process Control

Checkpoint questions
  • In the loop package, why does the valve sizing sketch precede tuning?
  • What does the test procedure verify that commissioning often skips?
