MNT 306 · YEAR 3 · SEMESTER 2

Maintenance Engineering Fundamentals

The discipline you came from, rebuilt as engineering: failure behavior over time, the P–F interval, maintenance strategy types, and the vocabulary — availability, MTBF, criticality — used precisely instead of loosely.

Syllabus

01

Why equipment fails

Failure as a process, not an event: load versus capability over time. Failure modes, mechanisms, and causes distinguished rigorously. The language that makes failure discussable.

Taught from — SMRP CMRP framework; NPTEL maintenance courses

Checkpoint questions
  • Failure mode versus mechanism versus cause: sort a seized bearing into all three.
  • Why is 'load exceeded capability' the universal failure sentence?
02

Failure patterns and the P–F curveCore 60 · in production

The six classic failure patterns and the uncomfortable evidence that most failures are not age-related. The P–F curve derived as a concept: potential failure, functional failure, and the inspection window between them. The single most consequential diagram in modern maintenance.

Taught from — CORE 60 · C1 · SMRP CMRP framework; RCM literature as surveyed in NPTEL reliability courses

Checkpoint questions
  • Classic studies found most failure modes are not age-related: what does that do to time-based overhauls?
  • Define P and F on the P–F curve, and state the rule for a legitimate inspection interval between them.
  • A defect is detectable ~3 months before functional failure: what is the longest defensible monitoring interval?
03

Maintenance strategy types

Run-to-failure, time-based, condition-based, and proactive strategies — each legitimate somewhere. Strategy selection by failure pattern and consequence. Why 'more PM' is not a strategy.

Taught from — SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Match each strategy to the failure pattern it suits.
  • Why is run-to-failure a legitimate strategy and not neglect?
04

Reliability vocabulary with numbers

MTBF, MTTR, availability, and utilization defined with equations and abused-examples. Inherent versus operational availability. Calculating them from real work-order data, including the data's lies.

Taught from — SMRP CMRP framework; NPTEL reliability courses

Checkpoint questions
  • Compute availability from MTBF 400 h and MTTR 8 h.
  • Inherent versus operational availability: what does the gap contain?
05

Criticality analysis

Ranking equipment by consequence and likelihood; criticality matrices built honestly. The 10% of assets that deserve 60% of engineering attention. Criticality as the master key to every later prioritization.

Taught from — SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Build a criticality matrix: which axes, and who scores?
  • Why does criticality drive every later prioritization?
06

Preventive maintenance engineering

PM tasks written against failure modes, not habits. Intervals from failure data and P–F logic. The PM review: deleting tasks as a legitimate outcome.

Taught from — SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Write a PM task against a specific failure mode — what makes it effective?
  • Why is deleting PMs sometimes the right outcome of a review?
07

Lubrication fundamentals

Lubrication regimes from journal-bearing theory; grease versus oil decisions. Selection, storage, and contamination control. Lubrication as the cheapest reliability program in the plant.

Taught from — NPTEL tribology-related content; SMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Which lubrication regime protects at startup, and why is startup the danger?
  • Grease versus oil: name two deciding factors.
08

Precision maintenance practices

Alignment, balancing, fastener torque, and installation standards as failure-prevention engineering. The craftsmanship–reliability link quantified where possible. Why 'fitted and ran' is not a completion standard.

Taught from — SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does precision alignment extend seal and bearing life?
  • What does 'fitted and ran' fail to verify?
09

Operator care and autonomous maintenance

CLAIR-style operator tasks: clean, lubricate, adjust, inspect, report. The operations–maintenance interface designed, not fought over. TPM origins acknowledged without the cult.

Taught from — SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Which operator tasks genuinely prevent failures?
  • Why does the operations–maintenance interface need design, not goodwill?
10

Maintenance economics

Cost of downtime versus cost of maintenance; direct, indirect, and consequence costs. The optimum that moves with criticality. Making a maintenance budget argument management can audit.

Taught from — SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Which downtime costs never appear on the maintenance budget?
  • Why does the maintenance optimum move with criticality?
11

The maintenance function in the organization

Roles: technician, planner, supervisor, reliability engineer — who owns what. Centralized versus area maintenance. Where the transitioning maintenance engineer's experience already maps onto this course's language.

Taught from — SMRP CMRP framework

Checkpoint questions
  • Map your current maintenance experience onto the roles in this lesson.
  • Which role owns strategy, and which owns this week's schedule?
  • NPTEL: Maintenance and reliability-related engineering courses (nptel.ac.in)
  • Practitioner curriculum structured against the SMRP CMRP body-of-knowledge domains (framework, not a textbook)
