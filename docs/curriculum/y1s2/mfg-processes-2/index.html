MFG 154Year 1 · Semester 2

Manufacturing Processes II — Machining & Joining

Chips and joints: machining theory and its operations, grinding, and the welding processes — the removal and joining half of the process world, with Kirby's fabrication lines as the standing example.

12 lessons · 4 in the Core 60 · Primary texts:

  • Groover, Fundamentals of Modern Manufacturing (Wiley) — machining & joining chapters
  • Kalpakjian & Schmid, Manufacturing Engineering and Technology (Pearson)
  • Black & Kohser, DeGarmo's Materials and Processes in Manufacturing (Wiley)
01

Theory of metal machiningCore 60 · guide queued

Orthogonal cutting model: shear plane, chip formation, cutting forces, and the Merchant relation. Cutting temperature and where tool wear comes from. Taylor tool-life equation with its exponents read as economics.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Cutting at 200 m/min gives 30 min of tool life; with Taylor exponent n = 0.25, evaluate the constant C = vTⁿ.
  • Where does most of the cutting energy end up, and why does that cap cutting speed?
  • If tool–chip friction falls, what does the Merchant relation predict for the shear angle and cutting force?

Taught from — CORE 60 · A9 · Groover, Ch. 21

02

Cutting tools and fluids

Tool materials from HSS to coated carbides and ceramics; wear modes — flank, crater, notch. Insert geometry and grades read from a catalog. Cutting fluids and their delivery, with health notes.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Flank wear versus crater wear: where does each live and what drives it?
  • What do coating layers on a carbide insert actually do?

Taught from — Groover, cutting-tool chapter; Kalpakjian & Schmid

03

Turning, milling, and drillingCore 60 · guide queued

The core operations with their speeds-and-feeds arithmetic: material removal rate, machining time, and power. Workholding logic. One condensed worked example runs a job from print to cycle estimate.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Compute MRR for turning at v = 150 m/min, f = 0.3 mm/rev, depth 3 mm.
  • How long is one 300 mm turning pass at 0.3 mm/rev and 800 rpm?
  • Why does chip evacuation dominate deep-hole drilling in a way milling never sees?

Taught from — CORE 60 · A10 · Groover, Ch. 22

04

Machine tools and CNC basics

Lathe and machining-center anatomy; axes and coordinate systems. What G-code programs actually command. Accuracy, repeatability, and backlash as machine-health quantities a maintenance engineer owns.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What do accuracy, repeatability, and backlash each mean for a CNC axis?
  • Why does climb versus conventional milling change finish and force?

Taught from — Groover, CNC sections; Kalpakjian & Schmid

05

Grinding and abrasive processesCore 60 · guide queued

Abrasive mechanics: grain depth of cut, specific energy, and why grinding burns parts when abused. Wheel designation decoded. Surface and cylindrical grinding with dressing and balancing practice.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why can grinding burn or crack a surface that turning would leave unharmed?
  • In a wheel marked A60KV, what do the A, the 60, and the K each specify?
  • What does dressing restore, and what workpiece symptom says it is overdue?

Taught from — CORE 60 · A11 · Groover, Ch. 25

06

Surface finish and integrity

Ra and Rz defined and measured; finish versus process capability charts. Residual stresses from machining and grinding. Why a ground bearing journal's finish is a reliability parameter.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Ra versus Rz: what does each capture about a surface?
  • Why can grinding leave tensile residual stress, and who cares?

Taught from — Groover, surface chapters; Kalpakjian & Schmid

07

Nontraditional machining

EDM, wire EDM, ECM, laser and abrasive-waterjet cutting. Where conventional cutting cannot go: hardened dies, delicate profiles. Selection by material, feature, and tolerance.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does EDM ignore hardness, and what does it demand instead?
  • Pick a process for a hardened die cavity's sharp internal corner.

Taught from — Groover, nontraditional processes chapter

08

Welding processes I — arc processesCore 60 · guide queued

SMAW, GMAW, FCAW, GTAW, SAW: how each arc works, its consumables, positions, and typical defects. Heat input defined and computed. The process lineup on Kirby's structural fabrication floor.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Compute heat input for 25 V, 200 A at 5 mm/s travel.
  • For structural work outdoors in wind, which arc process displaces GMAW and why?
  • Why is GTAW the default for open root passes despite its deposition rate?

Taught from — CORE 60 · A12 · Groover, welding chapters; Kalpakjian & Schmid

09

Welding processes II — resistance, gas, and solid state

Resistance spot and seam welding; oxyfuel processes; friction and explosion welding in outline. Brazing and soldering distinguished properly from welding. Matching process to joint, material, and volume.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Resistance spot welding: what generates the heat, and where?
  • Brazing versus welding: what melts, and what doesn't?

Taught from — Groover, welding chapters; Black & Kohser

10

Thermal cutting and joint preparation

Oxyfuel, plasma, and laser cutting; kerf and heat-affected edges. Joint types and edge preparation geometry. Fit-up as the quiet variable behind weld quality.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does plasma cutting beat oxyfuel on stainless?
  • What does poor fit-up do to a weld's defect odds?

Taught from — Kalpakjian & Schmid; Black & Kohser

11

Mechanical joining

Bolted joints: preload, torque–tension, and why gasketed flanges leak when torqued wrong. Riveting and adhesive bonding in outline. The maintenance view: joints are made to be remade.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does bolt preload, not bolt strength, decide a joint's fatigue life?
  • What does a gasket actually seal against, mechanically?

Taught from — Groover, assembly chapters; Kalpakjian & Schmid

12

Additive manufacturing first contact

The AM process families in one pass: PBF, DED, FDM, binder jetting. Where AM already earns keep: jigs, obsolete spares, prototypes. Honest limits — surface, properties, qualification.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Where does AM already earn its keep in a maintenance organization?
  • Why do printed load-bearing parts face a qualification problem?

Taught from — Groover, additive manufacturing chapter
