01 · The phases of steel

Steel is iron with up to about \(2\)% carbon, and its behaviour is the story of a few phases. Ferrite (\( \alpha \), body-centred cubic) is soft, ductile, and dissolves almost no carbon (\(0.022\)% maximum). Austenite (\( \gamma \), face-centred cubic) is the high-temperature phase and dissolves far more carbon (up to \(2.14\)%). Cementite (Fe\(_3\)C) is an iron carbide — hard and brittle, \(6.7\)% carbon. The workhorse microstructure is pearlite, a fine lamellar mixture of ferrite and cementite that forms when austenite is cooled slowly. Which phases you get, and in what proportions, is read directly from the iron–carbon diagram.

02 · The eutectoid reaction

The landmark of the steel diagram is the eutectoid point at \(0.76\)% carbon and \(727^\circ\)C. There, on cooling, solid austenite transforms into two solids at once — \( \gamma\to\alpha+\text{Fe}_3\text{C} \) — producing pearlite. A steel of exactly \(0.76\)% carbon (eutectoid steel) becomes fully pearlitic. A hypoeutectoid steel (less carbon) first forms soft proeutectoid ferrite above \(727^\circ\)C, then the remaining austenite turns to pearlite; a hypereutectoid steel (more carbon) first forms cementite. The eutectoid reaction is the hinge on which all plain-carbon steel microstructures turn.

03 · Reading amounts with the lever rule

The lever rule of Lesson 1 quantifies the microstructure. For a \(0.40\)% carbon (hypoeutectoid) steel just below \(727^\circ\)C, using \(C_\alpha=0.022\)% and the eutectoid \(0.76\)%: the proeutectoid ferrite fraction is \( \dfrac{0.76-0.40}{0.76-0.022}\approx0.49 \) and the pearlite fraction \( \dfrac{0.40-0.022}{0.76-0.022}\approx0.51 \). So a medium-carbon steel is roughly half soft ferrite and half pearlite — a balance of ductility and strength. More carbon means more pearlite (and eventually cementite), which is why hardness and strength climb, and ductility falls, with carbon content.

Key landmark — the eutectoid

\[ \gamma\ (0.76\%\ \text{C})\xrightarrow{727^\circ\text{C}}\ \alpha+\text{Fe}_3\text{C}\ (\text{pearlite}). \]

04 · Heat treatment: beyond equilibrium

The diagram assumes slow, equilibrium cooling — but the power of steel comes from breaking that assumption. Cool austenite fast (quench) and carbon has no time to diffuse out; the result is martensite, a hard, brittle, distorted structure that gives steel its strength. Because martensite is too brittle to use as-quenched, it is tempered — reheated moderately — to trade some hardness for toughness. How readily a steel forms martensite at depth is its hardenability, improved by alloying (next lessons). The tools for predicting non-equilibrium structures are the TTT and CCT diagrams, which plot transformation against time; the working method is to pick a composition for the carbon it provides, then a cooling path (anneal, normalize, quench-and-temper) for the structure and properties you need.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Microstructure by the lever rule

Problem. For a \(0.40\)% carbon steel cooled slowly to just below \(727^\circ\)C, estimate the fractions of proeutectoid ferrite and pearlite (use \(C_\alpha=0.022\)%, eutectoid \(0.76\)%).

Solution. Proeutectoid ferrite \( =\dfrac{0.76-0.40}{0.76-0.022}=\dfrac{0.36}{0.738}\approx0.49 \); pearlite \( =\dfrac{0.40-0.022}{0.738}\approx0.51 \). About half ferrite, half pearlite.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Steel designation

Problem. What carbon content does AISI 1040 steel contain, and what structure would slow cooling give it?

Solution. In the AISI 10xx plain-carbon series the last two digits are the carbon in hundredths of a percent, so 1040 is \(0.40\)% C — a hypoeutectoid steel. Slow cooling gives a mix of proeutectoid ferrite and pearlite (about 49%/51%, as above).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Eutectoid steel

Problem. What is special about a \(0.76\)% carbon steel, and what does it become on slow cooling?

Solution. It is the eutectoid composition. On cooling through \(727^\circ\)C its austenite transforms entirely by \( \gamma\to\alpha+\text{Fe}_3\text{C} \), so it becomes \(100\)% pearlite — no proeutectoid ferrite or cementite.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

The iron–carbon diagram and its heat treatments are the core knowledge of a steel mill and any heat-treat shop. Choosing a carbon level sets the achievable hardness; choosing a cooling path — full anneal for softness and machinability, normalize for a uniform structure, quench-and-temper for strength with toughness — sets the final properties. A rebar, a gear, and a spring may start from similar steel and diverge entirely through heat treatment, all predicted by this one diagram.

Getting the treatment wrong is a real failure mode maintenance sees. An over-quenched part left untempered is glass-hard and cracks in service; an under-hardened shaft wears and fatigues early. Reading a failed component's microstructure back against the iron–carbon diagram — was it pearlitic, martensitic, over-tempered? — is standard failure analysis, telling the investigator whether the metal or the process was at fault.
