Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Phase diagrams

01 · Phases, components, and equilibrium

A phase is a region of uniform structure and composition — solid, liquid, or a distinct crystal type; a component is a pure substance making up the alloy. A phase diagram maps, for an alloy of two components, which phases are present and in what amounts at each temperature and overall composition, assuming equilibrium. It is the metallurgist's master chart: from it you read melting behaviour, what forms on cooling, and how heat treatment will change the microstructure. The regions are bounded by the liquidus (above which all is liquid) and the solidus (below which all is solid); between them, solid and liquid coexist.

02 · The tie line and the lever rule

Inside a two-phase region, a point on the diagram tells you three things through a horizontal tie line drawn at that temperature. Its ends, where it meets the phase boundaries, give the compositions of the two phases — the liquid \( C_L \) and the solid \( C_\alpha \). The lever rule then gives the amounts: the fraction of each phase is the length of the opposite lever arm over the whole tie line, \( W_L=\dfrac{C_\alpha-C_0}{C_\alpha-C_L} \) and \( W_\alpha=\dfrac{C_0-C_L}{C_\alpha-C_L} \). For an overall composition \( C_0=40 \)% between \( C_L=30 \)% and \( C_\alpha=50 \)%: \( W_L=\dfrac{50-40}{50-30}=0.5 \), so the alloy is half liquid and half solid by mass. The nearer \( C_0 \) sits to one boundary, the more of that phase there is.

Key tool — the lever rule

\[ W_L=\frac{C_\alpha-C_0}{C_\alpha-C_L},\qquad W_\alpha=\frac{C_0-C_L}{C_\alpha-C_L}\quad(W_L+W_\alpha=1). \]

03 · Binary systems: isomorphous and eutectic

Two patterns cover most binary alloys. An isomorphous system (like copper–nickel) has complete solid solubility — one solid phase across all compositions — and a simple lens-shaped two-phase region between liquidus and solidus. A eutectic system (like lead–tin, or the iron–carbon system of the next lesson) has limited solubility and a special eutectic point: the lowest-melting composition, where liquid transforms directly into two solids at a single temperature, \( L\to\alpha+\beta \). The eutectic reaction gives the fine two-phase microstructures that control the strength of solders, cast alloys, and steels — which is why the eutectic and eutectoid points are the landmarks a metallurgist memorises.

04 · The phase rule and the method

How many variables you may change freely is fixed by the Gibbs phase rule, \( F=C-P+2 \), or at constant pressure \( F=C-P+1 \). For a binary alloy (\(C=2\)) in a single-phase region (\(P=1\)), \( F=2 \): temperature and composition both vary freely. In a two-phase region (\(P=2\)), \( F=1 \): fix the temperature and both phase compositions are pinned by the tie-line ends. The working method for reading any phase diagram: (1) locate the point by temperature and overall composition; (2) identify the phase(s) present; (3) if two phases, draw the tie line for their compositions and apply the lever rule for their amounts. This single procedure unlocks every alloy chart in the course.

Lesson 02 · The iron–carbon system and heat treatment

01 · The phases of steel

Steel is iron with up to about \(2\)% carbon, and its behaviour is the story of a few phases. Ferrite (\( \alpha \), body-centred cubic) is soft, ductile, and dissolves almost no carbon (\(0.022\)% maximum). Austenite (\( \gamma \), face-centred cubic) is the high-temperature phase and dissolves far more carbon (up to \(2.14\)%). Cementite (Fe\(_3\)C) is an iron carbide — hard and brittle, \(6.7\)% carbon. The workhorse microstructure is pearlite, a fine lamellar mixture of ferrite and cementite that forms when austenite is cooled slowly. Which phases you get, and in what proportions, is read directly from the iron–carbon diagram.

02 · The eutectoid reaction

The landmark of the steel diagram is the eutectoid point at \(0.76\)% carbon and \(727^\circ\)C. There, on cooling, solid austenite transforms into two solids at once — \( \gamma\to\alpha+\text{Fe}_3\text{C} \) — producing pearlite. A steel of exactly \(0.76\)% carbon (eutectoid steel) becomes fully pearlitic. A hypoeutectoid steel (less carbon) first forms soft proeutectoid ferrite above \(727^\circ\)C, then the remaining austenite turns to pearlite; a hypereutectoid steel (more carbon) first forms cementite. The eutectoid reaction is the hinge on which all plain-carbon steel microstructures turn.

03 · Reading amounts with the lever rule

The lever rule of Lesson 1 quantifies the microstructure. For a \(0.40\)% carbon (hypoeutectoid) steel just below \(727^\circ\)C, using \(C_\alpha=0.022\)% and the eutectoid \(0.76\)%: the proeutectoid ferrite fraction is \( \dfrac{0.76-0.40}{0.76-0.022}\approx0.49 \) and the pearlite fraction \( \dfrac{0.40-0.022}{0.76-0.022}\approx0.51 \). So a medium-carbon steel is roughly half soft ferrite and half pearlite — a balance of ductility and strength. More carbon means more pearlite (and eventually cementite), which is why hardness and strength climb, and ductility falls, with carbon content.

Key landmark — the eutectoid

\[ \gamma\ (0.76\%\ \text{C})\xrightarrow{727^\circ\text{C}}\ \alpha+\text{Fe}_3\text{C}\ (\text{pearlite}). \]

04 · Heat treatment: beyond equilibrium

The diagram assumes slow, equilibrium cooling — but the power of steel comes from breaking that assumption. Cool austenite fast (quench) and carbon has no time to diffuse out; the result is martensite, a hard, brittle, distorted structure that gives steel its strength. Because martensite is too brittle to use as-quenched, it is tempered — reheated moderately — to trade some hardness for toughness. How readily a steel forms martensite at depth is its hardenability, improved by alloying (next lessons). The tools for predicting non-equilibrium structures are the TTT and CCT diagrams, which plot transformation against time; the working method is to pick a composition for the carbon it provides, then a cooling path (anneal, normalize, quench-and-temper) for the structure and properties you need.

Lesson 03 · Annealing, normalizing, and surface hardening

01 · Softening and homogenizing: anneal, normalize, stress relieve, spheroidize

Four heat treatments share one purpose — leave the steel softer, more uniform, or less stressed than it arrived — and differ only in how far they go. A full anneal austenitizes the steel, then cools it slowly in the furnace itself. The long time spent transforming near 727°C lets carbon diffuse far, so the pearlite that forms is coarse and widely spaced — the softest, most ductile condition the steel can reach, at the cost of hours of furnace time. A normalize austenitizes the same way but cools in still air — faster, cheaper, and stopping the transformation before carbon has time to spread as far, giving fine pearlite: moderately stronger and harder than annealed, and — because austenitizing resets the grain size — more uniform than as-cast or as-forged stock.

02 · Reading the diagram — cooling rate sets lamellar spacing

The working method is the same tool from Lessons 1–2, run twice. Both curves leave the austenitizing temperature together and cross the 727°C eutectoid line — the furnace path far later than the air path. Time spent in the transformation range controls how far carbon can diffuse before the pearlite locks in: slow crossing → carbon travels far → thick, widely-spaced lamellae → soft. Fast crossing → carbon travels little → thin, closely-spaced lamellae → hard. This is the same principle as grain-size strengthening from Materials I: finer internal spacing resists dislocation motion more, so hardness rises as the microstructure gets finer — whether that spacing is grain size or lamellar spacing.

Stress relief and spheroidize stay below 727°C entirely, so neither re-forms austenite. Stress relief (roughly 600–650°C, held then air- or furnace-cooled) exists only to let locked-in residual stress from welding, machining, or cold forming relax — it changes no phase, only removes distortion risk. Spheroidize holds a high-carbon steel just under 727°C long enough that the cementite lamellae ball up into spheroids dispersed in ferrite — the softest, most formable condition a high-carbon steel reaches, used before heavy cold work such as cold heading or wire drawing.

03 · Case hardening: a hard shell, a tough core

A gear tooth or a cam lobe needs a wear-resistant surface and a fracture-resistant core at once — through-hardening the whole part would win the surface and lose the core to brittleness. Three processes solve this by hardening only a shell. Carburizing diffuses carbon into a low-carbon steel's surface at high temperature (roughly 900–950°C) in a carbon-rich atmosphere; the enriched surface then hardens fully on quenching while the low-carbon core stays tough. Because it is diffusion, case depth follows the same square-root-of-time law as any diffusion process: \( x\approx k\sqrt{t} \) — doubling the case depth takes four times the time, not twice.

Nitriding diffuses nitrogen into the surface at a much lower temperature (roughly 500–550°C), forming hard nitride compounds directly, with no quench afterward — the part was already finish-machined, so avoiding a quench avoids the distortion a quench would cause. Induction hardening diffuses nothing at all: a coil heats only a thin surface layer of an already carbon-adequate steel above the transformation temperature in seconds, and that layer alone is quenched — fast, atmosphere-free, well suited to high-volume shafts and gears.

04 · Choosing a treatment — the working method

Given a required property set, the sequence runs: (1) normalize or anneal the as-received bar stock so it machines cleanly and predictably; (2) machine to near-final shape; (3) case-harden the specific wear surfaces — carburizing where a deep case and a soft core both matter, induction hardening where speed and a defined shallow zone matter, nitriding where the part must not distort — leaving stock on the hardened surfaces for finishing, since hardening shifts dimensions slightly; (4) finish-grind the hardened surfaces to final size. A drawing callout that reads "case-harden journals, 0.8–1.0 mm effective case depth" is asking for exactly this sequence, in this order.

Key tool — diffusion case depth

\[ x \approx k\sqrt{t}\qquad\Rightarrow\qquad \frac{t_2}{t_1}=\left(\frac{x_2}{x_1}\right)^2. \]

Lesson 04 · Carbon and alloy steels

01 · Reading the designation

The AISI/SAE four-digit system encodes composition directly. The first two digits name the alloy family — 10xx is plain carbon, 41xx is chromium-molybdenum, 43xx is nickel-chromium-molybdenum, and so on. The last two digits give the carbon content in hundredths of a percent: 4140 is a Cr-Mo steel at \(0.40\)% C; 1020 is plain carbon at \(0.20\)% C. Reading a designation is reading two separate questions at once — which alloying family, and how much carbon — and the two answer different things about the steel entirely.

02 · What carbon buys, and what alloying buys

Carbon sets the ceiling: the maximum hardness any steel of that composition can reach, achieved right at a quenched surface where cooling is fastest. A 1040 and a 4140 — both \(0.40\)% C — quench to essentially the same peak hardness. What differs is how far that hardness extends inward. Alloying elements — manganese, chromium, molybdenum, nickel — slow the austenite-to-pearlite transformation, so a slower-cooling interior still has time to form the hard, non-equilibrium structure instead of soft pearlite. This property — how deep the hardening penetrates, independent of the peak value — is hardenability, and it is what alloying buys.

03 · The Jominy end-quench test

Hardenability is measured directly: a standard round bar is austenitized, then one end is water-quenched while the rest air-cools at a rate that falls off smoothly with distance from that end. Hardness measured along the bar's length — the Jominy curve — is a direct picture of hardenability. A shallow-hardenability steel (plain carbon) drops from peak hardness within a few millimetres of the quenched end; a deep-hardenability steel (alloyed) stays near-peak for tens of millimetres. At the classic reference distance of \(12.5\ \text{mm}\) (\(\tfrac{1}{2}\ \text{in}\)), a 1040-type curve has already fallen to the mid-20s HRC while a 4140-type curve is still in the low-to-mid 40s — the same numbers a heat-treater reads off a real Jominy chart to judge whether a given steel will through-harden a given section size.

04 · Selecting a grade — the working method

Selection runs from the part outward. First, the section size: a thin part cools fast everywhere and a plain-carbon steel may through-harden it fine; a thick shaft's core cools slowly no matter what quenchant is used, and only a deep-hardenability alloy steel will harden there. Second, the service demand: a shaft under bending and torsion needs toughness through its cross-section, which plain-carbon steel — hard skin, soft, weak core — cannot give at any real diameter. Third, cost: alloying elements and the slower, more controlled quenches deep-hardenability grades often need both add cost, so a plain-carbon grade is the right answer whenever section size and service load allow it. A 4140 shaft and a 1040 shaft can leave the quench at the identical surface hardness; only the Jominy curve — and the part's actual thickness — says which one is lying about its core.

Key distinction

Carbon content sets peak hardness. Alloy content sets how far that hardness reaches — hardenability, read directly off the Jominy curve.

Lesson 05 · Stainless steels and cast irons

01 · The stainless families

Any steel with roughly \(10.5\)–\(12\)% chromium or more forms a thin, adherent, self-healing chromium-oxide film — passivity — and stops rusting the way plain carbon steel does. Above that composition floor, four structural families cover almost every application. Austenitic stainless (the 300 series: Cr + Ni, FCC, non-magnetic) gives the best combination of corrosion resistance, ductility, and weldability, but cannot be hardened by heat treatment — only by cold work. Ferritic stainless (Cr only, BCC, magnetic) is cheaper and adequate where corrosion demands are moderate. Martensitic stainless carries enough carbon to quench-harden exactly like a plain carbon steel — traded corrosion resistance for the ability to reach real hardness, used for cutlery, shafts, and valve trim. Duplex stainless is a roughly 50/50 austenite–ferrite structure, combining ferritic-level strength with corrosion resistance that rivals or beats austenitic grades in chloride-rich environments.

02 · Sensitization — reading the diagram

Welding heats the metal beside the weld — the heat-affected zone — through the sensitizing range, roughly \(450\)–\(850^\circ\)C, for long enough that chromium carbides (\(\text{Cr}_{23}\text{C}_6\)) precipitate directly onto grain boundaries. Forming those carbides pulls chromium out of the matrix immediately next to the boundary — not from the bulk, which stays fully alloyed, but from a narrow band exactly where the carbides sit. If that local concentration drops below the passivity threshold, the boundary loses its protective film while the surrounding grains keep theirs, and corrosion attacks precisely along the grain boundaries — intergranular corrosion, sometimes called weld decay. The working method: find the heat-affected zone, confirm it passed through the sensitizing range, and treat every grain boundary there as the weak point, not the plate as a whole.

Three fixes address it directly: low-carbon "L" grades (304L, 316L, \(C<0.03\)%) simply have too little carbon to form much \(\text{Cr}_{23}\text{C}_6\); stabilized grades (321, 347) add titanium or niobium, which forms TiC or NbC preferentially and leaves the chromium in solution; and a post-weld solution anneal (reheat above \(\sim\!1050^\circ\)C, then quench) redissolves any carbides that did form and re-homogenizes the chromium before the part enters service.

03 · Cast irons — one base metal, three graphite shapes

Cast iron is iron with \(2\)–\(4\)% carbon, and its mechanical character comes almost entirely from what shape the excess carbon takes as graphite. Gray iron lets graphite grow as sharp, interconnected flakes; each flake is a built-in crack, so gray iron is brittle with near-zero elongation — but it is cheap, castable, an excellent vibration damper, and strong in compression, which is exactly what a machine base or engine block needs. Ductile (nodular) iron adds a trace of magnesium or cerium before pouring, which forces the graphite into spheroids instead of flakes; spheroids concentrate far less stress, so ductile iron reaches real tensile strength and real elongation (typically \(10\)–\(18\)%) — strong enough for crankshafts, gears, and pressure-piping fittings. White iron is cooled fast enough (or alloyed) that the carbon stays locked as cementite and never forms graphite at all — extremely hard, extremely brittle, used only where pure abrasive wear resistance matters and impact loading does not, such as mill liners and crusher jaws.

04 · Selecting a family — the working method

For stainless: default to austenitic unless a specific constraint rules it out — need to heat-treat-harden the part, go martensitic; need maximum strength with strong chloride resistance, go duplex; need to hold cost down where corrosion demands are mild, ferritic is enough. For cast iron: a non-critical, compressively-loaded, vibration-damped part is gray iron's job; anything that must also survive tension or shock — a crankshaft, a gear, a pipe fitting — needs ductile iron instead, even though it costs more than gray; a purely abrasive, low-impact wear surface is the one place white iron's brittleness is an acceptable price for its hardness. Both decisions run the same way: name the dominant load the part actually sees, then pick the family built for that load, not the cheapest one that merely looks similar on a datasheet.

Key mechanism — sensitization

Welding drives the HAZ through 450–850°C long enough for Cr₂₃C₆ to precipitate at grain boundaries, pulling local Cr below the ~12% passivity floor right at the boundary — while the bulk alloy is untouched.

Lesson 06 · Nonferrous alloys

01 · Aluminum and its temper system

Wrought aluminum alloys are named by a four-digit code whose first digit gives the principal alloying element — 2xxx is Al-Cu, 5xxx is Al-Mg, 6xxx is Al-Mg-Si, 7xxx is Al-Zn — the same logic as a steel designation's family digits, applied to a different metal. A temper suffix then says what was done to it: F is as-fabricated, O is fully annealed (softest), H covers strain-hardened tempers used on alloys that cannot be heat-treated to strengthen, and T covers thermally treated tempers. Of the T-series, T4 means solution heat treated and naturally aged at room temperature over days to weeks; T6 means solution heat treated and artificially aged at a moderate elevated temperature to reach peak strength in hours.

02 · Reading the age-hardening curve

Solution treatment heats an Al-Cu alloy hot enough to dissolve its copper fully into solid solution, then quenches fast enough to trap that copper in a supersaturated, still-soft state. Aging — holding at a moderate temperature — lets that trapped copper begin precipitating as extremely fine particles distributed through the matrix; those fine particles block dislocation motion far more effectively than the same copper sitting dissolved in solution, so strength climbs as aging proceeds. Underaged material simply hasn't aged long enough — precipitates are still too small and sparse. At the peak (T6), precipitate size and spacing are exactly tuned to obstruct dislocations most effectively. Held longer still, the precipitates keep growing — overaging — becoming fewer, larger, and more widely spaced, which dislocations can bypass more easily again, so strength falls even though the alloy spent more time at temperature, not less.

03 · Copper and nickel alloys

Copper's defining property is conductivity, and its central engineering trade-off follows directly: anything that strengthens copper — alloying or cold work — also degrades that conductivity. Conductor-grade copper is drawn through a series of dies to final wire size, and each pass work-hardens it, piling up dislocations and using up the ductility needed for the next pass; drawn far enough without relief, it cracks instead of continuing to draw down. An interpass anneal — a recrystallization heat treatment — restores ductility between passes so drawing can continue. Brass (Cu-Zn) is cheaper and more machinable; bronze (Cu-Sn or Cu-Al) is tougher and more wear- and corrosion-resistant; both are prized in hazardous atmospheres for being non-sparking. Nickel alloys hold their strength at temperatures where steel and aluminum have both lost most of theirs, and resist corrosion in chemical environments that attack stainless steel outright — earning their considerable cost premium only where nothing cheaper survives.

04 · Choosing a nonferrous family — the working method

Where weight and moderate strength matter most, aluminum wins — then the temper is chosen for the job: T6 for a structural part, O or an H-temper for sheet that still needs to be formed afterward. Where electrical or thermal conductivity is the actual requirement, copper is the answer, and the specification states plainly whether annealed (soft, most conductive, most formable) or hard-drawn (stronger, still adequately conductive) is needed. Where the service temperature or the chemical environment would defeat steel or stainless outright, a nickel alloy is the only real option, and its cost is simply the price of the part surviving at all. Each family earns its premium over plain steel by solving one specific problem steel cannot — the working method is naming that problem before naming the alloy.

Key curve — age hardening

Strength rises as fine, coherent precipitates form and block dislocations (underaged → peak, T6) — then FALLS as those same precipitates coarsen (overaged). More time at temperature is not always better.

Lesson 07 · Fatigue, fracture, and creep

01 · Fatigue and the S–N curve

Fatigue is failure under repeated, cyclic stress — often at amplitudes far below the material's static strength — and it is the dominant failure mode in any rotating or vibrating part: shafts, bearings, gears, piping under pulsation. The S–N curve plots stress amplitude against cycles to failure on a log scale and is the fundamental fatigue chart. For steel, the curve bends and flattens near \(10^6\)–\(10^7\) cycles into an endurance limit \(S_e\): stay below it and the steel can, in principle, survive indefinitely. A useful first-cut estimate is \( S_e\approx 0.5\times\text{UTS} \) — for a \(800\ \text{MPa}\) UTS steel, roughly \(400\ \text{MPa}\) — but this is a starting point only, always reduced by knockdown factors for surface finish, size, notches, temperature, and required reliability before it is trusted as a design number.

02 · Fracture mechanics — the simplest honest version

Every real part contains flaws — inclusions, porosity, machining marks, corrosion pits — and fracture mechanics asks a direct question: given a flaw of size \(a\) under applied stress \(\sigma\), will it grow catastrophically? The stress-intensity factor \( K=Y\sigma\sqrt{\pi a} \) (with \(Y\), a geometry factor, near \(1\) for simple cases) measures how strongly the crack tip is loaded. Fracture occurs when \(K\) reaches the material's fracture toughness \(K_{IC}\) — a measured, tabulated property. This is why a part can pass a smooth, unflawed tensile test and still fail in service at half that stress: the tensile specimen had no crack to grow, but a flawed in-service part can reach \(K_{IC}\) at a stress far below its own tensile strength. Toughness, not strength alone, sets the real limit once a flaw exists.

03 · Reading a fracture surface, and creep

A fatigue fracture surface carries two classic tells: beach marks (macroscopic concentric arcs, from crack growth pausing and resuming under varying load) and, under magnification, striations — one per load cycle. Neither appears on a ductile overload fracture, which instead shows necking, shear lips, and a fibrous, dimpled surface; a brittle overload fracture is flat, grainy, with little visible deformation. Reading which of these a fracture surface shows is the fastest diagnostic step in any failure investigation.

Creep is slow, time-dependent permanent deformation under sustained stress at elevated temperature — a practical concern once service temperature exceeds roughly \(0.4\) times the absolute melting temperature (the same homologous-temperature idea from Materials I). It runs in three stages: primary (decelerating strain rate), secondary or steady-state (constant, minimum rate — the regime used for life estimates), and tertiary (accelerating strain rate to rupture). Furnace hardware, turbine blades, and high-temperature piping running hot for years are creep-life designs, not simple strength designs.

04 · Reading a failure — the working method

Given a failed part: (1) read the fracture surface first — beach marks or striations mean fatigue; necking and dimples mean ductile overload; a flat, grainy surface with no deformation means brittle overload; heavy deformation with intergranular voids means creep. (2) If fatigue, find the initiation site — almost always a surface flaw or stress concentration, since fatigue cracks start where local stress is highest. (3) Compare the operating stress against the S–N curve (was this a foreseeable number of cycles at this amplitude) and, if a flaw is visible, against \(K_{IC}\) (was the flaw large enough to explain failure at this stress). That sequence is exactly how a failure investigation moves from "it broke" to a defensible reason why.

Key rule — first-cut endurance limit

\[ S_e \approx 0.5\times\text{UTS}\quad\text{(steel; before knockdown factors)}. \]

Lesson 08 · Polymers and elastomers

01 · Thermoplastics versus thermosets

Thermoplastics are chains — linear or branched — held together only by weak secondary (van der Waals, entanglement) forces between neighboring chains. Heat overcomes those weak forces, so a thermoplastic softens and melts, and can be re-melted and reshaped repeatedly: PE, PP, PVC, nylon, and PTFE all work this way. Thermosets instead cure through an irreversible chemical reaction that links chains together with strong covalent bonds into a single connected network. Heating a cured thermoset does not remelt it — there is no weak bond left to overcome — it chars or decomposes at high enough temperature instead. Epoxy, phenolic resin, and cured polyurethane are thermosets; vulcanized rubber behaves the same way, since vulcanization crosslinks its chains exactly as curing does.

02 · The glass transition — reading the diagram

Below the glass transition temperature \(T_g\), an amorphous polymer's chain segments are essentially frozen — too little thermal energy for them to rotate or slide past one another — and the material is glassy: stiff, and often brittle. Above \(T_g\), segments gain enough mobility to move, and modulus falls dramatically, often two to three orders of magnitude over a narrow temperature range. A crosslinked elastomer levels off above \(T_g\) at a low rubbery plateau — the crosslinks stop it from simply flowing away — and that rubbery, resilient state is exactly what makes an elastomer useful as a seal: it can be compressed and spring back. An uncrosslinked thermoplastic above its own \(T_g\) has no such plateau; it keeps softening toward viscous flow instead.

03 · Elastomers for seals — the O-ring, and the Gulf's real risk

An O-ring seals by staying elastically compressed between two mating surfaces, which only works while it remains in its rubbery state. Drop it below \(T_g\) and it goes glassy and stiff, loses its ability to spring back into the groove, and leaks — a cold-start O-ring failure is one of the most famous documented failure modes in engineering history, from a well-known spacecraft accident where an unusually cold morning stiffened the seals past their working range. In Gulf-region service, ambient and process heat sit well above almost any common elastomer's \(T_g\), so cold-embrittlement is rarely the practical risk — except where the equipment itself runs cold regardless of climate: refrigeration lines, cryogenic or LNG-adjacent service, chilled process streams. The diagram's other landmark, at the high-temperature end, is the one that actually bites in sustained Gulf heat: a practical service-temperature ceiling set by thermal and oxidative degradation — chain scission, hardening, cracking — a completely different mechanism from \(T_g\), reached from the opposite direction. A seal compound must also resist the specific fluid it contacts; one rated for water may swell or soften in a hydrocarbon it was never qualified for.

04 · Selecting a polymer or elastomer — the working method

Given a service temperature window and a chemical environment: (1) confirm the low end sits safely above the candidate's \(T_g\), with real margin, not right at the line; (2) confirm the high end stays below its practical thermal/oxidative service limit; (3) confirm chemical compatibility with the actual process fluid, not "elastomer" as a generic category; (4) choose thermoplastic (needs to be formed or re-formed) versus a crosslinked thermoset or elastomer (needs to flex and recover its shape). Skipping any one of these four checks is how the wrong seal compound ends up in service.

Key states

Below \(T_g\): glassy, stiff, brittle. Through \(T_g\): modulus falls 2–3 orders of magnitude. Above \(T_g\) (crosslinked): rubbery plateau — the state a seal must stay in to function.

Lesson 09 · Ceramics and composites

01 · Why ceramics fail statistically

Ceramics are strong in compression but weak and unreliable in tension, and the reason is the same in both cases: strength is controlled by the largest flaw — a micro-crack or pore — present in the stressed volume, not by an intrinsic bulk property the way a ductile metal's yield strength is. Every sample carries a different largest flaw, placed randomly by the manufacturing process, so identical samples tested identically fail at different stresses — strength is a distribution, not a single number. Under tension, a flaw concentrates stress at its tip and, with essentially no ductility available to blunt or redistribute that stress, can grow catastrophically; the same flaw under compression tends to close rather than open, which is why "tension is the enemy" for any ceramic component.

02 · The Weibull distribution — reading the diagram

The probability that a sample fails at or below stress \(\sigma\) follows the Weibull distribution, \( P_f(\sigma)=1-\exp\!\left[-(\sigma/\sigma_0)^m\right] \). \(\sigma_0\), the characteristic strength, is the stress at which about \(63\)% of samples are expected to have failed. \(m\), the Weibull modulus, describes the spread: a high \(m\) gives a narrow, predictable strength distribution (a well-controlled process); a low \(m\) gives a wide, unpredictable one, where some samples fail at far lower stress than others. Reading the curve directly answers why ceramic strength is statistical: at \(\sigma=250\ \text{MPa}\), roughly \(21\)% of identical samples are already expected to have failed; raise the stress by a modest \(40\)% to \(350\ \text{MPa}\) and failure probability jumps to about \(97\)%. That is an extremely narrow, unforgiving safe-operating window compared to a ductile metal — which is why ceramic components carry large safety margins and are often proof-tested: loaded once to a screening stress before service, specifically to break off the weak tail of the distribution before it can fail in the field.

03 · Refractories and fiber composites

Refractories are ceramics engineered specifically to survive sustained high temperature — furnace, kiln, and ladle linings — without melting, softening, or reacting with what they contain. A high melting point alone is not enough: the lining must also have low thermal conductivity (to insulate the steel shell behind it) and chemical compatibility with the slag or metal it contacts, since an incompatible refractory can be chemically attacked and dissolved over time well below its own melting point. Fiber composites pair a strong, stiff fiber (glass, carbon, aramid) that carries the load with a matrix — usually polymer — that holds the fibers in position, transfers load between them, and protects them from the environment. Neither material alone reaches the composite's combination of stiffness, strength, and low weight; industrial hardware built this way includes fiberglass piping and tanks in corrosive service and carbon-fiber components wherever weight is a genuine constraint.

04 · Working with ceramics and composites — the working method

For ceramics: design for compression wherever the geometry allows it, keep tensile stresses low with generous safety factors, avoid sharp corners or point contacts that locally raise tension, and proof-test when reliability truly matters. For composites: identify the direction the load actually travels — a composite is strongly directional, strong along the fiber and comparatively weak across it, unlike an isotropic metal — and orient plies to carry that load; then treat the matrix's temperature and chemical limits, usually lower than the fiber's own, as the real service ceiling for the whole part.

Key tool — Weibull strength distribution

\[ P_f(\sigma)=1-\exp\!\left[-(\sigma/\sigma_0)^m\right]. \]

High \(m\): narrow, predictable strength. Low \(m\): wide, unpredictable strength.

Lesson 10 · Environmental degradation preview

01 · Oxidation and aqueous corrosion

Oxidation is a metal reacting directly with oxygen (or another oxidizing gas) at its surface, forming an oxide layer. Some oxides are protective: aluminum's natural \(\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3\) and chromium's \(\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_3\) (stainless steel's passive film, from Lesson 5) are thin, dense, and tightly adherent, and effectively stop further oxidation once formed. Iron's oxide is the opposite — porous and poorly adherent, so it keeps flaking away, exposing fresh metal to keep oxidizing indefinitely. Whether an oxide protects or not comes down to how well it adheres and how completely it covers the surface, not simply whether one forms.

Aqueous corrosion is fundamentally electrochemical: an anode reaction dissolves metal, releasing electrons; a cathode reaction (commonly oxygen and water forming hydroxide, or hydrogen ions forming hydrogen gas) consumes those electrons elsewhere on the same or a connected surface. The metal itself carries the electron path, the surrounding water or moisture carries the ion path — a corroding part is, electrically, a small spontaneous battery.

02 · Galvanic couples and the area-ratio trap — reading the diagram

Connect two different metals electrically in the same electrolyte and one becomes the anode, the other the cathode — read directly off a galvanic series, a ranked list of relative nobility (exact voltages shift with the electrolyte, but the ranking of common metals is broadly consistent). The less noble metal corrodes faster than it would alone; the more noble metal is actually protected, corroding slower than it would alone — the working principle behind galvanizing (a sacrificial zinc coating protects the steel beneath it) and cathodic protection.

How badly the anode suffers depends heavily on the area ratio between the two metals. A small anode area next to a large cathode area is the dangerous combination: the same total corrosion current concentrates onto the small anode, driving a high local corrosion rate there. A large anode next to a small cathode is comparatively safe — the same current spreads over far more area, so the corrosion rate per unit area stays low. A small stainless-steel fastener in a large steel plate is fine; a small steel fastener in a large stainless plate is exactly the dangerous case — the size of the part, not just which metal is "better," decides the outcome.

03 · Passivity: both shield and trap

Stainless steel's passivity (Lesson 5) depends on a continuous supply of oxygen to maintain and self-heal its protective film. In a crevice — under a gasket, under a deposit, inside a thread — oxygen is starved, the film cannot repair itself there, and that narrow spot becomes a small, localized anode surrounded by the still-passive (and now cathodic) surface around it: the area-ratio trap of 2, this time inflicted by the material's own protective mechanism. This is crevice corrosion, a close cousin of pitting corrosion, and it is often more dangerous than corrosion on a "worse" material like plain steel — plain steel corrodes visibly and roughly uniformly, while passive-film breakdown is invisible until the damage is already deep and localized.

Hydrogen embrittlement is flagged here rather than developed in full: atomic hydrogen — introduced by a corrosion reaction, an electroplating step, or a poorly applied cathodic-protection system — can diffuse into a susceptible high-strength steel and cause sudden, low-stress, brittle cracking with no warning. It is a distinct mechanism from the fatigue and fracture of Lesson 7, and gets full treatment in Year 3's corrosion course.

04 · Screening a corrosion risk — the working method

At this preview depth, three questions cover most real situations: (1) Is more than one metal in electrical contact in a wet or damp environment? Check each metal's position on the galvanic series and, critically, the area ratio — a small anode is the danger sign. (2) Is there a crevice, a stagnant pocket, or a deposit anywhere on a "corrosion-resistant" material? Suspect local passive-film breakdown even where the bulk surface looks fine. (3) Is hydrogen present — pickling, plating, sour service, or cathodic protection — on a high-strength steel? Flag it for specialist review rather than dismissing it. This is a screening checklist, not a full design method; building the design method is Year 3's job.

Key trap — area ratio

A SMALL anode next to a LARGE cathode corrodes fast (concentrated current). A LARGE anode next to a small cathode is comparatively safe (the same current, spread thin).

Lesson 11 · Materials selection in practice

01 · Property indices — screening by ratio, not by one number

Comparing candidate materials on a single property — just stiffness, or just weight — routinely picks the wrong material for a real structural task, because the part's actual performance depends on a combination of properties tied to how it is loaded. A property index combines properties into one ratio matched to the specific failure mode and loading. For a stiff, light beam in bending, free to vary its cross-section, the index that falls out of minimizing mass for a given stiffness is \( E^{1/2}/\rho \) — modulus to the one-half power, over density. Comparing steel (\(E=200\ \text{GPa}\), \(\rho=7850\ \text{kg/m}^3\)) against aluminum (\(E=70\ \text{GPa}\), \(\rho=2700\ \text{kg/m}^3\)): steel's index is about \(57\), aluminum's about \(98\) — aluminum wins by roughly \(70\)%, despite steel's modulus being nearly three times higher, because the index rewards low density far more than it rewards high stiffness for this loading case.

02 · Building the index — the working method

Any index is built the same way: identify what is fixed (a performance requirement — stiffness, or strength, for a given geometry) and what is free (usually the cross-sectional dimensions, which can be scaled up or down), then derive which combination of material properties, raised to which powers, minimizes mass subject to that constraint. The exponent changes with the loading mode: a tie in simple tension gives index \(E/\rho\); a beam in bending with free cross-section gives \(E^{1/2}/\rho\); a panel loaded in bending gives \(E^{1/3}/\rho\). This is exactly why "just compare \(E\)" is the wrong instinct for a real structural screening problem — the correct comparison depends on how the part is loaded and what is allowed to change, not on the raw property alone.

03 · Cost, availability, and substitution

A property index screens candidates on performance alone; a real specification always adds two more first-class constraints. Cost matters most as cost per unit of the property actually needed — cost per unit stiffness delivered, not cost per kilogram in isolation. Availability asks whether the specified grade can actually be procured, in the needed form, quantity, and lead time — a technically ideal material that cannot be delivered on schedule has already failed the specification. When the specified grade is genuinely unobtainable, a substitution decision must prove, not merely assert, that the replacement satisfies every property the original was specified for — not just the one property that happens to look similar at a glance.

04 · Documenting a selection — the working method

A defensible selection record, one an auditor can follow, states in order: (1) the governing constraint and the free variable — what had to stay fixed, what was allowed to vary; (2) the property index derived from that constraint, and why that index fits the loading; (3) the shortlist of candidates screened by that index, with real property values and their sources, not estimates; (4) the cost and availability check on the leading candidate(s); (5) the final selection, with an explicit statement of which requirement each rejected candidate failed to meet. A substitution memo follows the identical shape: the original specification and why each key property mattered, the substitute's documented value for each of those same properties, and an explicit statement of any property where the substitute falls short and why that is — or is not — acceptable for this specific application.

Key indices — minimum mass at fixed stiffness

Tie (tension): maximize \(E/\rho\). Beam (bending, free shape): maximize \(E^{1/2}/\rho\). Panel (bending): maximize \(E^{1/3}/\rho\).
