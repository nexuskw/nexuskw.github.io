MAT 153Year 1 · Semester 2

Materials Science II

The metallurgy that runs a steel-centric region: phase diagrams, the iron–carbon system, heat treatment, alloy and stainless steels, and the failure mechanisms — fatigue, creep, embrittlement — that maintenance exists to outrun.

11 lessons · 2 in the Core 60 · Primary texts:

  • Callister & Rethwisch, Materials Science and Engineering: An Introduction (Wiley)
01

Phase diagrams

Phases, components, and equilibrium. Binary isomorphous and eutectic systems; tie lines and the lever rule worked carefully. Reading a phase diagram as a map of what microstructure is possible.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Read a eutectic diagram: what happens at the eutectic point on cooling?
  • Apply the lever rule: what does each lever arm represent?

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 9

02

The iron–carbon system and heat treatmentCore 60 · guide queued

Ferrite, austenite, cementite, pearlite; the eutectoid reaction. TTT/CCT thinking, quench-and-temper, hardenability, and the microstructures — martensite to tempered martensite — that set steel properties. The single most load-bearing lesson in this course.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • For 0.40 wt% C steel just below the eutectoid, use the lever rule: roughly what fraction is pearlite?
  • Quench austenite past the nose of the TTT curve: what forms, and why is tempering then non-negotiable?
  • Why does the core of a thick quenched shaft end up softer than its surface?

Taught from — CORE 60 · B2 · Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 9–10

03

Annealing, normalizing, and surface hardening

Full anneal, normalize, stress relief, and spheroidize — what each buys and costs. Case hardening: carburizing, nitriding, induction. Choosing a treatment from a drawing callout.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Full anneal versus normalize: what differs in cooling and in outcome?
  • Why does carburizing harden the case but not the core?

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 10–11

04

Carbon and alloy steels

Steel designation systems and what the digits encode. Effects of Mn, Cr, Mo, Ni on hardenability and toughness. Selecting among plain-carbon and low-alloy grades for shafts, gears, and structures.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What does each digit of a 4140-style designation encode?
  • Which alloying elements buy hardenability, and what does that word mean?

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 11

05

Stainless steels and cast irons

Austenitic, ferritic, martensitic, and duplex stainless families; sensitization as the classic trap. Gray, ductile, and white cast irons and their fracture signatures. Materials the Gulf's process plants order by the ton.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does sensitization make welded austenitic stainless vulnerable at grain boundaries?
  • Gray versus ductile iron: what changes in graphite shape and in fracture?

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 11

06

Nonferrous alloys

Aluminum and its temper designations; copper alloys — including the conductor grades drawn at Gulf Cable; nickel alloys for heat and corrosion. Where each family earns its cost premium.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What does a T6 temper tell you happened to the aluminum?
  • Why is copper drawn at Gulf Cable annealed between passes?

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 11

07

Fatigue, fracture, and creepCore 60 · guide queued

S–N behavior and endurance limits; fracture mechanics in its simplest honest form (K, toughness, flaw size); creep regimes and life estimation. The three mechanisms behind most rotating-equipment and high-temperature failures, with fracture-surface reading as the diagnostic skill.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A polished steel has UTS 800 MPa: what first-cut endurance limit does the 0.5·UTS rule suggest, before knockdown factors?
  • Which two fracture-surface features distinguish fatigue from ductile overload?
  • How can a part pass its tensile test yet fail in service at half that stress?

Taught from — CORE 60 · B3 · Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 8

08

Polymers and elastomers

Thermoplastics versus thermosets; glass transition; viscoelasticity. Elastomers for seals — the O-ring as an engineering component. Temperature and chemical limits that kill polymer parts in service.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What is the glass transition, and why does it matter for seals in Gulf heat?
  • Thermoplastic versus thermoset: which can be re-melted and why?

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, polymer chapters

09

Ceramics and composites

Ceramic strength statistics and why tension is the enemy. Refractories — the furnace linings of a melt shop. Fiber composites and where they appear in industrial hardware.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why do ceramics fail statistically rather than at one strength?
  • What property makes refractories survive a melt shop?

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, ceramics & composites chapters

10

Environmental degradation preview

Oxidation and aqueous corrosion in outline; galvanic couples and passivity. Hydrogen embrittlement flagged for later. The trailer for Year 3's full corrosion course.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Set up a galvanic couple: which metal corrodes, and what does area ratio do?
  • Why is passivity both stainless steel's shield and its trap?

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, Ch. 17

11

Materials selection in practice

Property indices and screening; cost and availability as first-class constraints. Substitution decisions when the specified grade is unobtainable — a real procurement scenario. Documenting a selection so an auditor can follow it.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Write a property index for a stiff, light beam — what ratio emerges?
  • A specified grade is unobtainable: what must a substitution memo prove?

Taught from — Callister & Rethwisch, closing chapters
