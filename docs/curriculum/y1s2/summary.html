MTH 151 — Engineering Mathematics II

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Vectors and vector geometry

01 · A vector is a magnitude with a direction

A vector carries two pieces of information at once — how much and which way — and in three dimensions it is written by its components \( \mathbf{a}=(a_1,a_2,a_3) \). Its magnitude is the ordinary distance formula \( |\mathbf{a}|=\sqrt{a_1^2+a_2^2+a_3^2} \); for \( \mathbf{a}=(3,4,0) \) this is \( \sqrt{9+16}=5 \). Dividing a vector by its own length produces a unit vector \( \hat{\mathbf{a}}=\mathbf{a}/|\mathbf{a}| \) that keeps the direction but has length one, so \( \hat{\mathbf{a}}=(0.6,\,0.8,\,0) \) here. Unit vectors are the currency of direction in engineering: a pipe axis, a surface normal, a line of action are all specified by a unit vector, and every projection and angle calculation below is really a statement about them. The whole of 3-D statics — the force balances of the last course — rests on being fluent with components, magnitudes, and unit vectors.

02 · The dot product measures alignment — and gives work

The dot product multiplies two vectors and returns a single number: \( \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3 \), which equals \( |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta \). It measures how much the two vectors point the same way. When they are perpendicular the cosine is zero, so a zero dot product is the algebraic test for a right angle — the single most used fact in the entire subject. Its physical reading is work: a force \( \mathbf{F} \) moving through a displacement \( \mathbf{d} \) does work \( W=\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{d} \), counting only the part of the force along the motion. A force \( \mathbf{F}=(120,90,0) \) N pushing a block through \( \mathbf{d}=(2,0,0) \) m does \( W=120(2)+90(0)=240 \) J; the \(90\)-N cross-component, perpendicular to the motion, contributes nothing.

Key identity — dot product

\[ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3=|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta \]

Reads as alignment. \( \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0 \iff \) the vectors are perpendicular. Physical use: work \( W=\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{d} \).

03 · Angles and projections

Because \( \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta \), the angle between two vectors is recovered directly: \( \cos\theta=\dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|} \). For \( \mathbf{a}=(3,4,0) \) and the \(x\)-axis \( (1,0,0) \), \( \cos\theta=3/5=0.6 \), so \( \theta\approx53.13^\circ \). The related idea is the projection of \( \mathbf{a} \) onto \( \mathbf{b} \), the shadow \( \mathbf{a} \) casts along \( \mathbf{b} \): its length is \( \dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{b}|} \) and the vector itself is \( \operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{b}}\mathbf{a}=\dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{b}}\,\mathbf{b} \). Projection is how a force is resolved along a chosen axis, how a velocity is split into along-track and cross-track parts, and how the "component in a direction" that statics kept asking for is computed honestly rather than by inspection.

04 · The cross product measures area — and gives moment

The cross product of two 3-D vectors returns a third vector, perpendicular to both, with magnitude \( |\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|=|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta \) equal to the area of the parallelogram they span, and direction set by the right-hand rule. Its supreme engineering use is the moment (torque) of a force about a point: \( \mathbf{M}=\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F} \), where \( \mathbf{r} \) is the position from the pivot to the force's line of action. A \(200\)-N force at \( \mathbf{r}=(0.5,0,0) \) m, acting in the \(+y\) direction \( (0,200,0) \), produces \( \mathbf{M}=\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F}=(0,0,100) \) N·m — a \(100\) N·m twist about the \(z\)-axis, turning counter-clockwise. Where the dot product discarded the perpendicular part, the cross product keeps only it: the two products are complementary halves of one geometry.

Key identity — cross product & moment

\[ |\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|=|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta=\text{area spanned};\qquad \mathbf{M}=\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F} \]

Direction ⟂ to both, by the right-hand rule. \( \mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{0} \iff \) the vectors are parallel.

05 · Lines and planes in space

Vectors give the cleanest description of geometry. A line through a point \( \mathbf{r}_0 \) along a direction \( \mathbf{v} \) is the set \( \mathbf{r}=\mathbf{r}_0+t\,\mathbf{v} \) as the parameter \( t \) runs over all reals. A plane is fixed by a point \( \mathbf{r}_0 \) and a normal vector \( \mathbf{n} \) perpendicular to it: every in-plane displacement \( \mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_0 \) satisfies \( \mathbf{n}\cdot(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_0)=0 \), which expands to \( n_1x+n_2y+n_3z=d \). With \( \mathbf{n}=(2,-1,2) \) through \( (1,1,1) \), \( d=\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{r}_0=3 \), giving \( 2x-y+2z=3 \). The perpendicular distance from any point \( \mathbf{q} \) to that plane is \( \dfrac{|\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{q}-d|}{|\mathbf{n}|} \); the origin sits \( |{-}3|/3=1 \) unit away. Clearances, interference checks, and contact analysis in CAD are all this one formula.

Key results — lines & planes

Line: \( \mathbf{r}=\mathbf{r}_0+t\mathbf{v} \). Plane: \( \mathbf{n}\cdot(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_0)=0 \Rightarrow n_1x+n_2y+n_3z=d \).

Point-to-plane distance: \( \dfrac{|\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{q}-d|}{|\mathbf{n}|} \).

06 · The two products, side by side

Keep the division clean and every 3-D problem sorts itself. The dot product is a number that peaks when vectors align and vanishes at right angles — reach for it for work, for components, for testing perpendicularity, for angles. The cross product is a vector that vanishes when vectors align and peaks at right angles — reach for it for moment, for area, for a normal to two directions, for testing parallelism. Almost every quantity in statics, dynamics, and electromagnetism is one of these two contractions of a pair of vectors, and choosing the right one is usually the whole battle. The scalar triple product \( \mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) \) combines both to give the signed volume of the parallelepiped on three vectors — the determinant test for whether three directions are independent, which the linear-algebra lessons ahead build on directly.

07 · Computing the cross product in practice

By hand, the cross product is read off a symbolic determinant with the unit vectors in the top row:

\[ \mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}=\det\begin{pmatrix}\hat{\imath}&\hat{\jmath}&\hat{k}\\a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\end{pmatrix} \]

which expands, component by component, to \( (a_2b_3-a_3b_2,\;a_3b_1-a_1b_3,\;a_1b_2-a_2b_1) \). This is the formula behind every moment in the lesson, and it is worth memorising as a pattern — each component skips its own index and cross-multiplies the other two. The determinant view also explains why \( \mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}=-\,\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{a} \): swapping two rows of a determinant flips its sign, so the cross product is anti-commutative, and the order of \( \mathbf{r} \) and \( \mathbf{F} \) in a moment therefore fixes the sense of the twist. Getting that order right is the difference between a bolt that tightens and one that loosens.

Lesson 02 · Partial derivatives

01 · Rates when there is more than one variable

Most engineering quantities depend on several inputs at once: the deflection of a beam depends on load and length; the efficiency of a cycle depends on two temperatures; the drag on a body depends on speed and area. A function of several variables \( f(x,y) \) has no single derivative, because "the rate of change" now depends on which input you vary. The device that answers the question one input at a time is the partial derivative: \( \partial f/\partial x \) is the ordinary derivative of \( f \) with respect to \( x \) while every other variable is held constant. That holding-constant is the whole idea — it isolates the sensitivity to one input, which is exactly what a designer needs when asking "if I change only the wall thickness, how much does the stress move?"

02 · Computing partials is one-variable calculus, repeated

No new differentiation rules are needed. To get \( \partial f/\partial x \), treat \( y \) as a frozen number and differentiate as usual. For \( f(x,y)=x^2y+3y \): holding \( y \) fixed, \( \partial f/\partial x=2xy \); holding \( x \) fixed, \( \partial f/\partial y=x^2+3 \). Evaluated at the point \( (2,1) \) these are \( f_x=2(2)(1)=4 \) and \( f_y=2^2+3=7 \). The two numbers say the surface climbs at rate \(4\) if you step in the \(x\)-direction and rate \(7\) if you step in the \(y\)-direction from that point. Each partial is a slope of a slice — the curve you get by cutting the surface with a plane that keeps the other variable fixed.

Definition — partial derivative

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h,y)-f(x,y)}{h} \]

Differentiate with respect to one variable, treating all others as constants. It is the sensitivity of \( f \) to that one input.

03 · The gradient: assembling the partials into a direction

Collect the partials into a vector and something powerful appears — the gradient \( \nabla f=(f_x,\,f_y) \). It is not just bookkeeping: the gradient points in the direction of steepest ascent of the surface, and its length \( |\nabla f| \) is the maximum rate of climb. At \( (2,1) \) above, \( \nabla f=(4,7) \) and \( |\nabla f|=\sqrt{4^2+7^2}=\sqrt{65}\approx8.06 \) — so the fastest uphill direction there is along \( (4,7) \), climbing at rate \(8.06\), steeper than either axis-aligned slice alone. Crucially, the gradient is always perpendicular to the level curves (the contours of constant \( f \)), which is why water runs straight down a hillside across the contour lines rather than along them.

Key idea — the gradient

\[ \nabla f=\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right);\qquad |\nabla f|=\text{steepest rate},\quad \nabla f\perp\text{level curves} \]

04 · Second and mixed partials

Partials can be taken again. Differentiating \( f_x \) with respect to \( x \) gives \( f_{xx} \), the curvature along \( x \); differentiating \( f_x \) with respect to \( y \) gives the mixed partial \( f_{xy} \). A remarkable fact — Clairaut's theorem — is that for any smooth function the order does not matter: \( f_{xy}=f_{yx} \). Check it on \( f=x^2y+3y \): from \( f_x=2xy \), \( f_{xy}=2x \); from \( f_y=x^2+3 \), \( f_{yx}=2x \). They agree, \(=4\) at \( x=2 \). This symmetry is not a curiosity — it underlies the exactness conditions for differential equations, the existence of potential functions, and the reciprocity relations of thermodynamics, all of which are Clairaut's theorem wearing different clothes.

05 · Linear approximation and error propagation

The partials give the best flat approximation to a surface near a point: \( \Delta f\approx f_x\,\Delta x+f_y\,\Delta y \). This is the engineer's sensitivity and error-propagation formula in one line. Near \( (2,1) \), a change \( \Delta x=0.1,\ \Delta y=0.05 \) predicts \( \Delta f\approx4(0.1)+7(0.05)=0.75 \); the exact change is \( f(2.1,1.05)-f(2,1)=0.7805 \), so the linear estimate is right to a few percent for a step this size. When \( x \) and \( y \) are measured quantities with uncertainties, this same formula tells you how their errors combine into the uncertainty of \( f \) — the partial derivatives are precisely the weights each input's error carries into the result.

Key formula — sensitivity / error

\[ \Delta f\approx \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\,\Delta x+\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\,\Delta y \]

The partials are the weights that turn input changes (or measurement errors) into the change (or uncertainty) of \( f \).

06 · The working method

Faced with a multivariable function, the disciplined order is: (1) identify the variables and which are held constant for each question; (2) take the partials one variable at a time, reusing ordinary calculus; (3) assemble \( \nabla f \) when a direction or a maximum rate is wanted, and read \( |\nabla f| \) for the steepest slope; (4) for a small-change or uncertainty question, apply the linear formula \( \Delta f\approx f_x\Delta x+f_y\Delta y \) rather than recomputing \( f \) twice. This toolkit — partials, gradient, linearization — is the entire language in which heat transfer writes temperature fields, in which optimization finds minima by setting \( \nabla f=\mathbf{0} \), and in which control theory measures sensitivity. It is multivariable calculus reduced to a small set of moves you will use for the next three years.

07 · The rate in any direction

The two partials give the slopes along the axes, but the slope in an arbitrary direction follows immediately from the gradient: the directional derivative in a unit direction \( \hat{\mathbf{u}} \) is \( D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f=\nabla f\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}} \), a dot product of the gradient with the way you intend to step. This single formula contains everything in the lesson — it is largest when \( \hat{\mathbf{u}} \) aligns with \( \nabla f \) (steepest ascent, value \( |\nabla f| \)), zero when \( \hat{\mathbf{u}} \) is perpendicular to \( \nabla f \) (moving along a level curve, no change), and negative downhill. At \( (2,1) \) with \( \nabla f=(4,7) \), stepping along \( \hat{\mathbf{u}}=(1,0) \) gives \( D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f=4 \), recovering \( f_x \) as the special case it is. The gradient plus one dot product answers the rate-of-change question in every direction at once.

Lesson 03 · Chain rule and total differentials

01 · The total differential

The partials of the last lesson combine into the total differential, the exact statement of how a function changes when all its inputs move together: \( df=f_x\,dx+f_y\,dy \). It is the linear approximation written as an identity between infinitesimals, and it is the workhorse for everything that follows — chain rules, error analysis, and the exactness tests of differential equations all start here. Read it plainly: the change in \( f \) is the sum of each input's change times how strongly \( f \) responds to that input. For a cylinder volume \( V=\pi r^2h \), \( dV=2\pi r h\,dr+\pi r^2\,dh \) — the first term is the volume gained by growing the radius, the second by growing the height.

02 · The chain rule along one variable

When \( x \) and \( y \) are themselves functions of a single driver \( t \), the function \( f(x,y) \) becomes a function of \( t \) alone, and its rate is the sum over both paths in the tree above: \( \dfrac{df}{dt}=f_x\dfrac{dx}{dt}+f_y\dfrac{dy}{dt} \). Each term is a path from \( f \) down to \( t \); you multiply the links along a path and add the paths. Take \( f=xy \) with \( x=t,\ y=t^2 \): then \( f_x=y=t^2,\ f_y=x=t \), and with \( x'=1,\ y'=2t \), \( \dfrac{df}{dt}=t^2(1)+t(2t)=3t^2 \). This matches differentiating \( f=t\cdot t^2=t^3 \) directly, \(=3t^2\) — a built-in check that the path-sum bookkeeping is right.

Key rule — the chain rule

\[ \frac{df}{dt}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt} \]

One term per path from \(f\) to the driver; multiply the links along a path, sum the paths.

03 · Several independent variables

If the intermediate variables depend on more than one driver — \( x=x(s,t),\ y=y(s,t) \) — the same path-sum gives a partial derivative for each driver, holding the others fixed: \( \dfrac{\partial z}{\partial s}=f_x\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial s}+f_y\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial s} \). For \( z=x^2+y^2 \) with \( x=s+t,\ y=s-t \): \( z_s=2x(1)+2y(1)=2(s+t)+2(s-t)=4s \), and likewise \( z_t=4t \). The tree simply grows another level, but the rule is unchanged: enumerate the paths to the driver in question, and add. This is precisely how a change of coordinates — Cartesian to polar, say — propagates derivatives, which heat transfer and fluid mechanics lean on constantly.

04 · Error propagation, the everyday payoff

The total differential is the engineer's error-propagation law. If each input carries an uncertainty, a worst-case bound adds the magnitudes: \( |\Delta f|\le|f_x||\Delta x|+|f_y||\Delta y| \). Dividing the cylinder's differential by \( V \) gives the tidy relative form \( \dfrac{dV}{V}=2\dfrac{dr}{r}+\dfrac{dh}{h} \) — the radius counts double because it is squared. With \( dr/r=0.5\% \) and \( dh/h=0.4\% \), the volume is uncertain by \( 2(0.5)+0.4=1.4\% \). But when the errors are independent random scatter rather than systematic, they combine in quadrature — root-sum-square — because unrelated errors partly cancel: \( \dfrac{\sigma_V}{V}=\sqrt{(2\cdot0.5\%)^2+(0.4\%)^2}\approx1.08\% \), smaller than the worst case. Knowing which combination applies is the difference between an honest tolerance and a padded one.

Key formulas — error propagation

Worst case: \( |\Delta f|\le|f_x||\Delta x|+|f_y||\Delta y| \). Independent random: \( \sigma_f=\sqrt{(f_x\sigma_x)^2+(f_y\sigma_y)^2} \).

05 · The working method

The routine is short. To differentiate a composite, draw the dependency tree, write one product-term per path from the function to the driver, and sum. To propagate uncertainty, take the total differential, divide by \( f \) if a relative error is wanted, and then either add magnitudes (worst case, correlated or unknown errors) or root-sum-square (independent random errors). The recurring engineering lesson is that a squared or cubed variable carries its exponent as a multiplier into the error budget — so the dimension measured least carefully, weighted by its exponent, is usually the one that dominates the tolerance, and the one worth a better instrument.

Lesson 04 · Multiple integrals

01 · Integrating over a region

A single integral accumulates along a line; a double integral \( \iint_R f\,dA \) accumulates over a two-dimensional region \( R \), summing the value of \( f \) on every infinitesimal tile \( dA=dx\,dy \). With \( f=1 \) it returns the plain area of \( R \); with \( f \) a height it returns a volume; with \( f \) a density it returns a mass. The practical key is Fubini's theorem: a double integral is computed as an iterated integral, one variable then the other, and for a well-behaved region the order may be swapped. Over a rectangle the two directions even separate: \( \iint_{[0,2]\times[0,3]}xy\,dA=\Big(\int_0^2 x\,dx\Big)\Big(\int_0^3 y\,dy\Big)=(2)(4.5)=9 \).

02 · Mass and centroid

Give the region a density \( \rho \) and the same integral computes physical properties of a plate or lamina. The mass is \( m=\iint_R \rho\,dA \), and the centroid — the balance point — is the density-weighted average position, \( \bar{x}=\dfrac{1}{m}\iint_R x\,\rho\,dA \), with \( \bar{y} \) similar. For a uniform rectangle \( [0,2]\times[0,3] \) the centroid sits at its geometric centre \( (1,\,1.5) \), as symmetry demands. For a right triangle with legs \( b,h \) the area is \( bh/2 \) and the centroid lands at \( (b/3,\,h/3) \) — one-third of the way from the right-angle along each leg, a result worth memorising because triangular cross-sections are everywhere in structures.

Key formulas — mass & centroid

\[ m=\iint_R \rho\,dA,\qquad \bar{x}=\frac{1}{m}\iint_R x\,\rho\,dA,\qquad \bar{y}=\frac{1}{m}\iint_R y\,\rho\,dA \]

03 · Moment of inertia

Weight the area by the square of distance from an axis and the integral becomes the second moment of area (area moment of inertia) — the geometric quantity that sets a beam's stiffness and a shaft's resistance to twist. About its base, a rectangle of width \( b \) and height \( h \) has \( I=\iint y^2\,dA=\int_0^h\!\int_0^b y^2\,dx\,dy=\dfrac{bh^3}{3} \); for \( b=3,\ h=2 \) this is \( 8 \). About the centroidal axis the value drops to \( \dfrac{bh^3}{12}=2 \). The two are linked by the parallel-axis theorem \( I=I_c+Ad^2 \): here \( I_c+Ad^2=2+6(1)^2=8 \), recovering the base value. The cubic dependence on \( h \) is why depth, far more than width, buys bending stiffness.

Key results — second moment of area

Rectangle: \( I_{\text{base}}=\dfrac{bh^3}{3},\quad I_{\text{centroid}}=\dfrac{bh^3}{12} \). Parallel axis: \( I=I_c+Ad^2 \).

04 · Triple integrals and the method

Adding a third direction gives the triple integral \( \iiint_V f\,dV \) over a solid: with \( f=1 \) it is the volume, with \( f=\rho \) the mass, with \( f=\rho\,r^2 \) the mass moment of inertia of a real three-dimensional part. A box \( [0,1]\times[0,2]\times[0,3] \) has volume \( 1\cdot2\cdot3=6 \). The method for every case is one habit: decide what quantity sits inside the integral (\(1\), a height, a density, or a squared distance), set the limits by sweeping the region in a sensible order, integrate one variable at a time holding the rest fixed, and check the units of the answer. Choosing the integration order to match the region — and, in the next lesson, choosing the right coordinates — is what turns a hard region into an easy calculation.

Lesson 05 · Vector fields, flux, and circulation

01 · Fields: a vector at every point

A vector field \( \mathbf{F}(x,y)=(P,Q) \) assigns a vector to each point of the plane — a picture of flow. The velocity of water in a channel, the force on a charge in space, the heat flux through a wall are all vector fields, and the calculus of this lesson is how you extract physical quantities from them: the work a field does along a path, the flow across a boundary, and the local tendencies to spread or to rotate. Two derived scalars, the divergence and the curl, summarise a field's local behaviour completely, and both are built from the partial derivatives of \( P \) and \( Q \) you already know.

02 · Line integrals: work and circulation

Integrating a field along a curve \( C \) accumulates the component of \( \mathbf{F} \) that lies along the path: \( \displaystyle\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} \). When \( \mathbf{F} \) is a force this is the work done moving along \( C \); when \( C \) is a closed loop it is the circulation, the net "push around the loop." For a constant field \( \mathbf{F}=(3,4) \) along the straight segment from \( (0,0) \) to \( (2,0) \), only the \(x\)-component acts and \( W=3(2)=6 \). For \( \mathbf{F}=(x,y) \) along the diagonal from \( (0,0) \) to \( (1,1) \), parametrise \( \mathbf{r}=(t,t) \): then \( \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=2t\,dt \) and \( \int_0^1 2t\,dt=1 \). The line integral is just the dot product of Lesson 1, summed continuously along a path.

03 · Flux: flow across a boundary

Rotate the question ninety degrees and ask how much field crosses a curve rather than runs along it — the flux \( \displaystyle\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,ds \), with \( \mathbf{n} \) the outward normal. Flux measures throughput: the water leaving a region, the heat escaping a surface, the current through a loop. Where circulation uses the component along the boundary, flux uses the component through it — the same field, read with the tangential versus the normal direction. These two integrals, circulation and flux, are the complete pair of things you can ask of a field on a boundary, and each connects to a local density in the interior.

04 · Divergence: sources and sinks

The local density behind flux is the divergence: \( \operatorname{div}\mathbf{F}=\dfrac{\partial P}{\partial x}+\dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial y} \), the net outflow per unit area at a point. A positive divergence marks a source (field spreading outward), a negative one a sink. For \( \mathbf{F}=(x,y) \), \( \operatorname{div}\mathbf{F}=1+1=2 \) everywhere — a uniform source, arrows radiating outward as in the left panel. For the rotation field \( \mathbf{F}=(-y,x) \), \( \operatorname{div}\mathbf{F}=0 \): nothing is created, the flow is incompressible. Zero divergence is exactly the condition that makes a fluid conserve volume, which is why it is the governing equation of incompressible flow.

Key operator — divergence

\[ \operatorname{div}\mathbf{F}=\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} \]

Net outflow per area. Positive ⇒ source, negative ⇒ sink, zero ⇒ incompressible.

05 · Curl: swirl and rotation

The local density behind circulation is the curl: in the plane, \( \operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F}=\dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\dfrac{\partial P}{\partial y} \), the swirl per unit area. For \( \mathbf{F}=(-y,x) \), \( \operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F}=1-(-1)=2 \): a uniform counter-clockwise rotation, the right panel. For the source field \( \mathbf{F}=(x,y) \), \( \operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F}=0 \) — pure spreading with no rotation. A field with zero curl everywhere is called irrotational, and it is precisely the condition for the field to be a gradient \( \nabla f \) of some potential, tying this lesson back to Lesson 2. Divergence and curl are the two independent things a field can do locally: spread, and spin.

Key operator — curl (2-D)

\[ \operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F}=\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \]

Swirl per area. Zero ⇒ irrotational (a gradient field). Nonzero ⇒ local rotation.

06 · Green's theorem and the method

The boundary integrals and the interior densities are linked by Green's theorem: the circulation around a closed curve equals the integrated curl inside it, \( \oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\iint_R \operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F}\,dA \), and likewise total flux equals integrated divergence. This turns a hard boundary integral into an easy area integral, or vice versa. So the rotation field \( (-y,x) \) circulating around the unit circle gives \( \iint 2\,dA=2\pi \). The working method: to read a field, compute \( \operatorname{div} \) (does it source?) and \( \operatorname{curl} \) (does it spin?); to accumulate work or flow, set up the line or flux integral, and invoke Green's theorem whenever swapping a boundary for an interior makes the arithmetic easier. This is the vector calculus that fluid mechanics and electromagnetism are written in.

Lesson 06 · Matrices and linear systems

01 · A system is one matrix equation

A set of linear equations is a single object. The pair \( 2x+y=5,\ x+3y=10 \) is the matrix equation \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \) with

\[ A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}. \]

with unknown vector \( \mathbf{x}=(x,\,y)^\top \) and right-hand side \( \mathbf{b}=(5,\,10)^\top \). The coefficients become the matrix, the unknowns a vector, the right-hand sides another vector. This packaging is what lets a system of two equations and one of two hundred be reasoned about with the same tools — the truss with its joint-equilibrium equations, the circuit with its node equations, the mixing plant with its mass balances all arrive as \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \). The product \( A\mathbf{x} \) dots each row of \(A\) into \(\mathbf{x}\); checking the solution \( (1,3) \) gives \( 2(1)+1(3)=5 \) and \( 1(1)+3(3)=10 \), reproducing \(\mathbf{b}\).

02 · Determinant, inverse, and a unique answer

For a \( 2\times2 \) matrix the determinant \( \det A=ad-bc \) decides solvability in one number: nonzero means exactly one solution exists for every \(\mathbf{b}\); zero means the matrix is singular and the system has either none or infinitely many. Here \( \det A=2(3)-1(1)=5\neq0 \). When it is nonzero the inverse solves the system in closed form as \( \mathbf{x}=A^{-1}\mathbf{b} \) (for a \(2\times2\), \( A^{-1}=\tfrac{1}{ad-bc} \) times the swap-and-negate of \(A\)), so here

\[ A^{-1}=\frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix}3&-1\\-1&2\end{pmatrix}. \]

and multiplying by \( \mathbf{b}=(5,10) \) returns \( (1,3) \). For large systems the inverse is too expensive; the method that scales is elimination.

03 · Gaussian elimination

Gaussian elimination uses three solution-preserving row operations — swap two rows, scale a row, add a multiple of one row to another — to carve the system into an upper-triangular staircase, then reads the unknowns by back-substitution from the bottom. Solve the system

\[ \begin{cases} x+y+z=6\\ 2x+y-z=1\\ x-y+2z=5 \end{cases} \]

Eliminating downward gives \( 7z=21 \Rightarrow z=3 \), then \( -y-3z=-11\Rightarrow y=2 \), then \( x+2+3=6\Rightarrow x=1 \). The solution \( (1,2,3) \) checks in all three equations. This single algorithm is what every computational solver, and every matrix inversion and determinant, is built on.

04 · Singularity is a physical warning

The nonzero leading entries elimination produces are the pivots, and their count is the rank — the number of genuinely independent equations. Three pivots in a \( 3\times3 \) system mean a unique solution; a missing pivot means a row collapsed to \( 0=0 \) (a redundant equation, infinitely many solutions) or \( 0=c \) (a contradiction, none). A matrix whose rows are \( (1,2) \) and \( (2,4) \) has \( \det=1(4)-2(2)=0 \) and rank 1 — the second row is exactly twice the first. In engineering this is not mere algebra: a singular stiffness matrix means a structure that is a mechanism, free to move without resistance; a singular circuit matrix means an under-determined network. Singularity is the mathematics warning you that the physical system is not properly constrained.

Key test — determinant & rank

\( \det A\neq0 \Rightarrow \) unique solution (full rank). \( \det A=0 \Rightarrow \) singular: a mechanism / under-constrained system, no unique answer.

05 · The working method

To solve any linear system: (1) write it as \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \); (2) for a small system compute \( \det A \) — if zero, stop and diagnose the physical under-constraint; (3) reduce by elimination to a triangle, taking the largest available pivot at each step for numerical stability, and back-substitute; (4) count the pivots to confirm the rank and therefore that the answer is unique. Reading a system as one matrix, and reading a zero determinant as a physical alarm rather than an arithmetic dead end, is the habit the eigenvalue lesson and all of later structural and control analysis are built upon.

Lesson 07 · Eigenvalues and eigenvectors

01 · The directions a matrix leaves alone

When a matrix \(A\) acts on a vector it generally changes both its length and its direction. A special few vectors are only scaled — their direction survives untouched. These are the eigenvectors, and the scale factor is the eigenvalue \( \lambda \), defined by \( A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v} \) with \( \mathbf{v}\neq\mathbf{0} \). Along an eigenvector the complicated action of \(A\) collapses to plain multiplication by a number. Finding these directions is finding the natural axes of the transformation — the coordinates in which the matrix does nothing but stretch — and it is why eigenvalues govern the natural frequencies of structures, the stability of systems, and the principal directions of stress.

02 · The characteristic equation

Rearrange \( A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v} \) into \( (A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0} \). A nonzero \( \mathbf{v} \) exists only if \( A-\lambda I \) is singular, i.e. \( \det(A-\lambda I)=0 \) — the characteristic equation. For any \( 2\times2 \) matrix it takes the compact form

\[ \lambda^2-(\operatorname{tr}A)\,\lambda+\det A=0, \]

using the trace (sum of the diagonal) and the determinant. For the matrix with rows \( (4,1) \) and \( (2,3) \), \( \operatorname{tr}A=7 \) and \( \det A=10 \), so \( \lambda^2-7\lambda+10=0 \), giving \( \lambda=2 \) and \( \lambda=5 \). Two checks are built in: the eigenvalues sum to the trace (\(2+5=7\)) and multiply to the determinant (\(2\times5=10\)).

03 · The eigenvectors

Each eigenvalue has its own direction, found by solving \( (A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0} \). For the matrix with rows \( (0,1) \) and \( (-2,-3) \), the characteristic equation is \( \lambda^2+3\lambda+2=0 \), so \( \lambda=-1,-2 \). For \( \lambda=-1 \), the equation \( (A+I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0} \) reduces to \( v_1+v_2=0 \), giving the direction \( \mathbf{v}=(1,-1) \). Check: \( A(1,-1)^\top=(-1,\,1)=-1\cdot(1,-1) \) ✓. Eigenvectors are directions, not unique vectors — any nonzero multiple is equally an eigenvector — because the defining relation scales freely. What matters is the line the eigenvector picks out.

04 · Why engineers compute them

The eigenvalues are the natural rates and frequencies of a system. A coupled system of differential equations \( \mathbf{x}'=A\mathbf{x} \) — the multi-variable successor to a single second-order equation — has solutions built from \( e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v} \), one exponential mode per eigenvalue evolving along its eigenvector. The sign of each eigenvalue's real part then decides stability at a glance: all negative means every mode decays and the system is stable; any positive means a mode grows and it is unstable; imaginary parts set oscillation frequencies. In a structure these same eigenvectors become the mode shapes — the patterns in which a machine or building naturally vibrates — which the Vibrations course reads directly off the matrix.

Key results — the eigenproblem

\( A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v} \); solve \( \det(A-\lambda I)=0 \). For \(2\times2\): \( \lambda^2-(\operatorname{tr}A)\lambda+\det A=0 \). Stability of \( \mathbf{x}'=A\mathbf{x} \): all \( \operatorname{Re}\lambda<0 \).

Lesson 08 · Second-order linear ODEs

01 · From calculus to a quadratic

The homogeneous second-order equation with constant coefficients, \( m x''+c x'+k x=0 \), is the mass–spring–damper — and by direct analogy every RLC circuit and simple oscillator you will meet. It is solved by a guess that converts calculus into algebra: try \( x=e^{rt} \). Then \( x'=re^{rt} \), \( x''=r^2e^{rt} \), and the equation becomes \( (mr^2+cr+k)e^{rt}=0 \). Since the exponential never vanishes, the bracket must — the characteristic equation \( mr^2+cr+k=0 \). A differential problem has become a quadratic, which is why the quadratic formula is the working tool of second-order dynamics.

02 · Three regimes, set by the discriminant

The discriminant \( \Delta=c^2-4mk \) sorts the behaviour completely. If \( \Delta>0 \), two distinct real negative roots give \( x=Ae^{r_1t}+Be^{r_2t} \) — a pure decay, overdamped. If \( \Delta=0 \), a repeated root gives \( x=(A+Bt)e^{rt} \) — the fastest return with no overshoot, critically damped. If \( \Delta<0 \), complex roots \( -\sigma\pm i\omega_d \) give \( x=e^{-\sigma t}(A\cos\omega_d t+B\sin\omega_d t) \) — a decaying oscillation, underdamped. For \( x''+5x'+6x=0 \), \( \Delta=25-24=1>0 \) with roots \( -2,-3 \): overdamped. For \( x''+2x'+5x=0 \), \( \Delta=4-20=-16<0 \) with roots \( -1\pm2i \): underdamped, oscillating at \( \omega_d=2 \) while decaying at rate \(1\).

Key test — the discriminant

\[ \Delta=c^2-4mk:\quad \Delta>0\ \text{overdamped},\ \ \Delta=0\ \text{critical},\ \ \Delta<0\ \text{underdamped}. \]

03 · Natural frequency and damping ratio

Two derived numbers name the system independently of its scale. The natural frequency \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) is the rate it would oscillate undamped; with \( m=1,\ k=100 \), \( \omega_n=10 \) rad/s. The damping ratio \( \zeta=\dfrac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}} \) measures the actual damping against the critical amount \( c_c=2\sqrt{mk} \); here \( c_c=20 \), so \( c=8 \) gives \( \zeta=0.4 \) — underdamped, since \( \zeta<1 \). The three regimes restate cleanly as \( \zeta<1 \) (under), \( \zeta=1 \) (critical), \( \zeta>1 \) (over). This pair \( (\omega_n,\zeta) \) is scale-free, so two systems with the same \( \zeta \) behave alike whatever their mass — which is why datasheets quote them.

Key numbers — \( \omega_n \) and \( \zeta \)

\( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \); \( \zeta=\dfrac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}} \). Regimes: \( \zeta<1 \) under, \( \zeta=1 \) critical, \( \zeta>1 \) over.

04 · The shape to memorise, and the method

Commit the underdamped shape to memory, because it is the response of almost every real machine: an oscillation at \( \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2} \) inside a decaying envelope \( e^{-\zeta\omega_n t} \). The disciplined method for any \( mx''+cx'+kx=0 \) is: (1) compute \( \Delta=c^2-4mk \) first — its sign is the entire qualitative story before a single root is found; (2) read \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) and \( \zeta=c/(2\sqrt{mk}) \) to put numbers on it; (3) fix the constants \(A,B\) from the initial conditions. Sign first, then magnitudes. This is exactly how vibration and control engineers diagnose a system from its coefficients — and the forced version, in the next lesson, adds a particular solution on top of this same skeleton.

Lesson 09 · Forced oscillations and resonance

01 · Forcing and the steady response

Add a driving force to the mass–spring–damper: \( m x''+c x'+k x=F_0\cos\omega t \). After the transient from the last lesson dies away, the system settles into a steady oscillation at the driving frequency \( \omega \) — not its own natural frequency — but with a shifted phase and an amplitude that depends strongly on how \( \omega \) compares with \( \omega_n \). The full solution is transient plus steady state, \( x=x_h+x_p \); the transient sets how it starts, the steady state sets where it ends up. For sustained forcing it is the steady amplitude that matters, and finding it is the whole point of this lesson.

02 · The amplitude curve and resonance

The steady amplitude is

\[ X(\omega)=\frac{F_0}{\sqrt{(k-m\omega^2)^2+(c\omega)^2}}. \]

At low frequency this reduces to the static deflection \( F_0/k \); as \( \omega \) climbs toward \( \omega_n \), the term \( (k-m\omega^2) \) collapses toward zero and the amplitude peaks — this is resonance. With no damping the peak is infinite; with damping it is finite but can be large. For \( m=1,\ k=100,\ c=4,\ F_0=10 \) driven at \( \omega=\omega_n=10 \): \( k-m\omega^2=0 \), so \( X=F_0/(c\omega)=10/40=0.25 \) m — against a static deflection of \( F_0/k=0.1 \) m, a magnification of \( 2.5\times \). Lighter damping gives a taller, sharper peak; that multiplier is why resonance destroys machines.

Key result — resonance

\[ X(\omega)=\frac{F_0}{\sqrt{(k-m\omega^2)^2+(c\omega)^2}},\qquad \text{peak near }\omega=\omega_n,\quad X_{\text{peak}}\approx\frac{F_0}{c\,\omega_n}. \]

03 · Phase, damping, and the peak

The response also lags the force by a phase that swings from near \(0^\circ\) well below resonance, through \(90^\circ\) exactly at \( \omega_n \), toward \(180^\circ\) far above it. Damping controls the whole picture: at resonance the amplitude is \( F_0/(c\omega) \), inversely proportional to \(c\), so more damping means a lower, broader peak, and the sharpness of the peak scales roughly as \( 1/(2\zeta) \). The two defences against resonance follow directly and are the only two that exist: detune — keep \( \omega \) away from \( \omega_n \) — or damp — raise \(c\) to cap the peak. Every vibration specification is a statement about one or both.

04 · Why every vibration spectrum is this curve

A real machine is driven at many frequencies at once — by imbalance at its running speed, by gear-mesh, by blade passing — and its response at each is this same amplitude curve evaluated there. That is why a vibration analyst's spectrum shows tall lines exactly at the frequencies where a forcing coincides with a structural natural frequency: those are resonances being excited. The working method is uniform: read \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) and \( \zeta \) from the system, compute the frequency ratio \( \omega/\omega_n \), and evaluate \( X(\omega) \) to see how near the peak you sit. Sit far from \( \omega_n \), or add damping — the mathematics of this lesson is, in the end, a maintenance instruction.

Lesson 10 · Laplace transforms

01 · What the transform is for

The Laplace transform \( \mathcal{L}\{f\}=F(s)=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-st}f(t)\,dt \) trades a function of time for a function of a new variable \( s \). Its purpose is not the integral but its consequence: differentiation in \( t \) becomes multiplication in \( s \). A differential equation therefore turns into an ordinary algebraic equation in \( F(s) \), which you solve by rearrangement rather than calculus. It is the same strategic move as the \( e^{rt} \) guess of Lesson 8, generalised — and it pays off most when the forcing is awkward: a switch thrown, a step applied, an impulse struck, all handled by the same bookkeeping.

02 · The table and the derivative rule

A handful of transforms, plus two rules, cover most problems: \( \mathcal{L}\{1\}=\tfrac1s \), \( \mathcal{L}\{e^{at}\}=\tfrac{1}{s-a} \), \( \mathcal{L}\{t\}=\tfrac{1}{s^2} \), and \( \mathcal{L}\{\sin\omega t\}=\tfrac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2} \), so \( \mathcal{L}\{\sin4t\}=\tfrac{4}{s^2+16} \). Linearity lets you transform term by term, and the derivative rule is the engine:

\[ \mathcal{L}\{y'\}=sY-y(0),\qquad \mathcal{L}\{y''\}=s^2Y-sy(0)-y'(0). \]

Read what this does: each derivative becomes a power of \( s \), and the initial conditions are folded in automatically at the transform step — no separate constant-fixing stage to forget.

Key rule — transform & solve

Method: transform every term (\( \mathcal{L}\{y'\}=sY-y(0) \)) → solve the algebra for \( Y(s) \) → invert with the table. Differentiation in \(t\) becomes multiplication by \(s\).

03 · Solving an equation by algebra

The method is three steps: transform, solve for \( Y(s) \), invert. Take \( y'+2y=0,\ y(0)=3 \): transforming gives \( sY-3+2Y=0 \), so \( Y=\tfrac{3}{s+2} \), and the table inverts this to \( y=3e^{-2t} \). A forced example \( y'+3y=6,\ y(0)=0 \): transforming gives \( sY+3Y=\tfrac6s \), so \( Y=\tfrac{6}{s(s+3)} \); splitting by partial fractions into \( \tfrac2s-\tfrac{2}{s+3} \) inverts to \( y=2-2e^{-3t} \). The constant \(2\) is the steady state, the \(-2e^{-3t}\) the transient — the whole solution, initial condition included, produced by algebra.

04 · The s-domain and the method

The ratio of output transform to input transform, with zero initial conditions, is the transfer function — the compact description of a system in the \( s \)-domain that control engineering is built on. The location of its poles (the roots of the denominator) is the system's stability: poles with negative real part mean decay, imaginary poles mean oscillation, exactly the eigenvalue reading of Lesson 7 in another language. The working method never changes: (1) transform every term, folding in initial data; (2) solve the algebraic equation for \( Y(s) \); (3) break \( Y(s) \) into table pieces by partial fractions and invert. Master this on the smooth cases here, and the same recipe handles the switched and impulsive inputs that real systems face.

Lesson 11 · Fourier series first contact

01 · Every periodic signal is a sum of sinusoids

A Fourier series expresses any reasonable periodic signal of period \( T \) as a sum of sinusoids at the fundamental frequency \( \omega_0=2\pi/T \) and its integer multiples — the harmonics: \( f(t)=\tfrac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{n\ge1}\big(a_n\cos n\omega_0 t+b_n\sin n\omega_0 t\big) \). The claim is remarkable: a jagged square wave, a ramping sawtooth, the vibration of a gearbox — each is nothing but a stack of pure tones added in the right amounts. The coefficients \( a_n,b_n \) say how much of each harmonic is present, and the set of them is the signal's spectrum — the same object a frequency analyser plots.

02 · Finding the amounts

Each coefficient is extracted by an integral over one period that isolates its harmonic: \( a_n=\tfrac2T\int_0^T f\cos n\omega_0 t\,dt \) and \( b_n=\tfrac2T\int_0^T f\sin n\omega_0 t\,dt \). The mechanism is orthogonality — different harmonics integrate to zero against each other over a period, so each integral picks out exactly one amount and ignores the rest. Symmetry shortcuts the work: an odd signal has only sine terms, an even signal only cosine terms, and a signal with half-wave symmetry has only odd harmonics. Reading the symmetry first tells you which coefficients can be nonzero before any integral is done.

03 · The square wave and its spectrum

An odd square wave of amplitude \(1\) works out to \( f(t)=\dfrac{4}{\pi}\Big(\sin\omega_0 t+\tfrac13\sin3\omega_0 t+\tfrac15\sin5\omega_0 t+\cdots\Big) \) — only odd harmonics, with amplitudes \( b_n=\dfrac{4}{n\pi} \). So \( b_1=\tfrac4\pi\approx1.27 \), \( b_3=\tfrac{4}{3\pi}\approx0.42 \), \( b_5=\tfrac{4}{5\pi}\approx0.25 \): each higher harmonic is weaker, falling as \( 1/n \), which is why a few terms already capture the shape and the corners sharpen as more are added. The diagram shows exactly this convergence, including the small persistent overshoot at the jumps (the Gibbs phenomenon).

Key result — square-wave spectrum

\[ f(t)=\frac{4}{\pi}\sum_{n\ \text{odd}}\frac{1}{n}\sin n\omega_0 t,\qquad b_n=\frac{4}{n\pi}\ (n\ \text{odd}). \]

04 · Why the FFT screen shows lines

A machine running at a steady speed produces a periodic vibration, so its spectrum is a set of discrete lines at the fundamental (the running speed) and its harmonics — precisely a Fourier series read off a screen. A healthy machine shows a clean fundamental; a developing fault adds energy at specific harmonics or sidebands — a cracked gear tooth, a worn bearing, a misalignment each has a signature line. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) is the algorithm that computes these coefficients from measured data in real time. The working method for any hand problem is the same three moves: read the symmetry to see which harmonics survive, integrate over one period for the coefficients, and keep as many harmonics as the required accuracy demands — the mathematics behind every condition-monitoring diagnosis.

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Particle kinematics

01 · Position, velocity, acceleration

Kinematics describes motion without yet asking what causes it. Along a straight line, velocity is the rate of change of position, \( v=dx/dt \), and acceleration the rate of change of velocity, \( a=dv/dt \). When the acceleration is constant these integrate to the three equations of motion that solve most rectilinear problems: \( v=v_0+at \), \( x=x_0+v_0t+\tfrac12at^2 \), and \( v^2=v_0^2+2a(x-x_0) \). A car starting from rest at \( a=3\ \text{m/s}^2 \) for \( 5 \) s reaches \( v=3(5)=15 \) m/s having travelled \( x=\tfrac12(3)(5^2)=37.5 \) m — and the third equation checks it, \( v=\sqrt{2(3)(37.5)}=15 \) m/s. These are the workhorses; the rest of the lesson is what changes when the path bends.

02 · Curved paths: normal and tangential

When a particle follows a curve, velocity stays tangent to the path but acceleration generally does not. The natural split is into a tangential component \( a_t=dv/dt \), which changes the speed, and a normal component \( a_n=\dfrac{v^2}{\rho} \), which changes the direction and always points toward the centre of curvature (here \( \rho \) is the radius of curvature). A car cornering at \( v=20 \) m/s on a curve of radius \( \rho=100 \) m has \( a_n=400/100=4\ \text{m/s}^2 \) inward even at constant speed; if it is also speeding up at \( a_t=2\ \text{m/s}^2 \), the total acceleration is \( a=\sqrt{a_t^2+a_n^2}=\sqrt{20}\approx4.47\ \text{m/s}^2 \). Direction change is itself an acceleration — the fact that makes cornering and rotating machinery demand force.

Key result — normal-tangential acceleration

\[ a_t=\frac{dv}{dt}\ \text{(speed change)},\qquad a_n=\frac{v^2}{\rho}\ \text{(direction change, toward centre)}. \]

03 · Projectile motion

A projectile is just two rectilinear motions at once: constant velocity horizontally, constant acceleration \( -g \) vertically. Launch speed \( v_0 \) at angle \( \theta \) gives horizontal \( v_0\cos\theta \) and vertical \( v_0\sin\theta \). The range on level ground is \( R=\dfrac{v_0^2\sin2\theta}{g} \) and the peak height \( H=\dfrac{v_0^2\sin^2\theta}{2g} \). For \( v_0=20 \) m/s at \( 30^\circ \): \( R=\dfrac{400\sin60^\circ}{9.81}\approx35.3 \) m and \( H=\dfrac{400(0.25)}{2(9.81)}\approx5.10 \) m. The two directions are solved separately and only share the clock — the single most useful idea in projectile problems.

04 · The working method

The disciplined routine is: (1) choose a coordinate description that matches the motion — rectilinear for straight-line, normal-tangential for curved paths, polar for radial problems; (2) write velocity and acceleration in that description; (3) for constant acceleration reach for the three equations of motion, otherwise integrate \( a\to v\to x \). Keep speed change (tangential) and direction change (normal) mentally separate on any curved path, because forgetting the \( v^2/\rho \) term is the commonest error in the subject — and it is exactly the term that, multiplied by mass in the next lesson, becomes the centripetal force a road, a bearing, or a turbine blade must supply.

Lesson 02 · Newton's second law for particles

01 · F = ma, applied through a free-body diagram

Newton's second law, \( \sum\mathbf{F}=m\mathbf{a} \), connects the forces on a particle to its acceleration. The whole discipline of using it is the free-body diagram (FBD): isolate the body, draw every force acting on it — weight, normal contact, friction, applied loads, tension — and nothing else, then sum components along convenient axes. On a curved or inclined path the axes are chosen to match the motion, which is why the normal–tangential split of the last lesson matters here. Get the FBD right and the algebra is routine; get it wrong — a missing or invented force — and no amount of algebra recovers. The FBD is the single most important habit in all of mechanics.

02 · Friction and the incline

On a plane inclined at \( \theta \), the weight \( mg \) resolves into a component \( mg\sin\theta \) driving the block down the slope and a component \( mg\cos\theta \) pressing it into the surface. The surface pushes back with the normal force \( N=mg\cos\theta \), and dry friction opposes sliding up to a limit \( f\le\mu N \). For a \( 10 \) kg block on a \( 30^\circ \) incline: \( N=10(9.81)\cos30^\circ\approx84.96 \) N, driving force \( mg\sin30^\circ=49.05 \) N, and maximum friction \( \mu N=0.2(84.96)\approx16.99 \) N. Since the drive exceeds friction, the block slides, and Newton's law along the slope gives \( a=g(\sin\theta-\mu\cos\theta)=9.81(0.5-0.173)\approx3.21\ \text{m/s}^2 \).

Key relations — incline & friction

\( N=mg\cos\theta \); driving force \( =mg\sin\theta \); friction \( f\le\mu N \). Sliding: \( a=g(\sin\theta-\mu\cos\theta) \).

03 · Curvilinear motion: the centripetal force

The normal acceleration \( a_n=v^2/\rho \) of the last lesson, multiplied by mass, is a real force requirement: turning a particle of mass \(m\) on a curve of radius \(r\) demands a centripetal force \( F=\dfrac{mv^2}{r} \) directed toward the centre. On a flat road that force can only come from friction, \( \mu mg\ge\dfrac{mv^2}{r} \), which caps the cornering speed at \( v_{\max}=\sqrt{\mu g r} \). For \( \mu=0.7,\ r=50 \) m: \( v_{\max}=\sqrt{0.7(9.81)(50)}\approx18.5 \) m/s (about \(67\) km/h). Exceed it and the friction budget is spent, the required centripetal force cannot be supplied, and the vehicle slides straight on — Newton's law making a hard physical prediction.

04 · The working method

For any kinetics problem: (1) draw the free-body diagram — every real force, no more; (2) choose axes that match the motion (along/across a slope, normal/tangential on a curve); (3) write \( \sum F=ma \) in each direction and solve. Two checks guard the work: the normal direction usually has no acceleration (so \( \sum F=0 \) there, fixing \(N\)), and units must close to newtons. This routine handles the block, the cornering car, the elevator, and the whole of the following energy and momentum lessons, which are Newton's law integrated over distance and over time respectively.

Lesson 03 · Work and energy

01 · Work: force acting through distance

The work done by a constant force is \( W=Fd\cos\theta \) — the force times the distance times the cosine of the angle between them, so only the component of force along the motion counts. A force perpendicular to the path (a normal force, the tension in a turning string) does no work at all. This is the dot product of the vector lessons, integrated along a path. Work is measured in joules, and it is the currency in which the whole energy method is denominated: instead of tracking forces and accelerations moment by moment, we track how much work is done and where it goes.

02 · Kinetic energy and the work–energy theorem

A moving particle carries kinetic energy \( KE=\tfrac12mv^2 \), and the work–energy theorem states that the net work done on it equals its change in kinetic energy: \( W_{\text{net}}=\Delta KE \). This is Newton's law integrated over distance, and it is an accounting identity — energy in equals energy stored. A \( 100 \) N force pushing a \( 20 \) kg block from rest over \( 10 \) m (frictionless) does \( W=1000 \) J, so \( \tfrac12(20)v^2=1000 \Rightarrow v=10 \) m/s. The power of the method is that it never asks about the acceleration or the time — only the endpoints — which is exactly what makes it the fast route through problems where forces vary along the way.

Key identity — work–energy

\[ W_{\text{net}}=\Delta KE=\tfrac12mv_2^2-\tfrac12mv_1^2. \]

03 · Potential energy and conservation

Some forces store the work done against them as potential energy, recoverable later: gravity stores \( PE=mgh \), a spring stores \( \tfrac12kx^2 \). When only such conservative forces act, the sum \( KE+PE \) is constant — conservation of energy. A mass dropped from height \( h \) converts all its \( mgh \) into \( \tfrac12mv^2 \), giving \( v=\sqrt{2gh} \); from \( h=5 \) m, \( v=\sqrt{2(9.81)(5)}\approx9.9 \) m/s, independent of the mass. A spring of stiffness \( k=2000 \) N/m compressed \( x=0.1 \) m stores \( \tfrac12(2000)(0.1)^2=10 \) J, enough to launch a \( 0.5 \) kg mass to \( v=\sqrt{2(10)/0.5}\approx6.3 \) m/s. Friction, by contrast, is non-conservative — it drains mechanical energy into heat, and its work is where the "lost" energy goes.

04 · Power and the working method

Power is the rate of doing work, \( P=\dfrac{dW}{dt}=Fv \) — the number that sizes motors and engines. A \( 500 \) N pull at \( 2 \) m/s delivers \( 1000 \) W. The energy method's routine is: (1) identify the states (start and end) and the energies present at each — kinetic, gravitational, spring; (2) account for any work added by applied forces or drained by friction; (3) set energy-in equal to energy-out and solve. Choose energy over \( F=ma \) whenever the question links speed to position and the time in between does not matter — falling bodies, spring launches, roller-coaster speeds — because it collapses a differential equation into one algebraic balance.

Lesson 04 · Impulse, momentum, and impact

01 · Impulse changes momentum

Integrate Newton's law over time instead of distance and you get the impulse–momentum principle: the impulse \( J=\int F\,dt \) of a force equals the change in momentum \( p=mv \) it produces, \( J=\Delta p=m\Delta v \). For a constant force this is simply \( J=F\,\Delta t \). A \( 200 \) N force acting for \( 0.1 \) s delivers \( J=20 \) N·s, which brings a \( 2 \) kg mass from rest to \( \Delta v=J/m=10 \) m/s. This is the natural tool for short, hard interactions — a hammer blow, a kick, a coupling impact — where the force is large, brief, and hard to measure, but its time-integral is exactly what changes the motion.

02 · Conservation of momentum

When no external impulse acts on a system, its total momentum is conserved — the internal forces between colliding bodies are equal and opposite, so they cancel in the sum. For two bodies, \( m_1v_1+m_2v_2=m_1v_1'+m_2v_2' \) through the collision. This holds whether the collision is bouncy or sticky, which is what makes it so powerful: it constrains the after-state without any knowledge of the messy forces during contact. It is the principle behind recoil, rocket thrust, and every impact calculation in the lesson.

Key principles — impulse & momentum

\[ J=\int F\,dt=\Delta p=m\Delta v;\qquad \sum m_iv_i=\sum m_iv_i'\ \text{(no external impulse)}. \]

03 · Impact and the coefficient of restitution

Momentum conservation gives one equation, but a collision of two bodies has two unknown final velocities, so a second relation is needed: the coefficient of restitution \( e=\dfrac{\text{separation speed}}{\text{approach speed}}=\dfrac{v_2'-v_1'}{v_1-v_2} \), a number between \(0\) and \(1\) set by the materials. A perfectly elastic impact has \( e=1 \) and conserves kinetic energy; a perfectly plastic one has \( e=0 \) and the bodies stick. Take a \( 2 \) kg body at \( 3 \) m/s striking a stationary \( 1 \) kg body and sticking (\( e=0 \)): momentum gives \( v'=\dfrac{2(3)}{3}=2 \) m/s. The kinetic energy falls from \( \tfrac12(2)(3^2)=9 \) J to \( \tfrac12(3)(2^2)=6 \) J — \( 3 \) J lost to deformation and heat. Energy is not conserved in impact unless \( e=1 \).

04 · The working method

For any impact: (1) draw the before and after states with velocities and signs on one axis; (2) write conservation of momentum \( \sum m_iv_i=\sum m_iv_i' \); (3) add the restitution relation \( e=(v_2'-v_1')/(v_1-v_2) \) as the second equation, and solve the pair. For a stick (\(e=0\)) the two bodies share a common final velocity and one equation suffices. Never assume kinetic energy is conserved — check \(e\) first, because treating a real, lossy impact as elastic is the classic error. Impulse–momentum over time and work–energy over distance are the two integrals of Newton's law, and together they answer almost every particle problem without solving a differential equation.

Lesson 05 · Systems of particles

01 · The centre of mass moves as one particle

A system of particles has a centre of mass at \( \bar{x}=\dfrac{\sum m_i x_i}{\sum m_i} \), the mass-weighted average position; two masses of \( 2 \) kg at \( x=0 \) and \( 3 \) kg at \( x=5 \) m have \( \bar{x}=\dfrac{2(0)+3(5)}{5}=3 \) m. The central theorem is that this point obeys Newton's law for the whole system as if it were a single particle carrying the total mass: \( \sum\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}}=M\mathbf{a}_{\text{cm}} \). Internal forces — however violent, like the forces inside an explosion — cancel in equal-and-opposite pairs and never move the centre of mass. This is why a spinning, tumbling wrench thrown across a room still has its centre of mass trace a clean parabola.

02 · Momentum of a system

Because internal forces cancel, the total momentum of a system changes only under external impulse, and with none it is conserved: \( \sum m_i\mathbf{v}_i=\text{const} \). This is the principle behind recoil and propulsion. A \( 60 \) kg person, initially still, throwing a \( 2 \) kg ball at \( 10 \) m/s must recoil so the total momentum stays zero: \( 60v=2(10)\Rightarrow v=0.33 \) m/s backward. The same balance, applied to gas thrown out of a nozzle, is how a rocket climbs. Momentum bookkeeping across a whole system needs no knowledge of the internal forces — only what crosses its boundary.

Key principle — systems of particles

\[ \sum\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}}=M\mathbf{a}_{\text{cm}};\qquad \text{no external force}\Rightarrow \sum m_i\mathbf{v}_i=\text{const}. \]

03 · Steady mass flow: the jet force

When mass flows steadily rather than in discrete lumps — a stream of water, a gas jet — the momentum principle takes a flow form. Fluid of density \( \rho \) crossing area \( A \) at speed \( v \) carries a mass flow \( \dot m=\rho A v \) (kg/s). If a plate stops the jet, it destroys that momentum every second, and by the momentum principle must supply a force \( F=\dot m\,v=\rho A v^2 \). A \( 20 \) m/s water jet through a \( A=0.001\ \text{m}^2 \) nozzle has \( \dot m=1000(0.001)(20)=20 \) kg/s and delivers \( F=20(20)=400 \) N to the plate. This single result — force equals mass-flow times velocity change — is the seed of turbomachinery, where the same momentum exchange between fluid and blade produces useful torque.

04 · The working method

For any system: (1) draw a boundary and identify what crosses it — external forces and any mass flow; (2) for the bulk motion apply \( \sum\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}}=M\mathbf{a}_{\text{cm}} \), ignoring all internal forces; (3) for conservation, set total momentum before equal to after; (4) for steady flow, use \( F=\dot m\,\Delta v \) with \( \dot m=\rho Av \). The recurring discipline is to separate internal from external: internal forces rearrange a system but never move its centre of mass or change its total momentum. That distinction is what makes a complicated many-body problem collapse to one equation about the boundary.

Lesson 06 · Rigid-body kinematics

01 · Three kinds of rigid-body motion

A rigid body — one whose points keep fixed distances — moves in three ways: pure translation (every point has the same velocity), fixed-axis rotation (points circle a fixed line), and general plane motion (a combination, like a rolling wheel or a connecting rod). For fixed-axis rotation at angular speed \( \omega \) and angular acceleration \( \alpha \), a point at radius \(r\) has speed \( v=\omega r \), a tangential acceleration \( a_t=\alpha r \), and a normal (centripetal) acceleration \( a_n=\omega^2 r \). A wheel of radius \( 0.3 \) m spinning at \( \omega=10 \) rad/s has rim speed \( v=3 \) m/s and inward acceleration \( a_n=10^2(0.3)=30\ \text{m/s}^2 \). Rotation is just the particle kinematics of Lesson 1 wrapped onto a circle.

02 · Relative velocity and the instant centre

General plane motion is handled by the relative-velocity equation: the velocity of any point \(B\) is the velocity of a reference point \(A\) plus a rotation about \(A\), \( \mathbf{v}_B=\mathbf{v}_A+\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}_{B/A} \). A powerful shortcut is the instant centre of rotation — the one point of the body momentarily at rest, about which the whole body appears to be purely rotating at that instant. For a wheel rolling without slipping, the contact point with the ground is the instant centre: it has zero velocity, the centre moves at \( v=\omega r \), and the top point, being twice as far from the instant centre, moves at \( 2v \). Finding the instant centre often turns a hard velocity problem into a single \( v=\omega d \).

Key relations — rigid-body kinematics

Fixed axis: \( v=\omega r,\ a_t=\alpha r,\ a_n=\omega^2 r \). General motion: \( \mathbf{v}_B=\mathbf{v}_A+\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}_{B/A} \). Rolling: contact point is the instant centre (\(v=0\)).

03 · Gears and linkages

Meshing gears and belts share a contact-point speed, so \( \omega_1 r_1=\omega_2 r_2 \): a smaller gear turns faster in the same ratio as the radii. A pinion of radius \( 0.1 \) m at \( \omega_1=20 \) rad/s driving a gear of radius \( 0.05 \) m makes the gear turn at \( \omega_2=\dfrac{\omega_1 r_1}{r_2}=\dfrac{20(0.1)}{0.05}=40 \) rad/s. Linkages — the connecting rods and rockers of machinery — are solved with the same relative-velocity equation, tracking each joint in turn. This is the kinematic backbone of every gearbox, cam, and mechanism, where the goal is to convert one motion into another at a designed ratio.

04 · The working method

For a rigid-body velocity problem: (1) classify the motion — translation, fixed-axis, or general; (2) for fixed-axis use \( v=\omega r \) directly; (3) for general plane motion either write \( \mathbf{v}_B=\mathbf{v}_A+\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}_{B/A} \) or, faster, locate the instant centre and use \( v=\omega d \) from it; (4) for connected bodies, match the shared point (gear teeth, pin joints, the no-slip contact). The recurring insight is that rotation adds a velocity proportional to distance from the axis or instant centre — which is exactly why the rim of a wheel outruns its hub, and why the tip of a turbine blade sees far higher speeds and stresses than its root.

Lesson 07 · Relative acceleration and Coriolis

01 · Acceleration of a rigid body

Differentiating the relative-velocity equation gives the relative-acceleration equation for a rigid body: the acceleration of a point \(B\) is that of a reference point \(A\) plus two rotational terms, \( \mathbf{a}_B=\mathbf{a}_A+\boldsymbol{\alpha}\times\mathbf{r}_{B/A}-\omega^2\mathbf{r}_{B/A} \). The \( \boldsymbol{\alpha}\times\mathbf{r} \) term is tangential (magnitude \( \alpha r \), from angular acceleration) and the \( -\omega^2\mathbf{r} \) term is normal (magnitude \( \omega^2 r \), always pointing back toward \(A\)). For a point at \( r=0.4 \) m on a link turning at \( \omega=5 \) rad/s, \( \alpha=3\ \text{rad/s}^2 \): the normal part is \( \omega^2 r=10\ \text{m/s}^2 \) and the tangential part \( \alpha r=1.2\ \text{m/s}^2 \). This solves the acceleration of linkages joint by joint.

02 · Rotating frames and the Coriolis term

When a particle also moves relative to a rotating body — a bead sliding in a spinning slot, a fluid particle in an impeller — the acceleration seen in the fixed frame gains an extra piece: the Coriolis acceleration \( \mathbf{a}_{\text{Cor}}=2\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}} \), of magnitude \( 2\omega v_{\text{rel}} \) and always perpendicular to the relative velocity. A bead sliding out at \( v_{\text{rel}}=2 \) m/s along an arm rotating at \( \omega=5 \) rad/s experiences \( a_{\text{Cor}}=2(5)(2)=20\ \text{m/s}^2 \) sideways. It is not a "force" but a real acceleration that appears because the frame itself is turning — the term that must be added whenever motion is described relative to rotating machinery.

Key term — Coriolis acceleration

\[ \mathbf{a}_{\text{Cor}}=2\,\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}},\qquad |\mathbf{a}_{\text{Cor}}|=2\omega v_{\text{rel}}\ \perp\ \mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}}. \]

03 · The full rotating-frame acceleration

Put together, a particle moving with velocity \( \mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}} \) and acceleration \( \mathbf{a}_{\text{rel}} \) relative to a body rotating at \( \omega \) has, in the fixed frame, \( \mathbf{a}=\mathbf{a}_{\text{rel}}+2\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}}+\boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r})+\boldsymbol{\alpha}\times\mathbf{r} \) — relative, Coriolis, centripetal, and tangential terms. For the bead above (\( r=0.4 \), \( \omega=5 \), \( v_{\text{rel}}=2 \), \( a_{\text{rel}}=0 \), \( \alpha=0 \)): a centripetal \( \omega^2 r=10\ \text{m/s}^2 \) inward and a Coriolis \( 2\omega v_{\text{rel}}=20\ \text{m/s}^2 \) sideways, combining to \( \sqrt{10^2+20^2}\approx22.4\ \text{m/s}^2 \). Miss the Coriolis term and the answer is wrong by the larger contribution.

04 · The working method

For acceleration analysis: (1) for a plain rigid body, use \( \mathbf{a}_B=\mathbf{a}_A+\boldsymbol{\alpha}\times\mathbf{r}-\omega^2\mathbf{r} \), tracking tangential and normal parts separately; (2) whenever a part slides relative to a rotating member — slider-in-slot mechanisms, Geneva drives, pump and turbine passages — add the Coriolis term \( 2\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}} \); (3) sum the vector components on chosen axes. The one habit that prevents the classic error is to ask, on every rotating-frame problem, "is anything moving relative to the rotating body?" — if yes, Coriolis is present, and it is often the dominant term.

Lesson 08 · Rigid-body kinetics: Newton–Euler

01 · Newton–Euler: force and torque

A rigid body needs two equations of motion: one for its translation and one for its rotation. Newton's law moves the centre of mass, \( \sum\mathbf{F}=m\mathbf{a}_{\text{cm}} \); Euler's law spins the body, \( \sum M=I\alpha \) for rotation about a fixed axis (or about the centre of mass). The new quantity is the mass moment of inertia \( I \), the rotational counterpart of mass — it measures how hard a body is to angularly accelerate, and unlike mass it depends on how the mass is arranged about the axis. Mass far from the axis counts far more, because \( I=\int r^2\,dm \) weights each element by the square of its distance.

02 · Moments of inertia and the parallel-axis theorem

Common shapes have tabulated inertias about their centres: a solid disk or cylinder \( \tfrac12mr^2 \), a thin ring \( mr^2 \), a slender rod \( \tfrac{1}{12}mL^2 \). To shift the reference axis off the centre by a distance \(d\), the parallel-axis theorem adds \( md^2 \): \( I=I_{\text{cm}}+md^2 \). A \( 6 \) kg rod of length \( 1 \) m has \( I_{\text{cm}}=\tfrac{1}{12}(6)(1^2)=0.5\ \text{kg·m}^2 \) about its centre, and about its end \( I=0.5+6(0.5)^2=2\ \text{kg·m}^2 \) — which matches the direct formula \( \tfrac13mL^2 \). Composite bodies are handled by adding the inertias of their parts, each shifted to the common axis.

Key laws — rotational kinetics

\( \sum M=I\alpha \); disk \( I=\tfrac12mr^2 \), rod \( \tfrac{1}{12}mL^2 \). Parallel axis: \( I=I_{\text{cm}}+md^2 \).

03 · Applying ΣM = Iα

The rotational law is used exactly like \( F=ma \): find the net torque, divide by the inertia, get the angular acceleration. A solid disk of \( m=10 \) kg, \( r=0.2 \) m has \( I=\tfrac12(10)(0.2^2)=0.2\ \text{kg·m}^2 \); a torque of \( 5 \) N·m spins it up at \( \alpha=\dfrac{T}{I}=\dfrac{5}{0.2}=25\ \text{rad/s}^2 \). For a body in general plane motion — a wheel that both rolls and accelerates — the two equations \( \sum F=ma_{\text{cm}} \) and \( \sum M=I\alpha \) are solved together, linked by the no-slip condition \( a_{\text{cm}}=\alpha r \). This pair handles rolling cylinders, driven pulleys, and every rotating machine element under load.

04 · The working method

For rigid-body kinetics: (1) draw the free-body diagram, now including where each force acts (its moment arm) — position matters for torque; (2) write \( \sum F=ma_{\text{cm}} \) for translation and \( \sum M=I\alpha \) for rotation, taking moments about the centre of mass or a fixed axis; (3) for a composite body, build \( I \) by adding tabulated parts via the parallel-axis theorem; (4) for rolling, add the constraint \( a_{\text{cm}}=\alpha r \). The essential new idea is that where mass sits, and where a force acts, both matter now — the same force applied further from the axis produces more angular acceleration, which is why levers, gears, and moment arms run all of machine design.

Lesson 09 · Rotational energy and momentum

01 · Rotational kinetic energy

A rotating body stores rotational kinetic energy \( KE=\tfrac12I\omega^2 \) — the exact analogue of \( \tfrac12mv^2 \), with inertia replacing mass and angular speed replacing linear speed. A flywheel with \( I=0.2\ \text{kg·m}^2 \) spinning at \( \omega=100 \) rad/s stores \( \tfrac12(0.2)(100^2)=1000 \) J. Because the energy scales with \( \omega^2 \), a modest increase in speed stores disproportionately more energy, which is why flywheels are run fast rather than made heavy. The work–energy theorem carries straight over: the net work of a torque equals the change in rotational kinetic energy, \( \int M\,d\theta=\Delta(\tfrac12I\omega^2) \).

02 · Angular momentum and angular impulse

The rotational counterpart of momentum is angular momentum \( L=I\omega \), and the counterpart of impulse is angular impulse \( \int M\,dt \), with the principle \( \int M\,dt=\Delta L \). The flywheel above has \( L=0.2(100)=20\ \text{kg·m}^2/\text{s} \); a steady torque of \( 4 \) N·m applied for \( 5 \) s delivers an angular impulse of \( 20 \), changing its angular speed by \( \Delta\omega=\dfrac{20}{0.2}=100 \) rad/s. This is the tool for spin-up and braking over time — sizing a starter torque, or the time for a brake to stop a rotor — just as linear impulse handled straight-line stopping.

Key quantities — rotational energy & momentum

\[ KE=\tfrac12I\omega^2,\qquad L=I\omega,\qquad \int M\,dt=\Delta L. \]

03 · Conservation of angular momentum

When no external torque acts, angular momentum is conserved: \( I_1\omega_1=I_2\omega_2 \). If a spinning body redistributes its mass — a skater pulling in their arms, a turntable — reducing \(I\) forces \( \omega \) to rise to keep the product fixed. Halve the inertia and the spin doubles. Notice the kinetic energy is not conserved here: pulling the masses inward does work against the outward "centrifugal" tendency, and that work shows up as extra rotational energy (\( \tfrac12I\omega^2 \) rises from \(1000\) J to \(2000\) J when \(I\) halves and \( \omega \) doubles). Momentum is conserved; energy is added by the muscles or the mechanism doing the pulling.

04 · Flywheels and the working method

These ideas make the flywheel — a rotor sized to store \( \tfrac12I\omega^2 \) and release it to smooth a fluctuating load. A press or engine that takes energy in bursts draws it from the flywheel between strokes, and the wheel's speed dips and recovers within a tolerance set by \( I \) and \( \omega \). The working method: (1) for a speed–angle question use energy, \( \int M\,d\theta=\Delta(\tfrac12I\omega^2) \); (2) for a speed–time question use angular impulse, \( \int M\,dt=\Delta(I\omega) \); (3) when no external torque acts, conserve \( I\omega \). Choosing the energy or the momentum route is the same decision as in particle dynamics — energy for position, momentum for time.

Lesson 10 · Balancing of rotors

01 · Where unbalance comes from

A rotor is balanced when its mass is distributed symmetrically about the axis of rotation. Any deviation — a heavy spot, a machining tolerance, a bit of trapped dirt — puts mass off-axis, and that offset mass generates an outward centrifugal force \( F=m r\omega^2 \) as it spins, where \(m\) is the offset mass and \(r\) its radius from the axis. The critical feature is the \( \omega^2 \): the force grows with the square of speed, so an unbalance harmless at low speed becomes violent at operating speed. A \( 50 \) g spot at \( r=0.1 \) m produces \( 0.05(0.1)(100^2)=50 \) N at \( 100 \) rad/s, but \( 450 \) N at \( 300 \) rad/s. This rotating force is what shakes the machine and loads its bearings.

02 · Static versus dynamic unbalance

Two distinct faults exist. Static unbalance is a single offset mass — the centre of mass lies off the axis — and it can be found at rest, since the heavy spot rolls to the bottom; correcting it needs one balancing mass in one plane. Dynamic unbalance is subtler: two equal offsets on opposite sides and in different planes along the shaft leave the centre of mass on the axis, so the rotor balances at rest, yet spinning it creates a rotating couple that wobbles the shaft. Dynamic unbalance cannot be detected statically and needs correction in two planes. Real rotors generally have both, which is why balancing is done spinning, on a balancing machine, not by hand.

Key result — unbalance force

\[ F=m\,r\,\omega^2\quad(\text{rotates at 1× shaft speed, grows as }\omega^2). \]

03 · Balancing logic

Balancing cancels the rotating force by adding (or removing) mass so the net \( \sum m_i r_i=0 \) as vectors. For a single static unbalance \( m r \), a correction mass \( m_b \) at radius \( r_b \), placed diametrically opposite, restores balance when \( m_b r_b=m r \) — so a \( 0.05\times0.1 \) unbalance is cured by \( 0.025 \) kg at \( 0.2 \) m. Because the force is \( m r\omega^2 \), only the product \( m r \) (the "unbalance," in g·mm) matters, not the speed — balance it once and it stays balanced at every speed. Dynamic unbalance is corrected the same way but in two planes at once, cancelling both the net force and the net couple.

04 · Why balancing matters, and the method

The rotating unbalance force appears in vibration at exactly \( 1\times \) the running speed — its signature line on the spectrum of the previous course's Fourier lesson. Left uncorrected it fatigues shafts, hammers bearings, loosens fasteners, and radiates noise, with severity climbing as \( \omega^2 \). The method: (1) identify the unbalance as static (one plane) or dynamic (two planes); (2) measure the magnitude and angular position of the heavy spot (a balancing machine reads the \(1\times\) response); (3) add or remove a correcting mass so \( m_b r_b=m r \) opposite the heavy spot, in the required number of planes. Balancing is the first defence against machinery vibration, applied before a rotor ever enters service.

Lesson 11 · Introduction to vibration

01 · The single-degree-of-freedom oscillator

The simplest vibrating system is a mass on a spring with damping — the single-degree-of-freedom (SDOF) oscillator. Newton's law for it is \( m\ddot{x}+c\dot{x}+kx=0 \), exactly the second-order equation of the mathematics course, now with a mechanical meaning: \(m\) the mass, \(k\) the spring stiffness, \(c\) the damping. Its free response is set by two numbers. The natural frequency \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) is the rate it rings undamped; for \( m=2 \) kg, \( k=800 \) N/m, \( \omega_n=\sqrt{400}=20 \) rad/s, or \( f=\omega_n/2\pi\approx3.18 \) Hz. This one system is the model for every structure, machine mount, and rotor-support that can vibrate — the whole of the Vibrations course is built on it.

02 · Damping ratio and the response

The second number is the damping ratio \( \zeta=\dfrac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}} \), the actual damping measured against the critical amount \( c_c=2\sqrt{mk} \). For the system above, \( c_c=2\sqrt{1600}=80 \), so a damper \( c=8 \) gives \( \zeta=0.1 \) — underdamped. An underdamped system oscillates inside a decaying envelope: it rings at the damped frequency \( \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\approx19.9 \) rad/s while its amplitude decays as \( e^{-\zeta\omega_n t} \). Small damping barely shifts the frequency but steadily bleeds the amplitude, which is exactly the ringing-and-fading response measured when a real structure is struck.

Key numbers — the SDOF oscillator

\[ \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m},\qquad \zeta=\frac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}},\qquad \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}. \]

03 · Measuring damping from a decay

The great practical value of the SDOF model is that both parameters can be read from a single measured decay — strike the structure and record the free vibration. The frequency of the ringing gives \( \omega_d \) (and hence \( \omega_n \)), and the rate of amplitude decay gives \( \zeta \) through the logarithmic decrement \( \delta=\ln\dfrac{x_1}{x_2} \), the natural log of the ratio of two successive peaks. For light damping \( \zeta\approx\dfrac{\delta}{2\pi} \). If successive peaks measure \( 10 \) mm and \( 8 \) mm, then \( \delta=\ln(10/8)=0.223 \) and \( \zeta\approx0.223/2\pi\approx0.036 \). No shaker or force measurement is needed — just the trace of a free decay, which is why the impact "bump test" is the workhorse of field vibration diagnosis.

04 · The working method

To characterise any vibrating system: (1) model it as an SDOF \( m,c,k \), lumping the effective mass and stiffness; (2) compute \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) and \( \zeta=c/(2\sqrt{mk}) \) — the natural frequency you must keep operating speeds away from, and the damping that limits any resonant response; (3) from a measured decay, extract \( \omega_d \) from the ringing frequency and \( \zeta \) from the logarithmic decrement. These two numbers, \( \omega_n \) and \( \zeta \), are the complete fingerprint of the free response — and, joined with the forced-response curve of the mathematics course, they are what the Vibrations and rotating-machinery courses spend the next years applying to real machines.

Lesson 01 · Phase diagrams

01 · Phases, components, and equilibrium

A phase is a region of uniform structure and composition — solid, liquid, or a distinct crystal type; a component is a pure substance making up the alloy. A phase diagram maps, for an alloy of two components, which phases are present and in what amounts at each temperature and overall composition, assuming equilibrium. It is the metallurgist's master chart: from it you read melting behaviour, what forms on cooling, and how heat treatment will change the microstructure. The regions are bounded by the liquidus (above which all is liquid) and the solidus (below which all is solid); between them, solid and liquid coexist.

02 · The tie line and the lever rule

Inside a two-phase region, a point on the diagram tells you three things through a horizontal tie line drawn at that temperature. Its ends, where it meets the phase boundaries, give the compositions of the two phases — the liquid \( C_L \) and the solid \( C_\alpha \). The lever rule then gives the amounts: the fraction of each phase is the length of the opposite lever arm over the whole tie line, \( W_L=\dfrac{C_\alpha-C_0}{C_\alpha-C_L} \) and \( W_\alpha=\dfrac{C_0-C_L}{C_\alpha-C_L} \). For an overall composition \( C_0=40 \)% between \( C_L=30 \)% and \( C_\alpha=50 \)%: \( W_L=\dfrac{50-40}{50-30}=0.5 \), so the alloy is half liquid and half solid by mass. The nearer \( C_0 \) sits to one boundary, the more of that phase there is.

Key tool — the lever rule

\[ W_L=\frac{C_\alpha-C_0}{C_\alpha-C_L},\qquad W_\alpha=\frac{C_0-C_L}{C_\alpha-C_L}\quad(W_L+W_\alpha=1). \]

03 · Binary systems: isomorphous and eutectic

Two patterns cover most binary alloys. An isomorphous system (like copper–nickel) has complete solid solubility — one solid phase across all compositions — and a simple lens-shaped two-phase region between liquidus and solidus. A eutectic system (like lead–tin, or the iron–carbon system of the next lesson) has limited solubility and a special eutectic point: the lowest-melting composition, where liquid transforms directly into two solids at a single temperature, \( L\to\alpha+\beta \). The eutectic reaction gives the fine two-phase microstructures that control the strength of solders, cast alloys, and steels — which is why the eutectic and eutectoid points are the landmarks a metallurgist memorises.

04 · The phase rule and the method

How many variables you may change freely is fixed by the Gibbs phase rule, \( F=C-P+2 \), or at constant pressure \( F=C-P+1 \). For a binary alloy (\(C=2\)) in a single-phase region (\(P=1\)), \( F=2 \): temperature and composition both vary freely. In a two-phase region (\(P=2\)), \( F=1 \): fix the temperature and both phase compositions are pinned by the tie-line ends. The working method for reading any phase diagram: (1) locate the point by temperature and overall composition; (2) identify the phase(s) present; (3) if two phases, draw the tie line for their compositions and apply the lever rule for their amounts. This single procedure unlocks every alloy chart in the course.

Lesson 02 · The iron–carbon system and heat treatment

01 · The phases of steel

Steel is iron with up to about \(2\)% carbon, and its behaviour is the story of a few phases. Ferrite (\( \alpha \), body-centred cubic) is soft, ductile, and dissolves almost no carbon (\(0.022\)% maximum). Austenite (\( \gamma \), face-centred cubic) is the high-temperature phase and dissolves far more carbon (up to \(2.14\)%). Cementite (Fe\(_3\)C) is an iron carbide — hard and brittle, \(6.7\)% carbon. The workhorse microstructure is pearlite, a fine lamellar mixture of ferrite and cementite that forms when austenite is cooled slowly. Which phases you get, and in what proportions, is read directly from the iron–carbon diagram.

02 · The eutectoid reaction

The landmark of the steel diagram is the eutectoid point at \(0.76\)% carbon and \(727^\circ\)C. There, on cooling, solid austenite transforms into two solids at once — \( \gamma\to\alpha+\text{Fe}_3\text{C} \) — producing pearlite. A steel of exactly \(0.76\)% carbon (eutectoid steel) becomes fully pearlitic. A hypoeutectoid steel (less carbon) first forms soft proeutectoid ferrite above \(727^\circ\)C, then the remaining austenite turns to pearlite; a hypereutectoid steel (more carbon) first forms cementite. The eutectoid reaction is the hinge on which all plain-carbon steel microstructures turn.

03 · Reading amounts with the lever rule

The lever rule of Lesson 1 quantifies the microstructure. For a \(0.40\)% carbon (hypoeutectoid) steel just below \(727^\circ\)C, using \(C_\alpha=0.022\)% and the eutectoid \(0.76\)%: the proeutectoid ferrite fraction is \( \dfrac{0.76-0.40}{0.76-0.022}\approx0.49 \) and the pearlite fraction \( \dfrac{0.40-0.022}{0.76-0.022}\approx0.51 \). So a medium-carbon steel is roughly half soft ferrite and half pearlite — a balance of ductility and strength. More carbon means more pearlite (and eventually cementite), which is why hardness and strength climb, and ductility falls, with carbon content.

Key landmark — the eutectoid

\[ \gamma\ (0.76\%\ \text{C})\xrightarrow{727^\circ\text{C}}\ \alpha+\text{Fe}_3\text{C}\ (\text{pearlite}). \]

04 · Heat treatment: beyond equilibrium

The diagram assumes slow, equilibrium cooling — but the power of steel comes from breaking that assumption. Cool austenite fast (quench) and carbon has no time to diffuse out; the result is martensite, a hard, brittle, distorted structure that gives steel its strength. Because martensite is too brittle to use as-quenched, it is tempered — reheated moderately — to trade some hardness for toughness. How readily a steel forms martensite at depth is its hardenability, improved by alloying (next lessons). The tools for predicting non-equilibrium structures are the TTT and CCT diagrams, which plot transformation against time; the working method is to pick a composition for the carbon it provides, then a cooling path (anneal, normalize, quench-and-temper) for the structure and properties you need.

Lesson 03 · Annealing, normalizing, and surface hardening

01 · Softening and homogenizing: anneal, normalize, stress relieve, spheroidize

Four heat treatments share one purpose — leave the steel softer, more uniform, or less stressed than it arrived — and differ only in how far they go. A full anneal austenitizes the steel, then cools it slowly in the furnace itself. The long time spent transforming near 727°C lets carbon diffuse far, so the pearlite that forms is coarse and widely spaced — the softest, most ductile condition the steel can reach, at the cost of hours of furnace time. A normalize austenitizes the same way but cools in still air — faster, cheaper, and stopping the transformation before carbon has time to spread as far, giving fine pearlite: moderately stronger and harder than annealed, and — because austenitizing resets the grain size — more uniform than as-cast or as-forged stock.

02 · Reading the diagram — cooling rate sets lamellar spacing

The working method is the same tool from Lessons 1–2, run twice. Both curves leave the austenitizing temperature together and cross the 727°C eutectoid line — the furnace path far later than the air path. Time spent in the transformation range controls how far carbon can diffuse before the pearlite locks in: slow crossing → carbon travels far → thick, widely-spaced lamellae → soft. Fast crossing → carbon travels little → thin, closely-spaced lamellae → hard. This is the same principle as grain-size strengthening from Materials I: finer internal spacing resists dislocation motion more, so hardness rises as the microstructure gets finer — whether that spacing is grain size or lamellar spacing.

Stress relief and spheroidize stay below 727°C entirely, so neither re-forms austenite. Stress relief (roughly 600–650°C, held then air- or furnace-cooled) exists only to let locked-in residual stress from welding, machining, or cold forming relax — it changes no phase, only removes distortion risk. Spheroidize holds a high-carbon steel just under 727°C long enough that the cementite lamellae ball up into spheroids dispersed in ferrite — the softest, most formable condition a high-carbon steel reaches, used before heavy cold work such as cold heading or wire drawing.

03 · Case hardening: a hard shell, a tough core

A gear tooth or a cam lobe needs a wear-resistant surface and a fracture-resistant core at once — through-hardening the whole part would win the surface and lose the core to brittleness. Three processes solve this by hardening only a shell. Carburizing diffuses carbon into a low-carbon steel's surface at high temperature (roughly 900–950°C) in a carbon-rich atmosphere; the enriched surface then hardens fully on quenching while the low-carbon core stays tough. Because it is diffusion, case depth follows the same square-root-of-time law as any diffusion process: \( x\approx k\sqrt{t} \) — doubling the case depth takes four times the time, not twice.

Nitriding diffuses nitrogen into the surface at a much lower temperature (roughly 500–550°C), forming hard nitride compounds directly, with no quench afterward — the part was already finish-machined, so avoiding a quench avoids the distortion a quench would cause. Induction hardening diffuses nothing at all: a coil heats only a thin surface layer of an already carbon-adequate steel above the transformation temperature in seconds, and that layer alone is quenched — fast, atmosphere-free, well suited to high-volume shafts and gears.

04 · Choosing a treatment — the working method

Given a required property set, the sequence runs: (1) normalize or anneal the as-received bar stock so it machines cleanly and predictably; (2) machine to near-final shape; (3) case-harden the specific wear surfaces — carburizing where a deep case and a soft core both matter, induction hardening where speed and a defined shallow zone matter, nitriding where the part must not distort — leaving stock on the hardened surfaces for finishing, since hardening shifts dimensions slightly; (4) finish-grind the hardened surfaces to final size. A drawing callout that reads "case-harden journals, 0.8–1.0 mm effective case depth" is asking for exactly this sequence, in this order.

Key tool — diffusion case depth

\[ x \approx k\sqrt{t}\qquad\Rightarrow\qquad \frac{t_2}{t_1}=\left(\frac{x_2}{x_1}\right)^2. \]

Lesson 04 · Carbon and alloy steels

01 · Reading the designation

The AISI/SAE four-digit system encodes composition directly. The first two digits name the alloy family — 10xx is plain carbon, 41xx is chromium-molybdenum, 43xx is nickel-chromium-molybdenum, and so on. The last two digits give the carbon content in hundredths of a percent: 4140 is a Cr-Mo steel at \(0.40\)% C; 1020 is plain carbon at \(0.20\)% C. Reading a designation is reading two separate questions at once — which alloying family, and how much carbon — and the two answer different things about the steel entirely.

02 · What carbon buys, and what alloying buys

Carbon sets the ceiling: the maximum hardness any steel of that composition can reach, achieved right at a quenched surface where cooling is fastest. A 1040 and a 4140 — both \(0.40\)% C — quench to essentially the same peak hardness. What differs is how far that hardness extends inward. Alloying elements — manganese, chromium, molybdenum, nickel — slow the austenite-to-pearlite transformation, so a slower-cooling interior still has time to form the hard, non-equilibrium structure instead of soft pearlite. This property — how deep the hardening penetrates, independent of the peak value — is hardenability, and it is what alloying buys.

03 · The Jominy end-quench test

Hardenability is measured directly: a standard round bar is austenitized, then one end is water-quenched while the rest air-cools at a rate that falls off smoothly with distance from that end. Hardness measured along the bar's length — the Jominy curve — is a direct picture of hardenability. A shallow-hardenability steel (plain carbon) drops from peak hardness within a few millimetres of the quenched end; a deep-hardenability steel (alloyed) stays near-peak for tens of millimetres. At the classic reference distance of \(12.5\ \text{mm}\) (\(\tfrac{1}{2}\ \text{in}\)), a 1040-type curve has already fallen to the mid-20s HRC while a 4140-type curve is still in the low-to-mid 40s — the same numbers a heat-treater reads off a real Jominy chart to judge whether a given steel will through-harden a given section size.

04 · Selecting a grade — the working method

Selection runs from the part outward. First, the section size: a thin part cools fast everywhere and a plain-carbon steel may through-harden it fine; a thick shaft's core cools slowly no matter what quenchant is used, and only a deep-hardenability alloy steel will harden there. Second, the service demand: a shaft under bending and torsion needs toughness through its cross-section, which plain-carbon steel — hard skin, soft, weak core — cannot give at any real diameter. Third, cost: alloying elements and the slower, more controlled quenches deep-hardenability grades often need both add cost, so a plain-carbon grade is the right answer whenever section size and service load allow it. A 4140 shaft and a 1040 shaft can leave the quench at the identical surface hardness; only the Jominy curve — and the part's actual thickness — says which one is lying about its core.

Key distinction

Carbon content sets peak hardness. Alloy content sets how far that hardness reaches — hardenability, read directly off the Jominy curve.

Lesson 05 · Stainless steels and cast irons

01 · The stainless families

Any steel with roughly \(10.5\)–\(12\)% chromium or more forms a thin, adherent, self-healing chromium-oxide film — passivity — and stops rusting the way plain carbon steel does. Above that composition floor, four structural families cover almost every application. Austenitic stainless (the 300 series: Cr + Ni, FCC, non-magnetic) gives the best combination of corrosion resistance, ductility, and weldability, but cannot be hardened by heat treatment — only by cold work. Ferritic stainless (Cr only, BCC, magnetic) is cheaper and adequate where corrosion demands are moderate. Martensitic stainless carries enough carbon to quench-harden exactly like a plain carbon steel — traded corrosion resistance for the ability to reach real hardness, used for cutlery, shafts, and valve trim. Duplex stainless is a roughly 50/50 austenite–ferrite structure, combining ferritic-level strength with corrosion resistance that rivals or beats austenitic grades in chloride-rich environments.

02 · Sensitization — reading the diagram

Welding heats the metal beside the weld — the heat-affected zone — through the sensitizing range, roughly \(450\)–\(850^\circ\)C, for long enough that chromium carbides (\(\text{Cr}_{23}\text{C}_6\)) precipitate directly onto grain boundaries. Forming those carbides pulls chromium out of the matrix immediately next to the boundary — not from the bulk, which stays fully alloyed, but from a narrow band exactly where the carbides sit. If that local concentration drops below the passivity threshold, the boundary loses its protective film while the surrounding grains keep theirs, and corrosion attacks precisely along the grain boundaries — intergranular corrosion, sometimes called weld decay. The working method: find the heat-affected zone, confirm it passed through the sensitizing range, and treat every grain boundary there as the weak point, not the plate as a whole.

Three fixes address it directly: low-carbon "L" grades (304L, 316L, \(C<0.03\)%) simply have too little carbon to form much \(\text{Cr}_{23}\text{C}_6\); stabilized grades (321, 347) add titanium or niobium, which forms TiC or NbC preferentially and leaves the chromium in solution; and a post-weld solution anneal (reheat above \(\sim\!1050^\circ\)C, then quench) redissolves any carbides that did form and re-homogenizes the chromium before the part enters service.

03 · Cast irons — one base metal, three graphite shapes

Cast iron is iron with \(2\)–\(4\)% carbon, and its mechanical character comes almost entirely from what shape the excess carbon takes as graphite. Gray iron lets graphite grow as sharp, interconnected flakes; each flake is a built-in crack, so gray iron is brittle with near-zero elongation — but it is cheap, castable, an excellent vibration damper, and strong in compression, which is exactly what a machine base or engine block needs. Ductile (nodular) iron adds a trace of magnesium or cerium before pouring, which forces the graphite into spheroids instead of flakes; spheroids concentrate far less stress, so ductile iron reaches real tensile strength and real elongation (typically \(10\)–\(18\)%) — strong enough for crankshafts, gears, and pressure-piping fittings. White iron is cooled fast enough (or alloyed) that the carbon stays locked as cementite and never forms graphite at all — extremely hard, extremely brittle, used only where pure abrasive wear resistance matters and impact loading does not, such as mill liners and crusher jaws.

04 · Selecting a family — the working method

For stainless: default to austenitic unless a specific constraint rules it out — need to heat-treat-harden the part, go martensitic; need maximum strength with strong chloride resistance, go duplex; need to hold cost down where corrosion demands are mild, ferritic is enough. For cast iron: a non-critical, compressively-loaded, vibration-damped part is gray iron's job; anything that must also survive tension or shock — a crankshaft, a gear, a pipe fitting — needs ductile iron instead, even though it costs more than gray; a purely abrasive, low-impact wear surface is the one place white iron's brittleness is an acceptable price for its hardness. Both decisions run the same way: name the dominant load the part actually sees, then pick the family built for that load, not the cheapest one that merely looks similar on a datasheet.

Key mechanism — sensitization

Welding drives the HAZ through 450–850°C long enough for Cr₂₃C₆ to precipitate at grain boundaries, pulling local Cr below the ~12% passivity floor right at the boundary — while the bulk alloy is untouched.

Lesson 06 · Nonferrous alloys

01 · Aluminum and its temper system

Wrought aluminum alloys are named by a four-digit code whose first digit gives the principal alloying element — 2xxx is Al-Cu, 5xxx is Al-Mg, 6xxx is Al-Mg-Si, 7xxx is Al-Zn — the same logic as a steel designation's family digits, applied to a different metal. A temper suffix then says what was done to it: F is as-fabricated, O is fully annealed (softest), H covers strain-hardened tempers used on alloys that cannot be heat-treated to strengthen, and T covers thermally treated tempers. Of the T-series, T4 means solution heat treated and naturally aged at room temperature over days to weeks; T6 means solution heat treated and artificially aged at a moderate elevated temperature to reach peak strength in hours.

02 · Reading the age-hardening curve

Solution treatment heats an Al-Cu alloy hot enough to dissolve its copper fully into solid solution, then quenches fast enough to trap that copper in a supersaturated, still-soft state. Aging — holding at a moderate temperature — lets that trapped copper begin precipitating as extremely fine particles distributed through the matrix; those fine particles block dislocation motion far more effectively than the same copper sitting dissolved in solution, so strength climbs as aging proceeds. Underaged material simply hasn't aged long enough — precipitates are still too small and sparse. At the peak (T6), precipitate size and spacing are exactly tuned to obstruct dislocations most effectively. Held longer still, the precipitates keep growing — overaging — becoming fewer, larger, and more widely spaced, which dislocations can bypass more easily again, so strength falls even though the alloy spent more time at temperature, not less.

03 · Copper and nickel alloys

Copper's defining property is conductivity, and its central engineering trade-off follows directly: anything that strengthens copper — alloying or cold work — also degrades that conductivity. Conductor-grade copper is drawn through a series of dies to final wire size, and each pass work-hardens it, piling up dislocations and using up the ductility needed for the next pass; drawn far enough without relief, it cracks instead of continuing to draw down. An interpass anneal — a recrystallization heat treatment — restores ductility between passes so drawing can continue. Brass (Cu-Zn) is cheaper and more machinable; bronze (Cu-Sn or Cu-Al) is tougher and more wear- and corrosion-resistant; both are prized in hazardous atmospheres for being non-sparking. Nickel alloys hold their strength at temperatures where steel and aluminum have both lost most of theirs, and resist corrosion in chemical environments that attack stainless steel outright — earning their considerable cost premium only where nothing cheaper survives.

04 · Choosing a nonferrous family — the working method

Where weight and moderate strength matter most, aluminum wins — then the temper is chosen for the job: T6 for a structural part, O or an H-temper for sheet that still needs to be formed afterward. Where electrical or thermal conductivity is the actual requirement, copper is the answer, and the specification states plainly whether annealed (soft, most conductive, most formable) or hard-drawn (stronger, still adequately conductive) is needed. Where the service temperature or the chemical environment would defeat steel or stainless outright, a nickel alloy is the only real option, and its cost is simply the price of the part surviving at all. Each family earns its premium over plain steel by solving one specific problem steel cannot — the working method is naming that problem before naming the alloy.

Key curve — age hardening

Strength rises as fine, coherent precipitates form and block dislocations (underaged → peak, T6) — then FALLS as those same precipitates coarsen (overaged). More time at temperature is not always better.

Lesson 07 · Fatigue, fracture, and creep

01 · Fatigue and the S–N curve

Fatigue is failure under repeated, cyclic stress — often at amplitudes far below the material's static strength — and it is the dominant failure mode in any rotating or vibrating part: shafts, bearings, gears, piping under pulsation. The S–N curve plots stress amplitude against cycles to failure on a log scale and is the fundamental fatigue chart. For steel, the curve bends and flattens near \(10^6\)–\(10^7\) cycles into an endurance limit \(S_e\): stay below it and the steel can, in principle, survive indefinitely. A useful first-cut estimate is \( S_e\approx 0.5\times\text{UTS} \) — for a \(800\ \text{MPa}\) UTS steel, roughly \(400\ \text{MPa}\) — but this is a starting point only, always reduced by knockdown factors for surface finish, size, notches, temperature, and required reliability before it is trusted as a design number.

02 · Fracture mechanics — the simplest honest version

Every real part contains flaws — inclusions, porosity, machining marks, corrosion pits — and fracture mechanics asks a direct question: given a flaw of size \(a\) under applied stress \(\sigma\), will it grow catastrophically? The stress-intensity factor \( K=Y\sigma\sqrt{\pi a} \) (with \(Y\), a geometry factor, near \(1\) for simple cases) measures how strongly the crack tip is loaded. Fracture occurs when \(K\) reaches the material's fracture toughness \(K_{IC}\) — a measured, tabulated property. This is why a part can pass a smooth, unflawed tensile test and still fail in service at half that stress: the tensile specimen had no crack to grow, but a flawed in-service part can reach \(K_{IC}\) at a stress far below its own tensile strength. Toughness, not strength alone, sets the real limit once a flaw exists.

03 · Reading a fracture surface, and creep

A fatigue fracture surface carries two classic tells: beach marks (macroscopic concentric arcs, from crack growth pausing and resuming under varying load) and, under magnification, striations — one per load cycle. Neither appears on a ductile overload fracture, which instead shows necking, shear lips, and a fibrous, dimpled surface; a brittle overload fracture is flat, grainy, with little visible deformation. Reading which of these a fracture surface shows is the fastest diagnostic step in any failure investigation.

Creep is slow, time-dependent permanent deformation under sustained stress at elevated temperature — a practical concern once service temperature exceeds roughly \(0.4\) times the absolute melting temperature (the same homologous-temperature idea from Materials I). It runs in three stages: primary (decelerating strain rate), secondary or steady-state (constant, minimum rate — the regime used for life estimates), and tertiary (accelerating strain rate to rupture). Furnace hardware, turbine blades, and high-temperature piping running hot for years are creep-life designs, not simple strength designs.

04 · Reading a failure — the working method

Given a failed part: (1) read the fracture surface first — beach marks or striations mean fatigue; necking and dimples mean ductile overload; a flat, grainy surface with no deformation means brittle overload; heavy deformation with intergranular voids means creep. (2) If fatigue, find the initiation site — almost always a surface flaw or stress concentration, since fatigue cracks start where local stress is highest. (3) Compare the operating stress against the S–N curve (was this a foreseeable number of cycles at this amplitude) and, if a flaw is visible, against \(K_{IC}\) (was the flaw large enough to explain failure at this stress). That sequence is exactly how a failure investigation moves from "it broke" to a defensible reason why.

Key rule — first-cut endurance limit

\[ S_e \approx 0.5\times\text{UTS}\quad\text{(steel; before knockdown factors)}. \]

Lesson 08 · Polymers and elastomers

01 · Thermoplastics versus thermosets

Thermoplastics are chains — linear or branched — held together only by weak secondary (van der Waals, entanglement) forces between neighboring chains. Heat overcomes those weak forces, so a thermoplastic softens and melts, and can be re-melted and reshaped repeatedly: PE, PP, PVC, nylon, and PTFE all work this way. Thermosets instead cure through an irreversible chemical reaction that links chains together with strong covalent bonds into a single connected network. Heating a cured thermoset does not remelt it — there is no weak bond left to overcome — it chars or decomposes at high enough temperature instead. Epoxy, phenolic resin, and cured polyurethane are thermosets; vulcanized rubber behaves the same way, since vulcanization crosslinks its chains exactly as curing does.

02 · The glass transition — reading the diagram

Below the glass transition temperature \(T_g\), an amorphous polymer's chain segments are essentially frozen — too little thermal energy for them to rotate or slide past one another — and the material is glassy: stiff, and often brittle. Above \(T_g\), segments gain enough mobility to move, and modulus falls dramatically, often two to three orders of magnitude over a narrow temperature range. A crosslinked elastomer levels off above \(T_g\) at a low rubbery plateau — the crosslinks stop it from simply flowing away — and that rubbery, resilient state is exactly what makes an elastomer useful as a seal: it can be compressed and spring back. An uncrosslinked thermoplastic above its own \(T_g\) has no such plateau; it keeps softening toward viscous flow instead.

03 · Elastomers for seals — the O-ring, and the Gulf's real risk

An O-ring seals by staying elastically compressed between two mating surfaces, which only works while it remains in its rubbery state. Drop it below \(T_g\) and it goes glassy and stiff, loses its ability to spring back into the groove, and leaks — a cold-start O-ring failure is one of the most famous documented failure modes in engineering history, from a well-known spacecraft accident where an unusually cold morning stiffened the seals past their working range. In Gulf-region service, ambient and process heat sit well above almost any common elastomer's \(T_g\), so cold-embrittlement is rarely the practical risk — except where the equipment itself runs cold regardless of climate: refrigeration lines, cryogenic or LNG-adjacent service, chilled process streams. The diagram's other landmark, at the high-temperature end, is the one that actually bites in sustained Gulf heat: a practical service-temperature ceiling set by thermal and oxidative degradation — chain scission, hardening, cracking — a completely different mechanism from \(T_g\), reached from the opposite direction. A seal compound must also resist the specific fluid it contacts; one rated for water may swell or soften in a hydrocarbon it was never qualified for.

04 · Selecting a polymer or elastomer — the working method

Given a service temperature window and a chemical environment: (1) confirm the low end sits safely above the candidate's \(T_g\), with real margin, not right at the line; (2) confirm the high end stays below its practical thermal/oxidative service limit; (3) confirm chemical compatibility with the actual process fluid, not "elastomer" as a generic category; (4) choose thermoplastic (needs to be formed or re-formed) versus a crosslinked thermoset or elastomer (needs to flex and recover its shape). Skipping any one of these four checks is how the wrong seal compound ends up in service.

Key states

Below \(T_g\): glassy, stiff, brittle. Through \(T_g\): modulus falls 2–3 orders of magnitude. Above \(T_g\) (crosslinked): rubbery plateau — the state a seal must stay in to function.

Lesson 09 · Ceramics and composites

01 · Why ceramics fail statistically

Ceramics are strong in compression but weak and unreliable in tension, and the reason is the same in both cases: strength is controlled by the largest flaw — a micro-crack or pore — present in the stressed volume, not by an intrinsic bulk property the way a ductile metal's yield strength is. Every sample carries a different largest flaw, placed randomly by the manufacturing process, so identical samples tested identically fail at different stresses — strength is a distribution, not a single number. Under tension, a flaw concentrates stress at its tip and, with essentially no ductility available to blunt or redistribute that stress, can grow catastrophically; the same flaw under compression tends to close rather than open, which is why "tension is the enemy" for any ceramic component.

02 · The Weibull distribution — reading the diagram

The probability that a sample fails at or below stress \(\sigma\) follows the Weibull distribution, \( P_f(\sigma)=1-\exp\!\left[-(\sigma/\sigma_0)^m\right] \). \(\sigma_0\), the characteristic strength, is the stress at which about \(63\)% of samples are expected to have failed. \(m\), the Weibull modulus, describes the spread: a high \(m\) gives a narrow, predictable strength distribution (a well-controlled process); a low \(m\) gives a wide, unpredictable one, where some samples fail at far lower stress than others. Reading the curve directly answers why ceramic strength is statistical: at \(\sigma=250\ \text{MPa}\), roughly \(21\)% of identical samples are already expected to have failed; raise the stress by a modest \(40\)% to \(350\ \text{MPa}\) and failure probability jumps to about \(97\)%. That is an extremely narrow, unforgiving safe-operating window compared to a ductile metal — which is why ceramic components carry large safety margins and are often proof-tested: loaded once to a screening stress before service, specifically to break off the weak tail of the distribution before it can fail in the field.

03 · Refractories and fiber composites

Refractories are ceramics engineered specifically to survive sustained high temperature — furnace, kiln, and ladle linings — without melting, softening, or reacting with what they contain. A high melting point alone is not enough: the lining must also have low thermal conductivity (to insulate the steel shell behind it) and chemical compatibility with the slag or metal it contacts, since an incompatible refractory can be chemically attacked and dissolved over time well below its own melting point. Fiber composites pair a strong, stiff fiber (glass, carbon, aramid) that carries the load with a matrix — usually polymer — that holds the fibers in position, transfers load between them, and protects them from the environment. Neither material alone reaches the composite's combination of stiffness, strength, and low weight; industrial hardware built this way includes fiberglass piping and tanks in corrosive service and carbon-fiber components wherever weight is a genuine constraint.

04 · Working with ceramics and composites — the working method

For ceramics: design for compression wherever the geometry allows it, keep tensile stresses low with generous safety factors, avoid sharp corners or point contacts that locally raise tension, and proof-test when reliability truly matters. For composites: identify the direction the load actually travels — a composite is strongly directional, strong along the fiber and comparatively weak across it, unlike an isotropic metal — and orient plies to carry that load; then treat the matrix's temperature and chemical limits, usually lower than the fiber's own, as the real service ceiling for the whole part.

Lesson 10 · Environmental degradation preview

01 · Oxidation and aqueous corrosion

Oxidation is a metal reacting directly with oxygen (or another oxidizing gas) at its surface, forming an oxide layer. Some oxides are protective: aluminum's natural \(\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3\) and chromium's \(\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_3\) (stainless steel's passive film, from Lesson 5) are thin, dense, and tightly adherent, and effectively stop further oxidation once formed. Iron's oxide is the opposite — porous and poorly adherent, so it keeps flaking away, exposing fresh metal to keep oxidizing indefinitely. Whether an oxide protects or not comes down to how well it adheres and how completely it covers the surface, not simply whether one forms.

Aqueous corrosion is fundamentally electrochemical: an anode reaction dissolves metal, releasing electrons; a cathode reaction (commonly oxygen and water forming hydroxide, or hydrogen ions forming hydrogen gas) consumes those electrons elsewhere on the same or a connected surface. The metal itself carries the electron path, the surrounding water or moisture carries the ion path — a corroding part is, electrically, a small spontaneous battery.

02 · Galvanic couples and the area-ratio trap — reading the diagram

Connect two different metals electrically in the same electrolyte and one becomes the anode, the other the cathode — read directly off a galvanic series, a ranked list of relative nobility (exact voltages shift with the electrolyte, but the ranking of common metals is broadly consistent). The less noble metal corrodes faster than it would alone; the more noble metal is actually protected, corroding slower than it would alone — the working principle behind galvanizing (a sacrificial zinc coating protects the steel beneath it) and cathodic protection.

How badly the anode suffers depends heavily on the area ratio between the two metals. A small anode area next to a large cathode area is the dangerous combination: the same total corrosion current concentrates onto the small anode, driving a high local corrosion rate there. A large anode next to a small cathode is comparatively safe — the same current spreads over far more area, so the corrosion rate per unit area stays low. A small stainless-steel fastener in a large steel plate is fine; a small steel fastener in a large stainless plate is exactly the dangerous case — the size of the part, not just which metal is "better," decides the outcome.

03 · Passivity: both shield and trap

Stainless steel's passivity (Lesson 5) depends on a continuous supply of oxygen to maintain and self-heal its protective film. In a crevice — under a gasket, under a deposit, inside a thread — oxygen is starved, the film cannot repair itself there, and that narrow spot becomes a small, localized anode surrounded by the still-passive (and now cathodic) surface around it: the area-ratio trap of 2, this time inflicted by the material's own protective mechanism. This is crevice corrosion, a close cousin of pitting corrosion, and it is often more dangerous than corrosion on a "worse" material like plain steel — plain steel corrodes visibly and roughly uniformly, while passive-film breakdown is invisible until the damage is already deep and localized.

Hydrogen embrittlement is flagged here rather than developed in full: atomic hydrogen — introduced by a corrosion reaction, an electroplating step, or a poorly applied cathodic-protection system — can diffuse into a susceptible high-strength steel and cause sudden, low-stress, brittle cracking with no warning. It is a distinct mechanism from the fatigue and fracture of Lesson 7, and gets full treatment in Year 3's corrosion course.

04 · Screening a corrosion risk — the working method

At this preview depth, three questions cover most real situations: (1) Is more than one metal in electrical contact in a wet or damp environment? Check each metal's position on the galvanic series and, critically, the area ratio — a small anode is the danger sign. (2) Is there a crevice, a stagnant pocket, or a deposit anywhere on a "corrosion-resistant" material? Suspect local passive-film breakdown even where the bulk surface looks fine. (3) Is hydrogen present — pickling, plating, sour service, or cathodic protection — on a high-strength steel? Flag it for specialist review rather than dismissing it. This is a screening checklist, not a full design method; building the design method is Year 3's job.

Key trap — area ratio

A SMALL anode next to a LARGE cathode corrodes fast (concentrated current). A LARGE anode next to a small cathode is comparatively safe (the same current, spread thin).

Lesson 11 · Materials selection in practice

01 · Property indices — screening by ratio, not by one number

Comparing candidate materials on a single property — just stiffness, or just weight — routinely picks the wrong material for a real structural task, because the part's actual performance depends on a combination of properties tied to how it is loaded. A property index combines properties into one ratio matched to the specific failure mode and loading. For a stiff, light beam in bending, free to vary its cross-section, the index that falls out of minimizing mass for a given stiffness is \( E^{1/2}/\rho \) — modulus to the one-half power, over density. Comparing steel (\(E=200\ \text{GPa}\), \(\rho=7850\ \text{kg/m}^3\)) against aluminum (\(E=70\ \text{GPa}\), \(\rho=2700\ \text{kg/m}^3\)): steel's index is about \(57\), aluminum's about \(98\) — aluminum wins by roughly \(70\)%, despite steel's modulus being nearly three times higher, because the index rewards low density far more than it rewards high stiffness for this loading case.

02 · Building the index — the working method

Any index is built the same way: identify what is fixed (a performance requirement — stiffness, or strength, for a given geometry) and what is free (usually the cross-sectional dimensions, which can be scaled up or down), then derive which combination of material properties, raised to which powers, minimizes mass subject to that constraint. The exponent changes with the loading mode: a tie in simple tension gives index \(E/\rho\); a beam in bending with free cross-section gives \(E^{1/2}/\rho\); a panel loaded in bending gives \(E^{1/3}/\rho\). This is exactly why "just compare \(E\)" is the wrong instinct for a real structural screening problem — the correct comparison depends on how the part is loaded and what is allowed to change, not on the raw property alone.

03 · Cost, availability, and substitution

A property index screens candidates on performance alone; a real specification always adds two more first-class constraints. Cost matters most as cost per unit of the property actually needed — cost per unit stiffness delivered, not cost per kilogram in isolation. Availability asks whether the specified grade can actually be procured, in the needed form, quantity, and lead time — a technically ideal material that cannot be delivered on schedule has already failed the specification. When the specified grade is genuinely unobtainable, a substitution decision must prove, not merely assert, that the replacement satisfies every property the original was specified for — not just the one property that happens to look similar at a glance.

04 · Documenting a selection — the working method

A defensible selection record, one an auditor can follow, states in order: (1) the governing constraint and the free variable — what had to stay fixed, what was allowed to vary; (2) the property index derived from that constraint, and why that index fits the loading; (3) the shortlist of candidates screened by that index, with real property values and their sources, not estimates; (4) the cost and availability check on the leading candidate(s); (5) the final selection, with an explicit statement of which requirement each rejected candidate failed to meet. A substitution memo follows the identical shape: the original specification and why each key property mattered, the substitute's documented value for each of those same properties, and an explicit statement of any property where the substitute falls short and why that is — or is not — acceptable for this specific application.

Key indices — minimum mass at fixed stiffness

Tie (tension): maximize \(E/\rho\). Beam (bending, free shape): maximize \(E^{1/2}/\rho\). Panel (bending): maximize \(E^{1/3}/\rho\).

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Systems, properties, and state

01 · Systems and their boundaries

A system is whatever is under study; everything else is the surroundings, separated by a boundary. A closed system has a boundary that energy may cross, but mass may not — gas trapped in a sealed piston-cylinder is the standard example. An open system, or control volume, has a boundary crossed by both mass and energy — a pump, a length of pipe, a compressor. Almost every real piece of plant equipment is a control volume, which is why its own energy balance (Lesson 5) carries most of the weight for the rest of this course. An isolated system is the idealization where neither mass nor energy crosses at all — a useful limit case for the second law later in Lesson 8.

02 · Properties: intensive versus extensive

An extensive property depends on how much of the system is present — mass, volume, and total internal energy all double if the system doubles. An intensive property does not — temperature, pressure, and density are the same everywhere in a uniform system regardless of how much of it is being examined. Dividing an extensive property by mass produces its specific (intensive) counterpart: specific volume \(v=V/m\), specific internal energy \(u=U/m\), specific enthalpy \(h=H/m\). This lower-case convention — capital for the total (extensive) quantity, lower-case for the per-unit-mass (intensive) quantity — is used without further comment for the rest of the course.

03 · Equilibrium, process paths, and the state postulate

A system is in equilibrium when no unbalanced thermal, mechanical, or chemical potential remains to drive further change; only then does every property have one well-defined value throughout the system. A process is the change from one equilibrium state to another, and the specific sequence of states passed through along the way is its path — different paths can connect the same two endpoints, a fact Lesson 3 uses directly. The state postulate, for the simple compressible substances this course works with, says that any two independent intensive properties fix the state completely — every other property then follows. This single idea is why steam tables are organized the way they are: look up two known properties, read everything else.

04 · Units and sign conventions — fixed once, forever

This course works in SI: kg, m, s, K for the base quantities; kJ for energy; kPa or bar for pressure; kJ/kg and kJ/(kg·K) for specific quantities. The sign convention, stated once so it never needs restating: heat transfer into the system is positive (\(+Q\)); work done by the system (output) is positive (\(+W\)) — the standard engineering convention. Under it, the first law (Lesson 4) reads \( \Delta U = Q - W \): heat in helps the balance, work out costs it. Inconsistent sign conventions across sources are a classic, avoidable source of error — this one is fixed here and used throughout.

Key rule — the state postulate

For a simple compressible substance, any TWO independent intensive properties fix the state completely.

Lesson 02 · Pure substance properties and steam tables

01 · The three regions

A pure substance's property chart has three regions. To the left of the dome: compressed (or subcooled) liquid — liquid below its saturation temperature at that pressure. Under the dome: the saturated mixture region, liquid and vapor coexisting at the saturation temperature for that pressure. To the right: superheated vapor — vapor above its saturation temperature, with no liquid present at all. The dome's peak is the critical point, where the distinction between liquid and vapor disappears entirely.

02 · Quality — reading a point inside the dome

Quality \(x\) is the vapor mass fraction, \(x=m_{\text{vapor}}/m_{\text{total}}\), and it is defined only inside the dome — it is meaningless anywhere else on the chart. \(x=0\) is saturated liquid, the dome's left edge; \(x=1\) is saturated vapor, its right edge. \(x=0.85\) means \(85\)% of the mass is saturated vapor and the remaining \(15\)% is still saturated liquid, both sitting at the same saturation temperature and pressure. Any property \(y\) (specific volume, internal energy, enthalpy, entropy) at that quality is a quality-weighted average between its saturated-liquid and saturated-vapor values, \( y=y_f+x(y_g-y_f) \) — the same lever-rule idea used for a two-phase metal alloy, applied here to a boiling substance instead.

03 · Superheat, subcooling, and why turbines want it

Superheat measures how far above the saturation temperature for its pressure the vapor actually sits; superheated vapor carries no liquid at all. Subcooling is the mirror case for a liquid below its saturation temperature. Turbines are supplied with superheated steam, not steam sitting inside the dome, because wet (two-phase) steam carries liquid droplets that strike the moving blades at high speed and erode them over years of service — keeping the steam superheated at inlet, and the expansion path through the turbine as dry as practical, protects the hardware.

04 · Steam-table fluency — the working method

Given any two independent intensive properties (the state postulate, Lesson 1), the method is always the same three steps. (1) Identify which two properties are known. (2) Locate the region: compare the known values against the saturation values at that pressure or temperature — between \(v_f\) and \(v_g\) places the state inside the dome; below \(v_f\), compressed liquid; above \(v_g\), superheated. (3) Read or interpolate the remaining properties from the matching table — the saturation table with quality if inside the dome, the superheated table directly otherwise. This three-step method, not memorized numbers, is the actual skill a steam table drill is building.

Key tool — quality-weighted average

\[ y = y_f + x\,(y_g - y_f) \]

Lesson 03 · Work and heat

01 · Boundary work — path-dependent, and why

Boundary (moving-boundary) work is \( W=\int P\,dV \) for a quasi-equilibrium process — the area under the process curve on a P–v diagram. Because different paths between the same two states can trace different curves, and therefore enclose different areas, work is path-dependent: it is not a property fixed by the state alone, only by the specific route taken to get there. This is the direct contrast with internal energy, which is fixed entirely by the state and does not care how that state was reached. Standard processes give simple results: isobaric (constant \(P\)), \(W=P(V_2-V_1)\); isochoric (constant \(V\)), \(W=0\); isothermal ideal gas, \(W=mRT\ln(V_2/V_1)\); polytropic (\(PV^n=\text{const}\)), \(W=\dfrac{P_2V_2-P_1V_1}{1-n}\) for \(n\neq1\).

02 · Heat — energy in transit, not a substance

Heat is energy crossing a boundary because of a temperature difference alone. A system does not "contain" heat the way it contains internal energy — heat, like work, is a path-dependent quantity describing how energy crosses the boundary, not a property of the state itself. An adiabatic process or boundary is one with \(Q=0\) — either perfectly insulated, or fast enough that meaningful heat transfer has no time to occur.

03 · Reading work from the diagram — the working method

The area between a process curve and the volume axis, bounded by the initial and final volumes, is the boundary work for that process. A curve running higher in pressure encloses more area for the same volume change, meaning more work. A cycle — a closed loop on the P–v diagram, returning to its starting state — has net work equal to the area enclosed by the loop; this is the exact idea Lesson 11's engine cycles build on.

04 · Working method for a work-and-heat problem

(1) Identify the process type — isobaric, isochoric, isothermal, polytropic, or a curve given directly — since that identification selects which formula or graphical area applies. (2) Compute or read off the boundary work. (3) If both work and an energy quantity are needed, combine with the first law (Lesson 4) to find whichever of heat or internal-energy change remains unknown. In a well-posed problem, work and heat are never both unknowns at once — one is always computable directly, and the first law supplies the other.

Key rule — boundary work

\[ W=\int P\,dV = \text{area under the process curve (P–v)}. \]

Lesson 04 · First law: closed systems

01 · The energy balance, stated once

For a closed system, \( \Delta U = Q - W \) (using the sign convention fixed in Lesson 1). When kinetic and potential energy changes are negligible — true for almost every closed-system problem in this course — this is the entire first law needed. Every problem in this lesson is a variation on filling in this one equation.

02 · Internal energy versus enthalpy — when each applies

Internal energy \(U\) is the natural variable at constant volume: a rigid tank does no boundary work (\(W=0\)), so \(Q=\Delta U\) directly — all the heat added raises internal energy. Enthalpy, \(H=U+PV\), is the natural variable at constant pressure: it can be shown that \(Q=\Delta H\) directly there, because the definition of \(H\) already absorbs the \(P\Delta V\) boundary-work term. This is exactly why enthalpy exists as a defined property — it is the energy accounting tool built for constant-pressure processes, the way \(U\) is built for constant-volume ones. For an ideal gas, both depend on temperature alone: \( \Delta U=mc_v\Delta T \), \( \Delta H=mc_p\Delta T \).

03 · Rigid tank versus piston-cylinder, heated equally

Add the same heat \(Q\) to the same gas from the same starting temperature, in two different confinements. In a rigid tank, all of \(Q\) raises internal energy: \( \Delta T=Q/(mc_v) \). In a piston-cylinder held at constant pressure, part of \(Q\) does boundary work pushing the piston, leaving less to raise temperature: \( \Delta T=Q/(mc_p) \). Because \(c_p>c_v\) always (\(c_p=c_v+R\) for an ideal gas), the same heat produces a smaller temperature rise at constant pressure. For \(1\ \text{kg}\) of air at \(300\ \text{K}\) receiving \(50\ \text{kJ}\) (\(c_v=0.718\), \(c_p=1.005\ \text{kJ/(kg·K)}\)): the rigid tank reaches \(369.6\ \text{K}\), the piston-cylinder reaches only \(349.8\ \text{K}\) — the rigid tank ends hotter, by exactly the factor \(c_p/c_v\approx1.4\).

04 · Working method for a first-law closed-system problem

(1) Identify the confinement: rigid means constant volume, use \(U\) and \(c_v\); a piston free against a constant external pressure means constant pressure, use \(H\) and \(c_p\); anything else, use the general \( \Delta U=Q-W \) with the boundary work computed separately, as in Lesson 3. (2) Write the energy balance with the fixed sign convention. (3) Substitute the ideal-gas relation for \(\Delta U\) or \(\Delta H\). (4) Solve for whichever single quantity — \(Q\), \(W\), or the temperature change — is unknown.

Key rule — U vs. H

Constant \(V\): \(Q=\Delta U=mc_v\Delta T\). Constant \(P\): \(Q=\Delta H=mc_p\Delta T\). This is the reason enthalpy is a defined property.

Lesson 05 · First law: control volumes

01 · The steady-flow energy equation, term by term

For a steady, single-inlet/single-outlet control volume, per unit mass: \[ q - w = (h_2-h_1) + \frac{V_2^2-V_1^2}{2} + g(z_2-z_1). \] Heat and work (\(q\), \(w\)) cross the boundary the same way they do in a closed system. Enthalpy \(h\), not internal energy alone, is the natural variable here because it automatically carries the flow work that mass brings with it as it crosses the boundary — extending the constant-pressure insight from Lesson 4 to a continuously flowing stream. \(V\) is velocity (the kinetic-energy term) and \(z\) is elevation (the potential-energy term). "Steady" means nothing inside the control volume changes with time — the same mass flow rate in as out, the same properties at every point, moment to moment; steady does not mean nothing is happening, only that nothing is changing.

02 · The five one-line devices

A nozzle or diffuser does no work and is usually adiabatic; it trades enthalpy for kinetic energy (nozzle) or the reverse (diffuser): \( \Delta h\approx-\Delta KE \). A turbine produces work, is usually adiabatic, and its KE/PE changes are typically negligible next to its enthalpy drop, so \( w\approx h_1-h_2 \) — the enthalpy drop across a real turbine is commonly hundreds of kJ/kg, while velocity changes contribute only a few kJ/kg, which is why KE is routinely dropped without changing the answer meaningfully. A compressor or pump consumes work with the same simplifications, energy flowing the other way: \( w\approx h_2-h_1 \). A throttle does no work, transfers negligible heat, and has negligible KE/PE change, leaving \(h_1=h_2\) exactly — an isenthalpic device. A heat exchanger does no work, has negligible KE/PE change, and reduces to \( q=h_2-h_1 \) for each stream.

03 · Reading a device — the working method

For any of these five: (1) start from the full SFEE; (2) cross out whatever the device's physical description rules out — no work means \(w=0\); adiabatic means \(q=0\); a large flow area and low speed mean \( \Delta KE\) is negligible; no elevation change means \( \Delta PE=0\); (3) whatever survives is the device's governing equation. This is not five separate formulas to memorize — it is one equation, specialized five different ways by exactly the same logic each time.

04 · Why this equation dominates the next four years

Pumps, compressors, turbines, heat exchangers, control valves, HVAC coils — nearly every piece of process, rotating, or thermal equipment a mechanical engineer touches reduces to some simplified form of this one balance. Fast, confident simplification, the three steps of 3, is the single most repeatedly used skill this course builds, and it is used weekly for the rest of a working career in this field.

Key result — the throttle

No work, negligible heat, negligible ΔKE/ΔPE ⇒ \( h_1=h_2 \). The sharpest example of "cross out what does not apply."

Lesson 06 · Transient flow processes

01 · Why steady-flow breaks down

The SFEE of Lesson 5 assumes nothing inside the control volume changes with time. Charging or discharging a tank violates that directly: the mass and stored energy inside change continuously as it fills or empties. The standard fix is the uniform-state, uniform-flow (USUF) model — the properties of fluid crossing the boundary are taken as uniform throughout the process, and the state inside the control volume is uniform at any given instant, even though it changes from instant to instant. This keeps the mass and energy balances tractable while still capturing the transient honestly.

02 · The transient energy balance

Mass balance: \( m_2-m_1=m_{in}-m_{out} \) — the final mass inside minus the initial mass equals the net mass that crossed the boundary. Energy balance: \( m_2u_2-m_1u_1=Q-W+m_{in}h_{in}-m_{out}h_{out} \). Mass flowing in or out carries enthalpy — it is moving, so it carries flow work exactly as in Lesson 5 — while mass already inside, or accumulating inside, is accounted with internal energy, since it is not flowing anywhere and so carries no flow-work term.

03 · Charging an evacuated, insulated tank

The sharpest special case: an evacuated tank (\(m_1=0\)), perfectly insulated (\(Q=0\)), rigid (\(W=0\)), charged from a supply line at constant state \(h_{in}\). The balances reduce to \(m_2=m_{in}\) and \( m_2u_2=m_{in}h_{in} \), so simply \( u_2=h_{in} \). For an ideal gas, \(u=c_vT\) and \(h=c_pT\), giving \( c_vT_2=c_pT_{in}\Rightarrow T_2=(c_p/c_v)T_{in}=kT_{in} \). For air (\(k\approx1.4\)) charged from a \(300\ \text{K}\) (\(27^\circ\)C) line, the tank ends at \(420\ \text{K}\) (\(147^\circ\)C) — nearly \(120^\circ\)C hotter than the line it was filled from, with no heater anywhere in the picture. The entering gas carries flow work in addition to its internal energy; once inside the tank, that flow work has nowhere to go except into raising the internal energy — the temperature — of the gas now sitting still. This is a real, measurable effect: rapid receiver filling genuinely runs hotter than the supply, occasionally hot enough to matter for downstream components rated to the supply-line temperature alone.

04 · Where steady-flow honestly fails — the working method

Ask: does anything inside the control volume's boundary change measurably over the timescale being studied? A pump or turbine running continuously at a fixed load: no — steady-flow is safe. A tank filling or draining, a startup or shutdown, a batch step: yes — the transient (USUF) balance is required instead. A slow transient can often be treated as a sequence of steady states ("quasi-steady"), but a genuinely fast charge or discharge, like this lesson's tank, needs the full transient treatment to avoid a real, sometimes large, error.

Key result — charging an evacuated tank

\[ T_2 = \frac{c_p}{c_v}\,T_{line} = k\,T_{line}\qquad(\text{insulated, rigid, initially evacuated}). \]

Lesson 07 · Second law and machines

01 · Two statements, one idea

The Kelvin-Planck statement says no heat engine can convert heat from a single reservoir completely into work while operating in a cycle — some heat must always be rejected to a colder reservoir. The Clausius statement says heat cannot spontaneously flow from a colder body to a hotter one without work being supplied from outside. These read as two different rules, but they are equivalent ways of stating the same underlying limit — the second law — a limit the first law, energy conservation alone, never imposes on its own.

02 · Thermal efficiency and COP, defined honestly

A heat engine's thermal efficiency is \( \eta=W_{net}/Q_H \) — useful work out over heat supplied — always below 100%, by Kelvin-Planck. A refrigerator's coefficient of performance is \( COP_R=Q_C/W_{net} \) — heat removed from the cold space over work input — and, unlike an efficiency, it can exceed 1. A heat pump measures the same cycle differently: \( COP_{HP}=Q_H/W_{net} \), heat delivered to the warm space over work input. Because the same device satisfies \(Q_H=Q_C+W\) by energy conservation, the two coefficients are always related by \( COP_{HP}=COP_R+1 \).

03 · The Carnot limit — a ceiling no vendor brochure escapes

The Carnot efficiency, \( \eta_{Carnot}=1-T_C/T_H \) (both absolute temperatures), is the maximum efficiency any heat engine — reversible or not — can achieve operating between two fixed reservoir temperatures. As \(T_H\) rises with \(T_C\) fixed, efficiency climbs toward 1 but never reaches it for any finite \(T_H\). Real engines fall short of this ceiling because of irreversibilities — friction, unrestrained expansion, heat transfer across a finite temperature difference — so Carnot efficiency is a theoretical benchmark for judging how much room for improvement remains, not a target any real machine actually reaches.

04 · Refrigerators and heat pumps as reversed engines

A refrigeration cycle is a heat engine run in reverse: instead of heat flowing hot to cold while producing work, work is supplied to force heat from cold to hot. The same Carnot logic applies backward: \( COP_{Carnot,R}=T_C/(T_H-T_C) \) is the best possible COP between two reservoirs, and real machines fall short of it exactly as real engines fall short of Carnot efficiency. The working method for any of these three devices: (1) identify which one it is; (2) compute its defining ratio from the actual heat and work values given; (3) if a theoretical-limit comparison is asked for, compute the Carnot value separately from the absolute reservoir temperatures, and compare the two.

Key ceiling — Carnot efficiency

\[ \eta_{Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}\qquad(\text{absolute temperatures}). \]

Lesson 08 · Entropy

01 · Entropy as a property

Entropy \(S\) (specific \(s\)) is a property — fixed by the state, like \(U\), \(H\), and \(v\), in direct contrast to heat and work from Lesson 3, which depend on path. For an internally reversible process, \( dS=\delta Q/T \) defines entropy operationally; for a real, irreversible process, that relation does not directly give \( \Delta S\) — entropy is instead evaluated as a state property from tables, exactly like any other property, and the actual heat transferred is accounted separately through entropy generation, developed next.

02 · The entropy increase principle

For an isolated system — nothing crosses its boundary — total entropy can only increase, or in the ideal reversible limit stay the same; it can never decrease: \( \Delta S_{isolated}\geq0 \). This is the second law in its most general, quantitative form, and both statements from Lesson 7 follow from it: it is what rules out spontaneous heat flow from cold to hot, and it is what caps engine efficiency at the Carnot value.

03 · Entropy generation — the bookkeeping of irreversibility

For any process, not only isolated ones, the full accounting reads \( \Delta S_{system}=\displaystyle\int\frac{\delta Q}{T_{boundary}}+S_{gen} \), where \( S_{gen}\geq0 \) is entropy generated by irreversibilities inside the process — friction, unrestrained expansion, mixing, heat transfer across a finite temperature difference. \(S_{gen}=0\) only for a reversible process; every real process has \(S_{gen}>0\). This is the key distinction: entropy change can be positive, negative, or zero depending on the direction of heat transfer, even for a perfectly reversible process — but entropy generation is always non-negative, and it is the actual measure of how irreversible a real process was.

04 · T–s diagrams and the isentropic reference

An isentropic process — constant entropy, a vertical line on a T–s diagram — is simultaneously reversible and adiabatic, the theoretical best case for a device like a turbine or compressor with no heat loss. Real devices deviate to the right of that line, ending at higher entropy for the same starting point and pressure change, because real irreversibilities generate entropy along the way. This is exactly why isentropic processes serve as the reference for real-machine performance: comparing a real machine's actual outcome to the isentropic ideal for the same pressure change — the subject of Lesson 9 — measures how much irreversibility actually occurred inside it.

Key equation — entropy generation

\[ \Delta S_{system} = \int\frac{\delta Q}{T} + S_{gen},\qquad S_{gen}\geq0\ (=0\text{ only if reversible}). \]

Lesson 09 · Isentropic efficiency

01 · Defining isentropic efficiency against the ideal

For a turbine, isentropic efficiency is \( \eta_T=w_{actual}/w_{isentropic} \) — actual work out over the ideal work out for the same pressure drop — always \(\leq1\), since the isentropic case is the best possible outcome, generating no entropy. For a compressor or pump, the ratio flips: \( \eta_C=w_{isentropic}/w_{actual} \) — ideal work in over actual work in — because for a work-consuming device, the ideal case needs the least work, so closeness to ideal is measured the other way around; this ratio, too, is always \(\leq1\), since a real compressor never needs less work than the isentropic minimum.

02 · Where the "lost" work goes

For a compressor at \(\eta_C=80\)%, with an isentropic requirement of \(150\ \text{kJ/kg}\): \( w_{actual}=150/0.80=187.5\ \text{kJ/kg} \) — \(37.5\ \text{kJ/kg}\) more than the ideal minimum. That extra energy does not vanish: by the first law, it appears as extra enthalpy rise compared to the isentropic case, meaning the actual gas leaves hotter than the ideal case would predict. The "lost" work becomes waste heat carried in the gas stream rather than useful compression — the same story as Lesson 8's entropy generation, now expressed directly in energy terms.

03 · Locating real versus ideal on the h–s diagram

The isentropic path is a vertical line — constant entropy — from the inlet state to the ideal exit enthalpy \(h_{2s}\). The actual path ends at \(h_2\), at higher entropy and, for a compressor, higher enthalpy than the isentropic point. Read directly off the diagram: \(h_2-h_1\) is the actual work in (compressor) or, for a turbine, \(h_1-h_2\) is the actual work out — always smaller than the isentropic \(h_1-h_{2s}\) — while \(h_{2s}-h_1\) is the isentropic ideal in either case.

04 · Why this number matters — the working method

Isentropic efficiency is the single number that separates a healthy machine from a worn one. Blade fouling, seal wear, erosion, and bearing problems all degrade it over time, and tracking measured isentropic efficiency against a machine's as-new baseline is standard condition-monitoring practice, not just a design-stage calculation. The working method: (1) find the ideal (isentropic) work from the inlet state and the actual pressure ratio; (2) find the actual work from measured inlet/outlet conditions; (3) take the appropriate ratio — actual over ideal for a turbine, ideal over actual for a compressor; (4) compare the result against the machine's rated or as-new value to judge its current health.

Key ratios — isentropic efficiency

Turbine: \( \eta_T=w_{actual}/w_{isentropic} \). Compressor/pump: \( \eta_C=w_{isentropic}/w_{actual} \) — flipped, because one produces work and the other consumes it.

Lesson 10 · Exergy first contact

01 · Available work and the dead state

Not all energy is equally useful. Exergy (or availability) is the maximum theoretical work extractable from a system as it comes into equilibrium with a defined reference environment, the dead state. Exergy only means something relative to that reference: "useful work" is measured against what the system would otherwise settle into on its own, so without a defined dead state there is no zero point to measure available work from. A stream far above the dead-state temperature has high exergy — an ideal engine could extract substantial work as it cools toward equilibrium. A stream only slightly above the dead-state temperature has low exergy even carrying substantial raw energy — low-grade heat. Exergy, unlike energy, discriminates by quality, not just quantity.

02 · Exergy destruction — the cost of irreversibility

Energy is conserved by the first law — never destroyed, only converted or transferred. Exergy is destroyed by irreversibility, a direct consequence of Lesson 8's entropy generation: \( X_{destroyed}=T_0\,S_{gen} \), where \(T_0\) is the dead-state temperature. Every real, irreversible process destroys some exergy permanently, even while its energy balance still closes perfectly. Energy accounting alone can make an inefficient process look fine — energy in always equals energy out — while exergy accounting reveals exactly where, and how much, true usefulness was actually thrown away.

03 · Why utilities audits speak this language

A boiler running at 85% energy efficiency sounds strong, but an exergy analysis of that same boiler often shows the large majority of the fuel's exergy is destroyed in the combustion process itself — an inherently irreversible, high-temperature-difference reaction — long before any heat-transfer inefficiency ever registers. Exergy accounting is what lets a utilities audit rank where in a plant the real thermodynamic losses concentrate, often combustion and large-temperature-difference heat exchange rather than the spots an energy-only audit would flag, directing capital toward the improvements that actually matter most.

04 · A preview, not the full method

This lesson introduces only the vocabulary — dead state, exergy, exergy destruction — and the core distinction from energy. The full exergy balance, second-law efficiency, and a complete plant exergy audit belong to Thermo II. The working method here: whenever a question asks where the real loss actually happened, not simply where the energy went, that is an exergy question, and its size traces directly back to entropy generation.

Key result — exergy destruction

\[ X_{destroyed} = T_0\,S_{gen}. \]

Energy is conserved. Exergy is destroyed by irreversibility.

Lesson 11 · Gas power cycle sketches

01 · Three idealized cycles

The Otto cycle models a spark-ignition engine: \(1\to2\) isentropic compression, \(2\to3\) heat addition at constant volume (idealized combustion), \(3\to4\) isentropic expansion (the power stroke), \(4\to1\) heat rejection at constant volume (idealized exhaust and intake). The Diesel cycle is identical except heat addition (\(2\to3\)) happens at constant pressure instead, modeling the slower, more gradual combustion as fuel is injected. The Brayton cycle models a gas turbine as an open, continuous-flow analog: \(1\to2\) isentropic compression (the compressor), \(2\to3\) heat addition at constant pressure (the combustor), \(3\to4\) isentropic expansion (the turbine), \(4\to1\) heat rejection at constant pressure, or simply exhaust to atmosphere in a true open cycle.

02 · Reading the Otto cycle P–v loop

The loop's enclosed area is the net work per cycle — Lesson 3's area-under-a-curve idea, now closed into a full loop. The compression ratio \(r=V_1/V_2\) is the single largest lever on ideal efficiency: \[ \eta_{Otto}=1-\frac{1}{r^{\gamma-1}}. \] A higher compression ratio gives higher ideal efficiency; real engines are held well below the ideal-efficiency-maximizing ratio by knock and detonation limits, a practical constraint outside this lesson's scope.

03 · Mean effective pressure and back-work ratio

Mean effective pressure (MEP) is the single constant pressure that, acting over the same swept volume, would produce the same net work as the actual cycle: \( \text{MEP}=W_{net}/(V_1-V_2) \) — a compact way to compare engines of different sizes on how hard they work per unit of displacement, independent of engine size. Back-work ratio (\(BWR=w_{compressor}/w_{turbine}\)) matters specifically for the Brayton cycle: it is the fraction of the turbine's own gross output consumed just running the compressor before any net useful work is left over. A gas turbine's back-work ratio typically runs \(40\)–\(60\)%, far higher than a comparable steam cycle's pump back-work ratio (often under \(2\)%), because compressing a gas costs far more work than pumping a nearly incompressible liquid to the same pressure ratio — this is exactly why compressor efficiency matters disproportionately for a gas turbine's already-thin net output, while a steam cycle's pump efficiency barely moves the needle.

04 · Cycle literacy — the working method

For any of the three cycles: (1) identify the four processes and which are isentropic versus constant-property; (2) apply the isentropic and constant-property relations already built in Lessons 3–4 to find each state's properties; (3) sum heat and work across the four legs for net work and thermal efficiency; (4) for Brayton specifically, always check the back-work ratio, since it dominates net output far more than in a piston cycle. Full cycle analysis with real component efficiencies, actual combustion, and multi-stage compression or expansion is Thermo II's job — this lesson exists so a mechanical engineer can hold an informed conversation about gas turbines and piston engines, not yet design one.

Key ratio — back-work ratio

\[ BWR = \frac{w_{compressor}}{w_{turbine}}. \]

Much higher for a gas turbine than a steam cycle's pump — why compressor efficiency matters so much more there.

ELE 156 — Electrical Fundamentals for Mechanical Engineers

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Charge, current, voltage, and power

01 · The quantities, briefly through water, then on their own

Charge (\(Q\), coulombs) is the fundamental quantity; current (\(I\), amperes) is its rate of flow, \(I=dQ/dt\). Voltage (\(V\), volts) is the electrical potential difference that drives current between two points. A water analogy — charge as water volume, current as flow rate, voltage as pressure difference — gives a useful first mental picture, but it breaks down quickly for AC, for reactive elements, and for anything beyond the simplest DC circuit. It is used once, here, and retired; from this point the course works directly with the real electrical quantities.

02 · Ohm's law and power dissipation

Ohm's law, \(V=IR\), relates voltage across a resistive element to the current through it, with resistance \(R\) (ohms) as the constant of proportionality. Power dissipated in a resistance can be written three equivalent ways — \( P=VI=I^2R=V^2/R \) — pick whichever form uses the two quantities already known. The \(I^2R\) form matters most for a loose or corroded connection: it is simply an extra resistance in series with the rest of the circuit, carrying the same current as everything else on that path. Because power there scales with the square of current, doubling the load current quadruples the heat generated at that one weak point, not merely doubles it — the direct reason a connection that runs only warm at light load can become a genuine fire risk once demand rises, and why torque-checking terminal connections is real maintenance, not a formality.

03 · Reading a multimeter without folklore

A meter set to measure voltage is connected in parallel across the two points of interest and must present very high input impedance, ideally infinite, so it draws negligible current and does not disturb the circuit or load down a sensitive source. A meter set to measure current is connected in series, breaking the circuit to insert itself, and must present very low input impedance, ideally zero, so it adds no meaningful resistance to the path. The classic, entirely avoidable mistake is leaving a meter in current mode — low internal impedance — and then connecting it in parallel across a voltage source: this creates a near short-circuit through the meter's low internal resistance, drawing a large current that can blow its fuse or damage it outright. Input impedance, matched to the mode setting, is exactly what protects against this in both directions.

04 · Working method for a basic circuit reading

(1) Identify what is actually being asked — voltage, a difference between two points measured in parallel, or current, a flow through one path measured in series. (2) Set the meter to the matching mode, which sets its internal impedance to match. (3) Apply \(V=IR\), or one of its power forms, using whichever two of \(V\), \(I\), \(R\), \(P\) are already known to find what is missing. This three-step sequence, more than any single formula, is the actual skill this lesson builds.

Key relation — power dissipation

\[ P = I^2R \]

Quadratic in current — the reason a loose connection is genuinely dangerous, not just inefficient.

Lesson 02 · DC circuit analysis

01 · Series-parallel reduction

Series resistors carry the same current, and their voltages add: \( R_{series}=R_1+R_2+\dots \) — always larger than the largest individual resistor. Parallel resistors share the same voltage, and their currents add: \( 1/R_{parallel}=1/R_1+1/R_2+\dots \) — always smaller than the smallest individual resistor, because every added parallel branch gives current an additional path, so total resistance to current flow can only fall, never rise, as a branch is added. The working method for any network: redraw it repeatedly, combining series and parallel groups from whichever end is simplest, until a single equivalent resistance remains.

02 · Kirchhoff's laws

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL): the sum of currents into any node equals the sum of currents out — charge does not accumulate at a node, so whatever flows in must flow out. Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL): the sum of voltage rises and drops around any closed loop equals zero. Physically, KVL conserves energy: starting and returning to the same point means the net potential change must be zero regardless of the path taken around the loop — the same underlying idea that makes a state property path-independent in an entirely different course's energy balances.

03 · Voltage and current dividers

A voltage divider (series resistors) gives the voltage across any one resistor as the source voltage times that resistor's share of the total series resistance: \( V_{R2}=V_{source}\times\dfrac{R_2}{R_1+R_2} \). A current divider (two parallel resistors) gives the current through one branch as \( I_{R1}=I_{total}\times\dfrac{R_2}{R_1+R_2} \) — note the other resistor appears in the numerator, since more current always takes the path of less resistance, so the fraction going through \(R_1\) depends on \(R_2\)'s size, not its own.

04 · Practical sources and battery-fed control circuits

A real (non-ideal) voltage source carries an internal resistance \(r_{int}\) in series with an ideal source, so its terminal voltage sags under load: \( V_{terminal}=V_{source}-I\,r_{int} \). A battery reads its full open-circuit voltage with nothing connected, but sags noticeably once a real load — a starter motor, a control relay coil — draws current. A weak or aged battery, with a higher \(r_{int}\), can show an entirely acceptable open-circuit voltage yet still fail to crank a real load, because the sag under actual current draw, not the resting voltage, is what determines whether the load actually runs.

Key rule — series and parallel

Series result is always larger than any part. Parallel result is always smaller than any part. Together, this is the entire reduction method.

Lesson 03 · Capacitance and inductance

01 · Energy storage in fields

A capacitor stores energy in an electric field between two conductive plates; its current-voltage relation is \( I=C\,dV/dt \) — current depends on how fast voltage is changing, not on the voltage itself, so a capacitor passes no current once fully charged at steady DC (\(dV/dt=0\)). An inductor stores energy in a magnetic field built by current flowing through a coil; its relation is \( V=L\,dI/dt \) — voltage depends on how fast current is changing, so an inductor looks like a plain wire, zero voltage drop, once its current is no longer changing at steady DC.

02 · First-order transients and time constants

An RC circuit's voltage or current approaches its final value exponentially with time constant \( \tau=RC \); an RL circuit's current does the same with \( \tau=L/R \). After one \(\tau\), a transient is roughly 63% complete; after about five \(\tau\), it is essentially settled. For a contactor coil specifically, \( \tau=L/R \) sets how quickly coil current — and therefore magnetic pull-in force — builds after the coil is energized; a coil with a larger \(\tau\) relative to its resistance takes measurably longer to pull in, a detail that matters for control-timing and for diagnosing a sluggish contactor.

03 · Why inductive loads arc switches

An inductor resists sudden changes in current: \(V=L\,dI/dt\) means a very fast \(di/dt\) implies, in principle, an unboundedly large voltage. Opening a switch or contact carrying current through an inductive load — a motor coil, a contactor coil, a solenoid — tries to force current from some value to zero almost instantly. The inductor responds by generating a large voltage spike across the opening contact gap, large enough to ionize the air and draw an arc, which continues carrying current — and eroding the contact surface — until the stored magnetic energy is fully dissipated. Suppression — a flyback diode across a DC coil, or an RC snubber across an AC contact — gives that current a controlled alternate path to decay through smoothly instead of being forced to an abrupt stop, keeping \(di/dt\), and the induced voltage, bounded.

04 · Working method

(1) Identify whether an element stores energy in an electric field (capacitor, current depends on \(dV/dt\)) or a magnetic field (inductor, voltage depends on \(dI/dt\)). (2) For a transient, identify \(R\) and either \(C\) or \(L\) to compute the time constant. (3) For a switching or interruption problem, recognize that any element carrying current through an inductance needs a path for that current to decay smoothly — or expect an arc.

Key relation — the inductive arc

\[ V = L\,\frac{dI}{dt} \]

Forcing di/dt large forces V large — the reason interrupting an inductive circuit arcs, and why suppression works.

Lesson 04 · AC fundamentals and phasors

01 · Sinusoids and RMS values

An AC quantity \(v(t)=V_{max}\sin(\omega t)\) constantly changes — its peak value alone overstates its actual heating or working effect, while its average over a full cycle is exactly zero. RMS (root-mean-square) is defined so that an RMS AC value produces the same heating effect — the same average power in a resistor — as an equal DC value. For a pure sinusoid, \( V_{rms}=V_{max}/\sqrt2\approx0.707\,V_{max} \). This is why "230 V AC" is an RMS rating, not a peak — the honest, working-equivalent number, with the actual peak running about \(\sqrt2\) times higher, near 325 V.

02 · Phasor representation

A phasor is a rotating-vector shorthand for a sinusoid at one fixed frequency: instead of tracking \(v(t)\) through time, a phasor captures a sinusoid's magnitude and phase angle as a single vector or complex number. This turns the calculus of sinusoids — derivatives and integrals — into simple vector or complex algebra, a simplification that works because every quantity in a linear AC circuit at one frequency is itself a sinusoid at that same frequency, differing only in magnitude and phase.

03 · Impedance of R, L, C

Impedance \(Z\) generalizes resistance to AC. A resistor's impedance is simply \(Z_R=R\), no phase shift. An inductor's impedance, \(Z_L=j\omega L\), grows with frequency and shifts voltage \(90^\circ\) ahead of current. A capacitor's impedance, \(Z_C=1/(j\omega C)\), shrinks with frequency and shifts the opposite way — current ahead of voltage. Impedance adds to resistance exactly the concept resistance alone in a DC circuit never needed: a phase relationship between voltage and current, not just a magnitude ratio. Series impedances combine the same way resistances do, \(Z_{total}=Z_1+Z_2+\dots\), but as complex numbers, so magnitudes generally do not simply add unless the phase angles happen to match.

04 · The 50 Hz world, quantified

At \(f=50\ \text{Hz}\) (or 60 Hz in some countries), \( \omega=2\pi f\approx314\ \text{rad/s} \) — this single number turns \(L\) (henries) and \(C\) (farads) into actual ohms of impedance at mains frequency: \(Z_L=\omega L\), \(Z_C=1/(\omega C)\). Working method: (1) convert every element to its impedance at the operating frequency; (2) combine impedances exactly as resistances combine — series adds, parallel via reciprocals — but with complex arithmetic; (3) apply the AC form of Ohm's law, \(V=IZ\), using RMS magnitudes and phase angles as needed.

Key rating — RMS

\[ V_{rms} = \frac{V_{max}}{\sqrt2} \]

The honest, heating-equivalent rating for a sinusoid — not the peak.

Lesson 05 · AC power and power factor

01 · Real, reactive, and apparent power

Real power \(P\) (watts, kW) is what is actually converted to useful work or heat — a motor's shaft output, a heater's element. Reactive power \(Q\) (VAR, kVAR) oscillates back and forth between the source and any inductive or capacitive element, doing no net useful work but still requiring current to flow to sustain the magnetic or electric field involved. Apparent power \(S\) (VA, kVA) is simply \(S=VI\) using RMS values — what the supply, the wiring, the transformer, the generator, must actually be sized to carry, since it must carry the current whether or not that current does useful work. The three relate as a right triangle, \(S^2=P^2+Q^2\), with the angle \(\varphi\) between \(P\) and \(S\) equal to the angle by which current lags or leads voltage.

02 · Power factor — why utilities bill for it

Power factor, \( PF=P/S=\cos\varphi \), is the fraction of apparent power actually doing useful work. A plant at \(PF=0.7\) draws \(S=P/0.7\approx1.43P\) in apparent power for the same real power — the wiring, transformers, and generation capacity serving that plant must all be sized roughly 43% larger than the real power alone would require, purely to carry reactive current that does no useful work. Utilities bill for poor power factor, directly through a penalty or indirectly through a demand/kVA charge, because that extra capacity is real infrastructure cost they bear whether or not it ever converts to useful output for the customer.

03 · Correction capacitors, sized

Most industrial reactive power is inductive — motors, transformers, ballasts — a lagging power factor. A capacitor bank connected locally supplies leading reactive power that cancels some or all of the inductive reactive power the load demands from the utility; the load still draws its full original reactive current, but in a short local loop with the capacitor rather than all the way back through the utility's own wiring and transformers. The reactive power the utility actually sees becomes \( Q_{new}=Q_{old}-Q_{capacitor} \); sizing a correction bank means computing the reactive-power reduction needed to reach a target power factor and selecting a capacitor rated for that many kVAR.

04 · Working method

(1) Find \(P\), the real power, from the load's actual work output or a direct measurement. (2) Find \(Q\) or \(S\) from measurement, or from the load's known power factor. (3) Apply \(S^2=P^2+Q^2\) for whichever of the three is still missing. (4) If correction is needed, compute the target \(Q\) at the desired power factor, and the capacitor kVAR required to close the gap between the actual \(Q\) and that target.

Key relations — power triangle

\[ S^2=P^2+Q^2,\qquad PF=\frac{P}{S}=\cos\varphi. \]

Lesson 06 · Three-phase systems

01 · Wye and delta connections

A wye (star) connection joins three windings or loads at a common neutral point, with each winding's other end brought out as a line — this gives access to both line-to-line and line-to-neutral voltages. A delta connection joins the three windings end-to-end in a closed loop, with each junction brought out as a line — there is no neutral point, and only line-to-line voltage exists. Line quantities, measured on the supply conductors, and phase quantities, measured across one winding or load element, are not automatically the same — the relationship between them depends entirely on which connection is used.

02 · Line vs. phase relationships

In a wye connection: \( V_{line}=\sqrt3\,V_{phase} \) — line voltage is about 1.732 times the phase voltage — while \( I_{line}=I_{phase} \), since line and winding share the same series path. The \(\sqrt3\) factor comes directly from the \(120^\circ\) phasor geometry: the vector difference between two phase voltages \(120^\circ\) apart has magnitude \(\sqrt3\) times either one's magnitude, not simply double. In a delta connection, it reverses: \( V_{line}=V_{phase} \), since each line connects directly across one winding, while \( I_{line}=\sqrt3\,I_{phase} \), since each line current is now the phasor difference of two phase currents.

03 · Three-phase power — constant delivery

Three-phase real power is given compactly by \( P=\sqrt3\,V_{line}I_{line}\,PF \), using line quantities directly regardless of whether the system is wye or delta — exactly why this is the practical field formula. A single-phase voltage and current both cross zero twice per cycle, so their instantaneous product — power — also dips to zero twice per cycle: single-phase power delivery pulses. With three phases spaced \(120^\circ\) apart, each phase's instantaneous power still pulses individually, but the three pulses stagger exactly enough that their sum is mathematically constant for a balanced load — the real reason three-phase motors run smoother than single-phase ones, with no torque pulsation traceable to the electrical supply itself.

04 · Why industrial plants are three-phase places

Three-phase delivers more power per conductor weight than an equivalent single-phase system, provides constant, non-pulsating power delivery to motors — smoother torque, less vibration, less mechanical stress — and is what allows a simple, cheap rotating magnetic field, the subject of Lesson 8, to be generated directly from the supply with no extra starting circuitry. Working method: (1) determine whether the connection is wye or delta; (2) convert between line and phase quantities using \(\sqrt3\) in the correct direction for that connection; (3) apply \(P=\sqrt3\,V_{line}I_{line}\,PF\) for total three-phase real power.

Key ratios — wye vs. delta

Wye: \( V_{line}=\sqrt3\,V_{phase} \), \( I_{line}=I_{phase} \). Delta: \( V_{line}=V_{phase} \), \( I_{line}=\sqrt3\,I_{phase} \).

Lesson 07 · Transformers

01 · Ideal transformer relations

A transformer transfers energy between two circuits through a shared magnetic core, with no direct electrical connection between primary and secondary windings. The turns ratio \(a=N_1/N_2\) sets the voltage transformation directly: \(V_1/V_2=N_1/N_2=a\). Current transforms the opposite way: \(I_1/I_2=N_2/N_1=1/a\) — more secondary turns means lower secondary current for the same power, and vice versa. What stays nearly constant is apparent power: \(S_1\approx S_2\) for an ideal device, since turns change the voltage-current split, not the total VA transferred.

02 · Real losses

Real transformers carry two loss types. Core losses — hysteresis and eddy-current losses in the iron — are roughly constant whenever the transformer is energized, independent of load. Copper losses — \(I^2R\) losses in the winding resistance — grow with the square of load current, and are zero at no load. Because one loss is roughly load-independent and the other grows with the square of load, peak efficiency occurs somewhere between no-load and full-load, where the two loss types are roughly balanced — not necessarily at full rated load, a common misreading of a nameplate.

03 · Nameplate data decoded

Two numbers matter first. The kVA rating is the transformer's apparent-power capacity — it sets the maximum current the unit can safely carry at rated voltage, tying directly to Lesson 5's apparent power. The voltage ratio (e.g. "11000/400 V") states what it is built to step between. Beyond those two: impedance percentage (\(\%Z\)) sets both voltage regulation under load and, critically, the fault current magnitude available if the secondary is bolted-shorted — a low \(\%Z\) transformer can deliver very high fault current, a real protection-coordination consideration. Vector group states the phase relationship between primary and secondary, which matters when paralleling transformers or matching downstream equipment.

04 · Distribution transformers in a plant one-line diagram

A one-line diagram shows a transformer as a simple symbol carrying its kVA and voltage-ratio label. Reading it: (1) identify the voltage transformation — what feeds in, what comes out; (2) check the kVA rating against the actual connected or expected load for real capacity headroom; (3) note \(\%Z\) for protective-device coordination downstream. This reading skill, not detailed transformer design, is what a mechanical engineer working around plant power systems actually needs.

Key relations — the ideal transformer

\[ \frac{V_1}{V_2}=\frac{N_1}{N_2},\qquad \frac{I_1}{I_2}=\frac{N_2}{N_1},\qquad S_1\approx S_2. \]

Lesson 08 · The induction motor

01 · The rotating field and slip

Three-phase stator windings, fed by currents \(120^\circ\) apart in time and arranged \(120^\circ\) apart in space (Lesson 6's phasor geometry), produce a magnetic field that physically rotates at synchronous speed \( N_s=120f/p \) (\(f\)=supply frequency, \(p\)=number of poles) — no moving parts required to create the rotation itself. The rotor is dragged along by induction — hence the name — but can never actually reach synchronous speed: doing so would eliminate relative motion between rotor and field, eliminating induced rotor current and therefore torque. Slip, \( s=(N_s-N)/N_s \), is always strictly greater than zero for a loaded induction motor.

02 · The torque-speed curve — reading it

Starting torque, at \(s=1\) (standstill), is moderate. As the rotor speeds up, torque rises to a peak — the breakdown (or pullout) torque — at some intermediate slip, then falls steeply as slip continues to fall toward zero. The motor's normal rated operating point sits at small slip, often \(2\)–\(5\)%, well past the breakdown-torque peak, on the steep falling section of the curve — exactly where a small speed change produces a proportionally larger torque change, giving the machine its natural, stable, self-correcting load-following behavior. If load torque exceeds breakdown torque, the motor stalls outright — a hard physical limit, not a gradual slowdown.

03 · Nameplate literacy

Power (kW or hp) is the rated mechanical shaft output, not electrical input, which runs somewhat higher by the efficiency. Current is the rated full-load current at rated voltage, frequency, and load — the number a thermal overload is sized against. Service factor (e.g. 1.15) is a margin above nameplate power the motor can sustain continuously without exceeding its insulation temperature limits, under the rated conditions — not a routine overload allowance, since sustained operation there runs the insulation closer to its ceiling and shortens expected life compared to running at the 100% nameplate rating. Insulation class (B, F, H) states the maximum continuous winding temperature the insulation is built to withstand — the real thermal ceiling that service factor and ambient-temperature derating both have to respect.

04 · Working method — the machine that drives nearly everything

(1) Read or compute slip and synchronous speed from \( N_s=120f/p \) and the nameplate's rated speed. (2) Locate the machine's normal operating point on the torque-speed curve — small slip, steep region, well below breakdown torque, for margin against load spikes. (3) Read power, current, service factor, and insulation class together as one coherent thermal and mechanical rating, not four unrelated numbers.

Key definition — slip

\[ s=\frac{N_s-N}{N_s} > 0\quad\text{always, for a loaded induction motor.} \]

Lesson 09 · Synchronous machines and standby generation

01 · The synchronous machine, from the other side

A synchronous machine's rotor carries a magnetic field — usually an electromagnet fed by DC excitation current through slip rings or a brushless exciter. Unlike an induction motor's rotor, dragged along behind the field by induced current, a synchronous machine's rotor field locks directly onto the stator's rotating field and turns with it, at exactly synchronous speed, with no slip at all. As a motor, it locks its rotor to the rotating stator field and turns at fixed synchronous speed regardless of load, up to its torque limit — beyond that limit it simply loses synchronism and stalls, rather than slowing gradually the way an induction motor does. As a generator (alternator), the same machine is spun by an external prime mover — a diesel engine, a steam or gas turbine — at synchronous speed, and its rotating DC-excited field induces AC voltage in the stationary stator windings. This is how virtually all utility and standby generation actually works.

02 · Standby diesel generator sets

A genset's kW/kVA rating reflects both the engine's mechanical power and the alternator's electrical capacity, sized to the actual critical load plus margin for motor-starting inrush — a large motor's starting current can run 5-7 times its running current, and the genset must supply that surge without its own voltage or frequency collapsing. Load acceptance describes how much load the set can accept in one step without stalling the engine or causing an unacceptable voltage or frequency dip — a genset sized only on steady-state kW can still fail in practice if it cannot accept the actual step-load a real outage throws at it. The transfer sequence: an automatic transfer switch detects utility failure, starts the genset, waits for stable voltage and frequency, then transfers the critical load — and reverses the process automatically once utility power returns and proves stable.

03 · Paralleling conditions

Before closing a breaker to connect a generator onto a live bus — paralleling with the utility, or with other generators — three quantities must match: voltage magnitude, frequency, and phase angle. Closing the breaker with any of these mismatched drives a large, potentially damaging circulating current between the generator and the bus, from the instantaneous voltage difference across the closing contacts — severe enough to damage the generator, the bus, or both. Automatic synchronizing relays exist specifically to check and hold off closure until all three genuinely match, rather than trusting a manual judgment call.

04 · Working method

(1) Identify whether the machine is acting as a motor, locked to the supply at fixed speed, or a generator, driven by a prime mover and creating the supply. (2) For standby power, size the genset against both steady-state load and the worst-case step-load or motor-starting inrush, never steady-state alone. (3) Before any paralleling operation, verify voltage, frequency, and phase match through the actual synchronizing equipment — never assume.

Key rule — paralleling

Voltage, frequency, and phase angle must all match before the breaker closes — mismatched closure drives damaging circulating current.

Lesson 10 · Motor starting, protection, and VFDs

01 · DOL and star-delta starting

Direct-on-line (DOL) starting connects the motor straight across full line voltage — simplest and cheapest, but drawing high inrush current, typically \(6\)–\(8\times\) rated full-load current, briefly at start, while delivering full starting torque. Star-delta starting temporarily connects windings normally run in delta into star (wye) for starting, then switches to delta once up to speed. Because star reduces both the line-current relationship (Lesson 6) and the voltage each winding actually sees, the two effects combine to cut inrush current to roughly a third of the DOL value. Starting torque, which scales with the square of applied voltage, falls by essentially the same proportion, to roughly a third — the reduced inrush is never free; it costs starting torque in direct proportion.

02 · Overloads and protection classes

Thermal overload relays protect a motor from sustained overcurrent — not short-circuit, which is the breaker or fuse's job — by modeling winding heating and tripping before insulation damage occurs. They are sized to the motor's rated full-load current, and their trip class (Class 10, 20, 30, and so on) states how many seconds the relay allows at locked-rotor current before tripping: a Class 10 relay trips within roughly 10 seconds at locked-rotor current, a Class 30 up to about 30 seconds. The class must match the motor's actual thermal capability during a normal start — a mismatched class can either nuisance-trip on routine starts or fail to protect the motor during a genuine stall.

03 · Variable-frequency drives — the torque curve and the bearings

A VFD changes both frequency and voltage supplied to the motor, shifting synchronous speed \( N_s=120f/p \) and therefore the entire torque-speed curve up or down the speed axis — the mechanism behind smooth, controllable speed rather than fixed-speed on/off control, and it allows a controlled, low-current start by ramping frequency up from near zero instead of DOL's inrush spike. What a VFD does that DOL never did: its high-frequency PWM switching can induce small stray voltages on the motor shaft itself, through capacitive coupling across the air gap. Left with no safe path to ground, those voltages discharge as tiny electrical arcs through the bearing's lubricant film — over time, this electrical-discharge-machining effect pits and flutes the bearing races, a failure mode that simply does not exist on a DOL-started motor with no VFD in the circuit.

04 · Motor circuit anatomy — the working method

From the motor control center bucket: incoming isolator/disconnect, then short-circuit protection (fuse or breaker), then the contactor (the switching element), then the overload relay (thermal protection), then cable, then the motor terminal box. Reading this chain in order — and knowing what each stage protects against, short-circuit versus sustained overload versus simple switching — is the working method for troubleshooting why a motor circuit tripped, and which single component is the actual suspect.

Key trade-off — star-delta

Starting current and starting torque both fall to roughly 1/3 of DOL — a reduced-current start always costs proportional starting torque.

Lesson 11 · Electrical safety and measurements

01 · Shock and arc-flash hazards, respected properly

Electric shock severity depends on the current actually driven through the body, not on voltage alone — even a modest voltage can drive dangerous current through wet skin or direct contact, while the same voltage may be harmless through dry, unbroken skin. This is why "low voltage" control circuits are never treated as automatically safe. Arc-flash is a distinct hazard entirely: a short-circuit fault in a high-energy system can release an explosive arc — intense heat, a pressure wave, molten metal spray — severe enough to injure someone standing near an energized panel without any direct contact at all, purely from proximity. Arc-flash risk assessment and the correct PPE are sized to the specific equipment and available fault current at that point, not a one-size-fits-all rule, and are a separate requirement from ordinary shock protection.

02 · Lockout-tagout, as a mechanical engineer practices it

Lockout-tagout means physically isolating a source of hazardous energy — electrical, but equally stored mechanical, hydraulic, or pneumatic energy — and applying a personal lock and tag before work begins, verified by an actual test for zero energy rather than trust in a switch position or a label. A mechanical engineer maintaining a pump or gearbox routinely locks out the electrical supply to that equipment as a standard first step, even though the hands-on work that follows is entirely mechanical.

03 · Insulation resistance and clamp-meter measurements

Insulation resistance testing (a megohmmeter or "megger" test) applies a DC test voltage across a winding's insulation to ground and reads the resulting leakage current as a resistance, typically in megohms. A single reading is of limited use alone — what counts as "good" varies with motor size, insulation class, age, and ambient conditions. The real diagnostic value is in the trend: a winding whose measured value falls steadily over successive tests, even while each individual reading still technically clears a generic threshold, is showing progressive degradation and heading toward failure, while a value stable near its historical baseline is healthy regardless of the absolute number. This is exactly why IR is logged and trended over a machine's life, not treated as a simple spot-check. Clamp-meter current measurements, taken without breaking the circuit, are the practical field tool for checking phase balance (Lesson 6), confirming actual running current against nameplate full-load current, and catching an abnormal draw before it causes a trip.

04 · The boundary line between mechanical and electrical scope

The practical, widely-used convention: a mechanical scope of work ends at the motor's terminal box — the point where field wiring lands on the motor's own terminals. Everything from there back toward the supply — cable, MCC, protection devices — is electrical scope, requiring an electrically qualified and authorized person; the motor frame, coupling, bearings, and driven equipment remain squarely mechanical scope. This boundary exists because working inside a terminal box, even just to check a connection, means working on live-capable electrical equipment, which requires specific electrical safety qualification and LOTO authority that a mechanical scope of work does not automatically confer. Crossing that line without the right authorization is both a safety violation and, in most well-run plants, a policy violation — regardless of how simple the task looks.

Key habit — read the trend, not the number

Insulation resistance is diagnostic as a trend over repeated tests, not as a single pass/fail reading.

MFG 105 — Manufacturing Processes I — Casting & Bulk Forming

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · The manufacturing plant: process taxonomy and material flow

01 · The six-family taxonomy

Processing operations transform a workpart. Shaping covers four distinct routes: casting and molding (start liquid), deformation — rolling, forging, extrusion, drawing, sheet forming (start solid, move it), particulate processing (press and sinter powders), and material removal (machining, grinding, cutting away). Property-enhancing operations — heat treatment above all — leave geometry unchanged and rebuild microstructure instead. Surface processing — cleaning, coating, plating — changes only the surface. Assembly operations join parts: permanent joining (welding, brazing, adhesives) and mechanical assembly (bolting, riveting, press fits). A seventh box, material handling and inspection, adds no value directly but consumes most of the lead time in a real plant.

02 · Primary vs. secondary, and reading a real process chain

Primary processing creates the basic shape — a billet, a casting — from raw material. Secondary processing refines that shape further — machining a bearing seat, grinding a journal onto an already-cast or already-forged part. The working method: trace any product from raw material to dispatch, naming which family each step belongs to. A rebar's chain is casting (shaping, primary) then hot rolling (shaping, deformation) then cooling, cutting, and bundling (handling) — two value-adding families for one product. A power-cable conductor's chain runs longer: wire drawing (deformation), annealing (property-enhancing), stranding (mechanical assembly), insulation extrusion over the conductor (shaping, polymer), spooling and test (handling, inspection) — four families in a single line.

03 · Why this taxonomy matters more than it looks

By tonnage, the world's metal is shaped overwhelmingly by casting and deformation, not machining — machining refines a surface or a fit onto a shape one of those other families already created. An engineer walking into "manufacturing" expecting mainly a machine shop is statistically far more likely to meet a caster, a rolling mill, a press, or a filling line as the dominant floor space. Casting and deformation are not legacy technology on the way out; they remain the primary-shaping backbone of everything heavier than electronics. What has changed around them is instrumentation, automation, and control — exactly where this curriculum's later Industry 4.0 material points.

04 · Working method — reading any plant with one map

Given an unfamiliar plant or process line: (1) identify the raw material entering; (2) trace each transformation step and classify it into one of the six families; (3) note where primary shaping ends and secondary refinement begins; (4) recognize that handling and inspection, though value-neutral, often dominate real lead time and hide a large share of any real improvement opportunity. This single procedure reads a steel mill, a cable plant, or a food-filling hall with equal clarity.

Key fact

By tonnage, casting and deformation shape the large majority of the world's metal — machining refines a shape another process already created, it does not usually create the shape itself.

Lesson 02 · Metal casting I — solidification, gating, and riser design

01 · Casting is a controlled thermal collapse

A casting process melts metal, pours it into a cavity shaped like the part, and lets it solidify — the oldest manufacturing process still run at industrial scale, surviving because nothing else produces complex internal geometry in one operation this cheaply. Expendable-mold processes (sand, shell, investment) destroy the mold to retrieve the part — cheap tooling, high geometric freedom. Permanent-mold processes (die casting, continuous casting) reuse a metal mold — expensive tooling that demands volume to pay for itself. This lecture develops the physics common to all of them using sand casting, since its assumptions are cleanest: pay energy to superheat the melt, spend that superheat transporting metal through the gating system before it freezes in the channels, then extract all of it back out through the mold wall at a rate the mold controls — and the volume deficit left behind by that extraction is the riser's problem.

02 · Gating: Bernoulli with consequences

The gating system has one job: deliver the melt to the cavity before superheat runs out, without turbulence tearing air and oxide into the stream. Applying Bernoulli's equation from the free metal surface in the pouring basin to the sprue base — both stations near atmospheric pressure, the basin kept topped up so its surface barely moves — gives Torricelli's result, \( v=\sqrt{2gh} \), with \(h\) the vertical head from the free metal surface, never from the sprue's own top. Because velocity rises with depth, a parallel-walled sprue lets the falling stream neck away from its walls and aspirate air; matching the sprue's taper to the falling jet via continuity, \( A_t/A_b=\sqrt{h_b/h_t} \), removes that defect with one line of algebra. The flow is, in practice, always fully turbulent (Reynolds numbers in the tens of thousands for liquid metal) — the designer's real tools are geometry (taper, gate area, filters), not pouring speed.

03 · Solidification, and the equation that runs the foundry

A pure metal freezes at one temperature; an alloy freezes across a range, liquidus to solidus, with a mushy zone of dendritic growth between them. For a sand mold — a poor enough conductor that the mold, not the metal, controls the rate — treating the mold wall as a semi-infinite solid gives a clean result: total solidification time follows Chvorinov's rule, \[ TST=C_m\left(\frac{V}{A}\right)^{n},\qquad n\approx2\text{ for sand molds}, \] where \(V/A\) is the casting's modulus. \(C_m\) is measured once from a test casting in the same sand and alloy, then the rule ranks every shape: thick, chunky regions (high modulus) freeze last; thin fins (low modulus) freeze first. This mold-controlled assumption fails in a water-cooled metal die, where interface and metal-side resistance dominate instead and \(n\) drifts toward 1 — carrying \(n=2\) into a die-casting estimate is a real, not cosmetic, error.

04 · Shrinkage, and the riser that pays for it

Metal contracts three separate times, compensated by three different people. Liquid contraction (pouring temperature down to freezing) is cheap — the gating system simply supplies a little more liquid while everything is still fluid. Solidification shrinkage — the liquid-to-solid density jump, roughly \(6\)–\(7\)% for aluminum, \(2.5\)–\(3\)% for low-carbon steel — is the riser's job: a sacrificial reservoir that must still be liquid, and must stay liquid longer than everything it feeds, or the shrinkage cavity forms in the part instead. Chvorinov turns "stay liquid longer" into arithmetic: sizing the riser so \( TST_{riser}=1.25\times TST_{casting} \) — a 25% margin covering what the model ignores — translates directly into a required riser modulus, \( (V/A)_{riser}=\sqrt{1.25}\,(V/A)_{casting} \). Solid contraction (freezing point down to room temperature) has no liquid left to feed it at all; the patternmaker builds the pattern oversize by a shrinkage allowance instead, so the part contracts down onto the drawing.

Key rule — Chvorinov (1940)

\[ TST = C_m\left(\frac{V}{A}\right)^{n},\quad n\approx2\ (\text{sand}).\qquad (V/A)_{riser}=\sqrt{1.25}\,(V/A)_{casting}. \]

Lesson 03 · Metal casting II — practice, quality, and defects

01 · Patterns, allowances, and molding sand

A mold cavity is deliberately wrong, in four specific ways. The shrinkage (patternmaker's) allowance builds the pattern oversize so solid contraction shrinks the casting back onto the drawing — it cannot compensate solidification shrinkage, which is the riser's job (Lesson 2). Draft, a taper of roughly \(1\)–\(3^\circ\) on surfaces perpendicular to the parting line, lets the pattern withdraw without tearing the sand. Machining allowance leaves extra stock on surfaces to be machined. Distortion allowance pre-bends a pattern opposite to its predicted distortion. Internal geometry comes from cores — bonded-sand shapes that are also a gas source and a restraint on contraction. Green sand itself is specified by test numbers, not by feel: green compressive strength (too low risks collapse; too high traps gas and resists contraction), permeability (how well gases vent — low permeability plus wet sand is a blowhole problem waiting to happen), and moisture (too dry crumbles, too wet boils).

02 · Fluidity, misruns, and cold shuts

Fluidity, in the foundry sense, is measured, not derived — the distance a standard test channel fills before the stream freezes shut. It is a race between the advancing stream and mold-controlled heat loss, and it rises with superheat, falls with a wide freezing range (a mushy alloy clogs the channel before it is fully solid), and falls with a stiff surface oxide film. When the race is lost, the resulting defect has two names with two different causes: a misrun is a section the metal never reached at all; a cold shut is where two streams met but were each too cold to fuse, leaving an oxide-lined seam that can look deceptively like a crack. The fixes differ — a misrun asks for more superheat or faster filling; a cold shut additionally asks for gating that avoids opposed, long-path streams.

03 · Gas porosity, and Sievert's law derived

Liquid metal dissolves gas far more readily than solid metal does — that solubility cliff at the freezing front is the entire mechanism of gas porosity. Hydrogen dissolves by splitting into two atoms, \( \text{H}_2\rightleftharpoons2[\text{H}] \), so the equilibrium constant carries the dissolved concentration squared against the gas partial pressure: \( K(T)=[\text{H}]^2/p_{\text{H}_2} \). Solving gives Sievert's law, \[ [\text{H}]=S_0(T)\sqrt{p/p_0}, \] a square root that is not an empirical fit — it is the stoichiometry of the dissolution reaction doing algebra. \(S_0\) rises steeply with temperature, the hidden price of superheat: a hotter melt runs farther (02) and dissolves more hydrogen from every damp tool or humid hour. At solidification, solubility drops sharply, the rejected gas recombines, and the result is pinholes — small, spherical, smooth-walled pores, their roundness the signature of having grown as bubbles in liquid. Mold and core gas — steam and binder combustion products that could not vent through the sand — is the other source, producing larger, often subsurface blowholes instead.

04 · Shrinkage porosity, and the working method for a defect

Solidification shrinkage the riser fails to feed becomes a cavity, at two scales. Macro-shrinkage is an open pipe or closed cavity at an unfed hot spot — a feeding-path failure, visible once sectioned. Microporosity is subtler: in the mushy zone of a wide-freezing-range alloy, the last interdendritic liquid is cut off by the surrounding dendrite forest and shrinks in place, leaving jagged, angular, tree-lined voids — a morphology that contrasts directly with gas porosity's round, smooth pores. The working method for any defect: (1) read the morphology — round and smooth points to gas, jagged and angular points to shrinkage; (2) trace it to its physical cause using the taxonomy — solubility drop, volume deficit, lost heat race, restrained contraction, mold failure, or melt cleanliness; (3) let the cause pick the fix, not the appearance alone, since a misrun and a cold shut can look similar but need different corrections.

Key tool — Sievert's law

\[ [\text{H}] = S_0(T)\sqrt{p/p_0} \]

A square-root relation from reaction stoichiometry — the mechanism behind pinhole porosity.

Lesson 04 · Continuous casting of steel

01 · The machine, traced

A continuous caster runs Lesson 2's solidification physics at steady state, around the clock. Steel flows ladle to tundish — a steady-head reservoir, doing the pouring basin's job at enormous scale — then into a water-cooled, oscillating copper mold, where a thin solid shell freezes against the walls while the core stays liquid. The strand then travels through spray-cooled support rollers, curving toward horizontal, before a straightener and finally a torch cutoff — which cannot operate until the core is actually solid.

02 · Shell growth and metallurgical length

The same \(1/\sqrt{t}\) heat-flow physics behind Chvorinov's rule gives the shell-growth law used across continuous casting: \[ \delta = K\sqrt{t}, \] with \(\delta\) the solid shell thickness, \(t\) the time since the strand entered the mold, and \(K\) a solidification constant (typically \(25\)–\(30\ \text{mm/min}^{1/2}\) for water-cooled casters). The strand is fully solid once \(\delta\) reaches the billet's half-thickness \(d/2\); at casting speed \(v\), that happens a fixed distance below the mold — the metallurgical length: \[ L_m = v\,t_{full} = v\left(\frac{d}{2K}\right)^{2}. \] Note that \(t_{full}\) itself depends only on \(d\) and \(K\) — it does not depend on casting speed at all; only the distance \(L_m\) does, since a faster strand simply covers more ground before the same fixed time elapses.

03 · Reading the numbers

For a \(130\ \text{mm}\) square billet with \(K=27\ \text{mm/min}^{1/2}\) and casting speed \(v=2.6\ \text{m/min}\): \( t_{full}=(65/27)^2\approx5.8\ \text{min} \), so \( L_m=2.6\times5.8\approx15\ \text{m} \) — fifteen meters of strand, through the mold and down the curve, still carries a liquid core; the torch is never positioned before that point. At the mold exit (a \(0.8\ \text{m}\) mold, \(t=0.8/2.6\approx0.31\ \text{min}\)): \( \delta=27\sqrt{0.31}\approx15\ \text{mm} \) — the entire ferrostatic head of that fifteen-meter liquid core is carried by a fifteen-millimeter skin. Push casting speed to \(3.4\ \text{m/min}\) and the exit shell thins to about \(13\ \text{mm}\) while \(L_m\) grows to nearly \(20\ \text{m}\) — casting speed is never a free productivity lever; it trades directly against shell thickness and the depth of the liquid core.

04 · Quality connection and working method

Billet defects trace to specific subsystems: pinholes to dissolved gas in the melt; internal and centerline cracks to thermal stress and bulging between misaligned or worn support rollers; rhomboidity to uneven mold cooling; centerline segregation to the last, solute-rich liquid at the core — Lesson 3's dendritic argument, running at machine scale. A shell rupture — a breakout — pours liquid steel over the machine and is among the most feared events in a melt shop. Working method: (1) find \(t_{full}\) and \(L_m\) for the section and machine in question; (2) treat any casting-speed change as a three-way trade against shell thickness, breakout risk, and segregation quality, never as a pure throughput knob; (3) trace a specific billet defect back to the subsystem — mold, spray, rollers — most likely responsible.

Key result — metallurgical length

\[ L_m = v\left(\frac{d}{2K}\right)^{2} \]

t_full does not depend on casting speed — only how far the strand travels before reaching it does.

Lesson 05 · Casting processes survey: sand to die casting

01 · The expendable-vs-permanent axis, revisited

Lesson 2 opened with the basic split: expendable-mold processes destroy the mold to retrieve the part, permanent-mold processes reuse a metal mold many times. This lesson surveys specific processes along that axis and compares them on the dimensions that actually drive a selection decision — tolerance, surface finish, minimum section thickness, and economics — dimensions the underlying physics from Lessons 2–3 does not address directly.

02 · Shell molding and investment casting — expendable, refined

Shell molding cures a thin, resin-bonded sand shell against a heated metal pattern — a real step up in dimensional accuracy and surface finish over green sand, still single-use, at moderate cost, well suited to medium-volume precision parts. Investment casting (lost-wax) builds a ceramic shell around a wax pattern, then melts the wax out before pouring metal into the resulting cavity. It achieves the best dimensional accuracy and surface finish of any casting process and can produce very complex internal geometry, but is slow and comparatively expensive per part — reserved for high-value, complex, precision components (turbine blades are the classic case) where machining the same geometry from solid would be impossible or far costlier.

03 · Permanent-mold and die casting

Gravity permanent-mold casting pours metal by gravity into a reusable metal mold — better surface finish and finer grain than sand casting, from faster metal-mold cooling, but geometry is more limited since the mold must survive many cycles rather than being sacrificed. Die casting injects molten metal — almost always a low-melting-point alloy, aluminum, zinc, or magnesium — under high pressure into a hardened steel die, filling extremely fast. It produces the thinnest sections, the finest surface finish, and the tightest tolerances of any casting process at very high production rates, but tooling cost is the highest of any casting process, demanding real volume to pay for itself, and the fast, turbulent fill entrains air the way Lesson 2's gating discussion warned against, at a far more extreme scale — the resulting porosity generally rules out heat-treating or welding a die casting afterward, since trapped gas expands and blisters the part on reheating.

04 · Choosing among them — the working method

Given a part: (1) check production volume — low volume favors expendable processes (sand, investment); high volume justifies permanent-mold or die-casting tooling; (2) check geometric complexity and required section thickness — investment casting and die casting both handle thin, complex geometry that sand casting cannot; (3) check surface finish and tolerance — die casting and investment casting both deliver near-net-shape parts needing little or no machining, while sand casting typically needs machining allowance on critical surfaces; (4) check whether the part will be heat-treated or welded afterward — a real constraint against die casting specifically, rarely against the others.

Key checklist — process selection

Volume → tooling cost justified? Complexity/thickness → which processes can hold it? Finish/tolerance → how much machining is budgeted? Post-processing → will it be heat-treated or welded?

Lesson 06 · Fundamentals of metal forming and workability

01 · Flow stress and the flow curve

Beyond yield, a metal's resistance to further plastic deformation — its flow stress \(Y_f\) — keeps rising with strain through strain hardening, commonly modeled as \[ Y_f = K\varepsilon^{n}, \] with \(K\) the strength coefficient and \(n\) the strain-hardening exponent, both material constants read from a tension test. The flow curve — true stress against true strain — is what a forming process actually pushes against, not the simpler engineering stress-strain curve, which is only valid up to necking. Forming operations generally impose a stress state that suppresses necking, so the flow curve remains meaningful at strains far beyond where an ordinary tension specimen would already have failed.

02 · Hot, warm, and cold working — defined by homologous temperature

The dividing line between these regimes is not an absolute temperature but homologous temperature, \(T/T_m\), both measured on an absolute (Kelvin) scale. Cold working, below roughly \(0.3\,T_m\), lets strain hardening accumulate fully — good surface finish and precision, but rising force and a limited total deformation before an anneal is needed. Hot working, above roughly \(0.6\,T_m\), runs hot enough that dynamic recrystallization continuously undoes strain hardening as it forms, so flow stress stays roughly constant regardless of strain — enormous shape change becomes possible for modest force, at the cost of poorer surface finish, looser tolerance, and oxide scale from the high temperature. Warm working sits between the two, trading some of each advantage.

03 · Strain-rate sensitivity

Flow stress also depends on how fast the material is deformed, particularly at hot-working temperature: \( Y_f=C\dot\varepsilon^{m} \), with \(m\) the strain-rate sensitivity exponent. At cold-working temperature, \(m\) is nearly zero — rate barely matters. At hot-working temperature, \(m\) rises meaningfully, and a higher \(m\) genuinely helps resist necking: a region that starts thinning locally strains faster there, and a high-\(m\) material responds by getting locally stronger, self-correcting the flaw rather than running away toward failure — the basis of superplastic forming in carefully controlled, very-high-\(m\) alloys.

04 · Friction, lubrication, and workability

Friction at the tool-workpiece interface resists metal flow and raises force — and, as later lessons on rolling and forging show, is sometimes the very mechanism a process depends on, sometimes purely a cost to minimize. Lubrication regimes differ sharply by temperature: hot working often runs with no lubricant, or a high-temperature glass or graphite coating; cold working uses oils, soaps, or coatings chosen to survive without breaking down under pressure. Workability — how much deformation a material tolerates before cracking — depends on both the material's inherent ductility and the stress state a specific process imposes: compressive-dominated processes (forging, rolling) are far more forgiving than tension-dominated ones (wire drawing), which is precisely why drawing carries a hard theoretical strain ceiling that compression-based processes do not share in the same way.

Key thresholds — homologous temperature

Cold work: \(T<0.3\,T_m\). Hot work: \(T>0.6\,T_m\) (both absolute). \( Y_f=K\varepsilon^n \) (cold, strain-hardening) vs. roughly flat (hot, dynamic recrystallization).

Lesson 07 · Rolling of metals

01 · The bite condition — checked before anything else

Flat rolling squeezes a slab or bar between two driven rolls, reducing thickness from \(h_0\) to \(h_f\). The draft, \(d=h_0-h_f\), cannot exceed \[ d_{max}=\mu^2R, \] or the rolls simply skid instead of biting into the stock — friction is what drives the material through the pass, not merely a cost to minimize as in some other forming processes. Checking this limit is the first step of any rolling calculation, before force, torque, or power are worth computing at all.

02 · Contact length, force, torque, and power

Once a pass is confirmed possible, the projected contact length is \( L=\sqrt{R(h_0-h_f)} \), and the remaining estimates follow directly: \[ F=\bar Y_f\,w\,L,\qquad T=0.5\,F\,L,\qquad P=2\pi N F L, \] with \(\bar Y_f\) the mean flow stress over the pass, \(w\) the strip width, and \(N\) the roll speed. For a hot-rolling pass — \(h_0=30\ \text{mm}\to h_f=22\ \text{mm}\), \(R=300\ \text{mm}\), \(w=250\ \text{mm}\), \(\mu=0.30\), \(\bar Y_f\approx180\ \text{MPa}\), \(N=50\ \text{rpm}\): the draft \(d=8\ \text{mm}\) is comfortably inside \(d_{max}=0.09\times300=27\ \text{mm}\); \(L=\sqrt{300\times8}\approx49\ \text{mm}\); \(F\approx180\times0.250\times0.049\approx2.2\ \text{MN}\) — roughly 225 tonnes-force on the stand; torque per roll \(\approx54\ \text{kN·m}\); power \(\approx566\ \text{kW}\).

03 · Hot vs. cold rolling — different products, not different settings

Hot rolling runs rough and grippy on purpose — friction is welcomed, because it buys large shape change cheaply above the recrystallization temperature (Lesson 6's hot-working regime), where flow stress stays comparatively low and roughly flat with strain. Cold rolling lubricates deliberately and accepts smaller drafts per pass, because it is selling a different outcome entirely: strain hardening raises strength, and the surface arrives clean, precise, and close to final dimension. These are two different products from two different working regimes, not the same process run at two settings.

04 · Reading the defect map

Wavy edges and center buckle trace to roll bending, making thickness — and therefore elongation — uneven across the strip width; crown and camber management on the rolls is the fix. Alligatoring, a centerline split at the exit, usually traces further upstream to casting quality (Lesson 4's centerline segregation returning at the mill). Rolled-in scale and seams trace to descaling practice and the incoming billet's own surface condition. In a rebar mill specifically, rib and grade-mark quality add roll wear as a first-class variable worth tracking, not an afterthought.

Key limit — the bite condition

\[ d_{max} = \mu^2 R \]

No friction, no bite, no process — but the same friction that drives the pass also raises force and wear.

Lesson 08 · Forging

01 · Open-die, impression-die, and flashless forging

Open-die forging uses simple, largely flat or lightly contoured dies that do not enclose the workpiece — metal flows outward freely as it compresses, good for large, simple shapes and preliminary shaping, with limited dimensional control. Impression-die (closed-die) forging constrains flow into a shaped cavity, giving far better dimensional control and complex shapes — but the excess metal has to go somewhere, which is what flash is for. Flashless forging matches starting billet volume precisely to the cavity so no flash forms at all, at the cost of needing tightly controlled billet volume and greater die and press investment.

02 · Upsetting analysis — force including friction's barreling penalty

For a cylinder upset from \(h_0\) to \(h\), true strain is \( \varepsilon=\ln(h_0/h) \) and flow stress follows the familiar \( Y_f=K\varepsilon^{n} \). Friction at the die faces resists the metal's outward radial flow near the platens more than at mid-height, producing the classic bulge — barreling. This friction penalty is captured by a multiplier \[ K_f = 1+\frac{0.4\mu D}{h}, \] which grows as the part gets shorter and wider (\(D/h\) increases) — the force penalty from friction worsens as upsetting proceeds, it does not stay constant. With the current cross-section \(A=V/h\) from volume conservation, force is \( F=K_f\,Y_f\,A \).

03 · Reading the numbers

Cold-upsetting a low-carbon steel slug, \(d_0=50\ \text{mm}\), \(h_0=70\ \text{mm}\to h=40\ \text{mm}\), \(K=500\ \text{MPa}\), \(n=0.25\), \(\mu=0.25\): volume \(V=137{,}400\ \text{mm}^3\), final area \(A=V/h=3{,}436\ \text{mm}^2\), diameter \(D=66.1\ \text{mm}\), strain \(\varepsilon=\ln(70/40)\approx0.56\), \( Y_f=500\times0.56^{0.25}\approx432\ \text{MPa} \), \( K_f=1+0.4\times0.25\times66.1/40\approx1.17 \), giving \( F\approx1.17\times432\times3{,}436\times10^{-3}\approx1.73\ \text{MN} \) — roughly 176 tonnes-force, and this is the peak force, occurring at the end of the stroke where both \(Y_f\) and \(K_f\) are largest.

04 · Grain flow and flash — what they genuinely buy

Forging refines structure, closes internal porosity, and — critically — aligns inclusion stringers and grain flow along the part's contour, rather than cutting across it the way machining from bar stock would. Properties improve specifically along the direction grain flow follows, particularly fatigue resistance; the advantage shrinks, and can even reverse, across the flow — so "forged is always stronger than cast or machined" is an oversimplification that ignores loading direction. Flash in impression-die forging is not wasted metal by accident: the thin flash land deliberately chokes outward flow, building the internal cavity pressure that forces metal into the last, hardest-to-fill corners of the die — engineered waste, trimmed off afterward, not an oversight.

Key multiplier — friction in upsetting

\[ K_f = 1+\frac{0.4\mu D}{h} \]

Grows as the part shortens and widens — peak force lands at the end of the stroke, not the start.

Lesson 09 · Extrusion and wire drawing

01 · Two die processes, opposite stress states

Extrusion pushes metal through a die under compression — a ram compresses the billet, forcing it through a reducing opening. That compressive state tolerates enormous single-pass strains (extrusion ratios of 40 or more for aluminum) and can even improve workability, since compression tends to close internal voids rather than open them. Wire and rod drawing pulls metal through a die instead — the already-reduced material downstream of the die supplies the pulling force, which means the drawn material itself must survive that tensile stress without yielding. That one difference in sign — compression versus tension — explains both processes' products: massive sections from extrusion, kilometres of fine wire from drawing.

02 · Extrusion: ratio, empirical strain, pressure

Extrusion ratio is \( r_x=A_0/A_f \). Johnson's empirical extrusion strain folds ideal work, friction, and redundant shear into one number, \( \varepsilon_x=a+b\ln r_x \) (typically \(a\approx0.8\), \(b\approx1.2\)–\(1.5\)), giving a ram pressure estimate \( p=\bar Y_f\,\varepsilon_x \).

03 · Drawing: stress, and the survival check

Drawing stress is \[ \sigma_d=\bar Y_f\left(1+\frac{\mu}{\alpha}\right)\phi\ln\!\left(\frac{A_0}{A_f}\right), \] with \(\mu\) the die friction, \(\alpha\) the die half-angle in radians, and \(\phi\geq1\) a redundant-work factor. The absolute constraint: \(\sigma_d\) must stay well below the drawn wire's own flow stress at that exit strain, or the wire yields and necks at the die exit instead of drawing cleanly through. This gives the classical result that even a perfectly frictionless, ideal pass cannot exceed roughly \(63\)% area reduction (\(\varepsilon=1\)); real passes, carrying friction and redundant work, run well below that. For a copper drawing pass, \(3.50\to3.03\ \text{mm}\) (\(25\)% area reduction), \(K=300\ \text{MPa}\), \(n=0.50\), \(\mu=0.07\), \(\alpha=6^\circ\): \(\bar Y_f\approx107\ \text{MPa}\), \(\phi\approx1.05\), giving \( \sigma_d\approx54\ \text{MPa} \) — comfortably below the drawn wire's own flow stress of \(\approx161\ \text{MPa}\), a safe pass with real margin.

04 · Tandem drawing — the working method

Because a single pass is capped well below the theoretical ceiling by real friction and redundant-work margins, producing fine wire from a much larger rod requires many passes in sequence — tandem drawing. Each pass is re-checked against its own \(\sigma_d\)-versus-flow-stress safety margin; because strain hardening accumulates pass over pass, that margin shrinks as drawing proceeds, which is exactly why intermediate anneals are scheduled partway through a tandem sequence — resetting accumulated hardening so later passes still have a comfortable margin, rather than letting it shrink toward zero and risking a break.

Key constraint — drawing stress

\(\sigma_d\) must stay below the drawn wire's own flow stress — the real, binding limit, well short of the ideal 63% ceiling.

Lesson 10 · Powder metallurgy

01 · The three-step process

Powder production — most commonly atomization, spraying molten metal into fine droplets that solidify as powder — sets particle size and shape, which strongly affects how well the powder compacts and flows. Compaction presses powder in a rigid die under high pressure into a green compact: a fragile, porous shape held together only by mechanical interlocking and cold welding at particle contact points, typically around \(80\)–\(85\)% of theoretical density — not yet a real structural part. Sintering heats that compact, below its melting point (typically \(70\)–\(90\)% of \(T_m\), Lesson 6's homologous-temperature logic returning here), in a controlled atmosphere, letting atomic diffusion bond particles together, close pores, and build real mechanical strength.

02 · Density is the controlling quality variable

Nearly every mechanical property of a PM part — strength, ductility, fatigue life, corrosion resistance — scales with its final density relative to the fully-dense wrought material of the same composition. A part at \(90\)% density can fall meaningfully short of wrought strength and ductility, because residual pores act as stress concentrators and crack-initiation sites — the same flaw logic that governs fracture mechanics generally. Density is set primarily by compaction pressure and sintering temperature and time, both pushing the compact closer to full density with diminishing returns; it approaches, but never quite reaches, \(100\)%. Secondary operations — repressing, infiltration with a lower-melting metal, resin impregnation — can push density and properties higher still, at added cost, used only when base sintered properties fall short of the target.

03 · Where PM wins

PM excels at small, complex parts at high volume: powder goes from raw material to near-net shape in one compaction step, needing little or no machining — the same volume-justifies-tooling economics as die casting. PM also uniquely delivers engineered porosity: a self-lubricating bronze or iron bearing is deliberately made at less than full density, then oil-impregnated into the connected pore network, so the part itself becomes a distributed oil reservoir — a property a fully-dense machined bearing simply cannot provide. Finally, PM handles materials no other route can: tungsten's melting point defeats casting, and a cemented-carbide cutting insert — hard tungsten-carbide particles bonded in a tougher cobalt matrix — is a composite structure that cannot be created by casting or by machining a homogeneous bar at all.

04 · Working method — selecting or specifying a PM part

(1) Translate the property target into a required density, or deliberately accept a lower density if porosity itself is the desired feature, as in a self-lubricating bearing. (2) Confirm volume justifies compaction die tooling cost, the same volume logic as Lesson 5's casting comparison. (3) Confirm the geometry is genuinely PM-favorable — no deep re-entrant features the die cannot eject — and that the material is either economical or genuinely hard to process any other way. (4) Decide sintered-only versus a secondary densification step based on whether base sintered properties actually meet the target.

Key fact — density controls properties

PM mechanical properties scale with final density relative to fully-dense material. Density is set by compaction pressure and sintering temperature/time — approaching, never reaching, 100%.

Lesson 11 · Process selection and cost fundamentals

01 · Property indices meet fixed vs. variable cost

Fixed cost — tooling, dies, molds, setup — is paid once (or per changeover) regardless of how many parts are made. Variable cost — material, energy, direct labor per cycle — scales directly with quantity. Total cost for a quantity \(Q\) is \[ C(Q) = \text{Fixed} + \text{Variable}\times Q. \] Two processes capable of making the identical part generally carry very different fixed and variable costs — a process with cheap tooling but a high per-part cost (sand casting) against one with expensive tooling but a low per-part cost (die casting) is exactly the volume logic Lessons 5 and 10 already touched, now made explicit and quantitative.

02 · Break-even volume — the working method

Setting two processes' total-cost lines equal and solving for \(Q\) gives the break-even volume: below it, the low-fixed-cost process is cheaper; above it, the low-variable-cost process is cheaper, by a widening margin as volume grows further. This single number is genuinely decision-driving, not academic — comparing a customer's actual expected volume against it is, in practice, how a manufacturing engineer chooses between competing processes more often than any single technical merit argument alone.

03 · Reading a real decision

Process A: \(\$2{,}000\) fixed, \(\$8\)/part. Process B: \(\$20{,}000\) fixed, \(\$2\)/part. Break-even: \( Q_{be}=(20{,}000-2{,}000)/(8-2)=3{,}000 \) units. Below \(3{,}000\) units, Process A wins; above it, Process B wins, and the gap widens quickly — at \(10{,}000\) units, Process A costs \(\$2{,}000+\$80{,}000=\$82{,}000\) against Process B's \(\$20{,}000+\$20{,}000=\$40{,}000\), roughly half. The method generalizes past two processes: with three or more candidates, compute every pair's break-even volume, and the cheapest process at any target volume is whichever line sits lowest at that specific \(Q\) — not necessarily the same process across the whole volume range.

04 · Beyond the break-even number

Break-even analysis assumes cost is the only criterion — a real decision still needs Lesson 5's and Lesson 10's technical screening first: can the process even achieve the required geometry, tolerance, finish, and material at all? Comparing costs between two processes that cannot both actually make the part is a wasted exercise. The analysis also assumes static per-part costs, ignoring that variable cost itself can fall with cumulative volume through learning-curve effects, and that any demand forecast carries real uncertainty. A decision made right at a razor-thin break-even margin is riskier than the arithmetic alone suggests — a prudent engineer builds in margin rather than committing to whichever process wins by a small amount at the forecast volume alone.

Key formula — break-even volume

\[ C(Q)=\text{Fixed}+\text{Variable}\times Q,\qquad Q_{be}=\frac{\text{Fixed}_B-\text{Fixed}_A}{\text{Variable}_A-\text{Variable}_B}. \]
