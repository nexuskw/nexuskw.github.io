Refresher — not new material

R1 · Sensible heat and latent heat (Thermodynamics I)

Raising the temperature of a phase costs sensible heat, proportional to the temperature change: Q = m·C·ΔT, where C is the specific heat of that phase. Changing phase at constant temperature costs latent heat: Q = m·Hf, where Hf is the heat of fusion. The two are separate line items in any energy balance, and C is different for the solid and the liquid — Cs and Cl are not interchangeable. That is the entire refresher; if the distinction feels vague, reread Çengel & Boles before continuing.

Refresher — not new material

R2 · Bernoulli and continuity (Fluid Mechanics I)

For steady, incompressible, frictionless flow along a streamline, mechanical energy per unit weight is conserved:

p1ρg + v122g + z1 = p2ρg + v222g + z2(R2.1)

and mass conservation in a duct of varying section is Q = A1v1 = A2v2. Every gating equation in 03 is these two lines and nothing else.

Refresher — not new material

R3 · Transient conduction into a semi-infinite solid (Heat Transfer I)

When the surface of a thick body initially at T0 is suddenly held at Ts, heat soaks inward and the surface heat flux decays with time as

q(t) = k (Ts − T0)√π α t(R3.1)

where k is the body's conductivity and α = k/ρc its diffusivity. The full error-function derivation of (R3.1) belongs to Heat Transfer — see Çengel & Ghajar, Ch. 4, "Transient Heat Conduction in Semi-Infinite Solids." We take the result as sourced and will use it, honestly, in 04. Note the physics it encodes: the flux falls as 1/√t because the already-heated layer of mold acts as a growing thermal resistance.

02The process, and the price of admission

Casting is a controlled thermal collapse

A casting process is any process in which metal is melted, poured into a cavity shaped like the part, and allowed to solidify. It is the oldest manufacturing process we still run at industrial scale, and it survives because nothing else produces complex internal geometry in one operation at that cost.

Classify every casting process by what happens to the mold. Expendable-mold processes — green-sand casting, shell molding, investment casting — destroy the mold to retrieve the part; the mold is cheap, the geometry freedom is high, the surface finish is moderate. Permanent-mold processes — die casting, gravity permanent mold, and, at the scale of a steel plant, continuous casting — reuse a metal mold; tooling is expensive, so the economics demand volume. Groover's Ch. 10–11 carry the full taxonomy; this lecture develops the physics common to all of them, using sand casting as the vehicle because its assumptions are cleanest.

The thermal arc of the process is the syllabus of this lecture in one sentence: we pay energy to superheat a melt (02), spend that superheat transporting metal through a gating system before it freezes in the channels (03), then extract all of it back out through the mold wall, latent heat included, at a rate the mold controls (04) — and the volume deficit left behind by that extraction is the riser's problem (05).

The pouring energy balance

Start from refresher R1 and simply walk the metal up the temperature axis. To take a mass m = ρV of metal from room temperature T0 to a pouring temperature Tp above its freezing point Tm, you pay three line items in sequence: sensible heat in the solid from T0 to Tm, latent heat of fusion at Tm, and sensible heat in the liquid from Tm to Tp. Summing them is the derivation:

Total heat to reach pouring temperature
H = ρ V [ Cs(Tm − T0) + Hf + Cl(Tp − Tm) ](1)
AssumptionCs and Cl are treated as constants over their temperature ranges. Real specific heats drift with temperature; for wide ranges (steel: ~25 °C to ~1600 °C) use mean values or enthalpy tables.
AssumptionA single melting point Tm. True for pure metals and eutectics only — an alloy melts across a liquidus–solidus range, and (1) becomes an approximation with Tm taken mid-range.
Limit(1) is the heat into the metal. Furnace energy consumption is far higher: refractory losses, slag, radiation. (1) sets the floor, not the bill.

The difference Tp − Tm is the superheat — the only thermal budget the metal has for its journey through ladle, sprue, and runner. Every degree lost in transit is a degree closer to freezing in the wrong place, which is the defect called a cold shut or misrun. This equation and its use here follow Groover, Fundamentals of Modern Manufacturing, Ch. 10.

Field note — — how close the ladle lip sits to the pouring basin. Free-fall height feeds directly into stream velocity, and velocity is the enemy: it entrains air and erodes the mold. The pourer is managing Bernoulli's equation by hand.

03Getting metal into the cavity

Gating: Bernoulli with consequences

The gating system — pouring basin, sprue, runner, gates — has one job: deliver the melt to the cavity before superheat runs out, without turbulence tearing air and oxide into the stream. Both requirements are set by two equations you already own.

Velocity at the sprue base — derived

Apply Bernoulli (R2.1) along a streamline from the free surface of metal in the pouring basin (station 1) to the base of the sprue (station 2). Three honest observations kill six terms: both stations are essentially at atmospheric pressure (the mold cavity vents through the sand), so p1 = p2; the basin is kept topped up, so the free surface barely moves and v1 ≈ 0; and we measure elevation downward from the free surface, so z1 − z2 = h. What survives is

v222g = h   ⟹   v = √2 g h(2)

— Torricelli's theorem, with h the vertical head from the free metal surface to the point in question, not the length of the sprue. Volumetric flow follows from continuity, and fill time from the cavity volume:

Q = A·v ,    tfill = VcavityQ(3)

Why sprues are tapered — derived

Here is the trap in (2): velocity increases with depth. A falling stream obeys continuity, so as v grows, the stream's own cross-section must shrink — the jet necks down. If the sprue walls are parallel, the metal pulls away from them partway down and the gap aspirates air and mold gas straight into the melt. The fix is to shape the sprue like the jet. Continuity between the sprue top (head ht) and base (head hb), with (2) substituted for each velocity:

At√2 g ht = Ab√2 g hb   ⟹   AtAb = √hb / ht(4)

The area ratio is fixed by the head ratio alone. That is the entire theory of the tapered sprue — one line of algebra that removes a whole defect family.

The honesty clause: friction and turbulence

Equation (2) is frictionless. Real gating systems lose head to wall friction and direction changes, so practice multiplies Q by an efficiency factor (0.85–0.90 for simple systems, lower for complex ones — Groover, Ch. 10). More important is which regime the flow is in. Check the Reynolds number, ρvD/μ, at the sprue base: for liquid metals it lands in the tens of thousands — fully turbulent. You cannot laminarize a foundry pour; you can only manage it: streamlined transitions, no sharp corners, gates sized to slow the stream before it enters the cavity, and ceramic foam filters that both screen dross and straighten the flow. Liquid aluminum oxidizes on contact with air in milliseconds, and every fold of that oxide skin swallowed by a turbulent stream becomes a crack-initiating bifilm inside the finished part.

04Freezing

Solidification, and the one equation that runs the foundry

Once the cavity is full, the problem changes character completely: from momentum transport to heat extraction. The rate at which the mold can pull heat out of the metal decides the microstructure, the defect population, and — through Chvorinov's rule — the entire geometry of risering.

How metals actually freeze

A pure metal freezes at a single temperature Tm. On a cooling curve the melt first sheds superheat, then undercools slightly below Tm — solid cannot form without nuclei, and nucleation needs a thermodynamic push — then snaps back to Tm as latent heat release balances extraction, and holds a plateau until the last liquid is gone. An alloy has no plateau: freezing begins at the liquidus TL and ends at the solidus TS, and between them solid and liquid coexist in a slushy region called the mushy zone. Growth in that zone is dendritic — solid advances as branched trees, and the last liquid to freeze is trapped between dendrite arms, enriched in solute (this is microsegregation, and it is why cast structures are chemically inhomogeneous at the scale of micrometres). The framework is Callister's Ch. 9–10 phase-diagram material put in motion; the authoritative treatment is Flemings, Solidification Processing.

Chvorinov's rule — derived, not asserted

Now the question that riser design hangs on: how long does a casting take to freeze? For a sand mold the answer has a clean derivation, because sand is such a poor conductor that the mold — not the metal, not the interface — is the bottleneck. Everything else in the heat path is fast by comparison. That single physical judgment (mold-controlled freezing) is the engine of the whole result.

Setup. Treat the mold wall as a semi-infinite solid, initially at T0, whose surface jumps to the metal's freezing temperature Tm at the instant of pouring and stays there while freezing proceeds (the plateau of Diagram 2 justifies this). Refresher R3 then hands us the instantaneous heat flux out through every unit area of mold–metal interface — equation (R3.1) with Ts = Tm.

Step 1 — total heat through the wall. Integrate the flux over time to get cumulative heat extracted per unit interface area:

QA = ∫0t k (Tm − T0)√π α τ dτ = 2 k (Tm − T0)√π α √t(5)

Step 2 — heat the metal must surrender. Per unit volume, the melt gives up its remaining superheat plus all of its latent heat: ρm[Cl(Tp − Tm) + Hf]. Call the bracket ΔH′. The casting's total bill is ρm ΔH′ V.

Step 3 — balance. Freezing is complete at the time ts when the wall has passed the whole bill. Set ρm ΔH′ V equal to (5) times the interface area A and solve for ts:

ts = π4 [ρm ΔH′Tm − T0]2 1k ρ c (VA)2(6)

where k, ρ, c are mold properties (α = k/ρc was substituted). Every term in the front factor is a property of the metal–mold pair, fixed once you choose an alloy, a sand, and a pouring practice. Collapse it into one constant and you have the working form:

Chvorinov's rule (1940)
TST = Cm (VA)n ,    n ≈ 2 for sand molds(7)

TST is total solidification time; V/A — volume over cooling surface area — is the casting's modulus. The rule's power is comparative: you almost never compute Cm from (6)'s front factor. You measure it once, from a test casting poured in the same sand with the same alloy, and then (7) ranks every shape you care about. Thick, chunky regions (high V/A) freeze last; thin fins and edges (low V/A) freeze first. Directional solidification — freezing that marches from the extremities toward a feeder — is engineered by manipulating exactly this ratio.

AssumptionMold-controlled heat flow. Valid for sand and other insulating molds. In a water-cooled metal die the interface and metal resistances dominate instead, and n drifts toward 1 — Chvorinov with n = 2 will mislead you in die casting.
AssumptionSemi-infinite mold: the heated layer never reaches the flask wall. Fails for thin mold sections between two cavities, which saturate and slow their cooling.
AssumptionPlanar interface at uniform Tm; edges and corners ignored. Corners are shared by two or three heat-flow directions and freeze faster than (7) predicts — the practical fix is judgment, or simulation (Lecture 11: casting simulation in Industry 4.0 workflows).

05Paying the volume debt

Shrinkage, and the riser that covers it

Metal contracts three separate times on the way from pouring temperature to room temperature, and the three contractions are compensated by three different people. Confusing them is the most reliable way to fail this course's exam — and to scrap castings professionally.

  • Liquid contraction (Tp → Tm): the melt shrinks as it sheds superheat. Cheap to fix — the gating system simply supplies a little more liquid while everything is still fluid.
  • Solidification shrinkage (at Tm): the liquid→solid density jump. For aluminum roughly 6–7 % by volume; for low-carbon steel roughly 2.5–3 %; gray cast iron is the famous exception, expanding slightly as low-density graphite flakes form. (Representative values — Groover Ch. 10 tabulates them by alloy.) This is the contraction that carves shrinkage cavities and porosity into the last regions to freeze, and this is the riser's job.
  • Solid contraction (Tm → room temperature): plain thermal contraction of the solid part. No liquid exists to feed it — instead the patternmaker builds the part oversize by the shrinkage allowance (order of 1–2 % linear, alloy-specific) so it contracts down onto the drawing.

Riser design logic

A riser is a sacrificial reservoir of liquid connected to the casting. While the casting solidifies and its volume deficit opens up, the riser must still be liquid, and must stay liquid longer than everything it feeds — otherwise the pipe of shrinkage forms in the part instead of in the riser. Chvorinov turns that sentence into arithmetic:

Riser requirement (design rule used in Groover's worked examples)
TSTriser = 1.25 × TSTcasting   ⟹   (V/A)riser = √1.25 (V/A)casting(8)

The 25 % margin covers what the model ignores (connection geometry, corner effects, Cm scatter). The geometry problem is then: choose a riser shape whose modulus meets (8) with the least wasted metal. Since a sphere maximizes V/A it is the thermally ideal riser — and an impractical one to mold and cut off, which is why practice settles on cylinders with height comparable to diameter. Every gram in the riser is remelted, so riser design is ultimately an economics problem: casting yield — part weight over total poured weight — is a line item the melt shop lives by.

06·AWorked example — on the board

Example 1 · Gating an aluminum casting

An aluminum casting of cavity volume 1,200 cm³ is gravity-poured. The sprue is 200 mm long, and the pouring basin holds the free metal surface 50 mm above the sprue entrance. The sprue base cross-section is 2.5 cm². Find the velocity at the sprue base, the flow rate, the mold fill time, the sprue top area that prevents aspiration, and the flow regime.

GIVEN  V = 1,200 cm³ · sprue L = 200 mm · basin head = 50 mm · Ab = 2.5 cm²
PROPERTIES  liquid Al near 700 °C: ρ ≈ 2,380 kg/m³, μ ≈ 1.3×10⁻³ Pa·s (handbook values)
ASSUME  frictionless flow (eq. 2), basin kept full (v₁ ≈ 0), cavity vented (atmospheric at both stations)
S1
Establish the head — from the free surface, not the sprue top

The streamline starts at the basin's free surface. Head at the sprue base is basin depth plus sprue length:

h_b = 0.050 m + 0.200 m = 0.250 m
S2
Velocity at the sprue base — equation (2)
v_b = √(2 · 9.81 m/s² · 0.250 m) = √(4.905 m²/s²) = 2.21 m/s
S3
Flow rate — continuity, equation (3)
Q = A_b · v_b = (2.5×10⁻⁴ m²)(2.21 m/s) = 5.54×10⁻⁴ m³/s = 554 cm³/s
S4
Mold fill time
t_fill = V / Q = 1,200 cm³ / 554 cm³/s = 2.17 s  — the melt's superheat must survive 2.2 s of transit; it will.
S5
Sprue top area — anti-aspiration taper, equation (4)

Head at the sprue top is the basin depth alone, h_t = 0.050 m:

A_t / A_b = √(h_b / h_t) = √(0.250 / 0.050) = √5 = 2.24
A_t = 2.24 × 2.5 cm² = 5.6 cm²  — the sprue narrows from 5.6 to 2.5 cm² over its length
S6
Flow regime — Reynolds number at the base

Equivalent diameter of the 2.5 cm² base section: D = √(4A/π) = √(4 · 2.5×10⁻⁴/π) = 0.0178 m.

Re = ρ v D / μ = (2,380)(2.21)(0.0178) / (1.3×10⁻³) ≈ 7.2×10⁴  — fully turbulent (transition ~2,300)
Engineering read-outAt Re ≈ 72,000 this pour is violently turbulent no matter how elegant the taper. The taper prevents aspiration; it does not prevent surface turbulence. A real aluminum gating design would now add a ceramic foam filter after the sprue base and enlarge the gates to slow the stream below the ~0.5 m/s oxide-entrainment threshold before metal enters the cavity.

06·BWorked example — on the board

Example 2 · Sizing a riser by Chvorinov's rule

A low-carbon-steel plate casting, 600 mm × 300 mm × 60 mm, is poured in green sand. A test casting poured under identical conditions established the mold constant Cm = 2.0 min/cm² (with n = 2). Design a cylindrical side riser with H = 1.5 D that satisfies the 25 % rule, and evaluate what it costs in yield.

GIVEN  plate 60 × 30 × 6 cm · C_m = 2.0 min/cm² · n = 2 · riser H = 1.5 D · rule: TST_r = 1.25 TST_c
ASSUME  riser cools on all surfaces (conservative is the opposite — see S5); plate cools on all six faces
S1
Modulus of the casting
V_c = 60 × 30 × 6 = 10,800 cm³
A_c = 2(60·30) + 2(60·6) + 2(30·6) = 3,600 + 720 + 360 = 4,680 cm²
(V/A)_c = 10,800 / 4,680 = 2.31 cm
S2
Solidification time of the casting — equation (7)
TST_c = 2.0 min/cm² × (2.31 cm)² = 2.0 × 5.33 = 10.7 min
S3
Required riser modulus — equation (8)
TST_r = 1.25 × 10.7 = 13.3 min
(V/A)_r = √(TST_r / C_m) = √(13.3 / 2.0) = √6.66 = 2.58 cm
S4
Translate modulus into cylinder dimensions

For a cylinder with H = 1.5 D, cooling on side, top and bottom:

V_r = (πD²/4)(1.5D) = 1.178 D³
A_r = πD(1.5D) + 2(πD²/4) = 1.5πD² + 0.5πD² = 2πD² = 6.283 D²
(V/A)_r = 1.178 D³ / 6.283 D² = 0.1875 D

0.1875 D = 2.58 cm  ⟹  D = 13.8 cm ,  H = 1.5 D = 20.6 cm
S5
Sanity checks — the part no one skips twice

Units: modulus in cm, Cm in min/cm², TST in minutes — consistent. Direction: (V/A)_r = 2.58 > 2.31 = (V/A)_c, so the riser does freeze last. Conservatism: a side riser shares its connection area with the casting, which removes cooling surface from A_r and makes the real riser freeze even later than computed — the error is on the safe side. The same argument warns you never to count a surface as "cooling" when it sits against an already-hot neighbor.

S6
The yield bill
V_r = 1.178 × (13.8)³ ≈ 3,090 cm³
yield (ignoring gating) = 10,800 / (10,800 + 3,090) = 77.8 %  — 22 % of every heat is remelted riser
Engineering read-outA fifth of the poured steel exists only to be sawed off and remelted. This is why foundries spend money on exothermic riser sleeves (they raise the riser's effective TST without raising its volume) and why the melt shop tracks casting yield as a first-class KPI. Thermal correctness and economic sanity are the same calculation run twice.

07Where students lose marks

Standing misconceptions, addressed directly

"h in v = √2gh is the sprue length."
h is the vertical distance from the free metal surface to the station you are evaluating. If the basin holds 50 mm of metal above a 200 mm sprue, the base sees 250 mm of head. Measure from the wrong datum and every downstream number — velocity, flow rate, fill time, taper ratio — inherits the error. Example 1, step S1 exists because of this mistake.
"A bigger riser always freezes later."
Chvorinov ranks shapes by V/A, not by V. A tall thin riser can hold more volume than a compact one and still freeze first, because it exposes disproportionate surface. When you enlarge a riser, check what happened to its modulus, not its mass. (The sphere wins precisely because it hides the most volume behind the least surface.)
"The pattern's shrinkage allowance takes care of shrinkage."
It takes care of solid contraction only — the part shrinking after it is already solid. Solidification shrinkage, the liquid→solid volume jump, happens while liquid still exists and must be fed by liquid, i.e., by the riser. Two different contractions, two different compensations, two different people responsible. Exam questions are written to catch exactly this conflation.
"n = 2 is part of Chvorinov's rule."
n = 2 is part of the sand-mold derivation — it came from the 1/√t flux of a semi-infinite insulating mold (04, Step 1). In permanent molds and die casting, interface and metal-side resistances take over and n falls toward 1. Carrying n = 2 into a die-casting calculation is not a small error; it changes the ranking of which sections freeze first.
"Pour faster — it can only help against misruns."
Faster pouring buys superheat margin and costs oxide entrainment, mold erosion, and gas pickup, because velocity is already deep in the turbulent regime (Example 1: Re ≈ 7×10⁴). The gating designer's actual instrument is geometry — taper, gate area, filters — not enthusiasm at the ladle. "As fast as necessary, as slow as possible" is the shop version of this trade-off.
Check your understanding — attempt before revealing

A cube and a sphere of the same alloy are poured into the same sand at the same time. Both have volume 512 cm³ (the cube is 8 cm on a side). The mold constant is Cm = 1.8 min/cm², n = 2.

Which solidifies last, and by how much? Compute both total solidification times. Then answer the design question hiding inside: what does this say about where a shrinkage cavity would form if the two were connected by a short bar?

Cube: V = 512 cm³, A = 6·(8 cm)² = 384 cm², so V/A = 1.33 cm and TST = 1.8 × (1.33)² = 3.2 min.

Sphere: r = (3V/4π)1/3 = (3·512/4π)1/3 = 4.96 cm. A = 4πr² = 309 cm². V/A = r/3 = 1.65 cm — note the elegant general result: a sphere's modulus is always r/3. TST = 1.8 × (1.65)² = 4.9 min.

The sphere solidifies last, by about 1.7 minutes — more than 50 % longer — despite identical volume, alloy, and mold. Surface area is the whole story: the sphere exposes 309 cm² against the cube's 384 cm².

Design reading: connect them with a bar and the pair freezes directionally: cube first, bar next (check its own modulus), sphere last. The sphere therefore acts as the riser, and the shrinkage cavity forms in the sphere, not the cube — which is precisely why risers are compact, chunky shapes and why (8) demanded the riser out-modulus the casting.

08Kuwait floor

The same equations, running around the clock at Kuwait Steel

Everything above was developed on a sand mold, but the highest-tonnage casting operation in Kuwait is a permanent-mold process: the continuous billet caster at Kuwait Steel (United Steel Industrial Company), where scrap is melted in an electric-arc furnace, refined at a ladle furnace, and cast into square billets that feed the rolling mill for construction rebar.

EAF melt shopScrap charge melted by electric arc; equation (1)'s energy bill, paid in megawatt-hours.
Ladle furnaceChemistry trim and temperature control — superheat is set here, deliberately, in degrees.
TundishBuffer reservoir; steady head above the mold — a pouring basin the size of a truck, doing v₁ ≈ 0's job.
Water-cooled Cu moldOscillating copper mold freezes a thin shell; mold-side control, the n → 1 world of 07.
Spray zonesSecondary cooling grows the shell as the strand curves toward horizontal.
Torch cutoffOnly past the metallurgical length — where the core is finally solid — can the strand be cut.

Shell growth is Chvorinov's cousin

Inside the caster, solidification runs continuously: a solid skin forms against the water-cooled copper mold and thickens as the strand travels. The same 1/√t heat-flow physics that gave us equation (6) gives the shell-growth law used across the continuous-casting literature:

δ = K √t(9)

with δ the shell thickness and K a solidification constant (for water-cooled billet and slab casters, typically quoted in the range 25–30 mm/min½). The strand is fully solid when δ reaches the billet's half-thickness d/2, and since the strand moves at casting speed v, that happens a fixed distance from the mold — the metallurgical length:

Metallurgical length
Lm = v · tfull = v (d2K)2(10)

Run the numbers for a 130 mm square billet — representative values for billet casters of this class, not published Kuwait Steel operating data — with K = 27 mm/min½ and casting speed v = 2.6 m/min:

t_full = (65 mm / 27 mm·min⁻½)² = (2.41)² = 5.8 min   ⟹   L_m = 2.6 m/min × 5.8 min ≈ 15 m
shell at mold exit (0.8 m mold): t = 0.8/2.6 = 0.31 min  ⟹  δ = 27√0.31 ≈ 15 mm

Read those two numbers the way the plant does. Fifteen metres of strand — through the mold, down the curve, past the straightener — still has liquid steel in its core; the torch is not allowed to exist before that point. And at the mold exit, the entire ferrostatic head of that liquid core is carried by a 15 mm skin. Push casting speed from 2.6 to 3.4 m/min and the exit shell thins to about 13 mm — this is why casting speed is not a free productivity knob, and why a breakout (shell rupture, liquid steel over the machine) is among the most feared events in a melt shop.

This is also where this course touches the four roles this curriculum aims at. The reliability engineer owns the mold-oscillator and spray-system health that keep (9)'s K stable; the asset-integrity engineer owns the mold copper and segment rollers that contain the liquid core; the production engineer trades casting speed against breakout risk using exactly equation (10); the maintenance planner schedules mold and segment changes against the wear that silently degrades K. One equation, four job descriptions.

Field note — — the vertical stack: ladle → tundish → mold is gravity gating, 03 scaled up ten-thousand-fold. The shrouds between vessels do the same job as a tapered sprue: keep the falling stream sealed from air.
Field note — — the billet is cut while glowing but fully solid. The torch station's position on a caster is not a layout convenience; it is a solved inequality — downstream of Lm, always.
