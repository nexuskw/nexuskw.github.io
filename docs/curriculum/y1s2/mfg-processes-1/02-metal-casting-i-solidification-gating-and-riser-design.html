01 · Casting is a controlled thermal collapse

A casting process melts metal, pours it into a cavity shaped like the part, and lets it solidify — the oldest manufacturing process still run at industrial scale, surviving because nothing else produces complex internal geometry in one operation this cheaply. Expendable-mold processes (sand, shell, investment) destroy the mold to retrieve the part — cheap tooling, high geometric freedom. Permanent-mold processes (die casting, continuous casting) reuse a metal mold — expensive tooling that demands volume to pay for itself. This lecture develops the physics common to all of them using sand casting, since its assumptions are cleanest: pay energy to superheat the melt, spend that superheat transporting metal through the gating system before it freezes in the channels, then extract all of it back out through the mold wall at a rate the mold controls — and the volume deficit left behind by that extraction is the riser's problem.

02 · Gating: Bernoulli with consequences

The gating system has one job: deliver the melt to the cavity before superheat runs out, without turbulence tearing air and oxide into the stream. Applying Bernoulli's equation from the free metal surface in the pouring basin to the sprue base — both stations near atmospheric pressure, the basin kept topped up so its surface barely moves — gives Torricelli's result, \( v=\sqrt{2gh} \), with \(h\) the vertical head from the free metal surface, never from the sprue's own top. Because velocity rises with depth, a parallel-walled sprue lets the falling stream neck away from its walls and aspirate air; matching the sprue's taper to the falling jet via continuity, \( A_t/A_b=\sqrt{h_b/h_t} \), removes that defect with one line of algebra. The flow is, in practice, always fully turbulent (Reynolds numbers in the tens of thousands for liquid metal) — the designer's real tools are geometry (taper, gate area, filters), not pouring speed.

03 · Solidification, and the equation that runs the foundry

A pure metal freezes at one temperature; an alloy freezes across a range, liquidus to solidus, with a mushy zone of dendritic growth between them. For a sand mold — a poor enough conductor that the mold, not the metal, controls the rate — treating the mold wall as a semi-infinite solid gives a clean result: total solidification time follows Chvorinov's rule, \[ TST=C_m\left(\frac{V}{A}\right)^{n},\qquad n\approx2\text{ for sand molds}, \] where \(V/A\) is the casting's modulus. \(C_m\) is measured once from a test casting in the same sand and alloy, then the rule ranks every shape: thick, chunky regions (high modulus) freeze last; thin fins (low modulus) freeze first. This mold-controlled assumption fails in a water-cooled metal die, where interface and metal-side resistance dominate instead and \(n\) drifts toward 1 — carrying \(n=2\) into a die-casting estimate is a real, not cosmetic, error.

04 · Shrinkage, and the riser that pays for it

Metal contracts three separate times, compensated by three different people. Liquid contraction (pouring temperature down to freezing) is cheap — the gating system simply supplies a little more liquid while everything is still fluid. Solidification shrinkage — the liquid-to-solid density jump, roughly \(6\)–\(7\)% for aluminum, \(2.5\)–\(3\)% for low-carbon steel — is the riser's job: a sacrificial reservoir that must still be liquid, and must stay liquid longer than everything it feeds, or the shrinkage cavity forms in the part instead. Chvorinov turns "stay liquid longer" into arithmetic: sizing the riser so \( TST_{riser}=1.25\times TST_{casting} \) — a 25% margin covering what the model ignores — translates directly into a required riser modulus, \( (V/A)_{riser}=\sqrt{1.25}\,(V/A)_{casting} \). Solid contraction (freezing point down to room temperature) has no liquid left to feed it at all; the patternmaker builds the pattern oversize by a shrinkage allowance instead, so the part contracts down onto the drawing.

Key rule — Chvorinov (1940)

\[ TST = C_m\left(\frac{V}{A}\right)^{n},\quad n\approx2\ (\text{sand}).\qquad (V/A)_{riser}=\sqrt{1.25}\,(V/A)_{casting}. \]

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Sprue base velocity and taper

Problem. A pouring basin holds 50 mm of metal above a 200 mm sprue. Find the velocity at the sprue base, and the top-to-base area ratio needed to prevent aspiration.

Solution. Head at the base: \(h_b=0.050+0.200=0.250\ \text{m}\). \( v=\sqrt{2\times9.81\times0.250}\approx2.21\ \text{m/s} \). Head at the top is the basin depth alone, \(h_t=0.050\ \text{m}\): \( A_t/A_b=\sqrt{h_b/h_t}=\sqrt{0.250/0.050}=\sqrt5\approx2.24 \) — the sprue narrows by that factor from top to base.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Sizing a riser by Chvorinov’s rule

Problem. A steel plate casting has modulus \((V/A)_c=2.31\ \text{cm}\), and the mold constant is \(C_m=2.0\ \text{min/cm}^2\). Find the casting’s solidification time and the modulus a riser needs to satisfy the 25% rule.

Solution. \( TST_c=2.0\times2.31^2\approx10.7\ \text{min} \). Required \( TST_r=1.25\times10.7\approx13.3\ \text{min} \), so \( (V/A)_r=\sqrt{13.3/2.0}\approx2.58\ \text{cm} \) — larger than the casting’s modulus, confirming the riser freezes last.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Cube vs. sphere — same volume, different modulus

Problem. A cube and a sphere of the same alloy, same volume 512 cm\(^3\) (the cube is 8 cm on a side), are poured in the same sand, \(C_m=1.8\ \text{min/cm}^2\). Which solidifies last, and by roughly how much?

Solution. Cube: \(A=6\times8^2=384\ \text{cm}^2\), \(V/A=1.33\ \text{cm}\), \(TST=1.8\times1.33^2\approx3.2\ \text{min}\). Sphere: \(r\approx4.96\ \text{cm}\), \(A=4\pi r^2\approx309\ \text{cm}^2\), \(V/A=r/3\approx1.65\ \text{cm}\), \(TST\approx1.8\times1.65^2\approx4.9\ \text{min}\). The sphere solidifies last, by roughly 1.7 minutes — over 50% longer — despite identical volume, because Chvorinov ranks by surface area exposed, not volume.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A foundry engineer designing a gating system runs 2 exactly as derived: head measured from the free metal surface, sprue taper set by the area-ratio rule, and gate sizing chosen to slow the stream below the velocity that entrains oxide — get the datum for h wrong, and every downstream number (velocity, fill time, taper) inherits the error.

The same Chvorinov analysis that sizes a riser also scales up directly to continuous casting — a solid shell growing inward from a water-cooled mold at steady state, governed by the identical square-root-of-time physics. A regional steel mill running a billet caster around the clock is this lesson's physics in continuous operation rather than a single pour; that machine gets its own full treatment next lesson.
