00Before we start

What this lecture is for

Casting defects are not bad luck. Every one of them is a conservation law or a rate process that was not respected — gas that had to come out of solution, volume that had to shrink, heat that had to leave through somewhere. This lecture teaches the practice layer (patterns, sand, pouring) and then the defect families, each tied to its physical cause and to the inspection method that finds it.

What you will be able to do after this lecture

  1. Specify pattern allowances — shrinkage, draft, machining — for a given part and alloy, and state which contraction each one compensates.
  2. Read molding-sand test results (green compressive strength, permeability, moisture) and connect each number to the defects it predicts.
  3. Rank the factors controlling fluidity and distinguish a misrun from a cold shut by cause, not just appearance.
  4. Derive Sievert's law from chemical equilibrium and compute the gas rejected by a solidifying melt.
  5. Classify porosity by morphology into gas versus shrinkage — and choose the corrective action that matches the class.
  6. Check a riser's feed-volume adequacy and identify whether the thermal or the volumetric criterion governs its size.

01Foundations layer

What this lecture stands on

Two refreshers. The first compresses Lecture 1 — if any line of it surprises you, go back before continuing. The second is chemistry you had in first year and probably let rust; Sievert's law in 04 is built directly on it.

Refresher — Lecture 1 in five lines

R1 · Solidification, shrinkage, and risers

Sand-mold freezing is mold-controlled, and total solidification time follows Chvorinov's rule, TST = Cm(V/A)n with n ≈ 2 — thick, chunky regions (high modulus V/A) freeze last. Metal contracts three separate times: liquid contraction (fed by the gating system), solidification shrinkage (fed by the riser, or turned into cavities), and solid contraction (compensated by the patternmaker, not by liquid). A riser must out-modulus the casting — the design rule used in our worked example was TSTriser = 1.25 × TSTcasting. Full development with derivations: Lecture 1.

Refresher — not new material

R2 · Chemical equilibrium and the equilibrium constant

For a reaction aA ⇌ bB at equilibrium, the ratio of product activities to reactant activities — each raised to its stoichiometric coefficient — is a constant K that depends only on temperature. For a gas dissolving into a liquid metal, the gas's activity is its partial pressure and the dissolved species' activity is its concentration. That single sentence, plus one square root, is the entire derivation of Sievert's law in 04.

02From drawing to cavity

Patterns, allowances, and molding sand

The mold cavity is not the part. It is the part, deliberately wrong in four ways — each allowance compensating a specific physical effect that will happen later.

Pattern allowances

  • Shrinkage (patternmaker's) allowance. The pattern is built oversize so that solid contraction — the third contraction of R1 — shrinks the casting down onto the drawing dimensions. It is linear, alloy-specific, and on the order of 1–2% for common alloys (Groover, Ch. 10, tabulates by alloy). Note carefully what it does not do: it cannot compensate solidification shrinkage, which happens while liquid still exists and is the riser's job.
  • Draft. A taper — commonly on the order of 1–3° — on surfaces perpendicular to the parting line, so the pattern withdraws without tearing the sand. No draft, no clean mold; the defect appears before any metal is poured.
  • Machining allowance. Extra stock on surfaces that will be machined, sized to the process's surface condition and the section's distortion risk — millimetres, not microns, for sand castings.
  • Distortion allowance. For long or unsymmetrical parts, the pattern is pre-bent opposite to the predicted distortion. An admission, in wood, that residual stress is coming.

Internal geometry comes from cores — bonded-sand shapes seated in core prints, occasionally supported by metal chaplets that fuse into the casting. Every core is also a gas source and a restraint on contraction; both facts return in 04.

Green sand as an engineering material

A green-sand mold is a composite: silica sand grains, bonded by a few percent of bentonite clay, activated by a few percent of water, plus additives (Groover, Ch. 11 gives representative recipes). It is cheap, reusable, and quietly sophisticated — and it is specified by test numbers, not by feel:

Green compressive strengthThe mold's ability to hold its shape against handling and metal pressure. Too low: wall collapse, swells, wash. Too high (over-rammed): gas cannot escape and the mold resists contraction — see hot tears.
PermeabilityThe rate at which gases vent through the compacted sand. Every pour generates steam and binder gas; if it cannot leave through the sand, it leaves through the metal — as blowholes.
MoistureThe clay's activator and the steam source in one number. It is a window, not a maximum: too dry crumbles, too wet boils.
Compactability / hardnessConsistency checks on ramming practice — the shop-floor guardrails that keep the first three numbers meaningful from mold to mold.

Read those four as a system and you can predict a foundry's defect profile before seeing a single casting: low permeability plus high moisture is a blowhole shop; low strength is a penetration-and-wash shop; over-rammed strong sand with hot metal is a hot-tear shop.

03Filling quality

Fluidity, misruns, and cold shuts

Lecture 1 computed how fast metal moves. This section asks how far it gets before it freezes — because a stream that stops early leaves a void with a name.

Fluidity in the foundry sense is not the inverse of viscosity. It is measured, not derived: the melt is poured into a standard spiral channel and the distance it runs before freezing — the fluidity length — is the fluidity. The physics behind the number is a race: the stream advances at the gating velocity while losing heat to the channel walls, and it stops when solidification pinches the flow. Flemings (Solidification Processing, fluidity chapter) models exactly this: fluidity length is the product of stream velocity and the time available before the flowing channel freezes shut,

Lf ≈ v · tf(1)

with tf governed by the same mold-controlled heat extraction as Chvorinov's rule — which is why fluidity rises with superheat (more heat to lose before freezing), falls with freezing range (mushy alloys clog the channel long before they are fully solid), and falls with oxide films on the stream front. We take (1) as a sourced order-of-magnitude model, not a design equation; its value is that it makes the factor list physical instead of folklore:

  • Superheat — the dominant controllable factor, bought at the price 04 will show.
  • Freezing range — pure metals and eutectics run farthest; wide-range alloys stop early. (Diagram 2 of Lecture 1 is the reason.)
  • Mold properties — a faster-extracting mold shortens tf.
  • Surface films — aluminum's instant oxide skin stiffens the stream front and cuts its run.

When fluidity loses the race, the defect has two names with two different causes. A misrun is a section the metal never reached — the stream froze short. A cold shut is where two streams met but were each too cold to fuse — a seam with an oxide film inside it, often looking deceptively like a crack. The distinction matters because the fixes differ: a misrun asks for more superheat, faster filling, or thicker sections; a cold shut asks additionally for gating that avoids opposed, long-path streams.

04The defect families

Every defect is a conservation law with a grievance

Classify before you fix. The families below are organized by cause — gas, volume, stress, mold, and cleanliness — because the cause picks the corrective action, and the morphology picks the family.

Family 1 — Gas porosity, and Sievert's law derived

Liquid metals dissolve gases; solids barely do. That solubility cliff at the freezing front is the entire mechanism of gas porosity. The quantitative law comes straight from refresher R2. Diatomic hydrogen dissolves by splitting into two atoms:

H2 (gas) ⇌ 2 [H]dissolved(2)

Write the equilibrium constant with the gas at partial pressure p and the dissolved atoms at concentration [H] — two of them, so the concentration is squared:

K(T) = [H]2pH₂   ⟹   [H] = √K(T) · √pH₂(3)
Sievert's law — dissolved diatomic gas scales with the square root of its pressure
[H] = S0(T) √p / p0(4)

The square root is not an empirical fit — it is the stoichiometry of (2) doing algebra. S0 rises steeply with temperature (K(T) is Arrhenius-like), which is the hidden price of superheat: a hotter melt runs farther (03) and drinks more hydrogen from every damp tool, unbaked ladle, and humid hour of a Gulf summer. At solidification the solubility drops by an order of magnitude, the rejected atoms recombine to H2, and the result is pinholes — small, spherical, smooth-walled pores, typically scattered near surfaces and upper regions. Their spherical smoothness is the signature: gas pores grew as bubbles in liquid. (Aluminum–hydrogen is the classic pair; Campbell's Complete Casting Handbook is the authority on both this and the oxide bifilms below.)

Mold and core gas is the other gas source: steam and binder combustion products that could not escape through the sand (02's permeability number) instead punch blowholes — larger, often subsurface cavities near cores and re-entrant mold features.

Family 2 — Shrinkage porosity

Lecture 1's third section said it: solidification shrinkage that the riser does not feed becomes a cavity in the casting. It appears at two scales. Macro-shrinkage — open pipe under the riser or a closed cavity at an unfed hot spot — is a feeding-path failure you can see. Microporosity is subtler: in the mushy zone of a wide-freezing-range alloy, the last interdendritic liquid is cut off from feed metal by the dendrite forest and shrinks in place, leaving jagged, angular, tree-lined voids. The morphology contrast with gas porosity is diagnostic and it is the substance of Diagram 2.

Family 3 — Hot tears

Between solidus and full strength, a casting is a hot, weak solid being squeezed by its own contraction against a mold and cores that do not move. Where sections meet — a T-junction, a boss, an internal corner — the junction is both the last region to freeze (highest local modulus; Lecture 1's corner argument in reverse) and the point where two contracting arms pull in different directions. If the demanded strain exceeds the near-solidus ductility, the metal tears: a ragged, branching, oxidized crack that follows grain boundaries. A cold crack, by contrast, happens far below the solidus — it is straight, clean-faced, and un-oxidized. Telling them apart matters because the tear asks for mold/core collapsibility and junction redesign; the crack asks for residual-stress and handling review.

Families 4 and 5 — Mold failures and cleanliness

Mold and sand defects read straight off 02's test sheet: penetration (metal soaks into weak or coarse sand — rough, sand-crusted surfaces), scabs and washes (mold surface spalled or eroded by the stream — 03's velocity discipline again), mismatch and flash (cope-to-drag alignment and clamping). Cleanliness defects are melt-practice failures: slag and dross carried in (gating design and filters — Lecture 1's Reynolds argument), loose sand swept along, and Campbell's bifilms — folded oxide skins entrained by turbulent streams that later open into cracks and leak paths. The gating system you learned to size in Lecture 1 was quietly doing defect prevention all along.

05·AWorked example — on the board

Example 1 · Hydrogen pickup and porosity potential in aluminum

An aluminum melt is held at about 750 °C under an atmosphere where moisture gives an effective hydrogen partial pressure of 0.04 atm. Representative handbook-order solubilities (per Campbell; values vary with alloy and temperature): liquid ≈ 1.0 cm³ of H₂ (at standard conditions) per 100 g at 1 atm; solid ≈ 0.05 cm³/100 g at 1 atm. Estimate the dissolved hydrogen, the gas rejected on freezing, and the worst-case porosity fraction.

GIVEN  p = 0.04 atm · S₀(liq) = 1.0 cm³/100 g · S₀(sol) = 0.05 cm³/100 g · ρ(liq Al) ≈ 2,380 kg/m³
MODEL  Sievert's law, eq. (4); complete rejection of excess gas as pores (worst case); gas volumes at standard conditions
S1
Dissolved hydrogen in the liquid — Sievert's law
[H]_liq = 1.0 × √(0.04 / 1) = 1.0 × 0.20 = 0.20 cm³/100 g
S2
What the solid can keep — same law, solid solubility
[H]_sol = 0.05 × √0.04 = 0.05 × 0.20 = 0.01 cm³/100 g
S3
Rejected at the freezing front
Δ[H] = 0.20 − 0.01 = 0.19 cm³/100 g = 1.9 cm³ of H₂ per kg  — gas volume at standard conditions
S4
Worst-case porosity fraction

One kilogram of liquid aluminum occupies:

V_metal = 1,000 g / 2.38 g·cm⁻³ = 420 cm³
porosity ≈ 1.9 / 420 = 0.0045 = ≈ 0.5 % by volume  — if every rejected atom became a retained pore
Engineering read-outHalf a percent porosity is enough to scrap a pressure-tight or fatigue-critical casting — from nothing but humid air over the melt. The levers all live in eq. (4): lower p (dry tools, dry charge, covered melt), lower S₀ (don't superheat beyond need — the 03 trade-off made quantitative), or strip the hydrogen before pouring (rotary degassing with an inert purge gas drops [H] by giving the melt a low-p atmosphere to equilibrate against — Sievert's law run in reverse, on purpose).

05·BWorked example — on the board

Example 2 · Is Lecture 1's riser big enough to feed?

Lecture 1, Example 2 sized a cylindrical riser (D = 13.8 cm, H = 20.6 cm, V ≈ 3,090 cm³) for a 10,800 cm³ low-carbon-steel plate on the thermal criterion — it freezes 25 % later than the casting. A riser must also pass the volumetric criterion: it must contain enough usable liquid to feed the casting's solidification shrinkage. Check it.

GIVEN  V_casting = 10,800 cm³ · V_riser = 3,090 cm³ · steel solidification shrinkage ≈ 3 % by volume (representative; Groover Ch. 10 tabulates by alloy)
MODEL  usable feed metal ≈ 14 % of an open cylindrical riser's volume — a conservative planning figure from foundry practice (the rest is lost to the riser's own pipe and skin; exothermic sleeves raise it)
S1
Feed demand from the casting
V_feed = 0.03 × 10,800 cm³ = 324 cm³
S2
Feed supply from the riser
V_supply = 0.14 × 3,090 cm³ = 433 cm³  — margin = 433/324 = 1.34 ✓ adequate
S3
Which criterion actually governed?

Invert the check: the smallest riser that feeds 324 cm³ at 14 % usable is

V_min(feeding) = 324 / 0.14 = 2,314 cm³ < 3,090 cm³
— the thermal (Chvorinov) criterion demanded the larger riser, so it governs for this steel plate.
S4
Now watch the criterion flip

Same geometry in aluminum, solidification shrinkage ≈ 6.6 % (representative, Groover):

V_feed = 0.066 × 10,800 = 713 cm³ ⟹ V_min(feeding) = 713 / 0.14 = 5,093 cm³
— now feeding demands a riser ~65 % larger than the thermal one. The volumetric criterion governs.
Engineering read-outA riser has two jobs — stay liquid longer (thermal) and hold enough liquid (volumetric) — and the binding one depends on the alloy's shrinkage appetite. Size against both, always; the check costs four lines. This is also why high-shrinkage alloys lean on exothermic sleeves and multiple risers: raising the usable fraction is cheaper than paying the remelt bill on a monster riser.

06Where students lose marks

Standing misconceptions, addressed directly

"Porosity is porosity — one fix fits all."
Gas porosity and shrinkage porosity have different causes, different morphologies (Diagram 2), and disjoint fixes. Degassing a shrinkage problem or re-risering a hydrogen problem each spends real money to fix the wrong physics. Classify from morphology and location first; only then touch the process.
"Degassing removes shrinkage porosity."
Degassing lowers [H] in eq. (4) — it addresses gas pores only. Shrinkage porosity is a volume deficit; no amount of hydrogen removal supplies the missing 3–7 % of liquid. That is feeding-path work: risers, chills, junction design. The two families share nothing but the word 'pore'.
"More superheat is free filling insurance."
Superheat buys fluidity (03) and simultaneously buys higher S₀ in Sievert's law, more mold-metal reaction, coarser structure, and more penetration into the sand. Example 1's 0.5 % porosity came from exactly this 'insurance'. Pour as hot as the thinnest section requires and no hotter — the window is an optimization, not a maximum.
"A crack is a crack — hot tears are just cracks that happened early."
A hot tear is ragged, branching, intergranular, and oxidized inside (it opened while hot, in air); a cold crack is straight and clean-faced. The tear indicts junction design, feeding, and sand collapsibility; the crack indicts residual stress, quenching, or handling. Fracture-surface reading is the cheapest failure analysis you will ever perform.
"It passed visual, so the casting is sound."
Every internal family — microporosity, blowholes, bifilms — is invisible from the surface by construction. Soundness claims need volumetric evidence (UT, RT) or a sectioning/test-bar program matched to the casting's duty. 'Looks good' is a statement about paint, not metallurgy; WLD 305 turns this into a full NDT selection lesson.
Check your understanding — attempt before revealing

A machined aluminum casting shows two populations of voids. Population A: smooth-walled, near-spherical pores, 0.1–0.5 mm, scattered through the upper machined face. Population B: a single jagged, branching cavity at the center of the thickest boss, far from the riser.

Classify each population and name the one corrective action that matches each. Then the calculation: if melt hygiene halves the effective hydrogen partial pressure over the melt, by what factor does dissolved hydrogen fall — and why is it not one-half?

Population A is gas (hydrogen) porosity: spherical and smooth because the pores grew as bubbles in liquid; scattered and surface-favoring is the classic pinhole distribution. Matching action: melt hygiene and rotary degassing — lower [H] via eq. (4). Riser changes do nothing for this population.

Population B is shrinkage microporosity/cavity: jagged and interdendritic at the thickest, last-to-freeze, worst-fed location. Matching action: fix the feeding path — a riser or chill re-arranged so the boss freezes toward feed metal (Lecture 1's directional-solidification argument). Degassing does nothing for this population.

The calculation: Sievert's law scales with √p, so halving p gives [H]′/[H] = √(½) ≈ 0.71 — a 29 % reduction, not 50 %. The square root comes from the 2:1 stoichiometry of H₂ ⇌ 2[H]: the dissolved concentration is squared in the equilibrium constant, so pressure changes reach it only through a square root. Halving dissolved hydrogen requires cutting p to one quarter.

07Kuwait floor

Where this lecture cashes out: cast components in a HEISCO-type service shop

Kuwait does not run large sand foundries — but it runs thousands of castings: pump casings, valve bodies, impellers, and marine components, bought cast and kept alive by fabrication-and-services companies. HEISCO's stated scope — fabrication, ship and industrial repair, and NDT services — is exactly the receiving end of this lecture. (The workflow below is representative of such service scopes, not a description of any specific HEISCO contract.)

Walk a cast seawater-pump casing through an overhaul bay with this lecture in hand. Incoming assessment: visual for the mold-family defects (penetration roughness, old repair scars), then PT on wetted and stressed surfaces — dye penetrant finds the surface-breaking family: hot tears at section junctions, cold shuts, cracks grown in service. Volumetric check: UT thickness and scanning on the thick sections the riser was supposed to feed — Diagram 2's jagged population, if the original foundry lost the feeding argument, plus erosion-corrosion thinning from Gulf seawater. Disposition: the 04 taxonomy decides it. Isolated gas pores in a low-stress wall may be acceptable per the applicable standard; interdendritic shrinkage in a pressure boundary is not. Repair: excavate the defect, verify removal by PT, weld repair with a qualified procedure, and re-inspect — the WPS/PQR discipline that WLD 305 teaches, applied to a casting instead of a plate.

The interview translation: when a Kirby or HEISCO panel asks "how would you disposition an indication on a cast component?", this lecture is the answer's spine — classify by family, judge by duty and standard, repair with qualified procedure, verify by the method that found it.

Field note — — even here, this lecture applies: internal shrinkage and pinhole populations in the cast billet become the rolled bar's inclusions and seams. Lesson 04 (continuous casting) picks up this thread at Kuwait Steel's caster.
