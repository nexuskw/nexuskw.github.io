01 · Patterns, allowances, and molding sand

A mold cavity is deliberately wrong, in four specific ways. The shrinkage (patternmaker's) allowance builds the pattern oversize so solid contraction shrinks the casting back onto the drawing — it cannot compensate solidification shrinkage, which is the riser's job (Lesson 2). Draft, a taper of roughly \(1\)–\(3^\circ\) on surfaces perpendicular to the parting line, lets the pattern withdraw without tearing the sand. Machining allowance leaves extra stock on surfaces to be machined. Distortion allowance pre-bends a pattern opposite to its predicted distortion. Internal geometry comes from cores — bonded-sand shapes that are also a gas source and a restraint on contraction. Green sand itself is specified by test numbers, not by feel: green compressive strength (too low risks collapse; too high traps gas and resists contraction), permeability (how well gases vent — low permeability plus wet sand is a blowhole problem waiting to happen), and moisture (too dry crumbles, too wet boils).

02 · Fluidity, misruns, and cold shuts

Fluidity, in the foundry sense, is measured, not derived — the distance a standard test channel fills before the stream freezes shut. It is a race between the advancing stream and mold-controlled heat loss, and it rises with superheat, falls with a wide freezing range (a mushy alloy clogs the channel before it is fully solid), and falls with a stiff surface oxide film. When the race is lost, the resulting defect has two names with two different causes: a misrun is a section the metal never reached at all; a cold shut is where two streams met but were each too cold to fuse, leaving an oxide-lined seam that can look deceptively like a crack. The fixes differ — a misrun asks for more superheat or faster filling; a cold shut additionally asks for gating that avoids opposed, long-path streams.

03 · Gas porosity, and Sievert's law derived

Liquid metal dissolves gas far more readily than solid metal does — that solubility cliff at the freezing front is the entire mechanism of gas porosity. Hydrogen dissolves by splitting into two atoms, \( \text{H}_2\rightleftharpoons2[\text{H}] \), so the equilibrium constant carries the dissolved concentration squared against the gas partial pressure: \( K(T)=[\text{H}]^2/p_{\text{H}_2} \). Solving gives Sievert's law, \[ [\text{H}]=S_0(T)\sqrt{p/p_0}, \] a square root that is not an empirical fit — it is the stoichiometry of the dissolution reaction doing algebra. \(S_0\) rises steeply with temperature, the hidden price of superheat: a hotter melt runs farther (02) and dissolves more hydrogen from every damp tool or humid hour. At solidification, solubility drops sharply, the rejected gas recombines, and the result is pinholes — small, spherical, smooth-walled pores, their roundness the signature of having grown as bubbles in liquid. Mold and core gas — steam and binder combustion products that could not vent through the sand — is the other source, producing larger, often subsurface blowholes instead.

04 · Shrinkage porosity, and the working method for a defect

Solidification shrinkage the riser fails to feed becomes a cavity, at two scales. Macro-shrinkage is an open pipe or closed cavity at an unfed hot spot — a feeding-path failure, visible once sectioned. Microporosity is subtler: in the mushy zone of a wide-freezing-range alloy, the last interdendritic liquid is cut off by the surrounding dendrite forest and shrinks in place, leaving jagged, angular, tree-lined voids — a morphology that contrasts directly with gas porosity's round, smooth pores. The working method for any defect: (1) read the morphology — round and smooth points to gas, jagged and angular points to shrinkage; (2) trace it to its physical cause using the taxonomy — solubility drop, volume deficit, lost heat race, restrained contraction, mold failure, or melt cleanliness; (3) let the cause pick the fix, not the appearance alone, since a misrun and a cold shut can look similar but need different corrections.

Key tool — Sievert's law

\[ [\text{H}] = S_0(T)\sqrt{p/p_0} \]

A square-root relation from reaction stoichiometry — the mechanism behind pinhole porosity.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Reading a defect’s morphology

Problem. A sectioned casting shows small, round, smooth-walled pores scattered near the upper surface. A second casting shows jagged, angular voids threaded between visible dendrite arms near its thermal center. Identify each defect family and its physical cause.

Solution. The first is gas porosity (pinholes) — round, smooth pores are the signature of gas bubbles that grew in the liquid before being trapped, caused by a solubility drop at freezing (Sievert's law). The second is microporosity — angular, interdendritic voids caused by the last liquid being cut off from feed metal in the mushy zone, a volume-deficit (shrinkage) cause, not a gas cause.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Applying Sievert’s law

Problem. At a reference pressure \(p_0\), a melt dissolves \(S_0=25\) ppm of hydrogen. If the effective hydrogen partial pressure is raised to \(4p_0\) (from humid tooling), find the new dissolved concentration.

Solution. \( [\text{H}]=S_0\sqrt{p/p_0}=25\times\sqrt4=25\times2=50\) ppm — doubling, not quadrupling, because the relationship is a square root of pressure, direct from the \(\text{H}_2\rightleftharpoons2[\text{H}]\) stoichiometry.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Misrun or cold shut?

Problem. A thin fin on a casting is simply absent — no metal reached that section at all. A separate casting shows a fused-looking seam where two streams met, but the seam pulls apart easily under light stress, with an oxide film visible inside. Identify each defect and state one distinct corrective action for each.

Solution. The absent fin is a misrun — the stream froze before reaching that section; corrective actions include more superheat or faster filling. The weak, oxide-lined seam is a cold shut — two streams met too cold to truly fuse; correction additionally requires gating that avoids opposed, long-path streams meeting head-on, not superheat alone.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A foundry's molding-sand test sheet — green compressive strength, permeability, moisture, logged shift by shift — predicts that shop's defect profile before a single casting is sectioned: low permeability paired with high moisture reads as a blowhole risk; low strength reads as a penetration-and-wash risk; over-rammed strong sand with hot metal reads as a hot-tear risk. A metallurgist reading that sheet is running 1 in reverse, from cause toward prediction rather than from defect toward diagnosis.

Sectioning a rejected casting and reading its pore morphology under a loupe — round and smooth versus jagged and angular — is standard first-pass failure triage in any melt shop, cheaper and faster than radiography for an initial call on whether a batch problem is gas-related (atmosphere, moisture, melt practice) or feeding-related (riser or gating design), before deciding which department's process actually needs adjusting.
