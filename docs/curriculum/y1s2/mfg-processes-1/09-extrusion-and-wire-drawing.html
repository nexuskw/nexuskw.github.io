Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What is the extrusion ratio when a 100 mm billet becomes a 25 mm rod?
  • Why is a 60% area reduction impossible in one wire-drawing pass even with a perfect, frictionless die?
  • A pass gives draw stress 60 MPa on wire whose exit flow stress is 160 MPa: safe or failing, and by what criterion?

01 · Two die processes, opposite stress states

Extrusion pushes metal through a die under compression — a ram compresses the billet, forcing it through a reducing opening. That compressive state tolerates enormous single-pass strains (extrusion ratios of 40 or more for aluminum) and can even improve workability, since compression tends to close internal voids rather than open them. Wire and rod drawing pulls metal through a die instead — the already-reduced material downstream of the die supplies the pulling force, which means the drawn material itself must survive that tensile stress without yielding. That one difference in sign — compression versus tension — explains both processes' products: massive sections from extrusion, kilometres of fine wire from drawing.

02 · Extrusion: ratio, empirical strain, pressure

Extrusion ratio is \( r_x=A_0/A_f \). Johnson's empirical extrusion strain folds ideal work, friction, and redundant shear into one number, \( \varepsilon_x=a+b\ln r_x \) (typically \(a\approx0.8\), \(b\approx1.2\)–\(1.5\)), giving a ram pressure estimate \( p=\bar Y_f\,\varepsilon_x \).

03 · Drawing: stress, and the survival check

Drawing stress is \[ \sigma_d=\bar Y_f\left(1+\frac{\mu}{\alpha}\right)\phi\ln\!\left(\frac{A_0}{A_f}\right), \] with \(\mu\) the die friction, \(\alpha\) the die half-angle in radians, and \(\phi\geq1\) a redundant-work factor. The absolute constraint: \(\sigma_d\) must stay well below the drawn wire's own flow stress at that exit strain, or the wire yields and necks at the die exit instead of drawing cleanly through. This gives the classical result that even a perfectly frictionless, ideal pass cannot exceed roughly \(63\)% area reduction (\(\varepsilon=1\)); real passes, carrying friction and redundant work, run well below that. For a copper drawing pass, \(3.50\to3.03\ \text{mm}\) (\(25\)% area reduction), \(K=300\ \text{MPa}\), \(n=0.50\), \(\mu=0.07\), \(\alpha=6^\circ\): \(\bar Y_f\approx107\ \text{MPa}\), \(\phi\approx1.05\), giving \( \sigma_d\approx54\ \text{MPa} \) — comfortably below the drawn wire's own flow stress of \(\approx161\ \text{MPa}\), a safe pass with real margin.

04 · Tandem drawing — the working method

Because a single pass is capped well below the theoretical ceiling by real friction and redundant-work margins, producing fine wire from a much larger rod requires many passes in sequence — tandem drawing. Each pass is re-checked against its own \(\sigma_d\)-versus-flow-stress safety margin; because strain hardening accumulates pass over pass, that margin shrinks as drawing proceeds, which is exactly why intermediate anneals are scheduled partway through a tandem sequence — resetting accumulated hardening so later passes still have a comfortable margin, rather than letting it shrink toward zero and risking a break.

Key constraint — drawing stress

\(\sigma_d\) must stay below the drawn wire's own flow stress — the real, binding limit, well short of the ideal 63% ceiling.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Checking a drawing pass for safety

Problem. Copper wire is drawn from 3.50 to 3.03 mm (25% area reduction), \(K=300\ \text{MPa}\), \(n=0.50\), \(\mu=0.07\), \(\alpha=6^\circ\). Given \(\bar Y_f\approx107\ \text{MPa}\) and \(\phi\approx1.05\), find the drawing stress and confirm the pass is safe.

Solution. \( \sigma_d=107\times(1+0.07/0.105)\times1.05\times\ln(1/0.75)\approx54\ \text{MPa} \). The drawn wire’s own flow stress is \(300\times0.288^{0.5}\approx161\ \text{MPa}\), far above 54 MPa — a safe pass with real margin.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Estimating extrusion pressure

Problem. A 200 mm diameter aluminum billet is extruded to 50 mm diameter. Find the extrusion ratio, and using \(a=0.8\), \(b=1.5\), \(\bar Y_f\approx70\ \text{MPa}\), estimate the ram pressure.

Solution. \( r_x=(200/50)^2=16 \). \( \varepsilon_x=0.8+1.5\ln16\approx0.8+4.16\approx4.96 \). \( p\approx70\times4.96\approx347\ \text{MPa} \).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

How many passes, and why anneal?

Problem. A wire must go from 10 mm to 3 mm diameter (91% area reduction total), but each pass is limited to about 30% area reduction before the safety margin runs thin. Estimate the number of passes needed, and explain why anneals are scheduled partway through.

Solution. Each pass leaves 70% of the entering area; reaching \((3/10)^2\approx9\)% of the original area needs \(n\) where \(0.70^n\approx0.09\), giving \(n\approx6.7\), so about 7 passes. Anneals are scheduled partway through because strain hardening accumulates pass over pass, raising the wire's flow stress and shrinking the safety margin between drawing stress and that flow stress — an anneal resets the accumulated hardening so later passes keep a workable margin instead of drawing toward a break.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A wire-drawing line's die-wear and lubricant-condition log is a direct read on 3's safety-margin logic: a die wearing out of round, or a lubricant breaking down under pressure, raises effective friction and redundant work, which raises drawing stress at the same nominal reduction — narrowing the margin against the wire's own flow stress until a wire break interrupts the line, often before any other symptom shows up on a routine visual inspection.

Extrusion tooling for aluminum sections is chosen and maintained around 2's pressure estimate: a die designed for a modest extrusion ratio pressed into service for a much higher ratio than it was rated for is a predictable, avoidable cause of premature die wear or failure, traceable directly back to the ram-pressure number a design review should have checked before the tooling order was placed.
