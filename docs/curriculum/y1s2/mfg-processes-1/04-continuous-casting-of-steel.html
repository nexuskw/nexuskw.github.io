01 · The machine, traced

A continuous caster runs Lesson 2's solidification physics at steady state, around the clock. Steel flows ladle to tundish — a steady-head reservoir, doing the pouring basin's job at enormous scale — then into a water-cooled, oscillating copper mold, where a thin solid shell freezes against the walls while the core stays liquid. The strand then travels through spray-cooled support rollers, curving toward horizontal, before a straightener and finally a torch cutoff — which cannot operate until the core is actually solid.

02 · Shell growth and metallurgical length

The same \(1/\sqrt{t}\) heat-flow physics behind Chvorinov's rule gives the shell-growth law used across continuous casting: \[ \delta = K\sqrt{t}, \] with \(\delta\) the solid shell thickness, \(t\) the time since the strand entered the mold, and \(K\) a solidification constant (typically \(25\)–\(30\ \text{mm/min}^{1/2}\) for water-cooled casters). The strand is fully solid once \(\delta\) reaches the billet's half-thickness \(d/2\); at casting speed \(v\), that happens a fixed distance below the mold — the metallurgical length: \[ L_m = v\,t_{full} = v\left(\frac{d}{2K}\right)^{2}. \] Note that \(t_{full}\) itself depends only on \(d\) and \(K\) — it does not depend on casting speed at all; only the distance \(L_m\) does, since a faster strand simply covers more ground before the same fixed time elapses.

03 · Reading the numbers

For a \(130\ \text{mm}\) square billet with \(K=27\ \text{mm/min}^{1/2}\) and casting speed \(v=2.6\ \text{m/min}\): \( t_{full}=(65/27)^2\approx5.8\ \text{min} \), so \( L_m=2.6\times5.8\approx15\ \text{m} \) — fifteen meters of strand, through the mold and down the curve, still carries a liquid core; the torch is never positioned before that point. At the mold exit (a \(0.8\ \text{m}\) mold, \(t=0.8/2.6\approx0.31\ \text{min}\)): \( \delta=27\sqrt{0.31}\approx15\ \text{mm} \) — the entire ferrostatic head of that fifteen-meter liquid core is carried by a fifteen-millimeter skin. Push casting speed to \(3.4\ \text{m/min}\) and the exit shell thins to about \(13\ \text{mm}\) while \(L_m\) grows to nearly \(20\ \text{m}\) — casting speed is never a free productivity lever; it trades directly against shell thickness and the depth of the liquid core.

04 · Quality connection and working method

Billet defects trace to specific subsystems: pinholes to dissolved gas in the melt; internal and centerline cracks to thermal stress and bulging between misaligned or worn support rollers; rhomboidity to uneven mold cooling; centerline segregation to the last, solute-rich liquid at the core — Lesson 3's dendritic argument, running at machine scale. A shell rupture — a breakout — pours liquid steel over the machine and is among the most feared events in a melt shop. Working method: (1) find \(t_{full}\) and \(L_m\) for the section and machine in question; (2) treat any casting-speed change as a three-way trade against shell thickness, breakout risk, and segregation quality, never as a pure throughput knob; (3) trace a specific billet defect back to the subsystem — mold, spray, rollers — most likely responsible.

Key result — metallurgical length

\[ L_m = v\left(\frac{d}{2K}\right)^{2} \]

t_full does not depend on casting speed — only how far the strand travels before reaching it does.

Metallurgical length

Problem. A 130 mm square billet caster runs \(K=27\ \text{mm/min}^{1/2}\) at \(v=2.6\ \text{m/min}\). Find the total solidification time and the metallurgical length.

Solution. \( t_{full}=(65/27)^2\approx5.8\ \text{min} \). \( L_m=2.6\times5.8\approx15\ \text{m} \).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Shell thickness at mold exit

Problem. For the same caster (K = 27 mm/min^0.5, v = 2.6 m/min), the mold is 0.8 m long. Find the shell thickness at mold exit.

Solution. Time to mold exit: \( t=0.8/2.6\approx0.31\ \text{min} \). \( \delta=27\sqrt{0.31}\approx15\ \text{mm} \).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

The speed trade-off

Problem. If casting speed on the same machine is raised to 3.4 m/min, find the new exit shell thickness and the new metallurgical length. Does t_full change?

Solution. \( t_{full} \) stays \(\approx5.8\ \text{min}\) — unchanged, since it depends only on d and K. Exit shell: \( t=0.8/3.4\approx0.235\ \text{min} \), \( \delta=27\sqrt{0.235}\approx13.1\ \text{mm} \) — thinner. Metallurgical length: \( L_m=3.4\times5.8\approx19.7\ \text{m} \) — longer. Speed trades a thinner exit shell and a deeper liquid core for more tonnage.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A regional steel mill's route — melting, ladle refining, billet casting, rolling — makes this lesson one of the most directly transferable process-knowledge units in the whole curriculum. All four reliability-adjacent roles this program targets touch the caster in a different way: mold and oscillator health (reliability), strand containment against breakout (asset integrity), the speed-versus-quality trade-off (production), and mold or segment-roller change planning (maintenance planning) — one machine, four job descriptions.

A billet certificate flagged for centerline segregation, read back against 4, points an investigating engineer toward casting-speed history and core-cooling records before anything else — the defect is generated at the machine, not downstream, and no amount of rolling or heat treatment afterward removes solute banding that formed while the core was still liquid.
