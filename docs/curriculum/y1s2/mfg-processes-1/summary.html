MFG 105 — Manufacturing Processes I — Casting & Bulk Forming

Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · The manufacturing plant: process taxonomy and material flow

01 · The six-family taxonomy

Processing operations transform a workpart. Shaping covers four distinct routes: casting and molding (start liquid), deformation — rolling, forging, extrusion, drawing, sheet forming (start solid, move it), particulate processing (press and sinter powders), and material removal (machining, grinding, cutting away). Property-enhancing operations — heat treatment above all — leave geometry unchanged and rebuild microstructure instead. Surface processing — cleaning, coating, plating — changes only the surface. Assembly operations join parts: permanent joining (welding, brazing, adhesives) and mechanical assembly (bolting, riveting, press fits). A seventh box, material handling and inspection, adds no value directly but consumes most of the lead time in a real plant.

02 · Primary vs. secondary, and reading a real process chain

Primary processing creates the basic shape — a billet, a casting — from raw material. Secondary processing refines that shape further — machining a bearing seat, grinding a journal onto an already-cast or already-forged part. The working method: trace any product from raw material to dispatch, naming which family each step belongs to. A rebar's chain is casting (shaping, primary) then hot rolling (shaping, deformation) then cooling, cutting, and bundling (handling) — two value-adding families for one product. A power-cable conductor's chain runs longer: wire drawing (deformation), annealing (property-enhancing), stranding (mechanical assembly), insulation extrusion over the conductor (shaping, polymer), spooling and test (handling, inspection) — four families in a single line.

03 · Why this taxonomy matters more than it looks

By tonnage, the world's metal is shaped overwhelmingly by casting and deformation, not machining — machining refines a surface or a fit onto a shape one of those other families already created. An engineer walking into "manufacturing" expecting mainly a machine shop is statistically far more likely to meet a caster, a rolling mill, a press, or a filling line as the dominant floor space. Casting and deformation are not legacy technology on the way out; they remain the primary-shaping backbone of everything heavier than electronics. What has changed around them is instrumentation, automation, and control — exactly where this curriculum's later Industry 4.0 material points.

04 · Working method — reading any plant with one map

Given an unfamiliar plant or process line: (1) identify the raw material entering; (2) trace each transformation step and classify it into one of the six families; (3) note where primary shaping ends and secondary refinement begins; (4) recognize that handling and inspection, though value-neutral, often dominate real lead time and hide a large share of any real improvement opportunity. This single procedure reads a steel mill, a cable plant, or a food-filling hall with equal clarity.

Key fact

By tonnage, casting and deformation shape the large majority of the world's metal — machining refines a shape another process already created, it does not usually create the shape itself.

Lesson 02 · Metal casting I — solidification, gating, and riser design

01 · Casting is a controlled thermal collapse

A casting process melts metal, pours it into a cavity shaped like the part, and lets it solidify — the oldest manufacturing process still run at industrial scale, surviving because nothing else produces complex internal geometry in one operation this cheaply. Expendable-mold processes (sand, shell, investment) destroy the mold to retrieve the part — cheap tooling, high geometric freedom. Permanent-mold processes (die casting, continuous casting) reuse a metal mold — expensive tooling that demands volume to pay for itself. This lecture develops the physics common to all of them using sand casting, since its assumptions are cleanest: pay energy to superheat the melt, spend that superheat transporting metal through the gating system before it freezes in the channels, then extract all of it back out through the mold wall at a rate the mold controls — and the volume deficit left behind by that extraction is the riser's problem.

02 · Gating: Bernoulli with consequences

The gating system has one job: deliver the melt to the cavity before superheat runs out, without turbulence tearing air and oxide into the stream. Applying Bernoulli's equation from the free metal surface in the pouring basin to the sprue base — both stations near atmospheric pressure, the basin kept topped up so its surface barely moves — gives Torricelli's result, \( v=\sqrt{2gh} \), with \(h\) the vertical head from the free metal surface, never from the sprue's own top. Because velocity rises with depth, a parallel-walled sprue lets the falling stream neck away from its walls and aspirate air; matching the sprue's taper to the falling jet via continuity, \( A_t/A_b=\sqrt{h_b/h_t} \), removes that defect with one line of algebra. The flow is, in practice, always fully turbulent (Reynolds numbers in the tens of thousands for liquid metal) — the designer's real tools are geometry (taper, gate area, filters), not pouring speed.

03 · Solidification, and the equation that runs the foundry

A pure metal freezes at one temperature; an alloy freezes across a range, liquidus to solidus, with a mushy zone of dendritic growth between them. For a sand mold — a poor enough conductor that the mold, not the metal, controls the rate — treating the mold wall as a semi-infinite solid gives a clean result: total solidification time follows Chvorinov's rule, \[ TST=C_m\left(\frac{V}{A}\right)^{n},\qquad n\approx2\text{ for sand molds}, \] where \(V/A\) is the casting's modulus. \(C_m\) is measured once from a test casting in the same sand and alloy, then the rule ranks every shape: thick, chunky regions (high modulus) freeze last; thin fins (low modulus) freeze first. This mold-controlled assumption fails in a water-cooled metal die, where interface and metal-side resistance dominate instead and \(n\) drifts toward 1 — carrying \(n=2\) into a die-casting estimate is a real, not cosmetic, error.

04 · Shrinkage, and the riser that pays for it

Metal contracts three separate times, compensated by three different people. Liquid contraction (pouring temperature down to freezing) is cheap — the gating system simply supplies a little more liquid while everything is still fluid. Solidification shrinkage — the liquid-to-solid density jump, roughly \(6\)–\(7\)% for aluminum, \(2.5\)–\(3\)% for low-carbon steel — is the riser's job: a sacrificial reservoir that must still be liquid, and must stay liquid longer than everything it feeds, or the shrinkage cavity forms in the part instead. Chvorinov turns "stay liquid longer" into arithmetic: sizing the riser so \( TST_{riser}=1.25\times TST_{casting} \) — a 25% margin covering what the model ignores — translates directly into a required riser modulus, \( (V/A)_{riser}=\sqrt{1.25}\,(V/A)_{casting} \). Solid contraction (freezing point down to room temperature) has no liquid left to feed it at all; the patternmaker builds the pattern oversize by a shrinkage allowance instead, so the part contracts down onto the drawing.

Key rule — Chvorinov (1940)

\[ TST = C_m\left(\frac{V}{A}\right)^{n},\quad n\approx2\ (\text{sand}).\qquad (V/A)_{riser}=\sqrt{1.25}\,(V/A)_{casting}. \]

Lesson 03 · Metal casting II — practice, quality, and defects

01 · Patterns, allowances, and molding sand

A mold cavity is deliberately wrong, in four specific ways. The shrinkage (patternmaker's) allowance builds the pattern oversize so solid contraction shrinks the casting back onto the drawing — it cannot compensate solidification shrinkage, which is the riser's job (Lesson 2). Draft, a taper of roughly \(1\)–\(3^\circ\) on surfaces perpendicular to the parting line, lets the pattern withdraw without tearing the sand. Machining allowance leaves extra stock on surfaces to be machined. Distortion allowance pre-bends a pattern opposite to its predicted distortion. Internal geometry comes from cores — bonded-sand shapes that are also a gas source and a restraint on contraction. Green sand itself is specified by test numbers, not by feel: green compressive strength (too low risks collapse; too high traps gas and resists contraction), permeability (how well gases vent — low permeability plus wet sand is a blowhole problem waiting to happen), and moisture (too dry crumbles, too wet boils).

02 · Fluidity, misruns, and cold shuts

Fluidity, in the foundry sense, is measured, not derived — the distance a standard test channel fills before the stream freezes shut. It is a race between the advancing stream and mold-controlled heat loss, and it rises with superheat, falls with a wide freezing range (a mushy alloy clogs the channel before it is fully solid), and falls with a stiff surface oxide film. When the race is lost, the resulting defect has two names with two different causes: a misrun is a section the metal never reached at all; a cold shut is where two streams met but were each too cold to fuse, leaving an oxide-lined seam that can look deceptively like a crack. The fixes differ — a misrun asks for more superheat or faster filling; a cold shut additionally asks for gating that avoids opposed, long-path streams.

03 · Gas porosity, and Sievert's law derived

Liquid metal dissolves gas far more readily than solid metal does — that solubility cliff at the freezing front is the entire mechanism of gas porosity. Hydrogen dissolves by splitting into two atoms, \( \text{H}_2\rightleftharpoons2[\text{H}] \), so the equilibrium constant carries the dissolved concentration squared against the gas partial pressure: \( K(T)=[\text{H}]^2/p_{\text{H}_2} \). Solving gives Sievert's law, \[ [\text{H}]=S_0(T)\sqrt{p/p_0}, \] a square root that is not an empirical fit — it is the stoichiometry of the dissolution reaction doing algebra. \(S_0\) rises steeply with temperature, the hidden price of superheat: a hotter melt runs farther (02) and dissolves more hydrogen from every damp tool or humid hour. At solidification, solubility drops sharply, the rejected gas recombines, and the result is pinholes — small, spherical, smooth-walled pores, their roundness the signature of having grown as bubbles in liquid. Mold and core gas — steam and binder combustion products that could not vent through the sand — is the other source, producing larger, often subsurface blowholes instead.

04 · Shrinkage porosity, and the working method for a defect

Solidification shrinkage the riser fails to feed becomes a cavity, at two scales. Macro-shrinkage is an open pipe or closed cavity at an unfed hot spot — a feeding-path failure, visible once sectioned. Microporosity is subtler: in the mushy zone of a wide-freezing-range alloy, the last interdendritic liquid is cut off by the surrounding dendrite forest and shrinks in place, leaving jagged, angular, tree-lined voids — a morphology that contrasts directly with gas porosity's round, smooth pores. The working method for any defect: (1) read the morphology — round and smooth points to gas, jagged and angular points to shrinkage; (2) trace it to its physical cause using the taxonomy — solubility drop, volume deficit, lost heat race, restrained contraction, mold failure, or melt cleanliness; (3) let the cause pick the fix, not the appearance alone, since a misrun and a cold shut can look similar but need different corrections.

Key tool — Sievert's law

\[ [\text{H}] = S_0(T)\sqrt{p/p_0} \]

A square-root relation from reaction stoichiometry — the mechanism behind pinhole porosity.

Lesson 04 · Continuous casting of steel

01 · The machine, traced

A continuous caster runs Lesson 2's solidification physics at steady state, around the clock. Steel flows ladle to tundish — a steady-head reservoir, doing the pouring basin's job at enormous scale — then into a water-cooled, oscillating copper mold, where a thin solid shell freezes against the walls while the core stays liquid. The strand then travels through spray-cooled support rollers, curving toward horizontal, before a straightener and finally a torch cutoff — which cannot operate until the core is actually solid.

02 · Shell growth and metallurgical length

The same \(1/\sqrt{t}\) heat-flow physics behind Chvorinov's rule gives the shell-growth law used across continuous casting: \[ \delta = K\sqrt{t}, \] with \(\delta\) the solid shell thickness, \(t\) the time since the strand entered the mold, and \(K\) a solidification constant (typically \(25\)–\(30\ \text{mm/min}^{1/2}\) for water-cooled casters). The strand is fully solid once \(\delta\) reaches the billet's half-thickness \(d/2\); at casting speed \(v\), that happens a fixed distance below the mold — the metallurgical length: \[ L_m = v\,t_{full} = v\left(\frac{d}{2K}\right)^{2}. \] Note that \(t_{full}\) itself depends only on \(d\) and \(K\) — it does not depend on casting speed at all; only the distance \(L_m\) does, since a faster strand simply covers more ground before the same fixed time elapses.

03 · Reading the numbers

For a \(130\ \text{mm}\) square billet with \(K=27\ \text{mm/min}^{1/2}\) and casting speed \(v=2.6\ \text{m/min}\): \( t_{full}=(65/27)^2\approx5.8\ \text{min} \), so \( L_m=2.6\times5.8\approx15\ \text{m} \) — fifteen meters of strand, through the mold and down the curve, still carries a liquid core; the torch is never positioned before that point. At the mold exit (a \(0.8\ \text{m}\) mold, \(t=0.8/2.6\approx0.31\ \text{min}\)): \( \delta=27\sqrt{0.31}\approx15\ \text{mm} \) — the entire ferrostatic head of that fifteen-meter liquid core is carried by a fifteen-millimeter skin. Push casting speed to \(3.4\ \text{m/min}\) and the exit shell thins to about \(13\ \text{mm}\) while \(L_m\) grows to nearly \(20\ \text{m}\) — casting speed is never a free productivity lever; it trades directly against shell thickness and the depth of the liquid core.

04 · Quality connection and working method

Billet defects trace to specific subsystems: pinholes to dissolved gas in the melt; internal and centerline cracks to thermal stress and bulging between misaligned or worn support rollers; rhomboidity to uneven mold cooling; centerline segregation to the last, solute-rich liquid at the core — Lesson 3's dendritic argument, running at machine scale. A shell rupture — a breakout — pours liquid steel over the machine and is among the most feared events in a melt shop. Working method: (1) find \(t_{full}\) and \(L_m\) for the section and machine in question; (2) treat any casting-speed change as a three-way trade against shell thickness, breakout risk, and segregation quality, never as a pure throughput knob; (3) trace a specific billet defect back to the subsystem — mold, spray, rollers — most likely responsible.

Key result — metallurgical length

\[ L_m = v\left(\frac{d}{2K}\right)^{2} \]

t_full does not depend on casting speed — only how far the strand travels before reaching it does.

Lesson 05 · Casting processes survey: sand to die casting

01 · The expendable-vs-permanent axis, revisited

Lesson 2 opened with the basic split: expendable-mold processes destroy the mold to retrieve the part, permanent-mold processes reuse a metal mold many times. This lesson surveys specific processes along that axis and compares them on the dimensions that actually drive a selection decision — tolerance, surface finish, minimum section thickness, and economics — dimensions the underlying physics from Lessons 2–3 does not address directly.

02 · Shell molding and investment casting — expendable, refined

Shell molding cures a thin, resin-bonded sand shell against a heated metal pattern — a real step up in dimensional accuracy and surface finish over green sand, still single-use, at moderate cost, well suited to medium-volume precision parts. Investment casting (lost-wax) builds a ceramic shell around a wax pattern, then melts the wax out before pouring metal into the resulting cavity. It achieves the best dimensional accuracy and surface finish of any casting process and can produce very complex internal geometry, but is slow and comparatively expensive per part — reserved for high-value, complex, precision components (turbine blades are the classic case) where machining the same geometry from solid would be impossible or far costlier.

03 · Permanent-mold and die casting

Gravity permanent-mold casting pours metal by gravity into a reusable metal mold — better surface finish and finer grain than sand casting, from faster metal-mold cooling, but geometry is more limited since the mold must survive many cycles rather than being sacrificed. Die casting injects molten metal — almost always a low-melting-point alloy, aluminum, zinc, or magnesium — under high pressure into a hardened steel die, filling extremely fast. It produces the thinnest sections, the finest surface finish, and the tightest tolerances of any casting process at very high production rates, but tooling cost is the highest of any casting process, demanding real volume to pay for itself, and the fast, turbulent fill entrains air the way Lesson 2's gating discussion warned against, at a far more extreme scale — the resulting porosity generally rules out heat-treating or welding a die casting afterward, since trapped gas expands and blisters the part on reheating.

04 · Choosing among them — the working method

Given a part: (1) check production volume — low volume favors expendable processes (sand, investment); high volume justifies permanent-mold or die-casting tooling; (2) check geometric complexity and required section thickness — investment casting and die casting both handle thin, complex geometry that sand casting cannot; (3) check surface finish and tolerance — die casting and investment casting both deliver near-net-shape parts needing little or no machining, while sand casting typically needs machining allowance on critical surfaces; (4) check whether the part will be heat-treated or welded afterward — a real constraint against die casting specifically, rarely against the others.

Key checklist — process selection

Volume → tooling cost justified? Complexity/thickness → which processes can hold it? Finish/tolerance → how much machining is budgeted? Post-processing → will it be heat-treated or welded?

Lesson 06 · Fundamentals of metal forming and workability

01 · Flow stress and the flow curve

Beyond yield, a metal's resistance to further plastic deformation — its flow stress \(Y_f\) — keeps rising with strain through strain hardening, commonly modeled as \[ Y_f = K\varepsilon^{n}, \] with \(K\) the strength coefficient and \(n\) the strain-hardening exponent, both material constants read from a tension test. The flow curve — true stress against true strain — is what a forming process actually pushes against, not the simpler engineering stress-strain curve, which is only valid up to necking. Forming operations generally impose a stress state that suppresses necking, so the flow curve remains meaningful at strains far beyond where an ordinary tension specimen would already have failed.

02 · Hot, warm, and cold working — defined by homologous temperature

The dividing line between these regimes is not an absolute temperature but homologous temperature, \(T/T_m\), both measured on an absolute (Kelvin) scale. Cold working, below roughly \(0.3\,T_m\), lets strain hardening accumulate fully — good surface finish and precision, but rising force and a limited total deformation before an anneal is needed. Hot working, above roughly \(0.6\,T_m\), runs hot enough that dynamic recrystallization continuously undoes strain hardening as it forms, so flow stress stays roughly constant regardless of strain — enormous shape change becomes possible for modest force, at the cost of poorer surface finish, looser tolerance, and oxide scale from the high temperature. Warm working sits between the two, trading some of each advantage.

03 · Strain-rate sensitivity

Flow stress also depends on how fast the material is deformed, particularly at hot-working temperature: \( Y_f=C\dot\varepsilon^{m} \), with \(m\) the strain-rate sensitivity exponent. At cold-working temperature, \(m\) is nearly zero — rate barely matters. At hot-working temperature, \(m\) rises meaningfully, and a higher \(m\) genuinely helps resist necking: a region that starts thinning locally strains faster there, and a high-\(m\) material responds by getting locally stronger, self-correcting the flaw rather than running away toward failure — the basis of superplastic forming in carefully controlled, very-high-\(m\) alloys.

04 · Friction, lubrication, and workability

Friction at the tool-workpiece interface resists metal flow and raises force — and, as later lessons on rolling and forging show, is sometimes the very mechanism a process depends on, sometimes purely a cost to minimize. Lubrication regimes differ sharply by temperature: hot working often runs with no lubricant, or a high-temperature glass or graphite coating; cold working uses oils, soaps, or coatings chosen to survive without breaking down under pressure. Workability — how much deformation a material tolerates before cracking — depends on both the material's inherent ductility and the stress state a specific process imposes: compressive-dominated processes (forging, rolling) are far more forgiving than tension-dominated ones (wire drawing), which is precisely why drawing carries a hard theoretical strain ceiling that compression-based processes do not share in the same way.

Key thresholds — homologous temperature

Cold work: \(T<0.3\,T_m\). Hot work: \(T>0.6\,T_m\) (both absolute). \( Y_f=K\varepsilon^n \) (cold, strain-hardening) vs. roughly flat (hot, dynamic recrystallization).

Lesson 07 · Rolling of metals

01 · The bite condition — checked before anything else

Flat rolling squeezes a slab or bar between two driven rolls, reducing thickness from \(h_0\) to \(h_f\). The draft, \(d=h_0-h_f\), cannot exceed \[ d_{max}=\mu^2R, \] or the rolls simply skid instead of biting into the stock — friction is what drives the material through the pass, not merely a cost to minimize as in some other forming processes. Checking this limit is the first step of any rolling calculation, before force, torque, or power are worth computing at all.

02 · Contact length, force, torque, and power

Once a pass is confirmed possible, the projected contact length is \( L=\sqrt{R(h_0-h_f)} \), and the remaining estimates follow directly: \[ F=\bar Y_f\,w\,L,\qquad T=0.5\,F\,L,\qquad P=2\pi N F L, \] with \(\bar Y_f\) the mean flow stress over the pass, \(w\) the strip width, and \(N\) the roll speed. For a hot-rolling pass — \(h_0=30\ \text{mm}\to h_f=22\ \text{mm}\), \(R=300\ \text{mm}\), \(w=250\ \text{mm}\), \(\mu=0.30\), \(\bar Y_f\approx180\ \text{MPa}\), \(N=50\ \text{rpm}\): the draft \(d=8\ \text{mm}\) is comfortably inside \(d_{max}=0.09\times300=27\ \text{mm}\); \(L=\sqrt{300\times8}\approx49\ \text{mm}\); \(F\approx180\times0.250\times0.049\approx2.2\ \text{MN}\) — roughly 225 tonnes-force on the stand; torque per roll \(\approx54\ \text{kN·m}\); power \(\approx566\ \text{kW}\).

03 · Hot vs. cold rolling — different products, not different settings

Hot rolling runs rough and grippy on purpose — friction is welcomed, because it buys large shape change cheaply above the recrystallization temperature (Lesson 6's hot-working regime), where flow stress stays comparatively low and roughly flat with strain. Cold rolling lubricates deliberately and accepts smaller drafts per pass, because it is selling a different outcome entirely: strain hardening raises strength, and the surface arrives clean, precise, and close to final dimension. These are two different products from two different working regimes, not the same process run at two settings.

04 · Reading the defect map

Wavy edges and center buckle trace to roll bending, making thickness — and therefore elongation — uneven across the strip width; crown and camber management on the rolls is the fix. Alligatoring, a centerline split at the exit, usually traces further upstream to casting quality (Lesson 4's centerline segregation returning at the mill). Rolled-in scale and seams trace to descaling practice and the incoming billet's own surface condition. In a rebar mill specifically, rib and grade-mark quality add roll wear as a first-class variable worth tracking, not an afterthought.

Key limit — the bite condition

\[ d_{max} = \mu^2 R \]

No friction, no bite, no process — but the same friction that drives the pass also raises force and wear.

Lesson 08 · Forging

01 · Open-die, impression-die, and flashless forging

Open-die forging uses simple, largely flat or lightly contoured dies that do not enclose the workpiece — metal flows outward freely as it compresses, good for large, simple shapes and preliminary shaping, with limited dimensional control. Impression-die (closed-die) forging constrains flow into a shaped cavity, giving far better dimensional control and complex shapes — but the excess metal has to go somewhere, which is what flash is for. Flashless forging matches starting billet volume precisely to the cavity so no flash forms at all, at the cost of needing tightly controlled billet volume and greater die and press investment.

02 · Upsetting analysis — force including friction's barreling penalty

For a cylinder upset from \(h_0\) to \(h\), true strain is \( \varepsilon=\ln(h_0/h) \) and flow stress follows the familiar \( Y_f=K\varepsilon^{n} \). Friction at the die faces resists the metal's outward radial flow near the platens more than at mid-height, producing the classic bulge — barreling. This friction penalty is captured by a multiplier \[ K_f = 1+\frac{0.4\mu D}{h}, \] which grows as the part gets shorter and wider (\(D/h\) increases) — the force penalty from friction worsens as upsetting proceeds, it does not stay constant. With the current cross-section \(A=V/h\) from volume conservation, force is \( F=K_f\,Y_f\,A \).

03 · Reading the numbers

Cold-upsetting a low-carbon steel slug, \(d_0=50\ \text{mm}\), \(h_0=70\ \text{mm}\to h=40\ \text{mm}\), \(K=500\ \text{MPa}\), \(n=0.25\), \(\mu=0.25\): volume \(V=137{,}400\ \text{mm}^3\), final area \(A=V/h=3{,}436\ \text{mm}^2\), diameter \(D=66.1\ \text{mm}\), strain \(\varepsilon=\ln(70/40)\approx0.56\), \( Y_f=500\times0.56^{0.25}\approx432\ \text{MPa} \), \( K_f=1+0.4\times0.25\times66.1/40\approx1.17 \), giving \( F\approx1.17\times432\times3{,}436\times10^{-3}\approx1.73\ \text{MN} \) — roughly 176 tonnes-force, and this is the peak force, occurring at the end of the stroke where both \(Y_f\) and \(K_f\) are largest.

04 · Grain flow and flash — what they genuinely buy

Forging refines structure, closes internal porosity, and — critically — aligns inclusion stringers and grain flow along the part's contour, rather than cutting across it the way machining from bar stock would. Properties improve specifically along the direction grain flow follows, particularly fatigue resistance; the advantage shrinks, and can even reverse, across the flow — so "forged is always stronger than cast or machined" is an oversimplification that ignores loading direction. Flash in impression-die forging is not wasted metal by accident: the thin flash land deliberately chokes outward flow, building the internal cavity pressure that forces metal into the last, hardest-to-fill corners of the die — engineered waste, trimmed off afterward, not an oversight.

Key multiplier — friction in upsetting

\[ K_f = 1+\frac{0.4\mu D}{h} \]

Grows as the part shortens and widens — peak force lands at the end of the stroke, not the start.

Lesson 09 · Extrusion and wire drawing

01 · Two die processes, opposite stress states

Extrusion pushes metal through a die under compression — a ram compresses the billet, forcing it through a reducing opening. That compressive state tolerates enormous single-pass strains (extrusion ratios of 40 or more for aluminum) and can even improve workability, since compression tends to close internal voids rather than open them. Wire and rod drawing pulls metal through a die instead — the already-reduced material downstream of the die supplies the pulling force, which means the drawn material itself must survive that tensile stress without yielding. That one difference in sign — compression versus tension — explains both processes' products: massive sections from extrusion, kilometres of fine wire from drawing.

02 · Extrusion: ratio, empirical strain, pressure

Extrusion ratio is \( r_x=A_0/A_f \). Johnson's empirical extrusion strain folds ideal work, friction, and redundant shear into one number, \( \varepsilon_x=a+b\ln r_x \) (typically \(a\approx0.8\), \(b\approx1.2\)–\(1.5\)), giving a ram pressure estimate \( p=\bar Y_f\,\varepsilon_x \).

03 · Drawing: stress, and the survival check

Drawing stress is \[ \sigma_d=\bar Y_f\left(1+\frac{\mu}{\alpha}\right)\phi\ln\!\left(\frac{A_0}{A_f}\right), \] with \(\mu\) the die friction, \(\alpha\) the die half-angle in radians, and \(\phi\geq1\) a redundant-work factor. The absolute constraint: \(\sigma_d\) must stay well below the drawn wire's own flow stress at that exit strain, or the wire yields and necks at the die exit instead of drawing cleanly through. This gives the classical result that even a perfectly frictionless, ideal pass cannot exceed roughly \(63\)% area reduction (\(\varepsilon=1\)); real passes, carrying friction and redundant work, run well below that. For a copper drawing pass, \(3.50\to3.03\ \text{mm}\) (\(25\)% area reduction), \(K=300\ \text{MPa}\), \(n=0.50\), \(\mu=0.07\), \(\alpha=6^\circ\): \(\bar Y_f\approx107\ \text{MPa}\), \(\phi\approx1.05\), giving \( \sigma_d\approx54\ \text{MPa} \) — comfortably below the drawn wire's own flow stress of \(\approx161\ \text{MPa}\), a safe pass with real margin.

04 · Tandem drawing — the working method

Because a single pass is capped well below the theoretical ceiling by real friction and redundant-work margins, producing fine wire from a much larger rod requires many passes in sequence — tandem drawing. Each pass is re-checked against its own \(\sigma_d\)-versus-flow-stress safety margin; because strain hardening accumulates pass over pass, that margin shrinks as drawing proceeds, which is exactly why intermediate anneals are scheduled partway through a tandem sequence — resetting accumulated hardening so later passes still have a comfortable margin, rather than letting it shrink toward zero and risking a break.

Key constraint — drawing stress

\(\sigma_d\) must stay below the drawn wire's own flow stress — the real, binding limit, well short of the ideal 63% ceiling.

Lesson 10 · Powder metallurgy

01 · The three-step process

Powder production — most commonly atomization, spraying molten metal into fine droplets that solidify as powder — sets particle size and shape, which strongly affects how well the powder compacts and flows. Compaction presses powder in a rigid die under high pressure into a green compact: a fragile, porous shape held together only by mechanical interlocking and cold welding at particle contact points, typically around \(80\)–\(85\)% of theoretical density — not yet a real structural part. Sintering heats that compact, below its melting point (typically \(70\)–\(90\)% of \(T_m\), Lesson 6's homologous-temperature logic returning here), in a controlled atmosphere, letting atomic diffusion bond particles together, close pores, and build real mechanical strength.

02 · Density is the controlling quality variable

Nearly every mechanical property of a PM part — strength, ductility, fatigue life, corrosion resistance — scales with its final density relative to the fully-dense wrought material of the same composition. A part at \(90\)% density can fall meaningfully short of wrought strength and ductility, because residual pores act as stress concentrators and crack-initiation sites — the same flaw logic that governs fracture mechanics generally. Density is set primarily by compaction pressure and sintering temperature and time, both pushing the compact closer to full density with diminishing returns; it approaches, but never quite reaches, \(100\)%. Secondary operations — repressing, infiltration with a lower-melting metal, resin impregnation — can push density and properties higher still, at added cost, used only when base sintered properties fall short of the target.

03 · Where PM wins

PM excels at small, complex parts at high volume: powder goes from raw material to near-net shape in one compaction step, needing little or no machining — the same volume-justifies-tooling economics as die casting. PM also uniquely delivers engineered porosity: a self-lubricating bronze or iron bearing is deliberately made at less than full density, then oil-impregnated into the connected pore network, so the part itself becomes a distributed oil reservoir — a property a fully-dense machined bearing simply cannot provide. Finally, PM handles materials no other route can: tungsten's melting point defeats casting, and a cemented-carbide cutting insert — hard tungsten-carbide particles bonded in a tougher cobalt matrix — is a composite structure that cannot be created by casting or by machining a homogeneous bar at all.

04 · Working method — selecting or specifying a PM part

(1) Translate the property target into a required density, or deliberately accept a lower density if porosity itself is the desired feature, as in a self-lubricating bearing. (2) Confirm volume justifies compaction die tooling cost, the same volume logic as Lesson 5's casting comparison. (3) Confirm the geometry is genuinely PM-favorable — no deep re-entrant features the die cannot eject — and that the material is either economical or genuinely hard to process any other way. (4) Decide sintered-only versus a secondary densification step based on whether base sintered properties actually meet the target.

Key fact — density controls properties

PM mechanical properties scale with final density relative to fully-dense material. Density is set by compaction pressure and sintering temperature/time — approaching, never reaching, 100%.

Lesson 11 · Process selection and cost fundamentals

01 · Property indices meet fixed vs. variable cost

Fixed cost — tooling, dies, molds, setup — is paid once (or per changeover) regardless of how many parts are made. Variable cost — material, energy, direct labor per cycle — scales directly with quantity. Total cost for a quantity \(Q\) is \[ C(Q) = \text{Fixed} + \text{Variable}\times Q. \] Two processes capable of making the identical part generally carry very different fixed and variable costs — a process with cheap tooling but a high per-part cost (sand casting) against one with expensive tooling but a low per-part cost (die casting) is exactly the volume logic Lessons 5 and 10 already touched, now made explicit and quantitative.

02 · Break-even volume — the working method

Setting two processes' total-cost lines equal and solving for \(Q\) gives the break-even volume: below it, the low-fixed-cost process is cheaper; above it, the low-variable-cost process is cheaper, by a widening margin as volume grows further. This single number is genuinely decision-driving, not academic — comparing a customer's actual expected volume against it is, in practice, how a manufacturing engineer chooses between competing processes more often than any single technical merit argument alone.

03 · Reading a real decision

Process A: \(\$2{,}000\) fixed, \(\$8\)/part. Process B: \(\$20{,}000\) fixed, \(\$2\)/part. Break-even: \( Q_{be}=(20{,}000-2{,}000)/(8-2)=3{,}000 \) units. Below \(3{,}000\) units, Process A wins; above it, Process B wins, and the gap widens quickly — at \(10{,}000\) units, Process A costs \(\$2{,}000+\$80{,}000=\$82{,}000\) against Process B's \(\$20{,}000+\$20{,}000=\$40{,}000\), roughly half. The method generalizes past two processes: with three or more candidates, compute every pair's break-even volume, and the cheapest process at any target volume is whichever line sits lowest at that specific \(Q\) — not necessarily the same process across the whole volume range.

04 · Beyond the break-even number

Break-even analysis assumes cost is the only criterion — a real decision still needs Lesson 5's and Lesson 10's technical screening first: can the process even achieve the required geometry, tolerance, finish, and material at all? Comparing costs between two processes that cannot both actually make the part is a wasted exercise. The analysis also assumes static per-part costs, ignoring that variable cost itself can fall with cumulative volume through learning-curve effects, and that any demand forecast carries real uncertainty. A decision made right at a razor-thin break-even margin is riskier than the arithmetic alone suggests — a prudent engineer builds in margin rather than committing to whichever process wins by a small amount at the forecast volume alone.

Key formula — break-even volume

\[ C(Q)=\text{Fixed}+\text{Variable}\times Q,\qquad Q_{be}=\frac{\text{Fixed}_B-\text{Fixed}_A}{\text{Variable}_A-\text{Variable}_B}. \]
