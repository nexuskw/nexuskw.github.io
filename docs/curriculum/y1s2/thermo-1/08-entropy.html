Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What does entropy generation measure that entropy change does not?
  • Why are isentropic processes the reference for real machines?

01 · Entropy as a property

Entropy \(S\) (specific \(s\)) is a property — fixed by the state, like \(U\), \(H\), and \(v\), in direct contrast to heat and work from Lesson 3, which depend on path. For an internally reversible process, \( dS=\delta Q/T \) defines entropy operationally; for a real, irreversible process, that relation does not directly give \( \Delta S\) — entropy is instead evaluated as a state property from tables, exactly like any other property, and the actual heat transferred is accounted separately through entropy generation, developed next.

02 · The entropy increase principle

For an isolated system — nothing crosses its boundary — total entropy can only increase, or in the ideal reversible limit stay the same; it can never decrease: \( \Delta S_{isolated}\geq0 \). This is the second law in its most general, quantitative form, and both statements from Lesson 7 follow from it: it is what rules out spontaneous heat flow from cold to hot, and it is what caps engine efficiency at the Carnot value.

03 · Entropy generation — the bookkeeping of irreversibility

For any process, not only isolated ones, the full accounting reads \( \Delta S_{system}=\displaystyle\int\frac{\delta Q}{T_{boundary}}+S_{gen} \), where \( S_{gen}\geq0 \) is entropy generated by irreversibilities inside the process — friction, unrestrained expansion, mixing, heat transfer across a finite temperature difference. \(S_{gen}=0\) only for a reversible process; every real process has \(S_{gen}>0\). This is the key distinction: entropy change can be positive, negative, or zero depending on the direction of heat transfer, even for a perfectly reversible process — but entropy generation is always non-negative, and it is the actual measure of how irreversible a real process was.

04 · T–s diagrams and the isentropic reference

An isentropic process — constant entropy, a vertical line on a T–s diagram — is simultaneously reversible and adiabatic, the theoretical best case for a device like a turbine or compressor with no heat loss. Real devices deviate to the right of that line, ending at higher entropy for the same starting point and pressure change, because real irreversibilities generate entropy along the way. This is exactly why isentropic processes serve as the reference for real-machine performance: comparing a real machine's actual outcome to the isentropic ideal for the same pressure change — the subject of Lesson 9 — measures how much irreversibility actually occurred inside it.

Key equation — entropy generation

\[ \Delta S_{system} = \int\frac{\delta Q}{T} + S_{gen},\qquad S_{gen}\geq0\ (=0\text{ only if reversible}). \]

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Isolating the generated entropy

Problem. A process has \( \Delta S_{system}=+2\ \text{kJ/K} \). Separately, \( \int\delta Q/T \) for the same process is found to equal \( +1.2\ \text{kJ/K} \). Find the entropy generated, and state what this number measures that \( \Delta S\) alone does not.

Solution. \( S_{gen}=\Delta S-\int\delta Q/T=2-1.2=0.8\ \text{kJ/K} \). This isolates the irreversibility actually produced inside the process; \( \Delta S\) alone mixes together the entropy carried in by heat transfer and the entropy generated internally, while \(S_{gen}\) captures only the internal, irreversible part.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Why the total entropy must rise

Problem. A hot block and a cold block of metal are placed in contact inside a perfectly insulated, sealed container and allowed to reach a common temperature. Without computing a number, explain why the total entropy of the two blocks together must have increased.

Solution. The combined system is isolated — nothing crosses its outer boundary. Heat transfer between the blocks across a finite temperature difference is an irreversible process, so by the entropy increase principle, total entropy must strictly increase, not merely stay the same, since this is a genuinely irreversible process rather than an idealized reversible limit.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Reading the gap on a T–s diagram

Problem. A compressor takes gas from state 1 to a higher pressure. The isentropic path lands at point 2s; the actual compressor lands at point 2, to the right of 2s at the same final pressure. What does the entropy difference between 2 and 2s represent, physically?

Solution. It represents the entropy generated by irreversibilities inside the real compressor — friction, turbulence — that do not exist in the idealized isentropic case. A real machine always generates at least as much entropy as the ideal case, never less, which is exactly why the actual point sits to the right of the isentropic one, never to the left.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A steam turbine's measured exit condition sitting noticeably to the right of its design isentropic point on a T–s or h–s diagram is exactly how a plant engineer confirms internal losses — blade erosion, seal leakage, fouling — without needing to open the casing; the horizontal distance from the isentropic reference is a direct, quantitative read on how much irreversibility (entropy generation) is happening inside the machine right now, tracked the same way over successive outages to catch a slow degradation trend.

A throttling valve dropping pressure across a control loop generates entropy even though it changes no enthalpy (Lesson 5's isenthalpic result) — a fact worth knowing when a process engineer is asked to justify why a valve, doing seemingly nothing to the energy balance, still shows up as a real loss in a plant's second-law (exergy) accounting rather than its first-law energy accounting.
