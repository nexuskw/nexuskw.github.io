Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • When does enthalpy replace internal energy in a balance — and why?
  • Rigid tank versus piston-cylinder heated equally: which ends hotter, and why?

01 · The energy balance, stated once

For a closed system, \( \Delta U = Q - W \) (using the sign convention fixed in Lesson 1). When kinetic and potential energy changes are negligible — true for almost every closed-system problem in this course — this is the entire first law needed. Every problem in this lesson is a variation on filling in this one equation.

02 · Internal energy versus enthalpy — when each applies

Internal energy \(U\) is the natural variable at constant volume: a rigid tank does no boundary work (\(W=0\)), so \(Q=\Delta U\) directly — all the heat added raises internal energy. Enthalpy, \(H=U+PV\), is the natural variable at constant pressure: it can be shown that \(Q=\Delta H\) directly there, because the definition of \(H\) already absorbs the \(P\Delta V\) boundary-work term. This is exactly why enthalpy exists as a defined property — it is the energy accounting tool built for constant-pressure processes, the way \(U\) is built for constant-volume ones. For an ideal gas, both depend on temperature alone: \( \Delta U=mc_v\Delta T \), \( \Delta H=mc_p\Delta T \).

03 · Rigid tank versus piston-cylinder, heated equally

Add the same heat \(Q\) to the same gas from the same starting temperature, in two different confinements. In a rigid tank, all of \(Q\) raises internal energy: \( \Delta T=Q/(mc_v) \). In a piston-cylinder held at constant pressure, part of \(Q\) does boundary work pushing the piston, leaving less to raise temperature: \( \Delta T=Q/(mc_p) \). Because \(c_p>c_v\) always (\(c_p=c_v+R\) for an ideal gas), the same heat produces a smaller temperature rise at constant pressure. For \(1\ \text{kg}\) of air at \(300\ \text{K}\) receiving \(50\ \text{kJ}\) (\(c_v=0.718\), \(c_p=1.005\ \text{kJ/(kg·K)}\)): the rigid tank reaches \(369.6\ \text{K}\), the piston-cylinder reaches only \(349.8\ \text{K}\) — the rigid tank ends hotter, by exactly the factor \(c_p/c_v\approx1.4\).

04 · Working method for a first-law closed-system problem

(1) Identify the confinement: rigid means constant volume, use \(U\) and \(c_v\); a piston free against a constant external pressure means constant pressure, use \(H\) and \(c_p\); anything else, use the general \( \Delta U=Q-W \) with the boundary work computed separately, as in Lesson 3. (2) Write the energy balance with the fixed sign convention. (3) Substitute the ideal-gas relation for \(\Delta U\) or \(\Delta H\). (4) Solve for whichever single quantity — \(Q\), \(W\), or the temperature change — is unknown.

Key rule — U vs. H

Constant \(V\): \(Q=\Delta U=mc_v\Delta T\). Constant \(P\): \(Q=\Delta H=mc_p\Delta T\). This is the reason enthalpy is a defined property.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Rigid tank vs. piston-cylinder, real numbers

Problem. 1 kg of air at 300 K receives 50 kJ of heat: (a) in a rigid, sealed tank; (b) in a piston-cylinder at constant pressure. Using \(c_v=0.718\), \(c_p=1.005\ \text{kJ/(kg·K)}\), find the final temperature in each case and say which ends hotter.

Solution. Rigid: \( \Delta T=50/0.718\approx69.6\ \text{K}\to T_2\approx369.6\ \text{K}\). Piston: \( \Delta T=50/1.005\approx49.8\ \text{K}\to T_2\approx349.8\ \text{K}\). The rigid tank ends hotter — none of its heat is diverted into boundary work.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Choosing U or H

Problem. A piston-cylinder holds air at constant pressure while \(30\ \text{kJ/kg}\) of heat is added. Which specific-heat relation applies directly, and why is internal energy alone not the right tool?

Solution. \(c_p\) applies directly: \(q=\Delta h=c_p\Delta T\). Internal energy alone is not enough because, at constant pressure, part of the added heat does boundary work rather than raising U directly — enthalpy is defined precisely to absorb that boundary-work term.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Same heat, different confinement — reasoning without numbers

Problem. A rigid tank of air rises 40 K for a given heat input. If the same heat input were applied instead at constant pressure to the same mass, would the resulting temperature rise be larger, smaller, or the same? Explain.

Solution. Smaller. Since \(c_p>c_v\), the same heat input produces a smaller \(\Delta T\) at constant pressure — part of the heat is diverted into boundary work pushing the piston, rather than entirely raising internal energy as it does in the rigid tank.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A relief-valve or overpressure study on a sealed, rigid vessel exposed to fire is a direct 3 application: with no way for the gas inside to expand and do work, every bit of absorbed heat drives temperature (and therefore pressure) up faster than the same heat would in an equivalent vessel with a free-moving boundary — a real reason rigid, fully-enclosed vessels are treated as a more severe fire case in relief-system sizing than a vented or piston-type system.

Choosing whether to model a heating or cooling step as constant-volume or constant-pressure is a routine first step before any plant energy balance is run, and getting it backward — using cv where cp applies, or the reverse — produces a temperature or heat-duty estimate that is wrong by roughly the cp/cv ratio, about 40% for air, which is large enough to matter in an equipment sizing calculation.
