Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Systems, properties, and state

01 · Systems and their boundaries

A system is whatever is under study; everything else is the surroundings, separated by a boundary. A closed system has a boundary that energy may cross, but mass may not — gas trapped in a sealed piston-cylinder is the standard example. An open system, or control volume, has a boundary crossed by both mass and energy — a pump, a length of pipe, a compressor. Almost every real piece of plant equipment is a control volume, which is why its own energy balance (Lesson 5) carries most of the weight for the rest of this course. An isolated system is the idealization where neither mass nor energy crosses at all — a useful limit case for the second law later in Lesson 8.

02 · Properties: intensive versus extensive

An extensive property depends on how much of the system is present — mass, volume, and total internal energy all double if the system doubles. An intensive property does not — temperature, pressure, and density are the same everywhere in a uniform system regardless of how much of it is being examined. Dividing an extensive property by mass produces its specific (intensive) counterpart: specific volume \(v=V/m\), specific internal energy \(u=U/m\), specific enthalpy \(h=H/m\). This lower-case convention — capital for the total (extensive) quantity, lower-case for the per-unit-mass (intensive) quantity — is used without further comment for the rest of the course.

03 · Equilibrium, process paths, and the state postulate

A system is in equilibrium when no unbalanced thermal, mechanical, or chemical potential remains to drive further change; only then does every property have one well-defined value throughout the system. A process is the change from one equilibrium state to another, and the specific sequence of states passed through along the way is its path — different paths can connect the same two endpoints, a fact Lesson 3 uses directly. The state postulate, for the simple compressible substances this course works with, says that any two independent intensive properties fix the state completely — every other property then follows. This single idea is why steam tables are organized the way they are: look up two known properties, read everything else.

04 · Units and sign conventions — fixed once, forever

This course works in SI: kg, m, s, K for the base quantities; kJ for energy; kPa or bar for pressure; kJ/kg and kJ/(kg·K) for specific quantities. The sign convention, stated once so it never needs restating: heat transfer into the system is positive (\(+Q\)); work done by the system (output) is positive (\(+W\)) — the standard engineering convention. Under it, the first law (Lesson 4) reads \( \Delta U = Q - W \): heat in helps the balance, work out costs it. Inconsistent sign conventions across sources are a classic, avoidable source of error — this one is fixed here and used throughout.

Key rule — the state postulate

For a simple compressible substance, any TWO independent intensive properties fix the state completely.

Lesson 02 · Pure substance properties and steam tables

01 · The three regions

A pure substance's property chart has three regions. To the left of the dome: compressed (or subcooled) liquid — liquid below its saturation temperature at that pressure. Under the dome: the saturated mixture region, liquid and vapor coexisting at the saturation temperature for that pressure. To the right: superheated vapor — vapor above its saturation temperature, with no liquid present at all. The dome's peak is the critical point, where the distinction between liquid and vapor disappears entirely.

02 · Quality — reading a point inside the dome

Quality \(x\) is the vapor mass fraction, \(x=m_{\text{vapor}}/m_{\text{total}}\), and it is defined only inside the dome — it is meaningless anywhere else on the chart. \(x=0\) is saturated liquid, the dome's left edge; \(x=1\) is saturated vapor, its right edge. \(x=0.85\) means \(85\)% of the mass is saturated vapor and the remaining \(15\)% is still saturated liquid, both sitting at the same saturation temperature and pressure. Any property \(y\) (specific volume, internal energy, enthalpy, entropy) at that quality is a quality-weighted average between its saturated-liquid and saturated-vapor values, \( y=y_f+x(y_g-y_f) \) — the same lever-rule idea used for a two-phase metal alloy, applied here to a boiling substance instead.

03 · Superheat, subcooling, and why turbines want it

Superheat measures how far above the saturation temperature for its pressure the vapor actually sits; superheated vapor carries no liquid at all. Subcooling is the mirror case for a liquid below its saturation temperature. Turbines are supplied with superheated steam, not steam sitting inside the dome, because wet (two-phase) steam carries liquid droplets that strike the moving blades at high speed and erode them over years of service — keeping the steam superheated at inlet, and the expansion path through the turbine as dry as practical, protects the hardware.

04 · Steam-table fluency — the working method

Given any two independent intensive properties (the state postulate, Lesson 1), the method is always the same three steps. (1) Identify which two properties are known. (2) Locate the region: compare the known values against the saturation values at that pressure or temperature — between \(v_f\) and \(v_g\) places the state inside the dome; below \(v_f\), compressed liquid; above \(v_g\), superheated. (3) Read or interpolate the remaining properties from the matching table — the saturation table with quality if inside the dome, the superheated table directly otherwise. This three-step method, not memorized numbers, is the actual skill a steam table drill is building.

Key tool — quality-weighted average

\[ y = y_f + x\,(y_g - y_f) \]

Lesson 03 · Work and heat

01 · Boundary work — path-dependent, and why

Boundary (moving-boundary) work is \( W=\int P\,dV \) for a quasi-equilibrium process — the area under the process curve on a P–v diagram. Because different paths between the same two states can trace different curves, and therefore enclose different areas, work is path-dependent: it is not a property fixed by the state alone, only by the specific route taken to get there. This is the direct contrast with internal energy, which is fixed entirely by the state and does not care how that state was reached. Standard processes give simple results: isobaric (constant \(P\)), \(W=P(V_2-V_1)\); isochoric (constant \(V\)), \(W=0\); isothermal ideal gas, \(W=mRT\ln(V_2/V_1)\); polytropic (\(PV^n=\text{const}\)), \(W=\dfrac{P_2V_2-P_1V_1}{1-n}\) for \(n\neq1\).

02 · Heat — energy in transit, not a substance

Heat is energy crossing a boundary because of a temperature difference alone. A system does not "contain" heat the way it contains internal energy — heat, like work, is a path-dependent quantity describing how energy crosses the boundary, not a property of the state itself. An adiabatic process or boundary is one with \(Q=0\) — either perfectly insulated, or fast enough that meaningful heat transfer has no time to occur.

03 · Reading work from the diagram — the working method

The area between a process curve and the volume axis, bounded by the initial and final volumes, is the boundary work for that process. A curve running higher in pressure encloses more area for the same volume change, meaning more work. A cycle — a closed loop on the P–v diagram, returning to its starting state — has net work equal to the area enclosed by the loop; this is the exact idea Lesson 11's engine cycles build on.

04 · Working method for a work-and-heat problem

(1) Identify the process type — isobaric, isochoric, isothermal, polytropic, or a curve given directly — since that identification selects which formula or graphical area applies. (2) Compute or read off the boundary work. (3) If both work and an energy quantity are needed, combine with the first law (Lesson 4) to find whichever of heat or internal-energy change remains unknown. In a well-posed problem, work and heat are never both unknowns at once — one is always computable directly, and the first law supplies the other.

Key rule — boundary work

\[ W=\int P\,dV = \text{area under the process curve (P–v)}. \]

Lesson 04 · First law: closed systems

01 · The energy balance, stated once

For a closed system, \( \Delta U = Q - W \) (using the sign convention fixed in Lesson 1). When kinetic and potential energy changes are negligible — true for almost every closed-system problem in this course — this is the entire first law needed. Every problem in this lesson is a variation on filling in this one equation.

02 · Internal energy versus enthalpy — when each applies

Internal energy \(U\) is the natural variable at constant volume: a rigid tank does no boundary work (\(W=0\)), so \(Q=\Delta U\) directly — all the heat added raises internal energy. Enthalpy, \(H=U+PV\), is the natural variable at constant pressure: it can be shown that \(Q=\Delta H\) directly there, because the definition of \(H\) already absorbs the \(P\Delta V\) boundary-work term. This is exactly why enthalpy exists as a defined property — it is the energy accounting tool built for constant-pressure processes, the way \(U\) is built for constant-volume ones. For an ideal gas, both depend on temperature alone: \( \Delta U=mc_v\Delta T \), \( \Delta H=mc_p\Delta T \).

03 · Rigid tank versus piston-cylinder, heated equally

Add the same heat \(Q\) to the same gas from the same starting temperature, in two different confinements. In a rigid tank, all of \(Q\) raises internal energy: \( \Delta T=Q/(mc_v) \). In a piston-cylinder held at constant pressure, part of \(Q\) does boundary work pushing the piston, leaving less to raise temperature: \( \Delta T=Q/(mc_p) \). Because \(c_p>c_v\) always (\(c_p=c_v+R\) for an ideal gas), the same heat produces a smaller temperature rise at constant pressure. For \(1\ \text{kg}\) of air at \(300\ \text{K}\) receiving \(50\ \text{kJ}\) (\(c_v=0.718\), \(c_p=1.005\ \text{kJ/(kg·K)}\)): the rigid tank reaches \(369.6\ \text{K}\), the piston-cylinder reaches only \(349.8\ \text{K}\) — the rigid tank ends hotter, by exactly the factor \(c_p/c_v\approx1.4\).

04 · Working method for a first-law closed-system problem

(1) Identify the confinement: rigid means constant volume, use \(U\) and \(c_v\); a piston free against a constant external pressure means constant pressure, use \(H\) and \(c_p\); anything else, use the general \( \Delta U=Q-W \) with the boundary work computed separately, as in Lesson 3. (2) Write the energy balance with the fixed sign convention. (3) Substitute the ideal-gas relation for \(\Delta U\) or \(\Delta H\). (4) Solve for whichever single quantity — \(Q\), \(W\), or the temperature change — is unknown.

Key rule — U vs. H

Constant \(V\): \(Q=\Delta U=mc_v\Delta T\). Constant \(P\): \(Q=\Delta H=mc_p\Delta T\). This is the reason enthalpy is a defined property.

Lesson 05 · First law: control volumes

01 · The steady-flow energy equation, term by term

For a steady, single-inlet/single-outlet control volume, per unit mass: \[ q - w = (h_2-h_1) + \frac{V_2^2-V_1^2}{2} + g(z_2-z_1). \] Heat and work (\(q\), \(w\)) cross the boundary the same way they do in a closed system. Enthalpy \(h\), not internal energy alone, is the natural variable here because it automatically carries the flow work that mass brings with it as it crosses the boundary — extending the constant-pressure insight from Lesson 4 to a continuously flowing stream. \(V\) is velocity (the kinetic-energy term) and \(z\) is elevation (the potential-energy term). "Steady" means nothing inside the control volume changes with time — the same mass flow rate in as out, the same properties at every point, moment to moment; steady does not mean nothing is happening, only that nothing is changing.

02 · The five one-line devices

A nozzle or diffuser does no work and is usually adiabatic; it trades enthalpy for kinetic energy (nozzle) or the reverse (diffuser): \( \Delta h\approx-\Delta KE \). A turbine produces work, is usually adiabatic, and its KE/PE changes are typically negligible next to its enthalpy drop, so \( w\approx h_1-h_2 \) — the enthalpy drop across a real turbine is commonly hundreds of kJ/kg, while velocity changes contribute only a few kJ/kg, which is why KE is routinely dropped without changing the answer meaningfully. A compressor or pump consumes work with the same simplifications, energy flowing the other way: \( w\approx h_2-h_1 \). A throttle does no work, transfers negligible heat, and has negligible KE/PE change, leaving \(h_1=h_2\) exactly — an isenthalpic device. A heat exchanger does no work, has negligible KE/PE change, and reduces to \( q=h_2-h_1 \) for each stream.

03 · Reading a device — the working method

For any of these five: (1) start from the full SFEE; (2) cross out whatever the device's physical description rules out — no work means \(w=0\); adiabatic means \(q=0\); a large flow area and low speed mean \( \Delta KE\) is negligible; no elevation change means \( \Delta PE=0\); (3) whatever survives is the device's governing equation. This is not five separate formulas to memorize — it is one equation, specialized five different ways by exactly the same logic each time.

04 · Why this equation dominates the next four years

Pumps, compressors, turbines, heat exchangers, control valves, HVAC coils — nearly every piece of process, rotating, or thermal equipment a mechanical engineer touches reduces to some simplified form of this one balance. Fast, confident simplification, the three steps of 3, is the single most repeatedly used skill this course builds, and it is used weekly for the rest of a working career in this field.

Key result — the throttle

No work, negligible heat, negligible ΔKE/ΔPE ⇒ \( h_1=h_2 \). The sharpest example of "cross out what does not apply."

Lesson 06 · Transient flow processes

01 · Why steady-flow breaks down

The SFEE of Lesson 5 assumes nothing inside the control volume changes with time. Charging or discharging a tank violates that directly: the mass and stored energy inside change continuously as it fills or empties. The standard fix is the uniform-state, uniform-flow (USUF) model — the properties of fluid crossing the boundary are taken as uniform throughout the process, and the state inside the control volume is uniform at any given instant, even though it changes from instant to instant. This keeps the mass and energy balances tractable while still capturing the transient honestly.

02 · The transient energy balance

Mass balance: \( m_2-m_1=m_{in}-m_{out} \) — the final mass inside minus the initial mass equals the net mass that crossed the boundary. Energy balance: \( m_2u_2-m_1u_1=Q-W+m_{in}h_{in}-m_{out}h_{out} \). Mass flowing in or out carries enthalpy — it is moving, so it carries flow work exactly as in Lesson 5 — while mass already inside, or accumulating inside, is accounted with internal energy, since it is not flowing anywhere and so carries no flow-work term.

03 · Charging an evacuated, insulated tank

The sharpest special case: an evacuated tank (\(m_1=0\)), perfectly insulated (\(Q=0\)), rigid (\(W=0\)), charged from a supply line at constant state \(h_{in}\). The balances reduce to \(m_2=m_{in}\) and \( m_2u_2=m_{in}h_{in} \), so simply \( u_2=h_{in} \). For an ideal gas, \(u=c_vT\) and \(h=c_pT\), giving \( c_vT_2=c_pT_{in}\Rightarrow T_2=(c_p/c_v)T_{in}=kT_{in} \). For air (\(k\approx1.4\)) charged from a \(300\ \text{K}\) (\(27^\circ\)C) line, the tank ends at \(420\ \text{K}\) (\(147^\circ\)C) — nearly \(120^\circ\)C hotter than the line it was filled from, with no heater anywhere in the picture. The entering gas carries flow work in addition to its internal energy; once inside the tank, that flow work has nowhere to go except into raising the internal energy — the temperature — of the gas now sitting still. This is a real, measurable effect: rapid receiver filling genuinely runs hotter than the supply, occasionally hot enough to matter for downstream components rated to the supply-line temperature alone.

04 · Where steady-flow honestly fails — the working method

Ask: does anything inside the control volume's boundary change measurably over the timescale being studied? A pump or turbine running continuously at a fixed load: no — steady-flow is safe. A tank filling or draining, a startup or shutdown, a batch step: yes — the transient (USUF) balance is required instead. A slow transient can often be treated as a sequence of steady states ("quasi-steady"), but a genuinely fast charge or discharge, like this lesson's tank, needs the full transient treatment to avoid a real, sometimes large, error.

Key result — charging an evacuated tank

\[ T_2 = \frac{c_p}{c_v}\,T_{line} = k\,T_{line}\qquad(\text{insulated, rigid, initially evacuated}). \]

Lesson 07 · Second law and machines

01 · Two statements, one idea

The Kelvin-Planck statement says no heat engine can convert heat from a single reservoir completely into work while operating in a cycle — some heat must always be rejected to a colder reservoir. The Clausius statement says heat cannot spontaneously flow from a colder body to a hotter one without work being supplied from outside. These read as two different rules, but they are equivalent ways of stating the same underlying limit — the second law — a limit the first law, energy conservation alone, never imposes on its own.

02 · Thermal efficiency and COP, defined honestly

A heat engine's thermal efficiency is \( \eta=W_{net}/Q_H \) — useful work out over heat supplied — always below 100%, by Kelvin-Planck. A refrigerator's coefficient of performance is \( COP_R=Q_C/W_{net} \) — heat removed from the cold space over work input — and, unlike an efficiency, it can exceed 1. A heat pump measures the same cycle differently: \( COP_{HP}=Q_H/W_{net} \), heat delivered to the warm space over work input. Because the same device satisfies \(Q_H=Q_C+W\) by energy conservation, the two coefficients are always related by \( COP_{HP}=COP_R+1 \).

03 · The Carnot limit — a ceiling no vendor brochure escapes

The Carnot efficiency, \( \eta_{Carnot}=1-T_C/T_H \) (both absolute temperatures), is the maximum efficiency any heat engine — reversible or not — can achieve operating between two fixed reservoir temperatures. As \(T_H\) rises with \(T_C\) fixed, efficiency climbs toward 1 but never reaches it for any finite \(T_H\). Real engines fall short of this ceiling because of irreversibilities — friction, unrestrained expansion, heat transfer across a finite temperature difference — so Carnot efficiency is a theoretical benchmark for judging how much room for improvement remains, not a target any real machine actually reaches.

04 · Refrigerators and heat pumps as reversed engines

A refrigeration cycle is a heat engine run in reverse: instead of heat flowing hot to cold while producing work, work is supplied to force heat from cold to hot. The same Carnot logic applies backward: \( COP_{Carnot,R}=T_C/(T_H-T_C) \) is the best possible COP between two reservoirs, and real machines fall short of it exactly as real engines fall short of Carnot efficiency. The working method for any of these three devices: (1) identify which one it is; (2) compute its defining ratio from the actual heat and work values given; (3) if a theoretical-limit comparison is asked for, compute the Carnot value separately from the absolute reservoir temperatures, and compare the two.

Key ceiling — Carnot efficiency

\[ \eta_{Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}\qquad(\text{absolute temperatures}). \]

Lesson 08 · Entropy

01 · Entropy as a property

Entropy \(S\) (specific \(s\)) is a property — fixed by the state, like \(U\), \(H\), and \(v\), in direct contrast to heat and work from Lesson 3, which depend on path. For an internally reversible process, \( dS=\delta Q/T \) defines entropy operationally; for a real, irreversible process, that relation does not directly give \( \Delta S\) — entropy is instead evaluated as a state property from tables, exactly like any other property, and the actual heat transferred is accounted separately through entropy generation, developed next.

02 · The entropy increase principle

For an isolated system — nothing crosses its boundary — total entropy can only increase, or in the ideal reversible limit stay the same; it can never decrease: \( \Delta S_{isolated}\geq0 \). This is the second law in its most general, quantitative form, and both statements from Lesson 7 follow from it: it is what rules out spontaneous heat flow from cold to hot, and it is what caps engine efficiency at the Carnot value.

03 · Entropy generation — the bookkeeping of irreversibility

For any process, not only isolated ones, the full accounting reads \( \Delta S_{system}=\displaystyle\int\frac{\delta Q}{T_{boundary}}+S_{gen} \), where \( S_{gen}\geq0 \) is entropy generated by irreversibilities inside the process — friction, unrestrained expansion, mixing, heat transfer across a finite temperature difference. \(S_{gen}=0\) only for a reversible process; every real process has \(S_{gen}>0\). This is the key distinction: entropy change can be positive, negative, or zero depending on the direction of heat transfer, even for a perfectly reversible process — but entropy generation is always non-negative, and it is the actual measure of how irreversible a real process was.

04 · T–s diagrams and the isentropic reference

An isentropic process — constant entropy, a vertical line on a T–s diagram — is simultaneously reversible and adiabatic, the theoretical best case for a device like a turbine or compressor with no heat loss. Real devices deviate to the right of that line, ending at higher entropy for the same starting point and pressure change, because real irreversibilities generate entropy along the way. This is exactly why isentropic processes serve as the reference for real-machine performance: comparing a real machine's actual outcome to the isentropic ideal for the same pressure change — the subject of Lesson 9 — measures how much irreversibility actually occurred inside it.

Key equation — entropy generation

\[ \Delta S_{system} = \int\frac{\delta Q}{T} + S_{gen},\qquad S_{gen}\geq0\ (=0\text{ only if reversible}). \]

Lesson 09 · Isentropic efficiency

01 · Defining isentropic efficiency against the ideal

For a turbine, isentropic efficiency is \( \eta_T=w_{actual}/w_{isentropic} \) — actual work out over the ideal work out for the same pressure drop — always \(\leq1\), since the isentropic case is the best possible outcome, generating no entropy. For a compressor or pump, the ratio flips: \( \eta_C=w_{isentropic}/w_{actual} \) — ideal work in over actual work in — because for a work-consuming device, the ideal case needs the least work, so closeness to ideal is measured the other way around; this ratio, too, is always \(\leq1\), since a real compressor never needs less work than the isentropic minimum.

02 · Where the "lost" work goes

For a compressor at \(\eta_C=80\)%, with an isentropic requirement of \(150\ \text{kJ/kg}\): \( w_{actual}=150/0.80=187.5\ \text{kJ/kg} \) — \(37.5\ \text{kJ/kg}\) more than the ideal minimum. That extra energy does not vanish: by the first law, it appears as extra enthalpy rise compared to the isentropic case, meaning the actual gas leaves hotter than the ideal case would predict. The "lost" work becomes waste heat carried in the gas stream rather than useful compression — the same story as Lesson 8's entropy generation, now expressed directly in energy terms.

03 · Locating real versus ideal on the h–s diagram

The isentropic path is a vertical line — constant entropy — from the inlet state to the ideal exit enthalpy \(h_{2s}\). The actual path ends at \(h_2\), at higher entropy and, for a compressor, higher enthalpy than the isentropic point. Read directly off the diagram: \(h_2-h_1\) is the actual work in (compressor) or, for a turbine, \(h_1-h_2\) is the actual work out — always smaller than the isentropic \(h_1-h_{2s}\) — while \(h_{2s}-h_1\) is the isentropic ideal in either case.

04 · Why this number matters — the working method

Isentropic efficiency is the single number that separates a healthy machine from a worn one. Blade fouling, seal wear, erosion, and bearing problems all degrade it over time, and tracking measured isentropic efficiency against a machine's as-new baseline is standard condition-monitoring practice, not just a design-stage calculation. The working method: (1) find the ideal (isentropic) work from the inlet state and the actual pressure ratio; (2) find the actual work from measured inlet/outlet conditions; (3) take the appropriate ratio — actual over ideal for a turbine, ideal over actual for a compressor; (4) compare the result against the machine's rated or as-new value to judge its current health.

Key ratios — isentropic efficiency

Turbine: \( \eta_T=w_{actual}/w_{isentropic} \). Compressor/pump: \( \eta_C=w_{isentropic}/w_{actual} \) — flipped, because one produces work and the other consumes it.

Lesson 10 · Exergy first contact

01 · Available work and the dead state

Not all energy is equally useful. Exergy (or availability) is the maximum theoretical work extractable from a system as it comes into equilibrium with a defined reference environment, the dead state. Exergy only means something relative to that reference: "useful work" is measured against what the system would otherwise settle into on its own, so without a defined dead state there is no zero point to measure available work from. A stream far above the dead-state temperature has high exergy — an ideal engine could extract substantial work as it cools toward equilibrium. A stream only slightly above the dead-state temperature has low exergy even carrying substantial raw energy — low-grade heat. Exergy, unlike energy, discriminates by quality, not just quantity.

02 · Exergy destruction — the cost of irreversibility

Energy is conserved by the first law — never destroyed, only converted or transferred. Exergy is destroyed by irreversibility, a direct consequence of Lesson 8's entropy generation: \( X_{destroyed}=T_0\,S_{gen} \), where \(T_0\) is the dead-state temperature. Every real, irreversible process destroys some exergy permanently, even while its energy balance still closes perfectly. Energy accounting alone can make an inefficient process look fine — energy in always equals energy out — while exergy accounting reveals exactly where, and how much, true usefulness was actually thrown away.

03 · Why utilities audits speak this language

A boiler running at 85% energy efficiency sounds strong, but an exergy analysis of that same boiler often shows the large majority of the fuel's exergy is destroyed in the combustion process itself — an inherently irreversible, high-temperature-difference reaction — long before any heat-transfer inefficiency ever registers. Exergy accounting is what lets a utilities audit rank where in a plant the real thermodynamic losses concentrate, often combustion and large-temperature-difference heat exchange rather than the spots an energy-only audit would flag, directing capital toward the improvements that actually matter most.

04 · A preview, not the full method

This lesson introduces only the vocabulary — dead state, exergy, exergy destruction — and the core distinction from energy. The full exergy balance, second-law efficiency, and a complete plant exergy audit belong to Thermo II. The working method here: whenever a question asks where the real loss actually happened, not simply where the energy went, that is an exergy question, and its size traces directly back to entropy generation.

Key result — exergy destruction

\[ X_{destroyed} = T_0\,S_{gen}. \]

Energy is conserved. Exergy is destroyed by irreversibility.

Lesson 11 · Gas power cycle sketches

01 · Three idealized cycles

The Otto cycle models a spark-ignition engine: \(1\to2\) isentropic compression, \(2\to3\) heat addition at constant volume (idealized combustion), \(3\to4\) isentropic expansion (the power stroke), \(4\to1\) heat rejection at constant volume (idealized exhaust and intake). The Diesel cycle is identical except heat addition (\(2\to3\)) happens at constant pressure instead, modeling the slower, more gradual combustion as fuel is injected. The Brayton cycle models a gas turbine as an open, continuous-flow analog: \(1\to2\) isentropic compression (the compressor), \(2\to3\) heat addition at constant pressure (the combustor), \(3\to4\) isentropic expansion (the turbine), \(4\to1\) heat rejection at constant pressure, or simply exhaust to atmosphere in a true open cycle.

02 · Reading the Otto cycle P–v loop

The loop's enclosed area is the net work per cycle — Lesson 3's area-under-a-curve idea, now closed into a full loop. The compression ratio \(r=V_1/V_2\) is the single largest lever on ideal efficiency: \[ \eta_{Otto}=1-\frac{1}{r^{\gamma-1}}. \] A higher compression ratio gives higher ideal efficiency; real engines are held well below the ideal-efficiency-maximizing ratio by knock and detonation limits, a practical constraint outside this lesson's scope.

03 · Mean effective pressure and back-work ratio

Mean effective pressure (MEP) is the single constant pressure that, acting over the same swept volume, would produce the same net work as the actual cycle: \( \text{MEP}=W_{net}/(V_1-V_2) \) — a compact way to compare engines of different sizes on how hard they work per unit of displacement, independent of engine size. Back-work ratio (\(BWR=w_{compressor}/w_{turbine}\)) matters specifically for the Brayton cycle: it is the fraction of the turbine's own gross output consumed just running the compressor before any net useful work is left over. A gas turbine's back-work ratio typically runs \(40\)–\(60\)%, far higher than a comparable steam cycle's pump back-work ratio (often under \(2\)%), because compressing a gas costs far more work than pumping a nearly incompressible liquid to the same pressure ratio — this is exactly why compressor efficiency matters disproportionately for a gas turbine's already-thin net output, while a steam cycle's pump efficiency barely moves the needle.

04 · Cycle literacy — the working method

For any of the three cycles: (1) identify the four processes and which are isentropic versus constant-property; (2) apply the isentropic and constant-property relations already built in Lessons 3–4 to find each state's properties; (3) sum heat and work across the four legs for net work and thermal efficiency; (4) for Brayton specifically, always check the back-work ratio, since it dominates net output far more than in a piston cycle. Full cycle analysis with real component efficiencies, actual combustion, and multi-stage compression or expansion is Thermo II's job — this lesson exists so a mechanical engineer can hold an informed conversation about gas turbines and piston engines, not yet design one.

Key ratio — back-work ratio

\[ BWR = \frac{w_{compressor}}{w_{turbine}}. \]

Much higher for a gas turbine than a steam cycle's pump — why compressor efficiency matters so much more there.
