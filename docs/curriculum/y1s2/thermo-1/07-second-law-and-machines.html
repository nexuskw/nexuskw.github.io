Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why can no engine beat Carnot between the same reservoirs?
  • COP above 1: why doesn't that violate anything?

01 · Two statements, one idea

The Kelvin-Planck statement says no heat engine can convert heat from a single reservoir completely into work while operating in a cycle — some heat must always be rejected to a colder reservoir. The Clausius statement says heat cannot spontaneously flow from a colder body to a hotter one without work being supplied from outside. These read as two different rules, but they are equivalent ways of stating the same underlying limit — the second law — a limit the first law, energy conservation alone, never imposes on its own.

02 · Thermal efficiency and COP, defined honestly

A heat engine's thermal efficiency is \( \eta=W_{net}/Q_H \) — useful work out over heat supplied — always below 100%, by Kelvin-Planck. A refrigerator's coefficient of performance is \( COP_R=Q_C/W_{net} \) — heat removed from the cold space over work input — and, unlike an efficiency, it can exceed 1. A heat pump measures the same cycle differently: \( COP_{HP}=Q_H/W_{net} \), heat delivered to the warm space over work input. Because the same device satisfies \(Q_H=Q_C+W\) by energy conservation, the two coefficients are always related by \( COP_{HP}=COP_R+1 \).

03 · The Carnot limit — a ceiling no vendor brochure escapes

The Carnot efficiency, \( \eta_{Carnot}=1-T_C/T_H \) (both absolute temperatures), is the maximum efficiency any heat engine — reversible or not — can achieve operating between two fixed reservoir temperatures. As \(T_H\) rises with \(T_C\) fixed, efficiency climbs toward 1 but never reaches it for any finite \(T_H\). Real engines fall short of this ceiling because of irreversibilities — friction, unrestrained expansion, heat transfer across a finite temperature difference — so Carnot efficiency is a theoretical benchmark for judging how much room for improvement remains, not a target any real machine actually reaches.

04 · Refrigerators and heat pumps as reversed engines

A refrigeration cycle is a heat engine run in reverse: instead of heat flowing hot to cold while producing work, work is supplied to force heat from cold to hot. The same Carnot logic applies backward: \( COP_{Carnot,R}=T_C/(T_H-T_C) \) is the best possible COP between two reservoirs, and real machines fall short of it exactly as real engines fall short of Carnot efficiency. The working method for any of these three devices: (1) identify which one it is; (2) compute its defining ratio from the actual heat and work values given; (3) if a theoretical-limit comparison is asked for, compute the Carnot value separately from the absolute reservoir temperatures, and compare the two.

Key ceiling — Carnot efficiency

\[ \eta_{Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}\qquad(\text{absolute temperatures}). \]

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

The Carnot ceiling, computed

Problem. A heat engine operates between a hot reservoir at 800 K and a cold reservoir at 300 K. Find the maximum possible (Carnot) efficiency, and explain why no real engine between these two reservoirs can beat it.

Solution. \( \eta_{Carnot}=1-300/800=1-0.375=0.625 \) (62.5%). No real engine can exceed this because it is the theoretical ceiling the second law sets for any engine between these fixed temperatures — irreversibilities only ever push a real engine below this ceiling, never above it.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Why COP above 1 is not a violation

Problem. A refrigerator has COP = 3.5. Explain in plain terms why a value greater than 1 does not violate energy conservation.

Solution. COP compares heat removed to work supplied, not energy created to energy consumed. The refrigerator does not manufacture energy — it relocates existing thermal energy from the cold space to the warm space, using the work input as the lever that makes that relocation possible. For every unit of work, 3.5 units of heat move; the heat delivered to the warm side is 3.5+1=4.5 units, fully consistent with conservation throughout.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

COP_HP = COP_R + 1, confirmed

Problem. A vapor-compression cycle removes 5 kW from a cold space while consuming 2 kW of work. Find COP_R and COP_HP, and confirm the relationship between them.

Solution. \( COP_R=5/2=2.5 \). Heat delivered to the warm side: \( Q_H=Q_C+W=5+2=7\ \text{kW} \), so \( COP_{HP}=7/2=3.5 \). Check: \(COP_R+1=2.5+1=3.5=COP_{HP}\). Confirmed.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A plant chiller nameplate quoting COP=4.0 is stating, in the language of 2, that for every unit of electrical work paid for, four units of heat are removed from the cooling load — a number worth comparing against the Carnot COP ceiling for the actual evaporator and condenser temperatures the chiller runs between, since that comparison is what tells a facilities engineer whether a poorly performing unit still has real headroom for improvement or is already running close to what physics allows at those operating temperatures.

A steam-turbine-generator set's stated thermal efficiency should always be read against the Carnot ceiling for its actual boiler and condenser temperatures, not against 100%: a well-run modern steam plant reaching roughly 40% actual efficiency against a Carnot ceiling of perhaps 60% for its operating temperatures is performing respectably, while the same 40% number would look very different if the Carnot ceiling for those temperatures were only 45%.
