Relative acceleration and Coriolis

Acceleration analysis of linkages. Rotating reference frames and the Coriolis term. Where slider-in-slot mechanisms get their surprising accelerations.

01 · Acceleration of a rigid body

Differentiating the relative-velocity equation gives the relative-acceleration equation for a rigid body: the acceleration of a point \(B\) is that of a reference point \(A\) plus two rotational terms, \( \mathbf{a}_B=\mathbf{a}_A+\boldsymbol{\alpha}\times\mathbf{r}_{B/A}-\omega^2\mathbf{r}_{B/A} \). The \( \boldsymbol{\alpha}\times\mathbf{r} \) term is tangential (magnitude \( \alpha r \), from angular acceleration) and the \( -\omega^2\mathbf{r} \) term is normal (magnitude \( \omega^2 r \), always pointing back toward \(A\)). For a point at \( r=0.4 \) m on a link turning at \( \omega=5 \) rad/s, \( \alpha=3\ \text{rad/s}^2 \): the normal part is \( \omega^2 r=10\ \text{m/s}^2 \) and the tangential part \( \alpha r=1.2\ \text{m/s}^2 \). This solves the acceleration of linkages joint by joint.

02 · Rotating frames and the Coriolis term

When a particle also moves relative to a rotating body — a bead sliding in a spinning slot, a fluid particle in an impeller — the acceleration seen in the fixed frame gains an extra piece: the Coriolis acceleration \( \mathbf{a}_{\text{Cor}}=2\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}} \), of magnitude \( 2\omega v_{\text{rel}} \) and always perpendicular to the relative velocity. A bead sliding out at \( v_{\text{rel}}=2 \) m/s along an arm rotating at \( \omega=5 \) rad/s experiences \( a_{\text{Cor}}=2(5)(2)=20\ \text{m/s}^2 \) sideways. It is not a "force" but a real acceleration that appears because the frame itself is turning — the term that must be added whenever motion is described relative to rotating machinery.

Key term — Coriolis acceleration

\[ \mathbf{a}_{\text{Cor}}=2\,\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}},\qquad |\mathbf{a}_{\text{Cor}}|=2\omega v_{\text{rel}}\ \perp\ \mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}}. \]

03 · The full rotating-frame acceleration

Put together, a particle moving with velocity \( \mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}} \) and acceleration \( \mathbf{a}_{\text{rel}} \) relative to a body rotating at \( \omega \) has, in the fixed frame, \( \mathbf{a}=\mathbf{a}_{\text{rel}}+2\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}}+\boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r})+\boldsymbol{\alpha}\times\mathbf{r} \) — relative, Coriolis, centripetal, and tangential terms. For the bead above (\( r=0.4 \), \( \omega=5 \), \( v_{\text{rel}}=2 \), \( a_{\text{rel}}=0 \), \( \alpha=0 \)): a centripetal \( \omega^2 r=10\ \text{m/s}^2 \) inward and a Coriolis \( 2\omega v_{\text{rel}}=20\ \text{m/s}^2 \) sideways, combining to \( \sqrt{10^2+20^2}\approx22.4\ \text{m/s}^2 \). Miss the Coriolis term and the answer is wrong by the larger contribution.

04 · The working method

For acceleration analysis: (1) for a plain rigid body, use \( \mathbf{a}_B=\mathbf{a}_A+\boldsymbol{\alpha}\times\mathbf{r}-\omega^2\mathbf{r} \), tracking tangential and normal parts separately; (2) whenever a part slides relative to a rotating member — slider-in-slot mechanisms, Geneva drives, pump and turbine passages — add the Coriolis term \( 2\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}} \); (3) sum the vector components on chosen axes. The one habit that prevents the classic error is to ask, on every rotating-frame problem, "is anything moving relative to the rotating body?" — if yes, Coriolis is present, and it is often the dominant term.

Coriolis acceleration

Problem. A bead slides outward at \( v_{\text{rel}}=2 \) m/s along an arm rotating at \( \omega=5 \) rad/s. Find the Coriolis acceleration.

Solution. \( a_{\text{Cor}}=2\omega v_{\text{rel}}=2(5)(2)=20\ \text{m/s}^2 \), directed perpendicular to the sliding velocity (the sense given by \( \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}} \)).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Point on a rotating link

Problem. A point sits at \( r=0.4 \) m on a link with \( \omega=5 \) rad/s and \( \alpha=3\ \text{rad/s}^2 \). Find its normal and tangential accelerations.

Solution. Normal (centripetal): \( \omega^2 r=25(0.4)=10\ \text{m/s}^2 \), toward the pivot. Tangential: \( \alpha r=3(0.4)=1.2\ \text{m/s}^2 \), along the path.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Total slider acceleration

Problem. For the bead of Problem 1, at \( r=0.4 \) m with constant slide speed and constant \( \omega \) (\( a_{\text{rel}}=0,\ \alpha=0 \)), find the magnitude of its total acceleration.

Solution. Two perpendicular parts: centripetal \( \omega^2 r=10\ \text{m/s}^2 \) inward and Coriolis \( 2\omega v_{\text{rel}}=20\ \text{m/s}^2 \) sideways. Total \( =\sqrt{10^2+20^2}=\sqrt{500}\approx22.4\ \text{m/s}^2 \).

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Coriolis is not a textbook curiosity to a pump or turbine engineer — it is a real acceleration inside every impeller. Fluid moves radially outward (its \( v_{\text{rel}} \)) through passages that are themselves rotating at \( \omega \), so each fluid particle feels \( 2\omega v_{\text{rel}} \) sideways, pressing on the trailing face of the blade. That Coriolis pressure is a major part of how a centrifugal machine transfers energy to the fluid, and ignoring it mis-predicts the head a pump develops.

The same term governs slider-in-slot mechanisms and indexing drives — Geneva wheels, quick-return mechanisms, rotating cutters fed radially. A designer computing the peak acceleration (and therefore the peak force and wear) on such a part must include Coriolis, because at speed it is frequently the largest term. The working question on the floor is simple: is anything sliding along something that spins? If so, Coriolis is loading the part, and the bearing and slot must be rated for it.
