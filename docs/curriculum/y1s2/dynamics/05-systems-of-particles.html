Systems of particles

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why do internal forces cancel in a system of particles?
  • Compute the force of a steady water jet on a plate — which principle applies?

01 · The centre of mass moves as one particle

A system of particles has a centre of mass at \( \bar{x}=\dfrac{\sum m_i x_i}{\sum m_i} \), the mass-weighted average position; two masses of \( 2 \) kg at \( x=0 \) and \( 3 \) kg at \( x=5 \) m have \( \bar{x}=\dfrac{2(0)+3(5)}{5}=3 \) m. The central theorem is that this point obeys Newton's law for the whole system as if it were a single particle carrying the total mass: \( \sum\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}}=M\mathbf{a}_{\text{cm}} \). Internal forces — however violent, like the forces inside an explosion — cancel in equal-and-opposite pairs and never move the centre of mass. This is why a spinning, tumbling wrench thrown across a room still has its centre of mass trace a clean parabola.

02 · Momentum of a system

Because internal forces cancel, the total momentum of a system changes only under external impulse, and with none it is conserved: \( \sum m_i\mathbf{v}_i=\text{const} \). This is the principle behind recoil and propulsion. A \( 60 \) kg person, initially still, throwing a \( 2 \) kg ball at \( 10 \) m/s must recoil so the total momentum stays zero: \( 60v=2(10)\Rightarrow v=0.33 \) m/s backward. The same balance, applied to gas thrown out of a nozzle, is how a rocket climbs. Momentum bookkeeping across a whole system needs no knowledge of the internal forces — only what crosses its boundary.

Key principle — systems of particles

\[ \sum\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}}=M\mathbf{a}_{\text{cm}};\qquad \text{no external force}\Rightarrow \sum m_i\mathbf{v}_i=\text{const}. \]

03 · Steady mass flow: the jet force

When mass flows steadily rather than in discrete lumps — a stream of water, a gas jet — the momentum principle takes a flow form. Fluid of density \( \rho \) crossing area \( A \) at speed \( v \) carries a mass flow \( \dot m=\rho A v \) (kg/s). If a plate stops the jet, it destroys that momentum every second, and by the momentum principle must supply a force \( F=\dot m\,v=\rho A v^2 \). A \( 20 \) m/s water jet through a \( A=0.001\ \text{m}^2 \) nozzle has \( \dot m=1000(0.001)(20)=20 \) kg/s and delivers \( F=20(20)=400 \) N to the plate. This single result — force equals mass-flow times velocity change — is the seed of turbomachinery, where the same momentum exchange between fluid and blade produces useful torque.

04 · The working method

For any system: (1) draw a boundary and identify what crosses it — external forces and any mass flow; (2) for the bulk motion apply \( \sum\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}}=M\mathbf{a}_{\text{cm}} \), ignoring all internal forces; (3) for conservation, set total momentum before equal to after; (4) for steady flow, use \( F=\dot m\,\Delta v \) with \( \dot m=\rho Av \). The recurring discipline is to separate internal from external: internal forces rearrange a system but never move its centre of mass or change its total momentum. That distinction is what makes a complicated many-body problem collapse to one equation about the boundary.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Centre of mass

Problem. Find the centre of mass of a \(2\) kg particle at \(x=0\) and a \(3\) kg particle at \(x=5\) m.

Solution. \( \bar x=\dfrac{\sum m_i x_i}{\sum m_i}=\dfrac{2(0)+3(5)}{2+3}=\dfrac{15}{5}=3 \) m — closer to the heavier mass, as expected.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Jet on a plate

Problem. A water jet (\( \rho=1000 \) kg/m³) of area \(A=0.001\) m² and speed \(v=20\) m/s strikes a plate and is stopped. Find the mass flow and the force on the plate.

Solution. Mass flow \( \dot m=\rho A v=1000(0.001)(20)=20 \) kg/s. The plate destroys the jet's momentum: \( F=\dot m\,v=20(20)=400 \) N (equivalently \( \rho A v^2 \)).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Recoil

Problem. A \(60\) kg person, initially at rest on frictionless ice, throws a \(2\) kg ball at \(10\) m/s. Find their recoil speed.

Solution. Total momentum stays zero: \( 60v=2(10)\Rightarrow v=\dfrac{20}{60}\approx0.33 \) m/s, directed opposite to the ball.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

The jet-force result \( F=\dot m\,\Delta v \) is the working principle of every pump, turbine, and thruster on the plant. In a centrifugal pump the impeller blades change the fluid's momentum, and the reaction is the torque the motor must overcome; in a steam or gas turbine the same momentum exchange drives the shaft. Sizing a nozzle's thrust or the anchor for a fire-water monitor is this calculation directly — the force scales with the square of jet velocity, so a small rise in speed is a large rise in reaction.

Centre-of-mass thinking guides lifting and rigging. A crane spreader or an irregular load hangs true only when the hook is above the centre of mass \( \bar x=\sum m_ix_i/\sum m_i \); off-centre, it tilts. For a composite assembly the centre of mass is found piece by piece, exactly the systems calculation of 1, which is why lift plans compute it before a heavy or awkward load ever leaves the ground.
