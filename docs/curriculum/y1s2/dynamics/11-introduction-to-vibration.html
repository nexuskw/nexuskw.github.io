01 · The single-degree-of-freedom oscillator

The simplest vibrating system is a mass on a spring with damping — the single-degree-of-freedom (SDOF) oscillator. Newton's law for it is \( m\ddot{x}+c\dot{x}+kx=0 \), exactly the second-order equation of the mathematics course, now with a mechanical meaning: \(m\) the mass, \(k\) the spring stiffness, \(c\) the damping. Its free response is set by two numbers. The natural frequency \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) is the rate it rings undamped; for \( m=2 \) kg, \( k=800 \) N/m, \( \omega_n=\sqrt{400}=20 \) rad/s, or \( f=\omega_n/2\pi\approx3.18 \) Hz. This one system is the model for every structure, machine mount, and rotor-support that can vibrate — the whole of the Vibrations course is built on it.

02 · Damping ratio and the response

The second number is the damping ratio \( \zeta=\dfrac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}} \), the actual damping measured against the critical amount \( c_c=2\sqrt{mk} \). For the system above, \( c_c=2\sqrt{1600}=80 \), so a damper \( c=8 \) gives \( \zeta=0.1 \) — underdamped. An underdamped system oscillates inside a decaying envelope: it rings at the damped frequency \( \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\approx19.9 \) rad/s while its amplitude decays as \( e^{-\zeta\omega_n t} \). Small damping barely shifts the frequency but steadily bleeds the amplitude, which is exactly the ringing-and-fading response measured when a real structure is struck.

Key numbers — the SDOF oscillator

\[ \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m},\qquad \zeta=\frac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}},\qquad \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}. \]

03 · Measuring damping from a decay

The great practical value of the SDOF model is that both parameters can be read from a single measured decay — strike the structure and record the free vibration. The frequency of the ringing gives \( \omega_d \) (and hence \( \omega_n \)), and the rate of amplitude decay gives \( \zeta \) through the logarithmic decrement \( \delta=\ln\dfrac{x_1}{x_2} \), the natural log of the ratio of two successive peaks. For light damping \( \zeta\approx\dfrac{\delta}{2\pi} \). If successive peaks measure \( 10 \) mm and \( 8 \) mm, then \( \delta=\ln(10/8)=0.223 \) and \( \zeta\approx0.223/2\pi\approx0.036 \). No shaker or force measurement is needed — just the trace of a free decay, which is why the impact "bump test" is the workhorse of field vibration diagnosis.

04 · The working method

To characterise any vibrating system: (1) model it as an SDOF \( m,c,k \), lumping the effective mass and stiffness; (2) compute \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) and \( \zeta=c/(2\sqrt{mk}) \) — the natural frequency you must keep operating speeds away from, and the damping that limits any resonant response; (3) from a measured decay, extract \( \omega_d \) from the ringing frequency and \( \zeta \) from the logarithmic decrement. These two numbers, \( \omega_n \) and \( \zeta \), are the complete fingerprint of the free response — and, joined with the forced-response curve of the mathematics course, they are what the Vibrations and rotating-machinery courses spend the next years applying to real machines.

Natural frequency and damping

Problem. For an SDOF system with \( m=2 \) kg, \( k=800 \) N/m, \( c=8 \) N·s/m, find \( \omega_n \), the frequency in Hz, and \( \zeta \).

Solution. \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m}=\sqrt{400}=20 \) rad/s, so \( f=\omega_n/2\pi\approx3.18 \) Hz. Critical damping \( c_c=2\sqrt{mk}=2\sqrt{1600}=80 \), so \( \zeta=c/c_c=8/80=0.1 \) — underdamped.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Logarithmic decrement

Problem. A struck structure's free vibration shows successive peaks of \(10\) mm and \(8\) mm. Estimate the damping ratio.

Solution. Logarithmic decrement \( \delta=\ln(x_1/x_2)=\ln(10/8)=\ln1.25\approx0.223 \). For light damping \( \zeta\approx\delta/2\pi\approx0.223/6.283\approx0.036 \).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Damped frequency

Problem. For the system of Problem 1 (\( \omega_n=20 \), \( \zeta=0.1 \)), find the damped natural frequency.

Solution. \( \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}=20\sqrt{1-0.01}=20\sqrt{0.99}\approx19.9 \) rad/s — only slightly below \( \omega_n \), as light damping predicts.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

The impact bump test of 3 is a technician's everyday diagnostic. Striking a pump casing, a pipe span, or a structural frame and recording the free ringing gives its natural frequency directly — and the number that matters most, because if that frequency sits near a running speed or a forcing frequency, resonance and high vibration follow. Field engineers run this test before commissioning to make sure a machine's operating speed is clear of the structure's \( \omega_n \).

The damping ratio, read from the decay, sizes isolation and tells how dangerous a resonance would be. A lightly damped structure (\( \zeta \) a few percent, typical of welded steel) rings for many cycles and amplifies any resonant forcing enormously, so added damping — isolator pads, tuned dampers, constrained-layer treatments — is specified to raise \( \zeta \). Measuring \( \omega_n \) and \( \zeta \) on the real machine is the first step of every vibration study, and the bridge from this course into Vibrations and condition monitoring.
