Work and energy

Work of forces and couples; kinetic and potential energy. The work–energy theorem as an accounting identity. When energy methods beat force methods.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • When does the work–energy theorem beat force analysis?
  • Why does a couple's work involve angle, not distance?

01 · Work: force acting through distance

The work done by a constant force is \( W=Fd\cos\theta \) — the force times the distance times the cosine of the angle between them, so only the component of force along the motion counts. A force perpendicular to the path (a normal force, the tension in a turning string) does no work at all. This is the dot product of the vector lessons, integrated along a path. Work is measured in joules, and it is the currency in which the whole energy method is denominated: instead of tracking forces and accelerations moment by moment, we track how much work is done and where it goes.

02 · Kinetic energy and the work–energy theorem

A moving particle carries kinetic energy \( KE=\tfrac12mv^2 \), and the work–energy theorem states that the net work done on it equals its change in kinetic energy: \( W_{\text{net}}=\Delta KE \). This is Newton's law integrated over distance, and it is an accounting identity — energy in equals energy stored. A \( 100 \) N force pushing a \( 20 \) kg block from rest over \( 10 \) m (frictionless) does \( W=1000 \) J, so \( \tfrac12(20)v^2=1000 \Rightarrow v=10 \) m/s. The power of the method is that it never asks about the acceleration or the time — only the endpoints — which is exactly what makes it the fast route through problems where forces vary along the way.

Key identity — work–energy

\[ W_{\text{net}}=\Delta KE=\tfrac12mv_2^2-\tfrac12mv_1^2. \]

03 · Potential energy and conservation

Some forces store the work done against them as potential energy, recoverable later: gravity stores \( PE=mgh \), a spring stores \( \tfrac12kx^2 \). When only such conservative forces act, the sum \( KE+PE \) is constant — conservation of energy. A mass dropped from height \( h \) converts all its \( mgh \) into \( \tfrac12mv^2 \), giving \( v=\sqrt{2gh} \); from \( h=5 \) m, \( v=\sqrt{2(9.81)(5)}\approx9.9 \) m/s, independent of the mass. A spring of stiffness \( k=2000 \) N/m compressed \( x=0.1 \) m stores \( \tfrac12(2000)(0.1)^2=10 \) J, enough to launch a \( 0.5 \) kg mass to \( v=\sqrt{2(10)/0.5}\approx6.3 \) m/s. Friction, by contrast, is non-conservative — it drains mechanical energy into heat, and its work is where the "lost" energy goes.

04 · Power and the working method

Power is the rate of doing work, \( P=\dfrac{dW}{dt}=Fv \) — the number that sizes motors and engines. A \( 500 \) N pull at \( 2 \) m/s delivers \( 1000 \) W. The energy method's routine is: (1) identify the states (start and end) and the energies present at each — kinetic, gravitational, spring; (2) account for any work added by applied forces or drained by friction; (3) set energy-in equal to energy-out and solve. Choose energy over \( F=ma \) whenever the question links speed to position and the time in between does not matter — falling bodies, spring launches, roller-coaster speeds — because it collapses a differential equation into one algebraic balance.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Work–energy theorem

Problem. A \(100\) N force pushes a \(20\) kg block from rest over \(10\) m on a frictionless floor. Find the work done and the final speed.

Solution. Work \( W=Fd=100(10)=1000 \) J. By the work–energy theorem \( W=\tfrac12mv^2 \), so \( \tfrac12(20)v^2=1000\Rightarrow v^2=100\Rightarrow v=10 \) m/s.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Falling body

Problem. A mass is released from rest and falls \(h=5\) m. Find its speed at the bottom (\( g=9.81 \)).

Solution. Conservation: \( mgh=\tfrac12mv^2 \), so \( v=\sqrt{2gh}=\sqrt{2(9.81)(5)}=\sqrt{98.1}\approx9.9 \) m/s — independent of the mass.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Spring launch

Problem. A spring of stiffness \(k=2000\) N/m is compressed \(x=0.1\) m. Find the stored energy and the launch speed of a \(0.5\) kg mass.

Solution. Stored energy \( \tfrac12kx^2=\tfrac12(2000)(0.1)^2=10 \) J. All of it becomes kinetic: \( \tfrac12(0.5)v^2=10\Rightarrow v^2=40\Rightarrow v\approx6.3 \) m/s.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

An engineer sizing a hoist or conveyor motor works straight from power, \( P=Fv \). Lifting a load at a steady rate needs at least the power to raise its weight, \( P=mg\,v_{\text{lift}} \), plus friction losses; picking a motor below that figure means it stalls under load. The energy view also sizes braking: stopping a moving mass means absorbing its kinetic energy \( \tfrac12mv^2 \), which is why a brake or a buffer at the end of a travel is rated in joules, and why doubling the speed quadruples the braking energy a crane or lift must dissipate.

Conservation of energy is the quick estimator on the plant floor. The speed a dropped tool or a falling load reaches is \( v=\sqrt{2gh} \) regardless of its mass — which is why fall-arrest and drop-zone calculations depend only on height, and why a modest drop can still produce a dangerous impact. Reading a hazard as an energy \( mgh \) to be caught, rather than a force to be resisted, is how guarding and containment are actually specified.
