01 · F = ma, applied through a free-body diagram

Newton's second law, \( \sum\mathbf{F}=m\mathbf{a} \), connects the forces on a particle to its acceleration. The whole discipline of using it is the free-body diagram (FBD): isolate the body, draw every force acting on it — weight, normal contact, friction, applied loads, tension — and nothing else, then sum components along convenient axes. On a curved or inclined path the axes are chosen to match the motion, which is why the normal–tangential split of the last lesson matters here. Get the FBD right and the algebra is routine; get it wrong — a missing or invented force — and no amount of algebra recovers. The FBD is the single most important habit in all of mechanics.

02 · Friction and the incline

On a plane inclined at \( \theta \), the weight \( mg \) resolves into a component \( mg\sin\theta \) driving the block down the slope and a component \( mg\cos\theta \) pressing it into the surface. The surface pushes back with the normal force \( N=mg\cos\theta \), and dry friction opposes sliding up to a limit \( f\le\mu N \). For a \( 10 \) kg block on a \( 30^\circ \) incline: \( N=10(9.81)\cos30^\circ\approx84.96 \) N, driving force \( mg\sin30^\circ=49.05 \) N, and maximum friction \( \mu N=0.2(84.96)\approx16.99 \) N. Since the drive exceeds friction, the block slides, and Newton's law along the slope gives \( a=g(\sin\theta-\mu\cos\theta)=9.81(0.5-0.173)\approx3.21\ \text{m/s}^2 \).

Key relations — incline & friction

\( N=mg\cos\theta \); driving force \( =mg\sin\theta \); friction \( f\le\mu N \). Sliding: \( a=g(\sin\theta-\mu\cos\theta) \).

03 · Curvilinear motion: the centripetal force

The normal acceleration \( a_n=v^2/\rho \) of the last lesson, multiplied by mass, is a real force requirement: turning a particle of mass \(m\) on a curve of radius \(r\) demands a centripetal force \( F=\dfrac{mv^2}{r} \) directed toward the centre. On a flat road that force can only come from friction, \( \mu mg\ge\dfrac{mv^2}{r} \), which caps the cornering speed at \( v_{\max}=\sqrt{\mu g r} \). For \( \mu=0.7,\ r=50 \) m: \( v_{\max}=\sqrt{0.7(9.81)(50)}\approx18.5 \) m/s (about \(67\) km/h). Exceed it and the friction budget is spent, the required centripetal force cannot be supplied, and the vehicle slides straight on — Newton's law making a hard physical prediction.

04 · The working method

For any kinetics problem: (1) draw the free-body diagram — every real force, no more; (2) choose axes that match the motion (along/across a slope, normal/tangential on a curve); (3) write \( \sum F=ma \) in each direction and solve. Two checks guard the work: the normal direction usually has no acceleration (so \( \sum F=0 \) there, fixing \(N\)), and units must close to newtons. This routine handles the block, the cornering car, the elevator, and the whole of the following energy and momentum lessons, which are Newton's law integrated over distance and over time respectively.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Block on an incline

Problem. A \(10\) kg block sits on a \(30^\circ\) incline with \( \mu=0.2 \). Find its acceleration down the slope (\( g=9.81 \)).

Solution. Normal: \( N=mg\cos\theta=84.96 \) N. Along the slope: driving \( mg\sin\theta=49.05 \) N, friction \( \mu N=16.99 \) N opposing. Newton's law: \( ma=mg\sin\theta-\mu N \), so \( a=g(\sin\theta-\mu\cos\theta)=9.81(0.5-0.173)\approx3.21\ \text{m/s}^2 \).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Cornering speed

Problem. A car corners on a flat road of radius \(50\) m with tyre friction \( \mu=0.7 \). Find the maximum speed before it slides.

Solution. Friction must supply the centripetal force: \( \mu mg\ge mv^2/r \). The mass cancels: \( v_{\max}=\sqrt{\mu g r}=\sqrt{0.7(9.81)(50)}=\sqrt{343.4}\approx18.5 \) m/s (\(\approx67\) km/h).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Centripetal force

Problem. Find the centripetal force on a \(1200\) kg car cornering at \(18.5\) m/s on a \(50\) m radius, and confirm it matches the available friction.

Solution. \( F=\dfrac{mv^2}{r}=\dfrac{1200(18.5)^2}{50}\approx8240 \) N. Available friction \( \mu mg=0.7(1200)(9.81)\approx8240 \) N — they match, confirming the car is exactly at its limit.

Problem 4 of 11 · multiple choice

Newton's second law states:

The net force equals mass times acceleration: \( \sum\mathbf{F}=m\mathbf{a} \).

Problem 5 of 11 · multiple choice

The maximum dry-friction force is:

\( f\le\mu N \) — the limit is \( \mu \) times the normal force (which is \(mg\) only on a level surface).

Problem 6 of 11 · multiple choice

On a \(30^\circ\) incline, the component of weight along the slope is:

The driving component is \( mg\sin\theta \); the pressing (normal) component is \( mg\cos\theta \).

Problem 7 of 11 · multiple choice

The force needed to turn a mass on a curve of radius \(r\) is:

The centripetal force \( mv^2/r \) points toward the centre — the outward "centrifugal" force is a reaction felt in a rotating frame, not the real applied force.

Problem 8 of 11 · multiple choice

The max cornering speed on a flat curve is:

Setting \( \mu mg=mv^2/r \) and cancelling \(m\) gives \( v_{\max}=\sqrt{\mu g r} \).

Problem 9 of 11 · multiple choice

In the normal (perpendicular-to-motion) direction on a slope, the acceleration is usually:

The block does not leave the surface, so there is no normal acceleration; \( \sum F=0 \) perpendicular gives \( N=mg\cos\theta \).

Problem 10 of 11 · multiple choice

An elevator accelerating upward makes the apparent weight of a passenger:

The floor must supply \( N=m(g+a) \) to accelerate the passenger up — the felt weight rises.

Problem 11 of 11 · multiple choice

The maximum cornering speed \( \sqrt{\mu g r} \) is independent of:

The mass cancels in \( \mu mg=mv^2/r \), so the limit depends only on \( \mu, g, r \) — not on how heavy the vehicle is.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A haul-road or ramp designer at a quarry or plant lives on the incline equations. Whether a loaded truck can climb a grade, or will slide on a wet slope, is decided by \( \tan\theta \) against the available friction \( \mu \): once \( \sin\theta \) exceeds \( \mu\cos\theta \) the vehicle accelerates downhill uncontrollably. The same balance sets how much a conveyor can incline before material slips back, and how steep a stored pile can stand — the angle of repose is just the slope at which \( \tan\theta=\mu \).

The cornering limit \( v_{\max}=\sqrt{\mu gr} \) governs every guided vehicle on site, from forklifts to AGVs to rail curves. Because the mass cancels, the safe speed depends only on the radius and the friction, which is why speed limits tighten on tight bends and why oil or dust on the floor — cutting \( \mu \) — is a genuine hazard: it lowers \( v_{\max} \) without changing anything the driver can see.
