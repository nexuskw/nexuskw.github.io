  • Locate the instant center of a rolling wheel — and use it to read velocities.

01 · Three kinds of rigid-body motion

A rigid body — one whose points keep fixed distances — moves in three ways: pure translation (every point has the same velocity), fixed-axis rotation (points circle a fixed line), and general plane motion (a combination, like a rolling wheel or a connecting rod). For fixed-axis rotation at angular speed \( \omega \) and angular acceleration \( \alpha \), a point at radius \(r\) has speed \( v=\omega r \), a tangential acceleration \( a_t=\alpha r \), and a normal (centripetal) acceleration \( a_n=\omega^2 r \). A wheel of radius \( 0.3 \) m spinning at \( \omega=10 \) rad/s has rim speed \( v=3 \) m/s and inward acceleration \( a_n=10^2(0.3)=30\ \text{m/s}^2 \). Rotation is just the particle kinematics of Lesson 1 wrapped onto a circle.

02 · Relative velocity and the instant centre

General plane motion is handled by the relative-velocity equation: the velocity of any point \(B\) is the velocity of a reference point \(A\) plus a rotation about \(A\), \( \mathbf{v}_B=\mathbf{v}_A+\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}_{B/A} \). A powerful shortcut is the instant centre of rotation — the one point of the body momentarily at rest, about which the whole body appears to be purely rotating at that instant. For a wheel rolling without slipping, the contact point with the ground is the instant centre: it has zero velocity, the centre moves at \( v=\omega r \), and the top point, being twice as far from the instant centre, moves at \( 2v \). Finding the instant centre often turns a hard velocity problem into a single \( v=\omega d \).

Key relations — rigid-body kinematics

Fixed axis: \( v=\omega r,\ a_t=\alpha r,\ a_n=\omega^2 r \). General motion: \( \mathbf{v}_B=\mathbf{v}_A+\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}_{B/A} \). Rolling: contact point is the instant centre (\(v=0\)).

03 · Gears and linkages

Meshing gears and belts share a contact-point speed, so \( \omega_1 r_1=\omega_2 r_2 \): a smaller gear turns faster in the same ratio as the radii. A pinion of radius \( 0.1 \) m at \( \omega_1=20 \) rad/s driving a gear of radius \( 0.05 \) m makes the gear turn at \( \omega_2=\dfrac{\omega_1 r_1}{r_2}=\dfrac{20(0.1)}{0.05}=40 \) rad/s. Linkages — the connecting rods and rockers of machinery — are solved with the same relative-velocity equation, tracking each joint in turn. This is the kinematic backbone of every gearbox, cam, and mechanism, where the goal is to convert one motion into another at a designed ratio.

04 · The working method

For a rigid-body velocity problem: (1) classify the motion — translation, fixed-axis, or general; (2) for fixed-axis use \( v=\omega r \) directly; (3) for general plane motion either write \( \mathbf{v}_B=\mathbf{v}_A+\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}_{B/A} \) or, faster, locate the instant centre and use \( v=\omega d \) from it; (4) for connected bodies, match the shared point (gear teeth, pin joints, the no-slip contact). The recurring insight is that rotation adds a velocity proportional to distance from the axis or instant centre — which is exactly why the rim of a wheel outruns its hub, and why the tip of a turbine blade sees far higher speeds and stresses than its root.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Fixed-axis rotation

Problem. A wheel of radius \(0.3\) m spins at \( \omega=10 \) rad/s and \( \alpha=2\ \text{rad/s}^2 \). Find the rim speed, normal acceleration, and tangential acceleration.

Solution. \( v=\omega r=10(0.3)=3 \) m/s. \( a_n=\omega^2 r=100(0.3)=30\ \text{m/s}^2 \) (toward the axis). \( a_t=\alpha r=2(0.3)=0.6\ \text{m/s}^2 \) (along the rim).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Gear pair

Problem. A pinion of radius \(0.1\) m turning at \(20\) rad/s meshes with a gear of radius \(0.05\) m. Find the gear's angular speed.

Solution. Meshing gears share the contact speed: \( \omega_1 r_1=\omega_2 r_2 \). So \( \omega_2=\dfrac{20(0.1)}{0.05}=40 \) rad/s — the smaller gear spins twice as fast.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Rolling wheel

Problem. A wheel of radius \(0.25\) m rolls without slipping with its centre at \(5\) m/s. Find \( \omega \) and the speed of the top point.

Solution. No-slip: \( v_{\text{centre}}=\omega r\Rightarrow\omega=5/0.25=20 \) rad/s. The contact point is the instant centre; the top point is twice as far from it, so its speed is \( 2v=10 \) m/s.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Gear and belt ratios \( \omega_1 r_1=\omega_2 r_2 \) are the everyday arithmetic of a maintenance engineer reading a drive train. Knowing a motor's speed and the pulley or gear sizes gives the driven speed of a pump, fan, or conveyor without a tachometer, and a belt slipping (breaking the shared contact speed) shows up as a driven-shaft speed below the ratio — a quick diagnostic. The tip-speed relation \( v=\omega r \) also sets hard limits: a fan or grinding wheel has a maximum rim speed above which centrifugal stress fails it, which is why maximum RPM is stamped on rotating tools.

The instant-centre idea guides anyone analysing a mechanism — a backhoe boom, a four-bar linkage, a rocker arm. Locating the point that is momentarily stationary turns the tangle of a moving linkage into a simple rotation, so the velocity of every pin and the pinch points of the mechanism can be read off. It is the same trick that explains why the bottom of a rolling tyre does not smear the road: at the instant of contact it is not moving.
