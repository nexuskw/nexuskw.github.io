01 · Impulse changes momentum

Integrate Newton's law over time instead of distance and you get the impulse–momentum principle: the impulse \( J=\int F\,dt \) of a force equals the change in momentum \( p=mv \) it produces, \( J=\Delta p=m\Delta v \). For a constant force this is simply \( J=F\,\Delta t \). A \( 200 \) N force acting for \( 0.1 \) s delivers \( J=20 \) N·s, which brings a \( 2 \) kg mass from rest to \( \Delta v=J/m=10 \) m/s. This is the natural tool for short, hard interactions — a hammer blow, a kick, a coupling impact — where the force is large, brief, and hard to measure, but its time-integral is exactly what changes the motion.

02 · Conservation of momentum

When no external impulse acts on a system, its total momentum is conserved — the internal forces between colliding bodies are equal and opposite, so they cancel in the sum. For two bodies, \( m_1v_1+m_2v_2=m_1v_1'+m_2v_2' \) through the collision. This holds whether the collision is bouncy or sticky, which is what makes it so powerful: it constrains the after-state without any knowledge of the messy forces during contact. It is the principle behind recoil, rocket thrust, and every impact calculation in the lesson.

Key principles — impulse & momentum

\[ J=\int F\,dt=\Delta p=m\Delta v;\qquad \sum m_iv_i=\sum m_iv_i'\ \text{(no external impulse)}. \]

03 · Impact and the coefficient of restitution

Momentum conservation gives one equation, but a collision of two bodies has two unknown final velocities, so a second relation is needed: the coefficient of restitution \( e=\dfrac{\text{separation speed}}{\text{approach speed}}=\dfrac{v_2'-v_1'}{v_1-v_2} \), a number between \(0\) and \(1\) set by the materials. A perfectly elastic impact has \( e=1 \) and conserves kinetic energy; a perfectly plastic one has \( e=0 \) and the bodies stick. Take a \( 2 \) kg body at \( 3 \) m/s striking a stationary \( 1 \) kg body and sticking (\( e=0 \)): momentum gives \( v'=\dfrac{2(3)}{3}=2 \) m/s. The kinetic energy falls from \( \tfrac12(2)(3^2)=9 \) J to \( \tfrac12(3)(2^2)=6 \) J — \( 3 \) J lost to deformation and heat. Energy is not conserved in impact unless \( e=1 \).

04 · The working method

For any impact: (1) draw the before and after states with velocities and signs on one axis; (2) write conservation of momentum \( \sum m_iv_i=\sum m_iv_i' \); (3) add the restitution relation \( e=(v_2'-v_1')/(v_1-v_2) \) as the second equation, and solve the pair. For a stick (\(e=0\)) the two bodies share a common final velocity and one equation suffices. Never assume kinetic energy is conserved — check \(e\) first, because treating a real, lossy impact as elastic is the classic error. Impulse–momentum over time and work–energy over distance are the two integrals of Newton's law, and together they answer almost every particle problem without solving a differential equation.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Impulse on a mass

Problem. A constant \(200\) N force acts on a \(2\) kg mass (initially at rest) for \(0.1\) s. Find the impulse and the final speed.

Solution. Impulse \( J=F\Delta t=200(0.1)=20 \) N·s. By impulse–momentum \( J=m\Delta v \), so \( \Delta v=20/2=10 \) m/s. Starting from rest, the final speed is \(10\) m/s.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Perfectly plastic collision

Problem. A \(2\) kg body moving at \(3\) m/s strikes a stationary \(1\) kg body and they stick together. Find the common velocity and the kinetic energy lost.

Solution. Momentum: \( 2(3)+1(0)=(2+1)v'\Rightarrow v'=\dfrac{6}{3}=2 \) m/s. \( KE \) before \( =\tfrac12(2)(3^2)=9 \) J; after \( =\tfrac12(3)(2^2)=6 \) J. Lost \( =9-6=3 \) J (to deformation and heat).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Restitution

Problem. Two equal masses collide, one at \(4\) m/s striking the other at rest, with \( e=0.5 \). Find the two final velocities.

Solution. Momentum (equal masses): \( v_1'+v_2'=4 \). Restitution: \( v_2'-v_1'=e(v_1-v_2)=0.5(4)=2 \). Solving: \( v_2'=3 \) m/s, \( v_1'=1 \) m/s. (Momentum check: \(1+3=4\) ✓.)

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

The impulse relation \( J=F\Delta t \) is the physics of every cushion, crumple zone, and pipe-hanger snubber on site. The momentum a moving mass must lose in a stop is fixed, so the only way to cut the peak force is to stretch the time of the stop: a longer \( \Delta t \) means a smaller \(F\). That is why buffers, shock mounts, and packaging exist, and why a hammer blow — a very short \( \Delta t \) — produces an enormous force from a modest swing, the tool a fitter uses to seat a pin that steady pressure cannot.

Momentum conservation sizes the reaction forces of fluid machinery. A jet of water striking a plate, or the change of direction inside a pump volute or a pipe bend, changes the fluid's momentum, and by Newton's third law the structure feels the equal and opposite force. Anchor blocks on large pipe bends and the thrust rating of a nozzle are momentum calculations — \( F=\dot m\,\Delta v \) — turned into bolts and concrete.
