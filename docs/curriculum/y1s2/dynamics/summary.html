Part 1 — Lectures

Lesson 01 · Particle kinematics

01 · Position, velocity, acceleration

Kinematics describes motion without yet asking what causes it. Along a straight line, velocity is the rate of change of position, \( v=dx/dt \), and acceleration the rate of change of velocity, \( a=dv/dt \). When the acceleration is constant these integrate to the three equations of motion that solve most rectilinear problems: \( v=v_0+at \), \( x=x_0+v_0t+\tfrac12at^2 \), and \( v^2=v_0^2+2a(x-x_0) \). A car starting from rest at \( a=3\ \text{m/s}^2 \) for \( 5 \) s reaches \( v=3(5)=15 \) m/s having travelled \( x=\tfrac12(3)(5^2)=37.5 \) m — and the third equation checks it, \( v=\sqrt{2(3)(37.5)}=15 \) m/s. These are the workhorses; the rest of the lesson is what changes when the path bends.

02 · Curved paths: normal and tangential

When a particle follows a curve, velocity stays tangent to the path but acceleration generally does not. The natural split is into a tangential component \( a_t=dv/dt \), which changes the speed, and a normal component \( a_n=\dfrac{v^2}{\rho} \), which changes the direction and always points toward the centre of curvature (here \( \rho \) is the radius of curvature). A car cornering at \( v=20 \) m/s on a curve of radius \( \rho=100 \) m has \( a_n=400/100=4\ \text{m/s}^2 \) inward even at constant speed; if it is also speeding up at \( a_t=2\ \text{m/s}^2 \), the total acceleration is \( a=\sqrt{a_t^2+a_n^2}=\sqrt{20}\approx4.47\ \text{m/s}^2 \). Direction change is itself an acceleration — the fact that makes cornering and rotating machinery demand force.

Key result — normal-tangential acceleration

\[ a_t=\frac{dv}{dt}\ \text{(speed change)},\qquad a_n=\frac{v^2}{\rho}\ \text{(direction change, toward centre)}. \]

03 · Projectile motion

A projectile is just two rectilinear motions at once: constant velocity horizontally, constant acceleration \( -g \) vertically. Launch speed \( v_0 \) at angle \( \theta \) gives horizontal \( v_0\cos\theta \) and vertical \( v_0\sin\theta \). The range on level ground is \( R=\dfrac{v_0^2\sin2\theta}{g} \) and the peak height \( H=\dfrac{v_0^2\sin^2\theta}{2g} \). For \( v_0=20 \) m/s at \( 30^\circ \): \( R=\dfrac{400\sin60^\circ}{9.81}\approx35.3 \) m and \( H=\dfrac{400(0.25)}{2(9.81)}\approx5.10 \) m. The two directions are solved separately and only share the clock — the single most useful idea in projectile problems.

04 · The working method

The disciplined routine is: (1) choose a coordinate description that matches the motion — rectilinear for straight-line, normal-tangential for curved paths, polar for radial problems; (2) write velocity and acceleration in that description; (3) for constant acceleration reach for the three equations of motion, otherwise integrate \( a\to v\to x \). Keep speed change (tangential) and direction change (normal) mentally separate on any curved path, because forgetting the \( v^2/\rho \) term is the commonest error in the subject — and it is exactly the term that, multiplied by mass in the next lesson, becomes the centripetal force a road, a bearing, or a turbine blade must supply.

Lesson 02 · Newton's second law for particles

01 · F = ma, applied through a free-body diagram

Newton's second law, \( \sum\mathbf{F}=m\mathbf{a} \), connects the forces on a particle to its acceleration. The whole discipline of using it is the free-body diagram (FBD): isolate the body, draw every force acting on it — weight, normal contact, friction, applied loads, tension — and nothing else, then sum components along convenient axes. On a curved or inclined path the axes are chosen to match the motion, which is why the normal–tangential split of the last lesson matters here. Get the FBD right and the algebra is routine; get it wrong — a missing or invented force — and no amount of algebra recovers. The FBD is the single most important habit in all of mechanics.

02 · Friction and the incline

On a plane inclined at \( \theta \), the weight \( mg \) resolves into a component \( mg\sin\theta \) driving the block down the slope and a component \( mg\cos\theta \) pressing it into the surface. The surface pushes back with the normal force \( N=mg\cos\theta \), and dry friction opposes sliding up to a limit \( f\le\mu N \). For a \( 10 \) kg block on a \( 30^\circ \) incline: \( N=10(9.81)\cos30^\circ\approx84.96 \) N, driving force \( mg\sin30^\circ=49.05 \) N, and maximum friction \( \mu N=0.2(84.96)\approx16.99 \) N. Since the drive exceeds friction, the block slides, and Newton's law along the slope gives \( a=g(\sin\theta-\mu\cos\theta)=9.81(0.5-0.173)\approx3.21\ \text{m/s}^2 \).

Key relations — incline & friction

\( N=mg\cos\theta \); driving force \( =mg\sin\theta \); friction \( f\le\mu N \). Sliding: \( a=g(\sin\theta-\mu\cos\theta) \).

03 · Curvilinear motion: the centripetal force

The normal acceleration \( a_n=v^2/\rho \) of the last lesson, multiplied by mass, is a real force requirement: turning a particle of mass \(m\) on a curve of radius \(r\) demands a centripetal force \( F=\dfrac{mv^2}{r} \) directed toward the centre. On a flat road that force can only come from friction, \( \mu mg\ge\dfrac{mv^2}{r} \), which caps the cornering speed at \( v_{\max}=\sqrt{\mu g r} \). For \( \mu=0.7,\ r=50 \) m: \( v_{\max}=\sqrt{0.7(9.81)(50)}\approx18.5 \) m/s (about \(67\) km/h). Exceed it and the friction budget is spent, the required centripetal force cannot be supplied, and the vehicle slides straight on — Newton's law making a hard physical prediction.

04 · The working method

For any kinetics problem: (1) draw the free-body diagram — every real force, no more; (2) choose axes that match the motion (along/across a slope, normal/tangential on a curve); (3) write \( \sum F=ma \) in each direction and solve. Two checks guard the work: the normal direction usually has no acceleration (so \( \sum F=0 \) there, fixing \(N\)), and units must close to newtons. This routine handles the block, the cornering car, the elevator, and the whole of the following energy and momentum lessons, which are Newton's law integrated over distance and over time respectively.

Lesson 03 · Work and energy

01 · Work: force acting through distance

The work done by a constant force is \( W=Fd\cos\theta \) — the force times the distance times the cosine of the angle between them, so only the component of force along the motion counts. A force perpendicular to the path (a normal force, the tension in a turning string) does no work at all. This is the dot product of the vector lessons, integrated along a path. Work is measured in joules, and it is the currency in which the whole energy method is denominated: instead of tracking forces and accelerations moment by moment, we track how much work is done and where it goes.

02 · Kinetic energy and the work–energy theorem

A moving particle carries kinetic energy \( KE=\tfrac12mv^2 \), and the work–energy theorem states that the net work done on it equals its change in kinetic energy: \( W_{\text{net}}=\Delta KE \). This is Newton's law integrated over distance, and it is an accounting identity — energy in equals energy stored. A \( 100 \) N force pushing a \( 20 \) kg block from rest over \( 10 \) m (frictionless) does \( W=1000 \) J, so \( \tfrac12(20)v^2=1000 \Rightarrow v=10 \) m/s. The power of the method is that it never asks about the acceleration or the time — only the endpoints — which is exactly what makes it the fast route through problems where forces vary along the way.

Key identity — work–energy

\[ W_{\text{net}}=\Delta KE=\tfrac12mv_2^2-\tfrac12mv_1^2. \]

03 · Potential energy and conservation

Some forces store the work done against them as potential energy, recoverable later: gravity stores \( PE=mgh \), a spring stores \( \tfrac12kx^2 \). When only such conservative forces act, the sum \( KE+PE \) is constant — conservation of energy. A mass dropped from height \( h \) converts all its \( mgh \) into \( \tfrac12mv^2 \), giving \( v=\sqrt{2gh} \); from \( h=5 \) m, \( v=\sqrt{2(9.81)(5)}\approx9.9 \) m/s, independent of the mass. A spring of stiffness \( k=2000 \) N/m compressed \( x=0.1 \) m stores \( \tfrac12(2000)(0.1)^2=10 \) J, enough to launch a \( 0.5 \) kg mass to \( v=\sqrt{2(10)/0.5}\approx6.3 \) m/s. Friction, by contrast, is non-conservative — it drains mechanical energy into heat, and its work is where the "lost" energy goes.

04 · Power and the working method

Power is the rate of doing work, \( P=\dfrac{dW}{dt}=Fv \) — the number that sizes motors and engines. A \( 500 \) N pull at \( 2 \) m/s delivers \( 1000 \) W. The energy method's routine is: (1) identify the states (start and end) and the energies present at each — kinetic, gravitational, spring; (2) account for any work added by applied forces or drained by friction; (3) set energy-in equal to energy-out and solve. Choose energy over \( F=ma \) whenever the question links speed to position and the time in between does not matter — falling bodies, spring launches, roller-coaster speeds — because it collapses a differential equation into one algebraic balance.

Lesson 04 · Impulse, momentum, and impact

01 · Impulse changes momentum

Integrate Newton's law over time instead of distance and you get the impulse–momentum principle: the impulse \( J=\int F\,dt \) of a force equals the change in momentum \( p=mv \) it produces, \( J=\Delta p=m\Delta v \). For a constant force this is simply \( J=F\,\Delta t \). A \( 200 \) N force acting for \( 0.1 \) s delivers \( J=20 \) N·s, which brings a \( 2 \) kg mass from rest to \( \Delta v=J/m=10 \) m/s. This is the natural tool for short, hard interactions — a hammer blow, a kick, a coupling impact — where the force is large, brief, and hard to measure, but its time-integral is exactly what changes the motion.

02 · Conservation of momentum

When no external impulse acts on a system, its total momentum is conserved — the internal forces between colliding bodies are equal and opposite, so they cancel in the sum. For two bodies, \( m_1v_1+m_2v_2=m_1v_1'+m_2v_2' \) through the collision. This holds whether the collision is bouncy or sticky, which is what makes it so powerful: it constrains the after-state without any knowledge of the messy forces during contact. It is the principle behind recoil, rocket thrust, and every impact calculation in the lesson.

Key principles — impulse & momentum

\[ J=\int F\,dt=\Delta p=m\Delta v;\qquad \sum m_iv_i=\sum m_iv_i'\ \text{(no external impulse)}. \]

03 · Impact and the coefficient of restitution

Momentum conservation gives one equation, but a collision of two bodies has two unknown final velocities, so a second relation is needed: the coefficient of restitution \( e=\dfrac{\text{separation speed}}{\text{approach speed}}=\dfrac{v_2'-v_1'}{v_1-v_2} \), a number between \(0\) and \(1\) set by the materials. A perfectly elastic impact has \( e=1 \) and conserves kinetic energy; a perfectly plastic one has \( e=0 \) and the bodies stick. Take a \( 2 \) kg body at \( 3 \) m/s striking a stationary \( 1 \) kg body and sticking (\( e=0 \)): momentum gives \( v'=\dfrac{2(3)}{3}=2 \) m/s. The kinetic energy falls from \( \tfrac12(2)(3^2)=9 \) J to \( \tfrac12(3)(2^2)=6 \) J — \( 3 \) J lost to deformation and heat. Energy is not conserved in impact unless \( e=1 \).

04 · The working method

For any impact: (1) draw the before and after states with velocities and signs on one axis; (2) write conservation of momentum \( \sum m_iv_i=\sum m_iv_i' \); (3) add the restitution relation \( e=(v_2'-v_1')/(v_1-v_2) \) as the second equation, and solve the pair. For a stick (\(e=0\)) the two bodies share a common final velocity and one equation suffices. Never assume kinetic energy is conserved — check \(e\) first, because treating a real, lossy impact as elastic is the classic error. Impulse–momentum over time and work–energy over distance are the two integrals of Newton's law, and together they answer almost every particle problem without solving a differential equation.

Lesson 05 · Systems of particles

01 · The centre of mass moves as one particle

A system of particles has a centre of mass at \( \bar{x}=\dfrac{\sum m_i x_i}{\sum m_i} \), the mass-weighted average position; two masses of \( 2 \) kg at \( x=0 \) and \( 3 \) kg at \( x=5 \) m have \( \bar{x}=\dfrac{2(0)+3(5)}{5}=3 \) m. The central theorem is that this point obeys Newton's law for the whole system as if it were a single particle carrying the total mass: \( \sum\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}}=M\mathbf{a}_{\text{cm}} \). Internal forces — however violent, like the forces inside an explosion — cancel in equal-and-opposite pairs and never move the centre of mass. This is why a spinning, tumbling wrench thrown across a room still has its centre of mass trace a clean parabola.

02 · Momentum of a system

Because internal forces cancel, the total momentum of a system changes only under external impulse, and with none it is conserved: \( \sum m_i\mathbf{v}_i=\text{const} \). This is the principle behind recoil and propulsion. A \( 60 \) kg person, initially still, throwing a \( 2 \) kg ball at \( 10 \) m/s must recoil so the total momentum stays zero: \( 60v=2(10)\Rightarrow v=0.33 \) m/s backward. The same balance, applied to gas thrown out of a nozzle, is how a rocket climbs. Momentum bookkeeping across a whole system needs no knowledge of the internal forces — only what crosses its boundary.

Key principle — systems of particles

\[ \sum\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}}=M\mathbf{a}_{\text{cm}};\qquad \text{no external force}\Rightarrow \sum m_i\mathbf{v}_i=\text{const}. \]

03 · Steady mass flow: the jet force

When mass flows steadily rather than in discrete lumps — a stream of water, a gas jet — the momentum principle takes a flow form. Fluid of density \( \rho \) crossing area \( A \) at speed \( v \) carries a mass flow \( \dot m=\rho A v \) (kg/s). If a plate stops the jet, it destroys that momentum every second, and by the momentum principle must supply a force \( F=\dot m\,v=\rho A v^2 \). A \( 20 \) m/s water jet through a \( A=0.001\ \text{m}^2 \) nozzle has \( \dot m=1000(0.001)(20)=20 \) kg/s and delivers \( F=20(20)=400 \) N to the plate. This single result — force equals mass-flow times velocity change — is the seed of turbomachinery, where the same momentum exchange between fluid and blade produces useful torque.

04 · The working method

For any system: (1) draw a boundary and identify what crosses it — external forces and any mass flow; (2) for the bulk motion apply \( \sum\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}}=M\mathbf{a}_{\text{cm}} \), ignoring all internal forces; (3) for conservation, set total momentum before equal to after; (4) for steady flow, use \( F=\dot m\,\Delta v \) with \( \dot m=\rho Av \). The recurring discipline is to separate internal from external: internal forces rearrange a system but never move its centre of mass or change its total momentum. That distinction is what makes a complicated many-body problem collapse to one equation about the boundary.

Lesson 06 · Rigid-body kinematics

01 · Three kinds of rigid-body motion

A rigid body — one whose points keep fixed distances — moves in three ways: pure translation (every point has the same velocity), fixed-axis rotation (points circle a fixed line), and general plane motion (a combination, like a rolling wheel or a connecting rod). For fixed-axis rotation at angular speed \( \omega \) and angular acceleration \( \alpha \), a point at radius \(r\) has speed \( v=\omega r \), a tangential acceleration \( a_t=\alpha r \), and a normal (centripetal) acceleration \( a_n=\omega^2 r \). A wheel of radius \( 0.3 \) m spinning at \( \omega=10 \) rad/s has rim speed \( v=3 \) m/s and inward acceleration \( a_n=10^2(0.3)=30\ \text{m/s}^2 \). Rotation is just the particle kinematics of Lesson 1 wrapped onto a circle.

02 · Relative velocity and the instant centre

General plane motion is handled by the relative-velocity equation: the velocity of any point \(B\) is the velocity of a reference point \(A\) plus a rotation about \(A\), \( \mathbf{v}_B=\mathbf{v}_A+\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}_{B/A} \). A powerful shortcut is the instant centre of rotation — the one point of the body momentarily at rest, about which the whole body appears to be purely rotating at that instant. For a wheel rolling without slipping, the contact point with the ground is the instant centre: it has zero velocity, the centre moves at \( v=\omega r \), and the top point, being twice as far from the instant centre, moves at \( 2v \). Finding the instant centre often turns a hard velocity problem into a single \( v=\omega d \).

Key relations — rigid-body kinematics

Fixed axis: \( v=\omega r,\ a_t=\alpha r,\ a_n=\omega^2 r \). General motion: \( \mathbf{v}_B=\mathbf{v}_A+\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}_{B/A} \). Rolling: contact point is the instant centre (\(v=0\)).

03 · Gears and linkages

Meshing gears and belts share a contact-point speed, so \( \omega_1 r_1=\omega_2 r_2 \): a smaller gear turns faster in the same ratio as the radii. A pinion of radius \( 0.1 \) m at \( \omega_1=20 \) rad/s driving a gear of radius \( 0.05 \) m makes the gear turn at \( \omega_2=\dfrac{\omega_1 r_1}{r_2}=\dfrac{20(0.1)}{0.05}=40 \) rad/s. Linkages — the connecting rods and rockers of machinery — are solved with the same relative-velocity equation, tracking each joint in turn. This is the kinematic backbone of every gearbox, cam, and mechanism, where the goal is to convert one motion into another at a designed ratio.

04 · The working method

For a rigid-body velocity problem: (1) classify the motion — translation, fixed-axis, or general; (2) for fixed-axis use \( v=\omega r \) directly; (3) for general plane motion either write \( \mathbf{v}_B=\mathbf{v}_A+\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}_{B/A} \) or, faster, locate the instant centre and use \( v=\omega d \) from it; (4) for connected bodies, match the shared point (gear teeth, pin joints, the no-slip contact). The recurring insight is that rotation adds a velocity proportional to distance from the axis or instant centre — which is exactly why the rim of a wheel outruns its hub, and why the tip of a turbine blade sees far higher speeds and stresses than its root.

Lesson 07 · Relative acceleration and Coriolis

01 · Acceleration of a rigid body

Differentiating the relative-velocity equation gives the relative-acceleration equation for a rigid body: the acceleration of a point \(B\) is that of a reference point \(A\) plus two rotational terms, \( \mathbf{a}_B=\mathbf{a}_A+\boldsymbol{\alpha}\times\mathbf{r}_{B/A}-\omega^2\mathbf{r}_{B/A} \). The \( \boldsymbol{\alpha}\times\mathbf{r} \) term is tangential (magnitude \( \alpha r \), from angular acceleration) and the \( -\omega^2\mathbf{r} \) term is normal (magnitude \( \omega^2 r \), always pointing back toward \(A\)). For a point at \( r=0.4 \) m on a link turning at \( \omega=5 \) rad/s, \( \alpha=3\ \text{rad/s}^2 \): the normal part is \( \omega^2 r=10\ \text{m/s}^2 \) and the tangential part \( \alpha r=1.2\ \text{m/s}^2 \). This solves the acceleration of linkages joint by joint.

02 · Rotating frames and the Coriolis term

When a particle also moves relative to a rotating body — a bead sliding in a spinning slot, a fluid particle in an impeller — the acceleration seen in the fixed frame gains an extra piece: the Coriolis acceleration \( \mathbf{a}_{\text{Cor}}=2\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}} \), of magnitude \( 2\omega v_{\text{rel}} \) and always perpendicular to the relative velocity. A bead sliding out at \( v_{\text{rel}}=2 \) m/s along an arm rotating at \( \omega=5 \) rad/s experiences \( a_{\text{Cor}}=2(5)(2)=20\ \text{m/s}^2 \) sideways. It is not a "force" but a real acceleration that appears because the frame itself is turning — the term that must be added whenever motion is described relative to rotating machinery.

Key term — Coriolis acceleration

\[ \mathbf{a}_{\text{Cor}}=2\,\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}},\qquad |\mathbf{a}_{\text{Cor}}|=2\omega v_{\text{rel}}\ \perp\ \mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}}. \]

03 · The full rotating-frame acceleration

Put together, a particle moving with velocity \( \mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}} \) and acceleration \( \mathbf{a}_{\text{rel}} \) relative to a body rotating at \( \omega \) has, in the fixed frame, \( \mathbf{a}=\mathbf{a}_{\text{rel}}+2\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}}+\boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r})+\boldsymbol{\alpha}\times\mathbf{r} \) — relative, Coriolis, centripetal, and tangential terms. For the bead above (\( r=0.4 \), \( \omega=5 \), \( v_{\text{rel}}=2 \), \( a_{\text{rel}}=0 \), \( \alpha=0 \)): a centripetal \( \omega^2 r=10\ \text{m/s}^2 \) inward and a Coriolis \( 2\omega v_{\text{rel}}=20\ \text{m/s}^2 \) sideways, combining to \( \sqrt{10^2+20^2}\approx22.4\ \text{m/s}^2 \). Miss the Coriolis term and the answer is wrong by the larger contribution.

04 · The working method

For acceleration analysis: (1) for a plain rigid body, use \( \mathbf{a}_B=\mathbf{a}_A+\boldsymbol{\alpha}\times\mathbf{r}-\omega^2\mathbf{r} \), tracking tangential and normal parts separately; (2) whenever a part slides relative to a rotating member — slider-in-slot mechanisms, Geneva drives, pump and turbine passages — add the Coriolis term \( 2\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}} \); (3) sum the vector components on chosen axes. The one habit that prevents the classic error is to ask, on every rotating-frame problem, "is anything moving relative to the rotating body?" — if yes, Coriolis is present, and it is often the dominant term.

Lesson 08 · Rigid-body kinetics: Newton–Euler

01 · Newton–Euler: force and torque

A rigid body needs two equations of motion: one for its translation and one for its rotation. Newton's law moves the centre of mass, \( \sum\mathbf{F}=m\mathbf{a}_{\text{cm}} \); Euler's law spins the body, \( \sum M=I\alpha \) for rotation about a fixed axis (or about the centre of mass). The new quantity is the mass moment of inertia \( I \), the rotational counterpart of mass — it measures how hard a body is to angularly accelerate, and unlike mass it depends on how the mass is arranged about the axis. Mass far from the axis counts far more, because \( I=\int r^2\,dm \) weights each element by the square of its distance.

02 · Moments of inertia and the parallel-axis theorem

Common shapes have tabulated inertias about their centres: a solid disk or cylinder \( \tfrac12mr^2 \), a thin ring \( mr^2 \), a slender rod \( \tfrac{1}{12}mL^2 \). To shift the reference axis off the centre by a distance \(d\), the parallel-axis theorem adds \( md^2 \): \( I=I_{\text{cm}}+md^2 \). A \( 6 \) kg rod of length \( 1 \) m has \( I_{\text{cm}}=\tfrac{1}{12}(6)(1^2)=0.5\ \text{kg·m}^2 \) about its centre, and about its end \( I=0.5+6(0.5)^2=2\ \text{kg·m}^2 \) — which matches the direct formula \( \tfrac13mL^2 \). Composite bodies are handled by adding the inertias of their parts, each shifted to the common axis.

Key laws — rotational kinetics

\( \sum M=I\alpha \); disk \( I=\tfrac12mr^2 \), rod \( \tfrac{1}{12}mL^2 \). Parallel axis: \( I=I_{\text{cm}}+md^2 \).

03 · Applying ΣM = Iα

The rotational law is used exactly like \( F=ma \): find the net torque, divide by the inertia, get the angular acceleration. A solid disk of \( m=10 \) kg, \( r=0.2 \) m has \( I=\tfrac12(10)(0.2^2)=0.2\ \text{kg·m}^2 \); a torque of \( 5 \) N·m spins it up at \( \alpha=\dfrac{T}{I}=\dfrac{5}{0.2}=25\ \text{rad/s}^2 \). For a body in general plane motion — a wheel that both rolls and accelerates — the two equations \( \sum F=ma_{\text{cm}} \) and \( \sum M=I\alpha \) are solved together, linked by the no-slip condition \( a_{\text{cm}}=\alpha r \). This pair handles rolling cylinders, driven pulleys, and every rotating machine element under load.

04 · The working method

For rigid-body kinetics: (1) draw the free-body diagram, now including where each force acts (its moment arm) — position matters for torque; (2) write \( \sum F=ma_{\text{cm}} \) for translation and \( \sum M=I\alpha \) for rotation, taking moments about the centre of mass or a fixed axis; (3) for a composite body, build \( I \) by adding tabulated parts via the parallel-axis theorem; (4) for rolling, add the constraint \( a_{\text{cm}}=\alpha r \). The essential new idea is that where mass sits, and where a force acts, both matter now — the same force applied further from the axis produces more angular acceleration, which is why levers, gears, and moment arms run all of machine design.

Lesson 09 · Rotational energy and momentum

01 · Rotational kinetic energy

A rotating body stores rotational kinetic energy \( KE=\tfrac12I\omega^2 \) — the exact analogue of \( \tfrac12mv^2 \), with inertia replacing mass and angular speed replacing linear speed. A flywheel with \( I=0.2\ \text{kg·m}^2 \) spinning at \( \omega=100 \) rad/s stores \( \tfrac12(0.2)(100^2)=1000 \) J. Because the energy scales with \( \omega^2 \), a modest increase in speed stores disproportionately more energy, which is why flywheels are run fast rather than made heavy. The work–energy theorem carries straight over: the net work of a torque equals the change in rotational kinetic energy, \( \int M\,d\theta=\Delta(\tfrac12I\omega^2) \).

02 · Angular momentum and angular impulse

The rotational counterpart of momentum is angular momentum \( L=I\omega \), and the counterpart of impulse is angular impulse \( \int M\,dt \), with the principle \( \int M\,dt=\Delta L \). The flywheel above has \( L=0.2(100)=20\ \text{kg·m}^2/\text{s} \); a steady torque of \( 4 \) N·m applied for \( 5 \) s delivers an angular impulse of \( 20 \), changing its angular speed by \( \Delta\omega=\dfrac{20}{0.2}=100 \) rad/s. This is the tool for spin-up and braking over time — sizing a starter torque, or the time for a brake to stop a rotor — just as linear impulse handled straight-line stopping.

Key quantities — rotational energy & momentum

\[ KE=\tfrac12I\omega^2,\qquad L=I\omega,\qquad \int M\,dt=\Delta L. \]

03 · Conservation of angular momentum

When no external torque acts, angular momentum is conserved: \( I_1\omega_1=I_2\omega_2 \). If a spinning body redistributes its mass — a skater pulling in their arms, a turntable — reducing \(I\) forces \( \omega \) to rise to keep the product fixed. Halve the inertia and the spin doubles. Notice the kinetic energy is not conserved here: pulling the masses inward does work against the outward "centrifugal" tendency, and that work shows up as extra rotational energy (\( \tfrac12I\omega^2 \) rises from \(1000\) J to \(2000\) J when \(I\) halves and \( \omega \) doubles). Momentum is conserved; energy is added by the muscles or the mechanism doing the pulling.

04 · Flywheels and the working method

These ideas make the flywheel — a rotor sized to store \( \tfrac12I\omega^2 \) and release it to smooth a fluctuating load. A press or engine that takes energy in bursts draws it from the flywheel between strokes, and the wheel's speed dips and recovers within a tolerance set by \( I \) and \( \omega \). The working method: (1) for a speed–angle question use energy, \( \int M\,d\theta=\Delta(\tfrac12I\omega^2) \); (2) for a speed–time question use angular impulse, \( \int M\,dt=\Delta(I\omega) \); (3) when no external torque acts, conserve \( I\omega \). Choosing the energy or the momentum route is the same decision as in particle dynamics — energy for position, momentum for time.

Lesson 10 · Balancing of rotors

01 · Where unbalance comes from

A rotor is balanced when its mass is distributed symmetrically about the axis of rotation. Any deviation — a heavy spot, a machining tolerance, a bit of trapped dirt — puts mass off-axis, and that offset mass generates an outward centrifugal force \( F=m r\omega^2 \) as it spins, where \(m\) is the offset mass and \(r\) its radius from the axis. The critical feature is the \( \omega^2 \): the force grows with the square of speed, so an unbalance harmless at low speed becomes violent at operating speed. A \( 50 \) g spot at \( r=0.1 \) m produces \( 0.05(0.1)(100^2)=50 \) N at \( 100 \) rad/s, but \( 450 \) N at \( 300 \) rad/s. This rotating force is what shakes the machine and loads its bearings.

02 · Static versus dynamic unbalance

Two distinct faults exist. Static unbalance is a single offset mass — the centre of mass lies off the axis — and it can be found at rest, since the heavy spot rolls to the bottom; correcting it needs one balancing mass in one plane. Dynamic unbalance is subtler: two equal offsets on opposite sides and in different planes along the shaft leave the centre of mass on the axis, so the rotor balances at rest, yet spinning it creates a rotating couple that wobbles the shaft. Dynamic unbalance cannot be detected statically and needs correction in two planes. Real rotors generally have both, which is why balancing is done spinning, on a balancing machine, not by hand.

Key result — unbalance force

\[ F=m\,r\,\omega^2\quad(\text{rotates at 1× shaft speed, grows as }\omega^2). \]

03 · Balancing logic

Balancing cancels the rotating force by adding (or removing) mass so the net \( \sum m_i r_i=0 \) as vectors. For a single static unbalance \( m r \), a correction mass \( m_b \) at radius \( r_b \), placed diametrically opposite, restores balance when \( m_b r_b=m r \) — so a \( 0.05\times0.1 \) unbalance is cured by \( 0.025 \) kg at \( 0.2 \) m. Because the force is \( m r\omega^2 \), only the product \( m r \) (the "unbalance," in g·mm) matters, not the speed — balance it once and it stays balanced at every speed. Dynamic unbalance is corrected the same way but in two planes at once, cancelling both the net force and the net couple.

04 · Why balancing matters, and the method

The rotating unbalance force appears in vibration at exactly \( 1\times \) the running speed — its signature line on the spectrum of the previous course's Fourier lesson. Left uncorrected it fatigues shafts, hammers bearings, loosens fasteners, and radiates noise, with severity climbing as \( \omega^2 \). The method: (1) identify the unbalance as static (one plane) or dynamic (two planes); (2) measure the magnitude and angular position of the heavy spot (a balancing machine reads the \(1\times\) response); (3) add or remove a correcting mass so \( m_b r_b=m r \) opposite the heavy spot, in the required number of planes. Balancing is the first defence against machinery vibration, applied before a rotor ever enters service.

Lesson 11 · Introduction to vibration

01 · The single-degree-of-freedom oscillator

The simplest vibrating system is a mass on a spring with damping — the single-degree-of-freedom (SDOF) oscillator. Newton's law for it is \( m\ddot{x}+c\dot{x}+kx=0 \), exactly the second-order equation of the mathematics course, now with a mechanical meaning: \(m\) the mass, \(k\) the spring stiffness, \(c\) the damping. Its free response is set by two numbers. The natural frequency \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) is the rate it rings undamped; for \( m=2 \) kg, \( k=800 \) N/m, \( \omega_n=\sqrt{400}=20 \) rad/s, or \( f=\omega_n/2\pi\approx3.18 \) Hz. This one system is the model for every structure, machine mount, and rotor-support that can vibrate — the whole of the Vibrations course is built on it.

02 · Damping ratio and the response

The second number is the damping ratio \( \zeta=\dfrac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}} \), the actual damping measured against the critical amount \( c_c=2\sqrt{mk} \). For the system above, \( c_c=2\sqrt{1600}=80 \), so a damper \( c=8 \) gives \( \zeta=0.1 \) — underdamped. An underdamped system oscillates inside a decaying envelope: it rings at the damped frequency \( \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\approx19.9 \) rad/s while its amplitude decays as \( e^{-\zeta\omega_n t} \). Small damping barely shifts the frequency but steadily bleeds the amplitude, which is exactly the ringing-and-fading response measured when a real structure is struck.

Key numbers — the SDOF oscillator

\[ \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m},\qquad \zeta=\frac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}},\qquad \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}. \]

03 · Measuring damping from a decay

The great practical value of the SDOF model is that both parameters can be read from a single measured decay — strike the structure and record the free vibration. The frequency of the ringing gives \( \omega_d \) (and hence \( \omega_n \)), and the rate of amplitude decay gives \( \zeta \) through the logarithmic decrement \( \delta=\ln\dfrac{x_1}{x_2} \), the natural log of the ratio of two successive peaks. For light damping \( \zeta\approx\dfrac{\delta}{2\pi} \). If successive peaks measure \( 10 \) mm and \( 8 \) mm, then \( \delta=\ln(10/8)=0.223 \) and \( \zeta\approx0.223/2\pi\approx0.036 \). No shaker or force measurement is needed — just the trace of a free decay, which is why the impact "bump test" is the workhorse of field vibration diagnosis.

04 · The working method

To characterise any vibrating system: (1) model it as an SDOF \( m,c,k \), lumping the effective mass and stiffness; (2) compute \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) and \( \zeta=c/(2\sqrt{mk}) \) — the natural frequency you must keep operating speeds away from, and the damping that limits any resonant response; (3) from a measured decay, extract \( \omega_d \) from the ringing frequency and \( \zeta \) from the logarithmic decrement. These two numbers, \( \omega_n \) and \( \zeta \), are the complete fingerprint of the free response — and, joined with the forced-response curve of the mathematics course, they are what the Vibrations and rotating-machinery courses spend the next years applying to real machines.
