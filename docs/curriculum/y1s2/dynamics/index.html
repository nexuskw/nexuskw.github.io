DYN 152 · YEAR 1 · SEMESTER 2

Engineering Dynamics

Motion with forces attached: kinematics of particles and rigid bodies, Newton–Euler methods, work–energy and impulse–momentum — aimed squarely at the rotating machinery this pathway lives on.

  • MIT OCW 2.003 Watch lectures ▶ Engineering Dynamics (ocw.mit.edu)
01

Particle kinematics

Position, velocity, acceleration in rectilinear and curvilinear motion. Normal-tangential and polar descriptions. Reading motion from data rather than formulas.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Distinguish normal and tangential acceleration on a curved conveyor path.
  • When is polar description more natural than Cartesian?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.003 Watch lectures ▶

02

Newton's second law for particles

F = ma applied with disciplined FBDs. Friction, drag, and constraint forces. Curvilinear applications: vehicles on curves, whirling masses.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why must the FBD precede F = ma, not follow it?
  • A mass on a curve at constant speed still accelerates: toward where, and why?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.003 Watch lectures ▶

03

Work and energy

Work of forces and couples; kinetic and potential energy. The work–energy theorem as an accounting identity. When energy methods beat force methods.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • When does the work–energy theorem beat force analysis?
  • Why does a couple's work involve angle, not distance?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.003 Watch lectures ▶

04

Impulse, momentum, and impact

Linear impulse–momentum; conservation in collisions. Coefficient of restitution. Hammer blows and coupling impacts as plant-floor examples.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What does the coefficient of restitution measure, and what are its limits 0 and 1?
  • Why do hammer blows deliver huge forces from modest energy?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.003 Watch lectures ▶

05

Systems of particles

Center of mass motion and internal versus external forces. Steady mass flow forces — the jet on a plate that becomes pump and turbine thinking. Rocket-style variable-mass reasoning in brief.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why do internal forces cancel in a system of particles?
  • Compute the force of a steady water jet on a plate — which principle applies?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.003 Watch lectures ▶

06

Rigid-body kinematics

Translation, fixed-axis rotation, and general plane motion. Relative velocity and instant centers. Gear and linkage motion analyzed cleanly.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Locate the instant center of a rolling wheel — and use it to read velocities.
  • What makes general plane motion decomposable into translation plus rotation?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.003 Watch lectures ▶

07

Relative acceleration and Coriolis

Acceleration analysis of linkages. Rotating reference frames and the Coriolis term. Where slider-in-slot mechanisms get their surprising accelerations.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Where does the Coriolis term appear in a slider-in-slot mechanism?
  • Why does acceleration analysis need velocity analysis first?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.003 Watch lectures ▶

08

Rigid-body kinetics: Newton–Euler

Mass moment of inertia — measured, tabulated, and composed. ΣM = Iα for fixed-axis rotation and general plane motion. Starting torque of a loaded drum computed honestly.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does mass moment of inertia depend on the axis chosen?
  • Starting torque of a loaded drum: which two terms add?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.003 Watch lectures ▶

09

Rotational energy and momentum

Kinetic energy of rotation; angular impulse and momentum. Flywheels as energy stores — sizing one for load smoothing. Conservation demonstrations that survive into turbine trip analysis.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does a flywheel smooth speed but not average torque?
  • Rotational KE: why does doubling speed quadruple stored energy?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.003 Watch lectures ▶

10

Balancing of rotors

Static versus dynamic unbalance defined precisely. Single- and two-plane balancing logic. Why unbalance shows at 1× running speed — the vibration analyst's first spectral line explained by dynamics.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Static versus dynamic unbalance: what does each do to bearing forces?
  • Why does pure static unbalance show at exactly 1× rotation frequency?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.003 Watch lectures ▶

11

Introduction to vibration

The single degree-of-freedom oscillator from Newton's law. Natural frequency and damping ratio measured from a decay trace. The bridge lesson into the Year 3 Vibrations course.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • From a decay trace, how do you extract damping ratio?
  • What does natural frequency depend on — and pointedly not depend on?

Taught from — MIT OCW 2.003 Watch lectures ▶
