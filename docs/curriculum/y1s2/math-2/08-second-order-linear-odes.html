Second-order linear ODEs

Homogeneous solutions: overdamped, critically damped, underdamped. The characteristic equation as the system's fingerprint. Mass-spring-damper solved completely — memorize this solution's shape.

01 · From calculus to a quadratic

The homogeneous second-order equation with constant coefficients, \( m x''+c x'+k x=0 \), is the mass–spring–damper — and by direct analogy every RLC circuit and simple oscillator you will meet. It is solved by a guess that converts calculus into algebra: try \( x=e^{rt} \). Then \( x'=re^{rt} \), \( x''=r^2e^{rt} \), and the equation becomes \( (mr^2+cr+k)e^{rt}=0 \). Since the exponential never vanishes, the bracket must — the characteristic equation \( mr^2+cr+k=0 \). A differential problem has become a quadratic, which is why the quadratic formula is the working tool of second-order dynamics.

02 · Three regimes, set by the discriminant

The discriminant \( \Delta=c^2-4mk \) sorts the behaviour completely. If \( \Delta>0 \), two distinct real negative roots give \( x=Ae^{r_1t}+Be^{r_2t} \) — a pure decay, overdamped. If \( \Delta=0 \), a repeated root gives \( x=(A+Bt)e^{rt} \) — the fastest return with no overshoot, critically damped. If \( \Delta<0 \), complex roots \( -\sigma\pm i\omega_d \) give \( x=e^{-\sigma t}(A\cos\omega_d t+B\sin\omega_d t) \) — a decaying oscillation, underdamped. For \( x''+5x'+6x=0 \), \( \Delta=25-24=1>0 \) with roots \( -2,-3 \): overdamped. For \( x''+2x'+5x=0 \), \( \Delta=4-20=-16<0 \) with roots \( -1\pm2i \): underdamped, oscillating at \( \omega_d=2 \) while decaying at rate \(1\).

Key test — the discriminant

\[ \Delta=c^2-4mk:\quad \Delta>0\ \text{overdamped},\ \ \Delta=0\ \text{critical},\ \ \Delta<0\ \text{underdamped}. \]

03 · Natural frequency and damping ratio

Two derived numbers name the system independently of its scale. The natural frequency \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) is the rate it would oscillate undamped; with \( m=1,\ k=100 \), \( \omega_n=10 \) rad/s. The damping ratio \( \zeta=\dfrac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}} \) measures the actual damping against the critical amount \( c_c=2\sqrt{mk} \); here \( c_c=20 \), so \( c=8 \) gives \( \zeta=0.4 \) — underdamped, since \( \zeta<1 \). The three regimes restate cleanly as \( \zeta<1 \) (under), \( \zeta=1 \) (critical), \( \zeta>1 \) (over). This pair \( (\omega_n,\zeta) \) is scale-free, so two systems with the same \( \zeta \) behave alike whatever their mass — which is why datasheets quote them.

Key numbers — \( \omega_n \) and \( \zeta \)

\( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \); \( \zeta=\dfrac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}} \). Regimes: \( \zeta<1 \) under, \( \zeta=1 \) critical, \( \zeta>1 \) over.

04 · The shape to memorise, and the method

Commit the underdamped shape to memory, because it is the response of almost every real machine: an oscillation at \( \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2} \) inside a decaying envelope \( e^{-\zeta\omega_n t} \). The disciplined method for any \( mx''+cx'+kx=0 \) is: (1) compute \( \Delta=c^2-4mk \) first — its sign is the entire qualitative story before a single root is found; (2) read \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m} \) and \( \zeta=c/(2\sqrt{mk}) \) to put numbers on it; (3) fix the constants \(A,B\) from the initial conditions. Sign first, then magnitudes. This is exactly how vibration and control engineers diagnose a system from its coefficients — and the forced version, in the next lesson, adds a particular solution on top of this same skeleton.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Natural frequency and damping

Problem. For \( m=1,\ k=100,\ c=8 \), find \( \omega_n \) and \( \zeta \), and state the regime.

Solution. \( \omega_n=\sqrt{k/m}=\sqrt{100}=10 \) rad/s. Critical damping \( c_c=2\sqrt{mk}=2\sqrt{100}=20 \), so \( \zeta=c/c_c=8/20=0.4 \). Since \( \zeta<1 \), the system is underdamped — it oscillates as it decays.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Complex roots

Problem. For \( x''+2x'+5x=0 \), find the roots and the damped frequency.

Solution. \( \Delta=4-20=-16<0 \). Roots \( r=\dfrac{-2\pm\sqrt{-16}}{2}=-1\pm2i \). Underdamped: decay rate \( \sigma=1 \), damped frequency \( \omega_d=2 \), so \( x=e^{-t}(A\cos2t+B\sin2t) \).

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

When a maintenance team tunes a machine mount or a vehicle suspension, they are choosing the damping ratio \( \zeta \). Too little damping (\( \zeta\ll1 \)) and the system rings for a long time after every bump — fatigue and noise; too much (\( \zeta>1 \)) and it responds sluggishly. The sweet spot for most equipment sits a little below critical, giving a fast return with only slight overshoot, which is why suspension and isolator datasheets quote a target \( \zeta \) near \(0.2\)–\(0.7\) rather than raw damper values.

The discriminant test is also a design guard on doors, actuators, and closers. A hydraulic door closer is deliberately set near critical damping so the door shuts quickly but does not slam or bounce — exactly \( \Delta\approx0 \). Reading a system's coefficients and computing \( \Delta \) before building it lets an engineer predict whether the finished device will crawl, snap, or ring, without a single prototype.
