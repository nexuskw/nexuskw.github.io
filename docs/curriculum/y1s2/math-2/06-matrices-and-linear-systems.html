Matrices and linear systems

Matrix algebra and Gaussian elimination. Solving real systems: truss equations, circuit equations, mass balances. Singularity as a physical warning, not just an algebraic one.

01 · A system is one matrix equation

A set of linear equations is a single object. The pair \( 2x+y=5,\ x+3y=10 \) is the matrix equation \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \) with

\[ A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}. \]

with unknown vector \( \mathbf{x}=(x,\,y)^\top \) and right-hand side \( \mathbf{b}=(5,\,10)^\top \). The coefficients become the matrix, the unknowns a vector, the right-hand sides another vector. This packaging is what lets a system of two equations and one of two hundred be reasoned about with the same tools — the truss with its joint-equilibrium equations, the circuit with its node equations, the mixing plant with its mass balances all arrive as \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \). The product \( A\mathbf{x} \) dots each row of \(A\) into \(\mathbf{x}\); checking the solution \( (1,3) \) gives \( 2(1)+1(3)=5 \) and \( 1(1)+3(3)=10 \), reproducing \(\mathbf{b}\).

02 · Determinant, inverse, and a unique answer

For a \( 2\times2 \) matrix the determinant \( \det A=ad-bc \) decides solvability in one number: nonzero means exactly one solution exists for every \(\mathbf{b}\); zero means the matrix is singular and the system has either none or infinitely many. Here \( \det A=2(3)-1(1)=5\neq0 \). When it is nonzero the inverse solves the system in closed form as \( \mathbf{x}=A^{-1}\mathbf{b} \) (for a \(2\times2\), \( A^{-1}=\tfrac{1}{ad-bc} \) times the swap-and-negate of \(A\)), so here

\[ A^{-1}=\frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix}3&-1\\-1&2\end{pmatrix}. \]

and multiplying by \( \mathbf{b}=(5,10) \) returns \( (1,3) \). For large systems the inverse is too expensive; the method that scales is elimination.

03 · Gaussian elimination

Gaussian elimination uses three solution-preserving row operations — swap two rows, scale a row, add a multiple of one row to another — to carve the system into an upper-triangular staircase, then reads the unknowns by back-substitution from the bottom. Solve the system

\[ \begin{cases} x+y+z=6\\ 2x+y-z=1\\ x-y+2z=5 \end{cases} \]

Eliminating downward gives \( 7z=21 \Rightarrow z=3 \), then \( -y-3z=-11\Rightarrow y=2 \), then \( x+2+3=6\Rightarrow x=1 \). The solution \( (1,2,3) \) checks in all three equations. This single algorithm is what every computational solver, and every matrix inversion and determinant, is built on.

04 · Singularity is a physical warning

The nonzero leading entries elimination produces are the pivots, and their count is the rank — the number of genuinely independent equations. Three pivots in a \( 3\times3 \) system mean a unique solution; a missing pivot means a row collapsed to \( 0=0 \) (a redundant equation, infinitely many solutions) or \( 0=c \) (a contradiction, none). A matrix whose rows are \( (1,2) \) and \( (2,4) \) has \( \det=1(4)-2(2)=0 \) and rank 1 — the second row is exactly twice the first. In engineering this is not mere algebra: a singular stiffness matrix means a structure that is a mechanism, free to move without resistance; a singular circuit matrix means an under-determined network. Singularity is the mathematics warning you that the physical system is not properly constrained.

Key test — determinant & rank

\( \det A\neq0 \Rightarrow \) unique solution (full rank). \( \det A=0 \Rightarrow \) singular: a mechanism / under-constrained system, no unique answer.

05 · The working method

To solve any linear system: (1) write it as \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \); (2) for a small system compute \( \det A \) — if zero, stop and diagnose the physical under-constraint; (3) reduce by elimination to a triangle, taking the largest available pivot at each step for numerical stability, and back-substitute; (4) count the pivots to confirm the rank and therefore that the answer is unique. Reading a system as one matrix, and reading a zero determinant as a physical alarm rather than an arithmetic dead end, is the habit the eigenvalue lesson and all of later structural and control analysis are built upon.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Determinant and solution

Problem. For \( A \) with rows \( (2,1) \) and \( (1,3) \), and \( \mathbf{b}=(5,10)^\top \), find \( \det A \) and solve \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \).

Solution. \( \det A=2(3)-1(1)=5\neq0 \), so a unique solution exists. By Cramer, \( x=\dfrac{5\cdot3-1\cdot10}{5}=\dfrac{15-10}{5}=1 \) and \( y=\dfrac{2\cdot10-1\cdot5}{5}=\dfrac{20-5}{5}=3 \). Check: \( 2(1)+3=5 \), \( 1+3(3)=10 \) ✓.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

3×3 by elimination

Problem. Solve the system:

\[ \begin{cases} x+y+z=6\\ 2x+y-z=1\\ x-y+2z=5 \end{cases} \]

Solution. R2−2R1: \( -y-3z=-11 \). R3−R1: \( -2y+z=-1 \). Eliminate \(y\): \( 7z=21\Rightarrow z=3 \). Back-substitute: \( -y-9=-11\Rightarrow y=2 \); \( x+2+3=6\Rightarrow x=1 \). Solution \( (1,2,3) \), verified in all three equations (6, 1, 5).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Singularity as a warning

Problem. Show that the matrix with rows \( (1,2) \) and \( (2,4) \) is singular and say what that means physically.

Solution. \( \det=1(4)-2(2)=0 \), so the matrix is singular (rank 1 — the second row is twice the first). The two equations are the same line: no unique solution. Physically, a singular stiffness matrix is a mechanism — a structure free to move without resistance, i.e. improperly constrained.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A structural engineer analysing a steel frame assembles a stiffness matrix \(K\) and solves \( K\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{F} \) for the joint displacements under load. The determinant is checked first for a reason: if \( \det K=0 \), the software returns no unique displacement, and that is not a numerical glitch — it means the frame, as modelled, is a mechanism that would collapse or drift because a support or brace is missing. The zero determinant on the screen is the algebra reporting a real buildability defect before any steel is cut.

The same \( A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \) governs a plant's mass balances and a building's electrical load flow: nodes conserve what enters and leaves, producing one equation per node. Gaussian elimination is exactly what the balancing software runs, and a near-singular system — rows almost proportional — warns that two measurements are redundant or two loops are effectively the same, so the network is being over-described and cannot be pinned down uniquely.
