Curriculum / MTH 151

MTH 151 · YEAR 1 · SEMESTER 2

Engineering Mathematics II

Multivariable calculus, linear algebra, and second-order differential equations — the mathematics that vibrations, control systems, and heat transfer will spend the next three years consuming.

11 lessons11 of 11 at full depth11 interactive quizzes
Start lesson 01 Course summary (PDF) Full curriculum

What you'll learn

✓Dot and cross products with their physical readings: work and moment.
✓Functions of several variables and their partial rates.
✓Multivariable chain rule and the total differential.
✓Double and triple integrals over regions.
✓Vector fields as flow pictures.
✓Matrix algebra and Gaussian elimination.

Syllabus

01

Vectors and vector geometryFull lesson

Dot and cross products with their physical readings: work and moment. Lines and planes in space. The geometry underneath 3-D statics revisited with better tools.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.02 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • What do the dot and cross products each measure physically — one plant example apiece?
  • Find the moment of a force about an axis: which product combination does it?
02

Partial derivativesFull lesson

Functions of several variables and their partial rates. The gradient as steepest ascent. Sensitivity thinking: which input moves the output most.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.02 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • What does the gradient's direction mean on a temperature field?
  • Which partial derivative answers 'which input moves the output most'?
03

Chain rule and total differentialsFull lesson

Multivariable chain rule and the total differential. Error propagation through a formula — the uncertainty calculation every measurement report owes. Linearization around an operating point.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.02 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Propagate ±2% flow and ±1% head errors into pump power: which dominates and why?
  • What does linearization around an operating point throw away?
04

Multiple integralsFull lesson

Double and triple integrals over regions. Mass, centroid, and moment of inertia of solid parts computed properly. Order of integration chosen for sanity.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.02 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Set up (don't solve) the triple integral for a stepped shaft's mass.
  • Why does integration order sometimes make an impossible integral easy?
05

Vector fields, flux, and circulationFull lesson

Vector fields as flow pictures. Line integrals and work; surface integrals and flux. The divergence and curl read physically — sources and swirl.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.02 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Flux through a surface: what does a zero answer mean physically?
  • Read divergence and curl as statements about sources and swirl.
06

Matrices and linear systemsFull lesson

Matrix algebra and Gaussian elimination. Solving real systems: truss equations, circuit equations, mass balances. Singularity as a physical warning, not just an algebraic one.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶ / 18.06 materials

Checkpoint questions
  • What does a singular coefficient matrix signal about your truss model?
  • Why does Gaussian elimination beat matrix inversion for one solve?
07

Eigenvalues and eigenvectorsFull lesson

The eigenproblem stated and solved for small systems. Eigenvalues as natural rates and frequencies. The concept that becomes mode shapes in Vibrations.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • What physical quantity do eigenvalues become in vibrating systems?
  • Why do eigenvectors define directions the system 'prefers'?
08

Second-order linear ODEsFull lesson

Homogeneous solutions: overdamped, critically damped, underdamped. The characteristic equation as the system's fingerprint. Mass-spring-damper solved completely — memorize this solution's shape.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Classify overdamped, critical, underdamped by the characteristic roots.
  • Sketch the underdamped step response and label its envelope.
09

Forced oscillations and resonanceFull lesson

Particular solutions for harmonic forcing. Amplitude and phase versus frequency; resonance and its danger. The mathematical skeleton of every vibration spectrum you will ever read.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does response amplitude peak near the natural frequency?
  • What happens to phase as forcing sweeps through resonance?
10

Laplace transformsFull lesson

Transform pairs and properties; solving ODEs by algebra. Transfer functions previewed. The s-domain as the control engineer's home ground.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does the Laplace transform turn an ODE into algebra?
  • What does a pole's location say about a response's shape?
11

Fourier series first contactFull lesson

Periodic signals decomposed into harmonics. Spectra of square and sawtooth waves computed by hand. Why a vibration analyst's FFT screen shows lines where the machine has defects.

Taught from — MIT OCW 18.03 Watch lectures ▶

Checkpoint questions
  • Why does a square wave contain only odd harmonics?
  • Connect Fourier series to the lines on a vibration analyzer's screen.
  • MIT OCW 18.02 Watch lectures ▶ Multivariable Calculus; 18.03 Differential Equations Watch lectures ▶ (ocw.mit.edu)
