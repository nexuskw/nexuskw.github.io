Multiple integrals

Double and triple integrals over regions. Mass, centroid, and moment of inertia of solid parts computed properly. Order of integration chosen for sanity.

01 · Integrating over a region

A single integral accumulates along a line; a double integral \( \iint_R f\,dA \) accumulates over a two-dimensional region \( R \), summing the value of \( f \) on every infinitesimal tile \( dA=dx\,dy \). With \( f=1 \) it returns the plain area of \( R \); with \( f \) a height it returns a volume; with \( f \) a density it returns a mass. The practical key is Fubini's theorem: a double integral is computed as an iterated integral, one variable then the other, and for a well-behaved region the order may be swapped. Over a rectangle the two directions even separate: \( \iint_{[0,2]\times[0,3]}xy\,dA=\Big(\int_0^2 x\,dx\Big)\Big(\int_0^3 y\,dy\Big)=(2)(4.5)=9 \).

02 · Mass and centroid

Give the region a density \( \rho \) and the same integral computes physical properties of a plate or lamina. The mass is \( m=\iint_R \rho\,dA \), and the centroid — the balance point — is the density-weighted average position, \( \bar{x}=\dfrac{1}{m}\iint_R x\,\rho\,dA \), with \( \bar{y} \) similar. For a uniform rectangle \( [0,2]\times[0,3] \) the centroid sits at its geometric centre \( (1,\,1.5) \), as symmetry demands. For a right triangle with legs \( b,h \) the area is \( bh/2 \) and the centroid lands at \( (b/3,\,h/3) \) — one-third of the way from the right-angle along each leg, a result worth memorising because triangular cross-sections are everywhere in structures.

Key formulas — mass & centroid

\[ m=\iint_R \rho\,dA,\qquad \bar{x}=\frac{1}{m}\iint_R x\,\rho\,dA,\qquad \bar{y}=\frac{1}{m}\iint_R y\,\rho\,dA \]

03 · Moment of inertia

Weight the area by the square of distance from an axis and the integral becomes the second moment of area (area moment of inertia) — the geometric quantity that sets a beam's stiffness and a shaft's resistance to twist. About its base, a rectangle of width \( b \) and height \( h \) has \( I=\iint y^2\,dA=\int_0^h\!\int_0^b y^2\,dx\,dy=\dfrac{bh^3}{3} \); for \( b=3,\ h=2 \) this is \( 8 \). About the centroidal axis the value drops to \( \dfrac{bh^3}{12}=2 \). The two are linked by the parallel-axis theorem \( I=I_c+Ad^2 \): here \( I_c+Ad^2=2+6(1)^2=8 \), recovering the base value. The cubic dependence on \( h \) is why depth, far more than width, buys bending stiffness.

Key results — second moment of area

Rectangle: \( I_{\text{base}}=\dfrac{bh^3}{3},\quad I_{\text{centroid}}=\dfrac{bh^3}{12} \). Parallel axis: \( I=I_c+Ad^2 \).

04 · Triple integrals and the method

Adding a third direction gives the triple integral \( \iiint_V f\,dV \) over a solid: with \( f=1 \) it is the volume, with \( f=\rho \) the mass, with \( f=\rho\,r^2 \) the mass moment of inertia of a real three-dimensional part. A box \( [0,1]\times[0,2]\times[0,3] \) has volume \( 1\cdot2\cdot3=6 \). The method for every case is one habit: decide what quantity sits inside the integral (\(1\), a height, a density, or a squared distance), set the limits by sweeping the region in a sensible order, integrate one variable at a time holding the rest fixed, and check the units of the answer. Choosing the integration order to match the region — and, in the next lesson, choosing the right coordinates — is what turns a hard region into an easy calculation.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

A separable double integral

Problem. Evaluate \( \displaystyle\int_0^3\!\!\int_0^2 xy\,dx\,dy \).

Solution. Inner: \( \int_0^2 xy\,dx=y\big[\tfrac{x^2}{2}\big]_0^2=2y \). Outer: \( \int_0^3 2y\,dy=\big[y^2\big]_0^3=9 \). Over a rectangle the factors separate: \( (\int_0^2 x\,dx)(\int_0^3 y\,dy)=(2)(4.5)=9 \).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Second moment of area

Problem. A rectangle has width \( b=3 \), height \( h=2 \). Find its second moment of area about the base and about the centroid, and check with the parallel-axis theorem.

Solution. About the base \( I=\iint y^2\,dA=\dfrac{bh^3}{3}=\dfrac{3\cdot8}{3}=8 \). About the centroid \( I_c=\dfrac{bh^3}{12}=\dfrac{24}{12}=2 \). Check: \( A=6 \), \( d=h/2=1 \), so \( I_c+Ad^2=2+6(1)=8 \) ✓.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Centroid of a triangle

Problem. Find the area and centroid of a right triangle with legs \( b=3 \) (along \(x\)) and \( h=6 \) (along \(y\)), right angle at the origin.

Solution. Area \( =\tfrac12 bh=\tfrac12(3)(6)=9 \). The centroid of a triangle is one-third of the way from the right angle along each leg: \( \bar x=b/3=1 \), \( \bar y=h/3=2 \). So \( (\bar x,\bar y)=(1,2) \).

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A structural draftsman selecting a floor beam relies directly on the second moment of area: a beam's stiffness scales as \( bh^3/12 \), so the depth \(h\) enters cubed while the width \(b\) enters only linearly. Doubling the depth stiffens the beam eightfold; doubling the width merely doubles it. That single integral result is why rolled sections are deep and thin rather than square, and why an I-beam pushes its material as far from the neutral axis as the geometry allows — the \( y^2 \) weight in \( \iint y^2\,dA \) rewards distance from the axis above all.

The centroid integral does the balancing. When a fabricator lifts an irregular steel plate with a single sling, the lift point must sit over the centroid \( \bar x=\frac1A\iint x\,dA \), or the plate swings and tilts. For composite plates the same rule is applied piecewise — locate each simple piece's centroid, then take the area-weighted average — which is the everyday shop version of evaluating the integral without calculus.
