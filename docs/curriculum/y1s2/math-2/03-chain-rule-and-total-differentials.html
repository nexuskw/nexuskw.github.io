Chain rule and total differentials

01 · The total differential

The partials of the last lesson combine into the total differential, the exact statement of how a function changes when all its inputs move together: \( df=f_x\,dx+f_y\,dy \). It is the linear approximation written as an identity between infinitesimals, and it is the workhorse for everything that follows — chain rules, error analysis, and the exactness tests of differential equations all start here. Read it plainly: the change in \( f \) is the sum of each input's change times how strongly \( f \) responds to that input. For a cylinder volume \( V=\pi r^2h \), \( dV=2\pi r h\,dr+\pi r^2\,dh \) — the first term is the volume gained by growing the radius, the second by growing the height.

02 · The chain rule along one variable

When \( x \) and \( y \) are themselves functions of a single driver \( t \), the function \( f(x,y) \) becomes a function of \( t \) alone, and its rate is the sum over both paths in the tree above: \( \dfrac{df}{dt}=f_x\dfrac{dx}{dt}+f_y\dfrac{dy}{dt} \). Each term is a path from \( f \) down to \( t \); you multiply the links along a path and add the paths. Take \( f=xy \) with \( x=t,\ y=t^2 \): then \( f_x=y=t^2,\ f_y=x=t \), and with \( x'=1,\ y'=2t \), \( \dfrac{df}{dt}=t^2(1)+t(2t)=3t^2 \). This matches differentiating \( f=t\cdot t^2=t^3 \) directly, \(=3t^2\) — a built-in check that the path-sum bookkeeping is right.

Key rule — the chain rule

\[ \frac{df}{dt}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt} \]

One term per path from \(f\) to the driver; multiply the links along a path, sum the paths.

03 · Several independent variables

If the intermediate variables depend on more than one driver — \( x=x(s,t),\ y=y(s,t) \) — the same path-sum gives a partial derivative for each driver, holding the others fixed: \( \dfrac{\partial z}{\partial s}=f_x\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial s}+f_y\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial s} \). For \( z=x^2+y^2 \) with \( x=s+t,\ y=s-t \): \( z_s=2x(1)+2y(1)=2(s+t)+2(s-t)=4s \), and likewise \( z_t=4t \). The tree simply grows another level, but the rule is unchanged: enumerate the paths to the driver in question, and add. This is precisely how a change of coordinates — Cartesian to polar, say — propagates derivatives, which heat transfer and fluid mechanics lean on constantly.

04 · Error propagation, the everyday payoff

The total differential is the engineer's error-propagation law. If each input carries an uncertainty, a worst-case bound adds the magnitudes: \( |\Delta f|\le|f_x||\Delta x|+|f_y||\Delta y| \). Dividing the cylinder's differential by \( V \) gives the tidy relative form \( \dfrac{dV}{V}=2\dfrac{dr}{r}+\dfrac{dh}{h} \) — the radius counts double because it is squared. With \( dr/r=0.5\% \) and \( dh/h=0.4\% \), the volume is uncertain by \( 2(0.5)+0.4=1.4\% \). But when the errors are independent random scatter rather than systematic, they combine in quadrature — root-sum-square — because unrelated errors partly cancel: \( \dfrac{\sigma_V}{V}=\sqrt{(2\cdot0.5\%)^2+(0.4\%)^2}\approx1.08\% \), smaller than the worst case. Knowing which combination applies is the difference between an honest tolerance and a padded one.

Key formulas — error propagation

Worst case: \( |\Delta f|\le|f_x||\Delta x|+|f_y||\Delta y| \). Independent random: \( \sigma_f=\sqrt{(f_x\sigma_x)^2+(f_y\sigma_y)^2} \).

05 · The working method

The routine is short. To differentiate a composite, draw the dependency tree, write one product-term per path from the function to the driver, and sum. To propagate uncertainty, take the total differential, divide by \( f \) if a relative error is wanted, and then either add magnitudes (worst case, correlated or unknown errors) or root-sum-square (independent random errors). The recurring engineering lesson is that a squared or cubed variable carries its exponent as a multiplier into the error budget — so the dimension measured least carefully, weighted by its exponent, is usually the one that dominates the tolerance, and the one worth a better instrument.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Chain rule, one driver

Problem. With \( f=xy \), \( x=t \), \( y=t^2 \), find \( df/dt \) two ways and evaluate at \( t=2 \).

Solution. Chain rule: \( f_x=y=t^2 \), \( f_y=x=t \), \( x'=1 \), \( y'=2t \), so \( df/dt=t^2(1)+t(2t)=3t^2 \). Directly: \( f=t\cdot t^2=t^3 \Rightarrow df/dt=3t^2 \). At \( t=2 \), \( df/dt=12 \).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Total differential of a volume

Problem. A cylinder has \( r=2 \) m, \( h=5 \) m. Estimate \( dV \) for \( dr=0.01 \) m, \( dh=0.02 \) m, and give the relative change.

Solution. \( dV=2\pi r h\,dr+\pi r^2\,dh=2\pi(2)(5)(0.01)+\pi(4)(0.02)=0.2\pi+0.08\pi=0.28\pi\approx0.880 \) m³. Relative: \( \dfrac{dV}{V}=2\dfrac{dr}{r}+\dfrac{dh}{h}=2(0.005)+0.004=0.014=1.4\% \).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Independent-error quadrature

Problem. For the same cylinder, \( r \) and \( h \) are measured with independent random relative uncertainties \(0.5\%\) and \(0.4\%\). Find the resulting relative uncertainty in \(V\).

Solution. The radius contributes with weight 2 (it is squared). Independent errors add in quadrature: \( \dfrac{\sigma_V}{V}=\sqrt{(2\cdot0.5\%)^2+(0.4\%)^2}=\sqrt{(1.0\%)^2+(0.4\%)^2}=\sqrt{1.16}\,\%\approx1.08\% \) — below the \(1.4\%\) worst case, because unrelated errors partly cancel.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A metrology technician certifying a machined shaft measures its diameter and length to compute volume or mass. Because volume goes as the diameter squared, the total-differential rule \( dV/V=2\,dd/d+dL/L \) warns that any error in the diameter is doubled in the result — so the caliper on the diameter must be twice as trustworthy as the tape on the length to reach the same volume tolerance. This is why inspection plans spend their tightest gauges on the dimensions that enter with the highest exponent; it is error propagation setting the measurement budget, not guesswork.

The quadrature rule decides how a stack of independent tolerances accumulates. When several machined parts assemble in series, their independent gap errors do not simply add to a worst case — they combine root-sum-square, so a five-part stack of \(\pm0.1\) mm features scatters by about \( \sqrt5\times0.1\approx0.22 \) mm, not \(0.5\) mm. Designing to the honest statistical stack rather than the pessimistic arithmetic one is what keeps assemblies both reliable and affordable.
