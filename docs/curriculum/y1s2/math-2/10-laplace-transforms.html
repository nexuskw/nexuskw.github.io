Laplace transforms

01 · What the transform is for

The Laplace transform \( \mathcal{L}\{f\}=F(s)=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-st}f(t)\,dt \) trades a function of time for a function of a new variable \( s \). Its purpose is not the integral but its consequence: differentiation in \( t \) becomes multiplication in \( s \). A differential equation therefore turns into an ordinary algebraic equation in \( F(s) \), which you solve by rearrangement rather than calculus. It is the same strategic move as the \( e^{rt} \) guess of Lesson 8, generalised — and it pays off most when the forcing is awkward: a switch thrown, a step applied, an impulse struck, all handled by the same bookkeeping.

02 · The table and the derivative rule

A handful of transforms, plus two rules, cover most problems: \( \mathcal{L}\{1\}=\tfrac1s \), \( \mathcal{L}\{e^{at}\}=\tfrac{1}{s-a} \), \( \mathcal{L}\{t\}=\tfrac{1}{s^2} \), and \( \mathcal{L}\{\sin\omega t\}=\tfrac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2} \), so \( \mathcal{L}\{\sin4t\}=\tfrac{4}{s^2+16} \). Linearity lets you transform term by term, and the derivative rule is the engine:

\[ \mathcal{L}\{y'\}=sY-y(0),\qquad \mathcal{L}\{y''\}=s^2Y-sy(0)-y'(0). \]

Read what this does: each derivative becomes a power of \( s \), and the initial conditions are folded in automatically at the transform step — no separate constant-fixing stage to forget.

Key rule — transform & solve

Method: transform every term (\( \mathcal{L}\{y'\}=sY-y(0) \)) → solve the algebra for \( Y(s) \) → invert with the table. Differentiation in \(t\) becomes multiplication by \(s\).

03 · Solving an equation by algebra

The method is three steps: transform, solve for \( Y(s) \), invert. Take \( y'+2y=0,\ y(0)=3 \): transforming gives \( sY-3+2Y=0 \), so \( Y=\tfrac{3}{s+2} \), and the table inverts this to \( y=3e^{-2t} \). A forced example \( y'+3y=6,\ y(0)=0 \): transforming gives \( sY+3Y=\tfrac6s \), so \( Y=\tfrac{6}{s(s+3)} \); splitting by partial fractions into \( \tfrac2s-\tfrac{2}{s+3} \) inverts to \( y=2-2e^{-3t} \). The constant \(2\) is the steady state, the \(-2e^{-3t}\) the transient — the whole solution, initial condition included, produced by algebra.

04 · The s-domain and the method

The ratio of output transform to input transform, with zero initial conditions, is the transfer function — the compact description of a system in the \( s \)-domain that control engineering is built on. The location of its poles (the roots of the denominator) is the system's stability: poles with negative real part mean decay, imaginary poles mean oscillation, exactly the eigenvalue reading of Lesson 7 in another language. The working method never changes: (1) transform every term, folding in initial data; (2) solve the algebraic equation for \( Y(s) \); (3) break \( Y(s) \) into table pieces by partial fractions and invert. Master this on the smooth cases here, and the same recipe handles the switched and impulsive inputs that real systems face.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

From the table

Problem. Find \( \mathcal{L}\{3+2t\} \) and \( \mathcal{L}\{e^{3t}\} \).

Solution. By linearity \( \mathcal{L}\{3+2t\}=\dfrac{3}{s}+\dfrac{2}{s^2} \). From the exponential pair \( \mathcal{L}\{e^{3t}\}=\dfrac{1}{s-3} \) (for \(s>3\)).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Solve an IVP

Problem. Solve \( y'+2y=0 \), \( y(0)=3 \) using the Laplace transform.

Solution. Transform: \( sY-3+2Y=0\Rightarrow (s+2)Y=3 \), so \( Y=\dfrac{3}{s+2} \). Invert: \( y=3e^{-2t} \). Check: \( y(0)=3 \) ✓, \( y'=-6e^{-2t}=-2y \) ✓.

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Forced first-order

Problem. Solve \( y'+3y=6 \), \( y(0)=0 \), and identify the steady state.

Solution. Transform: \( sY+3Y=\dfrac6s\Rightarrow Y=\dfrac{6}{s(s+3)} \). Partial fractions: \( \dfrac{2}{s}-\dfrac{2}{s+3} \). Invert: \( y=2-2e^{-3t} \). Steady state \(=2\); the transient \(-2e^{-3t}\) carries \(y\) from \(0\) up to \(2\).

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A controls or instrumentation engineer lives in the s-domain. When tuning a temperature loop or a level controller, the process and the controller are each written as transfer functions — output-over-input in \(s\) — and the loop's behaviour is read from where the combined poles sit. Poles in the left half-plane (negative real part) mean the loop settles; a pole crossing to the right means it will hunt or run away. The Laplace method is what turns a plant's differential equations into those algebraic transfer functions in the first place, which is why every controller datasheet and tuning rule is stated in \(s\).

The step-input case is the everyday test. Bumping a setpoint and watching the response is applying a step \( \tfrac{1}{s} \); the observed rise and settling are the inverse transform of the transfer function times that step. Reading whether the response is sluggish, snappy, or oscillatory — and adjusting the controller to fix it — is exactly the transform-solve-invert cycle of this lesson, run on real hardware.
