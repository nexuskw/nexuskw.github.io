Vector fields, flux, and circulation

Vector fields as flow pictures. Line integrals and work; surface integrals and flux. The divergence and curl read physically — sources and swirl.

01 · Fields: a vector at every point

A vector field \( \mathbf{F}(x,y)=(P,Q) \) assigns a vector to each point of the plane — a picture of flow. The velocity of water in a channel, the force on a charge in space, the heat flux through a wall are all vector fields, and the calculus of this lesson is how you extract physical quantities from them: the work a field does along a path, the flow across a boundary, and the local tendencies to spread or to rotate. Two derived scalars, the divergence and the curl, summarise a field's local behaviour completely, and both are built from the partial derivatives of \( P \) and \( Q \) you already know.

02 · Line integrals: work and circulation

Integrating a field along a curve \( C \) accumulates the component of \( \mathbf{F} \) that lies along the path: \( \displaystyle\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} \). When \( \mathbf{F} \) is a force this is the work done moving along \( C \); when \( C \) is a closed loop it is the circulation, the net "push around the loop." For a constant field \( \mathbf{F}=(3,4) \) along the straight segment from \( (0,0) \) to \( (2,0) \), only the \(x\)-component acts and \( W=3(2)=6 \). For \( \mathbf{F}=(x,y) \) along the diagonal from \( (0,0) \) to \( (1,1) \), parametrise \( \mathbf{r}=(t,t) \): then \( \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=2t\,dt \) and \( \int_0^1 2t\,dt=1 \). The line integral is just the dot product of Lesson 1, summed continuously along a path.

03 · Flux: flow across a boundary

Rotate the question ninety degrees and ask how much field crosses a curve rather than runs along it — the flux \( \displaystyle\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,ds \), with \( \mathbf{n} \) the outward normal. Flux measures throughput: the water leaving a region, the heat escaping a surface, the current through a loop. Where circulation uses the component along the boundary, flux uses the component through it — the same field, read with the tangential versus the normal direction. These two integrals, circulation and flux, are the complete pair of things you can ask of a field on a boundary, and each connects to a local density in the interior.

04 · Divergence: sources and sinks

The local density behind flux is the divergence: \( \operatorname{div}\mathbf{F}=\dfrac{\partial P}{\partial x}+\dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial y} \), the net outflow per unit area at a point. A positive divergence marks a source (field spreading outward), a negative one a sink. For \( \mathbf{F}=(x,y) \), \( \operatorname{div}\mathbf{F}=1+1=2 \) everywhere — a uniform source, arrows radiating outward as in the left panel. For the rotation field \( \mathbf{F}=(-y,x) \), \( \operatorname{div}\mathbf{F}=0 \): nothing is created, the flow is incompressible. Zero divergence is exactly the condition that makes a fluid conserve volume, which is why it is the governing equation of incompressible flow.

Key operator — divergence

\[ \operatorname{div}\mathbf{F}=\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} \]

Net outflow per area. Positive ⇒ source, negative ⇒ sink, zero ⇒ incompressible.

05 · Curl: swirl and rotation

The local density behind circulation is the curl: in the plane, \( \operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F}=\dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\dfrac{\partial P}{\partial y} \), the swirl per unit area. For \( \mathbf{F}=(-y,x) \), \( \operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F}=1-(-1)=2 \): a uniform counter-clockwise rotation, the right panel. For the source field \( \mathbf{F}=(x,y) \), \( \operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F}=0 \) — pure spreading with no rotation. A field with zero curl everywhere is called irrotational, and it is precisely the condition for the field to be a gradient \( \nabla f \) of some potential, tying this lesson back to Lesson 2. Divergence and curl are the two independent things a field can do locally: spread, and spin.

Key operator — curl (2-D)

\[ \operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F}=\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \]

Swirl per area. Zero ⇒ irrotational (a gradient field). Nonzero ⇒ local rotation.

06 · Green's theorem and the method

The boundary integrals and the interior densities are linked by Green's theorem: the circulation around a closed curve equals the integrated curl inside it, \( \oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\iint_R \operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F}\,dA \), and likewise total flux equals integrated divergence. This turns a hard boundary integral into an easy area integral, or vice versa. So the rotation field \( (-y,x) \) circulating around the unit circle gives \( \iint 2\,dA=2\pi \). The working method: to read a field, compute \( \operatorname{div} \) (does it source?) and \( \operatorname{curl} \) (does it spin?); to accumulate work or flow, set up the line or flux integral, and invoke Green's theorem whenever swapping a boundary for an interior makes the arithmetic easier. This is the vector calculus that fluid mechanics and electromagnetism are written in.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Divergence and curl of a source

Problem. For \( \mathbf{F}=(x,y) \), compute the divergence and curl, and describe the field.

Solution. \( \operatorname{div}\mathbf{F}=\partial_x x+\partial_y y=1+1=2 \) and \( \operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F}=\partial_x y-\partial_y x=0-0=0 \). The field is a uniform source (arrows radiate outward, divergence \(2\)) with no rotation (irrotational).

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Divergence and curl of a rotation

Problem. For \( \mathbf{F}=(-y,x) \), compute the divergence and curl, and describe the field.

Solution. \( \operatorname{div}\mathbf{F}=\partial_x(-y)+\partial_y(x)=0+0=0 \) and \( \operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F}=\partial_x(x)-\partial_y(-y)=1-(-1)=2 \). The field is incompressible (no source) and rotates counter-clockwise with uniform swirl \(2\).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Work as a line integral

Problem. Find the work done by \( \mathbf{F}=(x,y) \) along the straight path from \( (0,0) \) to \( (1,1) \).

Solution. Parametrise \( \mathbf{r}(t)=(t,t) \), \( 0\le t\le1 \), so \( d\mathbf{r}=(1,1)\,dt \) and \( \mathbf{F}=(t,t) \). Then \( \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=(t+t)\,dt=2t\,dt \), and \( \int_0^1 2t\,dt=\big[t^2\big]_0^1=1 \).

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

An HVAC or process engineer balancing a duct network is really enforcing zero divergence: whatever mass of air flows into a junction must flow out, so \( \operatorname{div}\mathbf{F}=0 \) at every node. When a survey finds a node where the measured inflow and outflow do not match — a nonzero divergence — it is a physical alarm: an unmetered leak, a blocked branch, or a faulty sensor. The mathematics that looks abstract on the page is, on the plant floor, the mass balance an instrument technician chases down.

Curl shows up wherever rotation is diagnosed. In a stirred mixing vessel or a pump volute, the swirl of the flow is a curl field, and too much of it near a pump inlet promotes the vortexing that starves the pump and invites cavitation. Reading a flow map for regions of high curl is how a rotating-equipment engineer spots the recirculation zones that wear impellers — the same operator that, in the next courses, becomes the vorticity of fluid mechanics and the rotation of a rigid body in dynamics.
