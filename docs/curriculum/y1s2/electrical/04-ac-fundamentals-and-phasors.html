01 · Sinusoids and RMS values

An AC quantity \(v(t)=V_{max}\sin(\omega t)\) constantly changes — its peak value alone overstates its actual heating or working effect, while its average over a full cycle is exactly zero. RMS (root-mean-square) is defined so that an RMS AC value produces the same heating effect — the same average power in a resistor — as an equal DC value. For a pure sinusoid, \( V_{rms}=V_{max}/\sqrt2\approx0.707\,V_{max} \). This is why "230 V AC" is an RMS rating, not a peak — the honest, working-equivalent number, with the actual peak running about \(\sqrt2\) times higher, near 325 V.

02 · Phasor representation

A phasor is a rotating-vector shorthand for a sinusoid at one fixed frequency: instead of tracking \(v(t)\) through time, a phasor captures a sinusoid's magnitude and phase angle as a single vector or complex number. This turns the calculus of sinusoids — derivatives and integrals — into simple vector or complex algebra, a simplification that works because every quantity in a linear AC circuit at one frequency is itself a sinusoid at that same frequency, differing only in magnitude and phase.

03 · Impedance of R, L, C

Impedance \(Z\) generalizes resistance to AC. A resistor's impedance is simply \(Z_R=R\), no phase shift. An inductor's impedance, \(Z_L=j\omega L\), grows with frequency and shifts voltage \(90^\circ\) ahead of current. A capacitor's impedance, \(Z_C=1/(j\omega C)\), shrinks with frequency and shifts the opposite way — current ahead of voltage. Impedance adds to resistance exactly the concept resistance alone in a DC circuit never needed: a phase relationship between voltage and current, not just a magnitude ratio. Series impedances combine the same way resistances do, \(Z_{total}=Z_1+Z_2+\dots\), but as complex numbers, so magnitudes generally do not simply add unless the phase angles happen to match.

04 · The 50 Hz world, quantified

At \(f=50\ \text{Hz}\) (or 60 Hz in some countries), \( \omega=2\pi f\approx314\ \text{rad/s} \) — this single number turns \(L\) (henries) and \(C\) (farads) into actual ohms of impedance at mains frequency: \(Z_L=\omega L\), \(Z_C=1/(\omega C)\). Working method: (1) convert every element to its impedance at the operating frequency; (2) combine impedances exactly as resistances combine — series adds, parallel via reciprocals — but with complex arithmetic; (3) apply the AC form of Ohm's law, \(V=IZ\), using RMS magnitudes and phase angles as needed.

Key rating — RMS

\[ V_{rms} = \frac{V_{max}}{\sqrt2} \]

The honest, heating-equivalent rating for a sinusoid — not the peak.

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Peak to RMS

Problem. A sinusoidal voltage has a peak value of 325 V. Find its RMS value, and explain briefly why RMS, not peak, is the honest rating for this quantity.

Solution. \( V_{rms}=325/\sqrt2\approx230\ \text{V} \). RMS is the honest rating because it is defined to give the same heating/working effect as an equal-valued DC source; quoting the 325 V peak would overstate the actual effect of the voltage, since the sinusoid spends most of the cycle below that peak.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Impedance from R and X

Problem. A series RL circuit has \(R=3\ \Omega\) and inductive reactance \(X_L=4\ \Omega\) at the operating frequency. Find the impedance magnitude.

Solution. \( Z=\sqrt{R^2+X_L^2}=\sqrt{9+16}=\sqrt{25}=5\ \Omega \).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Reactance vs. frequency

Problem. An inductor has \(L=0.1\ \text{H}\). Find its reactance at 50 Hz, and state whether reactance rises or falls as frequency increases.

Solution. \( X_L=\omega L=2\pi(50)(0.1)\approx31.4\ \Omega \). Inductive reactance rises with frequency — the opposite of a capacitor, whose reactance falls as frequency increases.

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

Nameplate voltage and current ratings on virtually every piece of plant electrical equipment are RMS values by convention — a fact worth remembering when a technician sees a scope trace peaking well above the nameplate number and mistakes an entirely normal sinusoid for an overvoltage event, when the RMS reading on a properly-set meter would show the equipment operating exactly within its rated envelope.

A cable or contactor sized only by its resistive (DC-style) current rating, without accounting for the reactive impedance of a long run or an inductive load, can be under-specified in a real AC installation — the working method of 4, converting every element to its actual impedance at 50 Hz before sizing anything, is what a competent electrical design review checks for on a new installation drawing.
