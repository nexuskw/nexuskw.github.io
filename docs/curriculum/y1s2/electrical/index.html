ELE 156 · YEAR 1 · SEMESTER 2

Electrical Fundamentals for Mechanical Engineers

The electrical literacy a plant demands of its mechanical engineers: DC and AC circuits, three-phase power, transformers, and above all the induction motor — starter to nameplate to failure modes.

01

Charge, current, voltage, and power

The electrical quantities with water analogies used and then retired. Ohm's law and power dissipation. Reading a multimeter without folklore.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does power dissipate as I²R, and what does that mean for loose connections?
  • What does a multimeter's input impedance protect against?

02

DC circuit analysis

Series-parallel reduction, Kirchhoff's laws, and voltage/current dividers. Practical sources and internal resistance. Battery-fed control circuits as examples.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Two resistors in parallel: why is the result smaller than either?
  • What does Kirchhoff's voltage law conserve around a loop?

Taught from — NPTEL: Basic Electrical Technology

03

Capacitance and inductance

Energy storage in fields; V–I relations for C and L. First-order transients and time constants. Why inductive loads arc switches and need suppression.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does interrupting an inductive circuit draw an arc?
  • What does a time constant mean for a motor contactor's coil?

Taught from — NPTEL: Basic Electrical Technology

04

AC fundamentals and phasors

Sinusoids, RMS values, and phasor representation. Impedance of R, L, C and series combinations. The 50 Hz world quantified.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why is RMS the honest rating for AC quantities?
  • What does impedance add to resistance, physically?

Taught from — NPTEL: Basic Electrical Technology

05

AC power and power factor

Real, reactive, and apparent power; the power triangle. Power factor and why utilities bill for it. Correction capacitors sized in a worked example.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • A plant at power factor 0.7: what is flowing that does no work?
  • Size the correction idea: what do capacitors supply locally?

Taught from — NPTEL: Basic Electrical Technology

06

Three-phase systems

Wye and delta connections; line versus phase quantities. Three-phase power calculation. Why industrial plants are three-phase places.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Line versus phase voltage in a wye system: which ratio connects them?
  • Why is three-phase power delivery constant while single-phase pulses?

Taught from — NPTEL: Basic Electrical Technology

07

Transformers

Ideal transformer relations and real losses. Nameplate data decoded. Distribution transformers in a plant one-line diagram.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What do the turns do in a transformer, and what stays (nearly) constant?
  • Read a transformer nameplate: which two numbers matter first?

Taught from — NPTEL: Basic Electrical Technology

08

The induction motor

Rotating field, slip, and the torque–speed curve. Nameplate literacy: power, current, service factor, insulation class. The machine that drives nearly every pump and fan you will ever maintain.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Define slip, and say why an induction motor cannot run at synchronous speed.
  • What does service factor 1.15 actually permit?

Taught from — NPTEL: Basic Electrical Technology

09

Motor starting, protection, and VFDs

DOL and star-delta starting; overloads and protection classes. Variable-frequency drives in outline: what they do to the torque curve and to bearings. Motor circuit anatomy from MCC to terminal box.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • Why does star-delta starting cut inrush, and what does it cost in torque?
  • Name one thing a VFD does to bearings that DOL never did.

Taught from — NPTEL: Basic Electrical Technology

10

Electrical safety and measurements

Shock and arc-flash hazards respected properly; lockout-tagout as a mechanical engineer practices it. Insulation resistance and clamp-meter measurements. The boundary line between mechanical and electrical work scopes.

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What makes insulation resistance a trend, not a pass/fail number?
  • Where does the mechanical scope end at a motor terminal box — and why?
