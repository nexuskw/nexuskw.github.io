01 · Wye and delta connections

A wye (star) connection joins three windings or loads at a common neutral point, with each winding's other end brought out as a line — this gives access to both line-to-line and line-to-neutral voltages. A delta connection joins the three windings end-to-end in a closed loop, with each junction brought out as a line — there is no neutral point, and only line-to-line voltage exists. Line quantities, measured on the supply conductors, and phase quantities, measured across one winding or load element, are not automatically the same — the relationship between them depends entirely on which connection is used.

02 · Line vs. phase relationships

In a wye connection: \( V_{line}=\sqrt3\,V_{phase} \) — line voltage is about 1.732 times the phase voltage — while \( I_{line}=I_{phase} \), since line and winding share the same series path. The \(\sqrt3\) factor comes directly from the \(120^\circ\) phasor geometry: the vector difference between two phase voltages \(120^\circ\) apart has magnitude \(\sqrt3\) times either one's magnitude, not simply double. In a delta connection, it reverses: \( V_{line}=V_{phase} \), since each line connects directly across one winding, while \( I_{line}=\sqrt3\,I_{phase} \), since each line current is now the phasor difference of two phase currents.

03 · Three-phase power — constant delivery

Three-phase real power is given compactly by \( P=\sqrt3\,V_{line}I_{line}\,PF \), using line quantities directly regardless of whether the system is wye or delta — exactly why this is the practical field formula. A single-phase voltage and current both cross zero twice per cycle, so their instantaneous product — power — also dips to zero twice per cycle: single-phase power delivery pulses. With three phases spaced \(120^\circ\) apart, each phase's instantaneous power still pulses individually, but the three pulses stagger exactly enough that their sum is mathematically constant for a balanced load — the real reason three-phase motors run smoother than single-phase ones, with no torque pulsation traceable to the electrical supply itself.

04 · Why industrial plants are three-phase places

Three-phase delivers more power per conductor weight than an equivalent single-phase system, provides constant, non-pulsating power delivery to motors — smoother torque, less vibration, less mechanical stress — and is what allows a simple, cheap rotating magnetic field, the subject of Lesson 8, to be generated directly from the supply with no extra starting circuitry. Working method: (1) determine whether the connection is wye or delta; (2) convert between line and phase quantities using \(\sqrt3\) in the correct direction for that connection; (3) apply \(P=\sqrt3\,V_{line}I_{line}\,PF\) for total three-phase real power.

Key ratios — wye vs. delta

Wye: \( V_{line}=\sqrt3\,V_{phase} \), \( I_{line}=I_{phase} \). Delta: \( V_{line}=V_{phase} \), \( I_{line}=\sqrt3\,I_{phase} \).

Problem 1 of 11 · solve, then check

Wye line voltage

Problem. A wye-connected three-phase supply has a phase voltage of 230 V. Find the line voltage, and state which ratio connects the two.

Solution. \( V_{line}=\sqrt3\times230\approx398\ \text{V} \) (a "400 V" system, the standard companion to a 230 V phase supply). The ratio is \(\sqrt3\), from the 120° phasor geometry between adjacent phase voltages.

Problem 2 of 11 · solve, then check

Three-phase power delivered

Problem. A three-phase motor draws a line current of 20 A from a 400 V line-to-line supply at power factor 0.85. Find the real power delivered.

Solution. \( P=\sqrt3\,V_{line}I_{line}\,PF=1.732\times400\times20\times0.85\approx11{,}778\ \text{W}\approx11.8\ \text{kW} \).

Problem 3 of 11 · solve, then check

Delta line current

Problem. A delta-connected load has a phase (winding) current of 15 A. Find the line current.

Solution. \( I_{line}=\sqrt3\,I_{phase}=1.732\times15\approx26.0\ \text{A} \).

Applied case — factory floor (Kuwait)

A plant one-line diagram showing "400/230 V" on the main distribution transformer is stating a wye secondary directly: 230 V is the phase (line-to-neutral) voltage feeding single-phase loads like lighting and small equipment, while 400 V is the line-to-line voltage feeding three-phase motors and larger loads, both delivered from the exact same winding by the √3 relationship of 2 — a detail worth understanding before assuming a "400 V" and a "230 V" circuit come from two different sources.

Balanced loading across the three phases is a standing electrical-maintenance concern precisely because 3's constant-power result only holds when the load actually is balanced; a plant that has grown single-phase loads unevenly across its three phases over the years loses some of three-phase's smooth-delivery advantage and can show measurable current imbalance between phases on a clamp-meter reading, worth correcting before it shows up as uneven heating in the supply transformer.
